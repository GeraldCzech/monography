# Chapter 2 — The Economics and Psychology of Charitable Decisions

## Scientific Question

How do people make charitable decisions when organisational impact, integrity, and quality cannot be fully observed? And what epistemic and motivational structures make brands relevant to these decisions?

---

## 2.1 Why Charitable Giving Is a Hard Decision

Donating to a nonprofit organisation is, at its core, an act of resource transfer under conditions of deep uncertainty. The donor cannot directly observe whether funds are used as intended, whether programme activities produce the claimed impact, or whether the organisation will continue to operate responsibly in the future. This is not a failure of individual donors' judgment — it is a structural property of the exchange.

Economic theory provides the first vocabulary for this structure. Akerlof's (1970) analysis of markets with asymmetric information shows that when sellers possess private information that buyers cannot verify, market mechanisms break down: credible quality signals become necessary for exchange to occur at all. In charitable giving, the nonprofit organisation is the "seller" of social impact; the donor is the "buyer." But unlike market transactions, the donor has no right to enforce the transaction outcome, no feedback loop through consumption experience, and often no legal recourse. This asymmetry is more severe than in typical consumer markets.

Arrow (1963) introduced the concept of **credence goods** — goods whose quality cannot be verified even after consumption. Medical services are the canonical example: you cannot be certain that the treatment actually cured the illness rather than merely coinciding with natural recovery. Charitable giving is a credence good in the same sense: even having donated, you cannot determine whether your contribution made a marginal difference, whether it was used efficiently, or whether the reported impact accurately reflects the reality of programme delivery (Andreoni, 1990).[^2-CG-000006]

This creates a fundamental problem of motivation: why do people donate at all, given that they cannot verify the outcome of their gift?

---

## 2.2 The Impure Altruism Model and Warm Glow

The dominant economic answer to this puzzle is the **impure altruism model** developed by Andreoni (1989, 1990).[^2-IA-000001] The pure altruism hypothesis — that donors care only about the total provision of the public good — fails empirically: it predicts complete crowd-out when government or other donors provide the same good. But crowd-out is consistently found to be partial, not complete, in experimental and natural study designs (Ribar & Wilhelm, 2002).[^2-WG-000001]

The impure altruism model resolves this by introducing a **warm glow** component: private, non-pecuniary utility that the donor derives from the act of giving itself, independent of its effect on total charitable output.[^2-WG-000002] This warm glow is not purely selfish — it coexists with genuine concern for recipients — but it is private. Its utility accrues to the donor regardless of whether their contribution has a marginal impact on the funded cause.[^2-WG-000002]

The warm glow construct has been operationalised in several psychological directions. **Self-image** is one key mechanism: charitable giving activates the donor's sense of being a generous, moral, effective person (Aquino & Reed, 2002).[^2-SI-000001] This self-image motivation primarily affects participation — the decision *whether* to donate — rather than the donation amount. Donors who give primarily to maintain a positive self-image tend to give *something* across many causes rather than concentrating resources on a single cause.[^2-SI-000001]

**Social-image** is a distinct but related mechanism: charitable giving signals prosocial character to observers (Ariely et al., 2009; Harbaugh, 1998).[^2-SI-000003] Social-image motivations primarily affect the *amount* donated among individuals who have already decided to give: observable gifts, named donations, and publicly visible charity engagements generate social approval and prestige that increase the utility of giving (Andreoni & Petrie, 2004).[^2-SI-000002] This explains the effectiveness of naming rights, public donor recognition, and social media-visible charity campaigns.

A third direction is the **psychological distance** between earned and windfall income. Experimental evidence suggests that donating personally earned funds generates greater warm glow utility than donating equivalent windfall amounts — a finding with direct implications for fundraising strategy (Cherry et al., 2002).[^2-WG-000003]

## 2.3 Bounded Rationality and Heuristic Processing in Giving

The economic framing of warm glow makes an implicit assumption: that donors, while partly selfish, are sufficiently rational to compute the utility consequences of their giving decisions. Behavioural science qualifies this substantially.

Simon's (1955) concept of **bounded rationality** holds that decision-makers operate with limited cognitive resources, limited information, and limited time. Rather than maximising expected utility, they use **heuristics** — simplified decision rules that are computationally tractable but systematically diverge from optimal choice (Kahneman, 2011; Tversky & Kahneman, 1974).

In charitable giving, several heuristics have documented behavioural effects. The **identifiable victim effect** (Small & Loewenstein, 2003) shows that donations to specific, named individuals are substantially higher than donations to statistical populations, even when the latter represent larger aggregates of suffering. The **social proof heuristic** shows that providing information about what other donors gave serves as a reference point that increases average donation amounts among high-capacity donors (Martin & Randal, 2008). The **administrative cost heuristic** shows that donors use reported overhead ratios as a proxy for organisational efficiency, even when this proxy is theoretically and empirically unreliable (Lecy & Searing, 2015).

What these heuristics share is a common structure: they substitute simple, observable cues for complex, unobservable information. Donors cannot evaluate programme effectiveness directly, so they use a cue — a victim's face, a reference gift, an overhead ratio — as a proxy for the unobservable.

**Nonprofit brands function as heuristics of exactly this type.** A brand condenses the complex, unobservable attributes of an organisation — integrity, effectiveness, trustworthiness, mission alignment — into a cognitively manageable signal. Strong brands reduce the cognitive effort required to make a giving decision and increase the donor's confidence in that decision.[^2-BA-000081]

---

## 2.4 Trust as the Central Governance Mechanism

If charitable giving is structurally analogous to a credence good transaction, and if donors use heuristics to navigate information asymmetry, then the central question becomes: *what makes a donor trust an organisation enough to give?*

Luhmann (1979) distinguishes between **confidence** (reliance on system functioning without conscious risk assessment) and **trust** (a deliberately adopted strategy to cope with uncertainty). Charitable giving operates primarily in the register of trust: donors are conscious of the uncertainty involved and choose to act despite it. Trust is therefore not a stable trait but a dynamic strategic response to an uncertain environment.

In the nonprofit context, Sargeant and Lee (2004) identified trust as the primary antecedent of charitable giving, explaining more variance in donation behaviour than attitudes toward the cause, prior giving history, or social norms.[^2-BT-000036] Trust in this context has at least three analytically distinct components:

1. **Competence trust:** Belief that the organisation has the capacity to deliver what it promises.
2. **Integrity trust:** Belief that the organisation behaves consistently with stated values and does not misuse resources.
3. **Benevolence trust:** Belief that the organisation genuinely prioritises beneficiaries' welfare over organisational interests.

These components are not always positively correlated. An organisation may be perceived as highly competent but integrity-deficient (efficient but self-serving) or highly integrity-credible but competence-limited (sincere but ineffective). The brand's role is to provide credible signals across all three dimensions.[^2-BT-000037]

Trust works as a governance mechanism by reducing the perceived *psychological cost of risk* in the donation decision. High-trust donors experience uncertainty as manageable; low-trust donors experience the same uncertainty as prohibitive. Trust therefore shifts the effective threshold at which the donor's warm glow and altruistic motivations become sufficient to overcome inaction.

## 2.5 The Motivational Complexity of Charitable Behaviour

The preceding analysis reveals that charitable giving is not a single-motive act. It is driven by a **mix of motivations** that vary across individuals, contexts, and donation episodes (Bekkers & Wiepking, 2011).[^2-CG-000006] These include:

- **Altruistic motives:** Genuine concern for recipient welfare and social impact.
- **Warm glow / self-image:** Private utility from the act of giving itself.
- **Social-image:** Utility from observable prosociality and social approval.
- **Identity alignment:** Giving as an expression of who the donor perceives themselves to be.
- **Reciprocity and social obligations:** Giving in response to perceived obligations to community or in-group.
- **Religious and moral duty:** Giving framed as obligation rather than choice, particularly in contexts with strong religious norms.[^2-RG-000001]

This motivational plurality has a direct implication for brand strategy: a nonprofit brand that speaks only to altruistic motives will fail to activate the warm glow, social-image, and identity mechanisms that account for a substantial share of giving behaviour. Conversely, a brand that appeals only to self-interested warm glow motivations may undermine perceptions of mission authenticity.

The theoretical synthesis proposed in the BEBA project is that **nonprofit brand equity can be understood as a measurable organisational construct that serves as a proxy for the bundle of mixed motives driving donor behaviour** (BE-000066).[^2-BE-000066] Different brand equity dimensions map onto different motivational pathways: brand image and identity dimensions engage self-image and identity alignment; brand trust engages competence and integrity trust; brand awareness and salience engage heuristic processing; brand personality dimensions engage social-image and relational identification.

---

## 2.6 The Research Gap: Where Brand Equity and Behaviour Models Have Not Been Connected

Despite the substantial bodies of literature on charitable giving motivation, on nonprofit brand equity, and on donor behaviour models (Theory of Planned Behaviour, Integrated Behavioural Model), these streams have developed in partial isolation. A validated, theoretically integrated model that connects **donor-based brand equity** with the **Theory of Planned Behaviour** in a manner that accounts for the motivational complexity and uncertainty structure described in this chapter is, as of the time of writing, absent from the literature.[^2-DB-000010]

This gap — between what is known about giving psychology, what is known about nonprofit brands, and what is known about intention formation — is the space that the BEBA project occupies.

---

## 2.7 Transition: From Decision Psychology to Brand Architecture

The analysis in this chapter establishes three conditions under which brands become relevant to charitable decisions:

1. **Information asymmetry:** Donors cannot directly verify organisational quality, effectiveness, or integrity. They need credible signals.
2. **Heuristic processing:** Under bounded rationality, donors substitute observable cues for unobservable qualities. Brands are exactly such cues.
3. **Mixed motivations:** Giving is not a simple altruistic calculation but a complex motivational act. Brand dimensions can address multiple motivational pathways simultaneously.

The following chapter traces how commercial brand equity theory — developed primarily to understand consumer markets — has been extended, adapted, and (imperfectly) translated to the nonprofit context, where these three conditions hold with particular force.
