# Kapitel 4 §2 — Brand Equity als Verhaltensmechanismus
## Literatursynthese (Entwurf)

> **Wissensgraph:** 20 belegte Kanten | Stärkste Kante: BI→ATT (posterior=0.921, 16 EVIDs) | Schwächste: BD→BEH (posterior=0.611, 6 EVIDs)

# Scientific Summary

The provided evidence synthesizes research on the relationship between nonprofit
brand equity and stakeholder donation behaviour. There is a strong consensus 
that integrating brand equity constructs into behavioural models like the Theory
of Planned Behavior (TPB) significantly enhances the explanation of donation 
*intention*. The core of this relationship is not a simple, direct link. 
Instead, brand equity appears to function as an "upstream" architecture that 
influences the primary antecedents of intention. Specifically, perceptual 
dimensions like Brand Image and Brand Personality consistently predict 
*Attitude* toward donating. Relational dimensions, most notably Brand Trust, 
emerge as the single most critical mechanism, reducing donor uncertainty and 
perceived risk, and thereby strongly influencing both *Intention* and, to a 
lesser extent, actual *Behaviour*.

However, the synthesis reveals two major weaknesses in the current state of 
knowledge. First, there is a significant and repeatedly identified 
"intention-behaviour gap," where the effects of brand constructs on intention 
are far larger than their effects on actual behaviour. Second, the direct, 
causal link between certain brand dimensions, particularly Brand 
Differentiation, and actual donation behaviour is not well-supported by robust 
empirical evidence.

# Core Mechanisms

1. **Attitude Formation:** Perceptual components (Brand Image, Brand Personality) shape affective and cognitive evaluation of donating.
2. **Uncertainty Reduction:** Brand Trust is the central mechanism for mitigating information asymmetry. A trusted brand reduces perceived risks.
3. **Heuristic Processing:** In high-uncertainty contexts, a strong brand serves as a cognitive shortcut simplifying the donation decision.
4. **Social Norm Shaping:** A highly salient brand can frame donating as socially approved behaviour (descriptive norms).
5. **Capital Conversion (Bourdieu):** The nonprofit brand functions as Symbolic Capital, enabling stakeholders to convert economic capital into symbolic capital.

# Consensus

- Integration of brand equity constructs into TPB/IBM significantly improves prediction of donation intention.
- Brand Image → Attitude is the best-supported perceptual pathway.
- Brand Trust is the most robust relational predictor of both intention and behaviour.
- Nonprofit context requires adaptation of commercial brand equity models (credence goods, accountability, uncertainty).
- Brand Equity is inherently multidimensional; unidimensional measures are insufficient.

# Disagreement

- Direct vs. mediated effects of overall Brand Equity on intention: no consensus on unmediated pathways.
- Brand Awareness: contested as direct driver vs. mere precondition for trust effects.
- Brand Differentiation → Behaviour: theoretically plausible, empirically weak (posterior = 0.611).

# Boundary Conditions

- Effects strongest under high stakeholder uncertainty (credence good conditions).
- Moderated by stakeholder experience (new vs. recurring donors).
- Negative information (scandals, low effectiveness ratings) can reverse brand equity effects.
- Cultural and economic context moderates relative importance of brand dimensions.

# Research Gaps

- **Intention-Behaviour Gap:** dominant gap — mechanisms for conversion from intention to action are underdeveloped.
- **Longitudinal dynamics:** overwhelming cross-sectional bias; brand equity and sustained giving untested.
- **Brand Differentiation → Behaviour:** lack of direct causal evidence.
- **Sociological constructs (Bourdieu):** promising but lack validated operationalizations.

# BEBA Implications

1. **Validates the BEBA Premise:** Brand equity as a behaviour-enabling architecture under uncertainty is empirically supported.
2. **Trust as first-class construct:** Brand Trust must be the primary uncertainty-reduction mechanism in the ontology.
3. **Intention-Behaviour Gap as central problem:** BEBA must include post-intention constructs (habit, implementation intentions).
4. **Competing pathways:** BEBA should model both mediated and context-contingent direct pathways.
5. **Rethink Brand Differentiation:** Its role is attitude/efficacy formation, not direct behaviour driver.

## Neue Candidate Claims (aus Synthese)

**[CC-BEBA-04]** *(Trust as Primary Mechanism)*
> Within a Brand-Enabled Behaviour Architecture, Brand Trust is the primary mechanism that converts brand perceptions into behavioural commitment by reducing stakeholder uncertainty regarding organizational integrity and impact.

**[CC-BEBA-05]** *(Upstream Architectural Function)*
> Nonprofit Brand Equity functions as an upstream architecture, primarily influencing behaviour indirectly by shaping foundational TPB/IBM constructs: Brand Image shapes Attitude, Brand Trust shapes both Intention and Perceived Behavioural Control.

**[CC-BEBA-06]** *(The Behavioural Enactment Problem)*
> The behavioural effectiveness of a nonprofit brand is fundamentally constrained by the intention-behaviour gap; a complete BEBA model must account for post-intention factors that enable or inhibit behavioural enactment.

**[CC-BEBA-07]** *(The Role of Differentiation)*
> Perceived Brand Differentiation does not have a strong direct effect on donation behaviour (posterior=0.611); its primary role within BEBA is to enhance the formation of favourable attitudes and perceptions of unique efficacy.

**[CC-BEBA-08]** *(Architectural Necessity)*
> The fragmented evidence and documented research gaps necessitate an integrative, multi-level framework like BEBA, which simultaneously models cognitive (TPB), relational (Trust), and social (Symbolic Capital) mechanisms.
