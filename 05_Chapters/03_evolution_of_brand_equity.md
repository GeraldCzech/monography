# Chapter 3 — The Evolution of Brand Equity: From Marketing Asset to Behaviour Architecture

## Scientific Question

How did the concept of brand equity develop from a commercial marketing construct to a candidate mechanism for explaining behaviour under uncertainty — and what adaptations are required for its application in the nonprofit context?

---

## 3.1 Origins: Brand Equity as Financial and Perceptual Value

The modern concept of brand equity emerged from two parallel traditions in the late 1980s. The first was financial: brand equity as the premium a firm could charge for a branded versus an unbranded product, or as the incremental cash flow attributable to brand ownership (Simon & Sullivan, 1993). This financial tradition understood brand equity primarily as a balance-sheet item — an intangible asset with measurable economic value.

The second tradition was perceptual, and it is the one that has shaped most subsequent theoretical development. Aaker (1991) defined **brand equity** as the set of brand assets and liabilities linked to a brand name and symbol that add to or subtract from the value provided by a product or service. His five-component model — brand loyalty, brand name awareness, perceived quality, brand associations, and other proprietary brand assets — positioned brand equity as a multi-dimensional construct residing in consumer perception rather than on a balance sheet.

Keller (1993) offered a complementary and more psychologically precise formulation: **customer-based brand equity** (CBBE) as the differential effect that brand knowledge has on consumer response to the marketing of a brand. This definition centres the concept on the *consumer* — what matters is not what the brand costs to build but what it does to consumer cognition and choice. Keller's Brand Knowledge Model decomposed brand equity into brand awareness (recognition and recall) and brand image (the network of brand associations in memory), providing the first systematic account of the psychological mechanisms through which brands influence consumer behaviour.

Together, Aaker and Keller established the foundational architecture that subsequent researchers have elaborated: brand equity is multi-dimensional, it resides primarily in stakeholder perception, it generates behavioural consequences through identifiable psychological mechanisms, and it is both a cause of short-term choice and a long-term organisational asset.

---

## 3.2 The Dimensionality Debate: What Does Brand Equity Consist Of?

The decades following Aaker and Keller produced a proliferation of brand equity models, each proposing different dimensional structures, measurement scales, and causal architectures. This proliferation reflects a genuine theoretical disagreement rather than merely different measurement preferences.

The BEBA evidence base indicates broad consensus on two points and continuing disagreement on a third.[^3-BE-000002]

**Consensus 1:** Brand equity is inherently multi-dimensional. Unidimensional measures of overall brand equity — single-item or composite-without-structure — are insufficient for modelling the behavioural effects of brands (Wu et al., 2020).[^3-fn-multidim] Different dimensions relate differently to different behavioural outcomes, and collapsing them into a single score destroys this structural information.

**Consensus 2:** The core dimensions cluster into three broad categories: **perceptual** (brand image, brand associations, brand personality — what the brand means to the stakeholder), **relational** (brand trust, brand loyalty, commitment — how the stakeholder relates to the brand), and **capability** (perceived quality, competence, effectiveness — what the brand promises to deliver). Chatzipanagiotou et al. (2019) represent these as a configural architecture of brand building, brand understanding, and brand relationship blocks.[^3-BE-000003]

**Disagreement:** Whether brand equity is best understood as a hierarchical construct (higher-order brand equity causing lower-order dimensions), a formative construct (dimensions forming brand equity), or a network of mutually influencing perceptions without a single superordinate factor. This disagreement has direct methodological implications: reflective measurement models, formative measurement models, and network models produce different empirical estimates and require different analytical approaches.[^3-fn-measurement]

## 3.3 Brand Equity and Behavioural Intention: The IBM/TPB Integration

The most important theoretical development for the BEBA project is the integration of brand equity constructs with **intention-behaviour models** — specifically, the Theory of Planned Behaviour (Ajzen, 1991) and the Integrated Behavioural Model (Fishbein & Cappella, 2006). This integration attempts to connect the perceptual architecture of brand equity with the decision-psychological architecture of pro-social behaviour.

The key structural insight — established across multiple studies — is that brand equity does not directly cause behaviour. Instead, brand equity dimensions function as **upstream antecedents** of the proximal determinants of behavioural intention: attitude, subjective norms, and perceived behavioural control (Zheng et al., 2025; Rambocas et al., 2017).[^3-BE-000001]

The strongest perceptual pathways are:

- **Brand image → Attitude:** The affective and evaluative associations linked to the brand shape how favourably stakeholders view brand-related behaviour.[^3-A-000001]
- **Brand associations → Attitude:** The specific meaning networks activated by the brand influence attitudinal evaluations.[^3-A-000002]
- **Brand personality → Attitude:** The human-like characteristics attributed to the brand predict attitudinal orientation toward brand-related behaviour.[^3-A-000003]

The strongest relational pathways are:

- **Brand trust → Perceived Behavioural Control:** A trusted brand makes action feel more manageable and reduces perceived risk.[^3-PB-000002]
- **Brand awareness → Perceived Behavioural Control:** Awareness of a brand and its action pathways reduces the cognitive effort required to act.[^3-PB-000001]
- **Perceived quality → Perceived Behavioural Control:** Confidence in an organisation's quality increases the stakeholder's belief that acting through it will be effective.[^3-PB-000003]

Additionally, the overall brand equity construct has been found to exert a **direct positive effect on behavioural intention** beyond its effects through IBM/TPB constructs — suggesting that brand equity does not operate exclusively through deliberative intention-formation mechanisms (Singh et al., 2023).[^3-BI-000001]

Customer satisfaction has been documented as a **partial mediator** between brand equity and behavioural outcomes, providing a confirmatory loop: brand equity generates expectations, experience confirms or disconfirms those expectations, and satisfaction reinforces or erodes the brand-to-behaviour pathway (Yeğin & Ikram, 2022).[^3-S-000001]

**Trust occupies a unique position** in this architecture. It appears not only as a brand equity dimension (a relational perception) but also as a mechanism (a causal pathway to behaviour) — a duality that reflects trust's double function as both a brand attribute and a governance response to uncertainty.[^3-TR-000001]

## 3.4 Context Dependency: The Limits of Universal Brand Models

A consistent finding across the CBBE-IBM/TPB integration literature is that the relationships between brand equity dimensions and behavioural outcomes are **context-dependent**. The BEBA evidence base identifies at least five classes of moderating conditions:[^3-fn-context]

1. **Cultural context:** Individualism-collectivism dimensions and other cultural values moderate the relative importance of brand equity blocks.[^3-CD-000001] In more collectivist cultures, social-image and norm-conforming pathways may dominate; in more individualistic cultures, self-image and efficacy pathways may dominate.

2. **Stakeholder demographics:** Age, income, education, prior giving experience, and life stage moderate the strength of brand equity-behaviour relationships.[^3-CD-000003]

3. **Channel integration:** Digital, hybrid, and omnichannel brand experiences alter the pathways and relative importance of brand equity dimensions.[^3-CD-000004]

4. **Value orientation:** Ecological consciousness and other moral or political value orientations moderate brand-behaviour pathways.[^3-CD-000005]

5. **Economic perception:** Perceived financial capacity and cost sensitivity alter the relationship between brand equity and resource allocation decisions.[^3-CD-000002]

The practical implication is that a universal brand equity model — a single structural architecture assumed to hold across all cultural, demographic, channel, and value contexts — is theoretically implausible and empirically unsupported. The BEBA project treats context dependency not as a nuisance to be controlled but as a **boundary condition** to be specified: which mechanisms operate under which conditions.

## 3.5 Nonprofit Brand Equity: Necessary Adaptations

The brand equity literature reviewed above is predominantly rooted in commercial, consumer-product contexts. Its translation to the nonprofit sector requires two classes of adaptation: one structural (the characteristics of nonprofit exchange), one motivational (the psychology of donor versus consumer).

**Structural adaptations** arise from the nonprofit organisation's distinctive position in the exchange. Nonprofit brands must appeal simultaneously to multiple stakeholder groups — donors, volunteers, beneficiaries, policy actors, staff — whose relationship to the organisation differs fundamentally. A donor's relationship to a nonprofit is not the same as a customer's relationship to a commercial brand: there is no personal consumption of the funded outputs, no direct feedback through product experience, and no contractual guarantee of service delivery. The absence of a market price mechanism removes the most powerful quality signal available in commercial contexts.

Key researchers in nonprofit brand equity — Faircloth (2005), Boenigk and Helmig (2013), and Rios Romero et al. (2023) — have developed adaptations that address these structural differences:

- **Faircloth** (2005) established that nonprofit brand image positively influences brand attitude, which in turn predicts supportive behaviour, but noted that the commercial CBBE framework requires modification to account for the absence of price cues and the multi-stakeholder nature of nonprofit exchange.[^3-fn-faircloth]
- **Boenigk and Helmig** (2013) demonstrated the role of organisational identification and identity salience in mediating the relationship between brand perceptions and donation behaviour — constructs without direct commercial analogues.
- **Rios Romero et al.** (2023) conducted a systematic review of nonprofit brand equity dimensions, identifying nonprofit-specific components including mission alignment, social impact credibility, and accountability transparency.

**Motivational adaptations** arise from the donor psychology analysed in Chapter 2. Commercial brand equity models assume consumer motivation is primarily hedonic, utilitarian, or a mix — the consumption experience is the primary locus of value. Nonprofit brand equity must account for a fundamentally different motivational structure: altruistic-impure motivation, warm glow, identity-expression, social-image, and moral duty. Each of these motivational pathways places different demands on brand equity dimensions.[^3-fn-motivation]

The incompleteness of this translation — the remaining dependence of nonprofit brand equity research on commercial CBBE models — constitutes a significant limitation of the existing literature, and one of the central empirical challenges the BEBA project addresses.

## 3.6 A Sociological Supplement: Bourdieu and the Philanthropic Field

A different theoretical tradition offers a complementary perspective on nonprofit brand dynamics that commercial brand theory does not provide. Pierre Bourdieu's sociology of practice — developed principally in *Distinction* (1984) and *The Logic of Practice* (1990) — introduces three concepts with direct applicability to philanthropic giving: **Field**, **Habitus**, and **Capital**.[^3-TB-000001]

In Bourdieu's framework, social life is organised into semi-autonomous **fields** — structured spaces of positions and position-takings governed by their own specific logic and stakes. The philanthropic or civil society field is such a space: organisations occupy positions within it according to the volume and composition of their capital, and actors' behaviour within the field is shaped by the competitive dynamics of position-seeking and legitimation.

**Symbolic Capital** — the accumulated prestige, recognition, and legitimacy that an actor commands within a field — is the form of capital most directly relevant to nonprofit brand dynamics.[^3-SC-000018] A nonprofit organisation's brand can be understood as its principal vehicle of Symbolic Capital accumulation. Brand recognition, brand reputation, and brand trust are not merely marketing metrics in this framing; they are representations of the organisation's position within the philanthropic field.

**Habitus** introduces the donor's side of this dynamic: the socially conditioned system of durable, transposable dispositions that generates practices without deliberate intention or rational calculation (Bourdieu, 1990).[^3-H-000006] Donors do not approach charitable giving as a blank-slate rational calculator; they come with a habituated orientation toward certain types of organisations, causes, and giving practices shaped by class position, educational experience, social milieu, and cultural field.[^3-H-000007] A nonprofit brand's effectiveness depends not only on its intrinsic signal quality but on its alignment with the habitus of its target donor populations.

The Bourdieusian framework introduces a crucial **boundary condition** for nonprofit brand equity: the competitive advantage of symbolic capital (i.e., brand-as-signal) over programmatic outcomes as a driver of donor support is most pronounced in philanthropic fields characterised by **high uncertainty** — where donors cannot easily verify impact claims, where organisational quality is opaque, and where the field lacks transparent performance metrics.[^3-SC-000019] In more transparent, performance-accountable fields, this boundary condition may be relaxed.

A critical warning from the BEBA evidence base concerns the **operationalisation** of Bourdieusian concepts in empirical research: field, habitus, and symbolic capital have been widely referenced in nonprofit studies but rarely operationalised consistently, limiting their integration into quantitative research designs.[^3-CC-000010] The BEBA project's ontological approach — creating formal, canonical concept definitions — is a direct response to this problem.

## 3.7 The State of Integration: What We Know, and What We Do Not

The literature reviewed in this chapter supports the following evidence-based summary:

**What we know with reasonable confidence:**

1. Brand equity is multi-dimensional and operates through identifiable psychological pathways to behavioural intention (BE-000001, BE-000002).
2. Brand image, associations, and personality are significant antecedents of attitude (A-000001 to A-000003).
3. Brand trust, awareness, and perceived quality influence perceived behavioural control (PB-000001 to PB-000003).
4. Brand trust is the most robust brand equity mechanism for reducing uncertainty and predicting donation behaviour (TR-000001, BT-000036).
5. Brand equity adds explanatory power over standard IBM/TPB models (BE-000001, BI-000002).
6. Context dependency is real and must be modelled rather than assumed away (CD-000001 to CD-000005).

**What we do not yet know with sufficient confidence:**

1. Whether the commercial CBBE architecture translates directly to nonprofit donor behaviour, or whether nonprofit-specific mechanisms require different constructs.
2. How warm glow, identity, and social-image motivations are *mediated* by specific brand equity dimensions rather than merely correlated with overall brand equity.
3. Whether brand equity effects on *behaviour* (as opposed to intention) are robust across cultural and contextual conditions.
4. Whether the Bourdieusian field dynamics — symbolic capital, habitus alignment — can be operationalised in a way that adds unique variance beyond psychological brand equity constructs.

These gaps define the empirical programme that the following chapter's theoretical synthesis — the BEBA architecture — is designed to address.
