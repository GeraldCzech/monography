# Research Compass - Chapter 2

## Chapter

The Economics and Psychology of Charitable Decisions

## Scientific Question

How do people make charitable decisions when organisational impact, integrity and quality cannot be fully observed?

## Purpose

This chapter provides the foundational problem for the entire monograph. It frames charitable giving as decision-making under uncertainty and shows why signals, trust and brands become necessary.

## Theoretical Landscape

- Bounded rationality.
- Heuristics and biases.
- Prospect Theory and risk perception.
- Information asymmetry.
- Credence goods.
- Trust as governance mechanism.
- Charitable giving mechanisms.

## Core Argument

Charitable giving is structurally different from ordinary consumption because donors usually do not consume the output themselves and cannot reliably evaluate the final impact of their contribution. This makes nonprofit organisations especially dependent on trust, reputation and signals.

## Literature Review Tasks

1. Review bounded rationality and decision-making under uncertainty.
2. Review credence goods and their transfer to services, health, expertise and nonprofit contexts.
3. Review nonprofit trust and accountability.
4. Review donor motivation and charitable giving mechanisms.
5. Identify where existing donor behaviour models ignore brands or signals.

## Boundary Conditions

- Not all donors seek information actively.
- Emotional, moral and social motives may dominate information processing.
- Institutional trust may substitute for brand-specific trust.
- High-involvement and low-involvement giving may follow different processes.

## Link to Next Chapter

Once charitable giving is framed as uncertainty management, brand equity can be introduced as one possible institutionalised signal system.
