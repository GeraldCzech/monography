# Topic

Warm glow giving: the private emotional utility or “feel-good” reward donors derive from the act of charitable giving itself, independent of the donation’s actual consequential impact on recipients or aggregate charitable output.

# Scope of Evidence

The reviewed material covers warm glow giving across:

- Public economics and impure altruism theory
- Experimental charitable giving and crowd-out studies
- Neuroimaging evidence on voluntary giving
- Self-image and social-image accounts
- Cause-related marketing and consumer behaviour
- Fundraising appeal framing
- Religious, moral, and cultural boundary conditions
- Connections to nonprofit brand equity and BEBA as a possible non-brand-mediated pathway to giving

The evidence primarily concerns monetary giving, with some extension to volunteering, pro-environmental behaviour, checkout charity, and cause-related marketing.

# Methods Represented

The reviewed literature includes:

- Formal utility-theoretic modelling
- Laboratory experiments
- Crowd-out experiments
- Field experiments
- Neuroimaging studies
- Survey and natural-data analyses
- Cause-related marketing experiments
- Conceptual/theoretical critiques
- Cross-cultural or religious-context studies, though these appear less developed

# Positive Evidence

- Warm glow helps explain incomplete crowd-out in charitable giving: donors continue to give even when government or researcher contributions offset their donation.
- Direct crowd-out experiments show that giving persists even when the donor’s contribution has no net effect on the charity’s receipts.
- Crumpler and Grossman (2008) are presented as providing especially clean experimental evidence: approximately 20% of endowments were donated under full crowd-out, and roughly half of participants gave something.
- Wilhelm et al. (2014) and Konow (2010) are cited as supporting incomplete crowd-out in natural and survey data.
- Harbaugh et al. (2007) provide neuroimaging evidence that voluntary giving activates reward circuitry differently from mandatory transfers.
- Donor-benefit framing outperforms recipient-benefit framing in fundraising appeals according to List et al. (2021).
- Self-image and social-image components of warm glow are supported in Grossman and Levy’s work, with self-image linked to participation and social image linked to amount.
- Earned income appears to generate greater warm-glow utility than windfall income in laboratory evidence.
- Cause-related marketing studies support warm glow as a consumer affect construct that can mediate purchase intention or loyalty effects.
- Warm glow is relevant beyond monetary donations, including volunteering and pro-environmental behaviour, according to the reviewed material.

# Mixed Evidence

- The relative balance between pure altruism and warm glow varies across contexts and populations.
- Warm glow and prestige/social image are not always clearly separable. Some work treats prestige as distinct from warm glow, while other work treats social image as a component of warm-glow motivation.
- Self-image and social-image components appear to have different behavioural implications, but whether they should be treated as one construct or two remains unresolved.
- Nudge effects are mixed: some nudge designs appear to crowd in prosocial motivation, while others may crowd it out.
- The relationship between wealth/income and giving is non-linear and not fully explained by standard warm-glow models.
- Warm glow may interact with brand trust, authenticity, reputation, and commitment, but these links are not empirically examined in the reviewed warm-glow literature.
- Religious and non-Western contexts suggest that warm glow may operate alongside other motives or may not be the primary explanatory mechanism.

# Negative Evidence

- Pure altruism alone is not sufficient to explain observed incomplete crowd-out.
- Standard warm-glow or impure altruism models may be insufficient in faith-based or obligation-driven giving contexts.
- Warm-glow preferences may lead to inefficient charitable allocation, such as spreading donations across multiple similar charities.
- Large donation sizes in cause-related marketing contexts may trigger consumer skepticism and reduce positive warm-glow effects.
- High moral relativism may suppress the subjective experience of warm glow after giving.
- Warm glow may conflict with effective altruism or impact-maximising norms because donors may prioritise emotional reward over impact efficiency.
- The warm-glow model has limited predictive precision regarding the magnitude of warm-glow motivation relative to altruism in specific contexts.

# Mediators

The reviewed material identifies or discusses the following mediators:

- Warm-glow feelings / positive affect
- Donor well-being
- Self-image activation
- Social-image activation
- Consumer skepticism in cause-related marketing contexts
- Donor affect / emotional reward
- Affective attitude toward giving in TPB/IBM-related interpretation

# Moderators

The reviewed material identifies or discusses the following moderators:

- Income source: earned versus windfall income
- Social visibility or observability of the giving act
- Donation size in cause-related marketing
- Religious or cultural context
- Moral relativism
- Wealth or income level
- Nudge type
- Availability of self-interested exit options
- Identity congruence between donor and recipient/cause
- Potentially brand trust, brand authenticity, brand reputation, and brand-self congruence, but these are not sufficiently covered empirically in the reviewed literature.

# Boundary Conditions

- Religious and faith-based contexts: obligation, faith, community duty, or transcendence may be primary rather than warm glow.
- Non-Western cultural contexts: generalisability is not established; Thai Buddhist donor evidence suggests culturally specific motivational structures.
- High moral relativism: warm-glow experience may be weakened or absent.
- Cause-related marketing with large visible donation amounts: skepticism may override warm-glow affect.
- Self-interested exit options: warm-glow giving may be attenuated but not eliminated.
- Effective altruism or impact-focused giving contexts: warm-glow motives may conflict with impact-maximising norms.
- Western sample concentration: the empirical base is geographically limited.
- Lack of longitudinal evidence: persistence, decay, or habituation of warm glow over repeated giving is not sufficiently covered.

# Research Gaps

- Whether nonprofit brand trust moderates warm-glow utility. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- Whether brand authenticity amplifies the self-image component of warm glow. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- Whether warm glow mediates or moderates the brand equity → donation intention pathway. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- Whether warm-glow motivations decay over repeated donations. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- Whether brand commitment buffers possible warm-glow decay. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- How warm glow operates in the Austrian charitable giving context. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- Whether warm glow can be measured as a latent construct distinct from altruism, brand attitude, and intention in BEBA-compatible SEM architecture. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- How digital fundraising interfaces affect warm-glow motivation. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- Whether brand equity amplifies warm glow by confirming donor self-image as a discerning or effective giver. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- Field replication of the earned-income effect. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- Longitudinal dynamics of warm glow, habit, and repeated giving. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

# Evidence Strength Assessment

The evidence for warm glow giving is strong overall.

Strongest evidence:

- Incomplete crowd-out evidence
- Full crowd-out experiments
- Laboratory evidence for continued giving without consequential impact
- Neuroimaging evidence distinguishing voluntary from mandatory transfers
- Field evidence on donor-benefit appeal framing
- Experimental evidence for self-image and social-image components

Moderate evidence:

- Cause-related marketing applications
- Earned versus windfall income effects
- Nudge-based amplification or crowding effects
- Extension to volunteering and pro-environmental behaviour

Weaker or underdeveloped evidence:

- Non-Western and religious contexts
- Longitudinal effects
- Warm-glow decay or habituation
- Interaction between warm glow and nonprofit brand equity constructs
- Measurement of warm glow as a distinct latent construct in BEBA-compatible survey models

Overall assessment:

Warm glow is well-supported as a motivational explanation for charitable giving and as a boundary condition for models that explain donation behaviour through brand equity alone. However, its interaction with nonprofit brand trust, authenticity, commitment, reputation, and donation intention is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
