abstract: Numerous theories in social and health psychology assume that intentions
  cause behaviors. However, most tests of the intention- behavior relation involve
  correlational studies that preclude causal inferences. In order to determine whether
  changes in behavioral intention engender behavior change, participants should be
  assigned randomly to a treatment that significantly increases the strength of respective
  intentions relative to a control condition, and differences in subsequent behavior
  should be compared. The present research obtained 47 experimental tests of intention-behavior
  relations that satisfied these criteria. Meta-analysis showed that a medium-to-large
  change in intention (d = 0.66) leads to a small-to-medium change in behavior (d
  = 0.36). The review also identified several conceptual factors, methodological features,
  and intervention characteristics that moderate intention-behavior consistency.
