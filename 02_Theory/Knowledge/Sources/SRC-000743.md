abstract: Nonprofit organizations (NPOs) becoming business-like is a contested issue.
  Some understand the adoption of business-like practices by NPOs as a case of adopting
  rational myths through institutional isomorphism and thus potentially dangerous
  for NPOs’ ability to fulfill their unique societal roles. Others are more optimistic,
  arguing that technical rationality is possible in the adoption of business-like
  practices, and that such practices can therefore support NPOs in fulfilling a wide
  range of societal roles. Drawing on survey data of NPOs in Flanders, we examine
  the relationship between the extent to which NPOs use business-like practices, and
  the extent to which they engage in various societal roles. We find that business-like
  practices are weakly related to NPOs’ societal roles. All roles are positively related
  to nonprofit managerialism and unrelated to NPOs’ reliance on commercial funding.
  Our results suggest that a certain optimism regarding the rational use of business-like
  approaches is justified.
