abstract: Recent research in information economics has focused on signals as mechanisms
  to solve problems that arise under asymmetric information. A firm or individual
  credibly communicates the level of some unobservable element in a transaction by
  providing an observable signal. When applied to conveying product quality information,
  this issue is of particular interest to the discipline of marketing. In this article,
  the authors focus on the ways a firm may signal the unobservable quality of its
  products through several marketing-mix variables. The authors develop a typology
  that classifies signals and discuss the available empirical evidence on the signaling
  properties of several marketing variables. They consider managerial implications
  of signaling and outline an agenda for future empirical research.
