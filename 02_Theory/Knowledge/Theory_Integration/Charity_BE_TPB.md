# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set strongly supports BEBA’s central premise that nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective when they shape donor intention under uncertainty. The strongest theoretical contribution is not merely that brand equity predicts donation intention, but that brand-related perceptions appear to operate through behavioural architecture: trust, attitude toward donating, TPB antecedents, perceived risk reduction, and decision simplification.

The claims therefore support a BEBA interpretation in which nonprofit brand equity is not treated as a static marketing asset, but as a behaviour-enabling structure that helps potential donors form intentions when outcomes, organizational quality, impact, and trustworthiness are uncertain.

However, the evidence also cautions against a single universal pathway. The literature appears fragmented across brand equity, TPB, trust, brand image, social media, donor disposition, and contextual moderators. This supports BEBA’s commitment to competing model structures rather than one canonical structural model.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## Primary Layer: Individual

Most claims operate at the individual donor level:

- brand equity predicting donation intention;
- brand image shaping attitude toward donating;
- trust influencing or mediating intention;
- brand salience affecting new donor choice;
- donor-level dispositions such as materialistic attitude moderating brand effects;
- uncertainty reduction through brand heuristics;
- attitude and TPB antecedents mediating brand effects.

This strengthens the Individual layer as the core behavioural mechanism layer in BEBA.

## Secondary Layer: Organisational

Several claims affect the Organisational layer:

- social media marketing activities shape brand image;
- CEO reputation moderates brand perception effects;
- organisational communication can feed into trust and donation intention.

This implies that BEBA should represent organisational brand-building and communication activity as upstream inputs into individual-level behavioural mechanisms.

## Secondary Layer: Market / Context Layer

Several claims affect the Market or Context layer:

- digital versus offline donation context;
- monetary versus time-based donation setting;
- social media environment;
- general donation context variability.

These claims support modelling BEBA mechanisms as context-sensitive rather than universally invariant.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Any claim implying a single universal BEBA pathway should be weakened

`BE-000011` states that the reviewed literature does not use a single standardized structural model for integrating brand equity constructs with TPB constructs.

Therefore, BEBA should avoid claiming that there is one settled pathway such as:

```text
brand equity → trust → attitude → intention → donation
```

Instead, this should remain one candidate pathway among several.

## 2. Claims about sustained giving require caution

`LD-000001` states that longitudinal evidence on whether integrated brand equity and TPB models predict sustained giving behaviour is not sufficiently covered.

Therefore, BEBA can currently support claims about donation intention more strongly than claims about durable or repeated donation behaviour.

## 3. Claims about positive brand effects should not be generalized to negative or ambivalent perceptions

`NB-000017` indicates that negative or ambivalent nonprofit brand perceptions are not sufficiently covered.

Therefore, BEBA should not assume that positive and negative brand perceptions are symmetrical. Negative brand perceptions may disrupt trust, increase perceived risk, or activate avoidance pathways differently from how positive perceptions enable donation.

## 4. Brand equity should not be treated as the only route to donation intention

The moderation claims involving CEO reputation, social media context, corporate brand, and donor dispositions suggest that non-brand or adjacent cues may condition, amplify, or bypass nonprofit brand equity.

BEBA should therefore allow for parallel routes to intention formation, including:

```text
leadership cue → trust/intention
social proof/context cue → attitude/intention
donor disposition → intention
prior donor experience → intention
```

Status: candidate.

Status: candidate.

Models combining nonprofit brand equity constructs with TPB constructs are theoretically preferable when the research aim is to explain donation intention, because they connect brand perceptions to behavioural antecedents.

The behavioural effect of nonprofit brand equity varies across donation contexts, communication environments, donor experience levels, and donor dispositions.

Status: candidate.

Because the reviewed literature does not converge on a standardized brand equity–TPB model, BEBA should represent multiple competing pathways rather than one canonical structural sequence.

Status: candidate.

The current evidence base is insufficient for specifying how negative or ambivalent nonprofit brand perceptions alter TPB pathways to donation intention.

Status: candidate.

Evidence for donation intention should not be treated as evidence for sustained giving unless longitudinal behavioural data are available.

Status: candidate.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Donor Experience

`NB-000016` indicates that brand salience is especially relevant for new donors. This suggests that donor experience is a boundary condition.

Possible implication:

```text
low prior experience → greater reliance on salience/trust/heuristics
high prior experience → greater reliance on past interaction, satisfaction, habit, or commitment
```

## 2. Donation Context

`DC-000001` indicates that effects vary across donation contexts.

Named contexts include:

- digital versus offline donation;
- monetary versus time-based donation.

This means BEBA should not assume that the same brand equity dimension has the same effect across all giving situations.

## 3. Communication Environment

`SM-000002` and `NB-000015` indicate that social media matters both as an antecedent environment and a moderator.

BEBA should distinguish between:

- social media marketing as organisational activity;
- social media context as communication environment;
- social media engagement as possible donor behaviour or exposure state.

## 4. Leadership Cues

`CR-000002` suggests that CEO reputation can moderate the effect of nonprofit brand perceptions.

This introduces leadership reputation as an organisational-level cue that can strengthen, weaken, or potentially override brand-based perceptions.

## 5. Donor Dispositions

`MA-000001` suggests that materialistic attitude moderates the influence of corporate brand cues on donation intention.

This supports including donor-level dispositions as boundary conditions in BEBA.

## 6. Valence of Brand Perceptions

`NB-000017` identifies insufficient evidence regarding negative or ambivalent brand perceptions.

BEBA should therefore treat brand perception valence as unresolved. Positive brand pathways should not be assumed to mirror negative brand pathways.

## 7. Time Horizon

`LD-000001` identifies insufficient longitudinal evidence.

BEBA should distinguish between:

- donation intention;
- one-time donation behaviour;
- repeated giving;
- sustained donor relationship;
- donor loyalty or retention.

# Model Competition Implications

The claim set strongly supports model competition inside BEBA.

## Model Family 1: Direct Brand Equity Model

Basic structure:

```text
nonprofit brand equity → donation intention
```

Supported by `NB-000011`.

This is the simplest model but theoretically under-specified because it does not explain how brand equity becomes behaviourally effective.

## Model Family 2: Trust-Mediated Model

Possible structure:

```text
brand perceptions → brand trust → donation intention
```

Supported by `NB-000013` and `NB-000014`.

This model fits BEBA’s uncertainty-reduction account especially well.

## Model Family 3: Brand Image–Attitude–Intention Model

Possible structure:

```text
brand image → attitude toward donating → donation intention
```

Supported by `NB-000012` and `AT-000002`.

This model connects brand perception to TPB-style evaluative intention formation.

## Model Family 4: Brand Equity–TPB Integration Model

Possible structure:

```text
brand constructs → attitude / subjective norm / perceived behavioural control → donation intention
```

Supported by `TO-000002` and `BE-000010`.

This model is theoretically attractive because it links brand architecture to behavioural theory.

## Model Family 5: Heuristic / Risk-Reduction Model

Possible structure:

```text
brand familiarity + trust → reduced perceived risk/complexity → donation intention
```

Supported by `BH-000003`.

This model is especially important for BEBA because it directly addresses the current research question: how nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under uncertainty.

## Model Family 6: Organisational Communication Pathway

Possible structure:

```text
social media marketing activity → brand image → brand trust → donation intention
```

Supported by `NB-000015`.

This links organisational activity to individual behavioural mechanisms.

## Model Family 7: Moderated Context Model

Possible structure:

```text
brand equity dimension → donation intention
moderated by donation context / social media context / donor disposition / CEO reputation / donor experience
```

Supported by `DC-000001`, `SM-000002`, `MA-000001`, `CR-000002`, and `NB-000016`.

## Bypass Route Implications

The reviewed claims suggest possible bypass routes around nonprofit brand equity. These should be represented explicitly in BEBA rather than suppressed.

Possible bypasses include:

```text
CEO reputation → trust → donation intention
```

```text
social media context/social proof → attitude or subjective norm → donation intention
```

```text
donor disposition → donation intention
```

```text
prior experience → trust/intention
```

These do not invalidate brand equity, but they challenge any model in which brand equity is the only upstream driver of donation intention.

# Recommendation

## Theory Integration Recommendation

Integrate this claim set into BEBA as a high-value candidate synthesis supporting the Individual-level behavioural architecture of nonprofit brand equity.

The strongest integration pathway is:

```text
nonprofit brand perceptions
→ trust / attitude / TPB antecedents / heuristic simplification
→ donation intention
```

However, BEBA should not canonicalize one structural sequence yet. The absence of a standardized brand equity–TPB model supports maintaining competing model families.

## Recommended Status

Recommended status: candidate theory integration, not canonical ontology.
