# Core Theoretical Implication

The claim set strengthens the central BEBA proposition that nonprofit brand equity becomes behaviourally effective because charitable giving occurs under uncertainty, and because brands can structure both intention formation and behavioural enactment.

The most important implication is that BEBA should not be treated as a simple “brand equity → donation intention” model. Instead, the reviewed claims support a layered architecture:

```text
Credence-good uncertainty
→ Brand perceptions and relational brand equity
→ Trust, attitude, subjective norm, moral norm, perceived behavioural control
→ Donation intention
→ Enactment conditions: knowledge, salience, habit, reduced constraints
→ Donation behaviour
```

The review therefore supports BEBA as a mechanism-oriented explanatory architecture rather than a conventional brand equity outcome model.

# Existing Claims Supported

## Supported: Donations as uncertainty-laden credence-good exchanges

`CG-000004` strongly supports the premise that donation decisions differ from ordinary consumer exchanges because donors cannot directly verify outcome quality or social impact at the point of decision.

This supports BEBA’s current research question:

> How do nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty?

## Supported: Trust as a central mechanism

`TR-000007` and `TR-000008` support trust as a core mechanism in nonprofit giving.

Trust is not merely an attitudinal outcome. In BEBA, it should be positioned as a mechanism that:

- reduces uncertainty,
- lowers perceived risk,
- increases confidence in the organisation,
- potentially facilitates intention formation,
- potentially supports behavioural enactment.

## Supported: TPB as intention-formation layer

`TO-000005` supports the inclusion of TPB constructs:

- attitude,
- subjective norm,
- perceived behavioural control,
- moral norm.

The reviewed claims justify using TPB as the belief-formation layer of BEBA, but not as the full behavioural architecture.

## Supported: Intention and behaviour must be separated

`IB-000012` strongly supports BEBA’s distinction between donation intention and actual donation behaviour.

This is foundational because it prevents BEBA from collapsing behavioural effectiveness into stated willingness to donate.

## Supported: Existing brand equity models under-specify mechanisms

`NB-000035` directly supports BEBA’s theoretical contribution. The claim that existing nonprofit brand equity models establish associations but under-specify belief-formation mechanisms strengthens the need for BEBA.

## Supported: Brand equity is multidimensional but structurally unsettled

`BE-000027` and `NB-000036` support the need to model nonprofit brand equity as multidimensional while leaving its internal structure open to competing specifications.

This supports the competing-model logic of BEBA.

## Supported: Relational brand equity may be behaviourally central

`RB-000003`, `TR-000007`, `CO-000005`, and `BE-000031` support the importance of relational constructs such as trust and commitment.

This strengthens the claim that nonprofit brand equity may operate less through static image or awareness alone and more through relational mechanisms that support sustained giving.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## Challenged: Brand attitude as the dominant pathway

`MN-000007` challenges brand-attitude-centric explanations by stating that brand attitudes can become non-significant when moral norms and identity-based constructs are controlled.

This suggests that BEBA should not over-prioritise attitude as the primary mediator between brand equity and donation behaviour.

Implication:

```text
Brand image → attitude → intention
```

should be treated as only one possible pathway, not the dominant or exclusive pathway.

## Challenged: Simple subjective-norm assumptions

`SN-000006` challenges simple assumptions that social information or subjective norm always increases giving.

Social influence may produce:

- positive effects,
- null effects,
- negative effects.

Therefore, subjective norm should be modelled as context-dependent rather than universally positive.

## Challenged: Universal portability of nonprofit brand equity models

`BE-000028` suggests that the Rios Romero et al. nonprofit brand equity model failed to replicate in an Austrian context, though the reviewed material does not provide empirical details.

Because confidence is low and empirical details are missing, this should be treated cautiously.

The theoretical implication is nevertheless important: nonprofit brand equity architectures may be context-sensitive rather than universally portable.

## Challenged: Intention-only explanations of donation behaviour

`IB-000012`, `BE-000029`, `BE-000030`, and `IB-000014` challenge models that explain donation behaviour primarily through intention.

BEBA should maintain a dual architecture:

```text
brand equity → intention formation
brand equity → enactment conditions → behaviour
```

rather than assuming that intention fully mediates brand effects.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Credence-good condition

BEBA is especially relevant where donors cannot directly verify:

- impact,
- quality,
- efficiency,
- beneficiary outcomes,
- organisational integrity.

This is a core boundary condition rather than a peripheral contextual variable.

## 2. Market crowding and information overload

The lighthouse mechanism implies that brand equity becomes more behaviourally effective when donors face many competing nonprofit options.

Under low-crowding conditions, the orienting function of brand equity may be weaker.

## 3. Contextual portability

`BE-000028` suggests possible national or cultural context dependence. However, because empirical details are not provided, this should remain a low-confidence boundary condition.

Recommended wording:

> Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

or:

> The reviewed material suggests possible context dependence, but empirical details must be verified before treating this as an established boundary condition.

## 4. Social influence ambiguity

Subjective norm effects depend on context. Social information may backfire or produce no effect.

Therefore, BEBA should avoid specifying subjective norm as a uniformly positive pathway.

## 5. Moral and identity salience

Where moral norms and donor identity are highly salient, brand attitude may become less important as a predictor.

This creates a boundary condition for attitude-based brand equity explanations.

## 6. Donation type and behavioural repetition

Habit and commitment pathways are likely more relevant for repeated giving than for one-off giving.

This is implied by the habit and commitment claims but requires further evidence before being canonical.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed claims strongly support BEBA’s competing-model approach.

## 1. Brand-equity-only model is insufficient

A model containing only brand equity constructs and donation intention is theoretically under-specified because it does not explain:

- how brand perceptions become beliefs,
- how beliefs become intention,
- how intention becomes behaviour,
- why intentions fail to translate into behaviour.

## 2. TPB-only model is insufficient

A TPB-only model may explain intention formation but does not explain how brand equity structures the belief inputs that feed into attitude, subjective norm, perceived behavioural control, or moral norm.

## 3. IBM-only model is insufficient

An IBM-only model may explain enactment conditions but does not explain why brand-related constructs shape knowledge, salience, habit, or perceived constraints.

## 4. BEBA integrated model has comparative promise

`IM-000002` supports the candidate proposition that an integrated model may explain donation behaviour better than single-component models.

The model competition set should include at least:

```text
Model A: Brand Equity → Intention
Model B: TPB → Intention → Behaviour
Model C: IBM Enactment Conditions → Behaviour
Model D: Brand Equity → TPB → Intention → Behaviour
Model E: Brand Equity → TPB → Intention + IBM Enactment → Behaviour
Model F: Relational Brand Equity Priority Model
Model G: Processual or Network Brand Equity Model
```

## 5. Competing brand equity structures remain necessary

`NB-000036` and `BE-000032` imply that the internal architecture of nonprofit brand equity should not be assumed.

Alternative structural forms should remain in competition:

- additive model,
- hierarchical model,
- relational model,
- processual model,
- network model.

## 6. Moral norm and identity should be included as competing pathways

Because `MN-000007` challenges brand-attitude-centric explanations, model competition should include moral norm and identity-mediated alternatives.

Possible competing pathway:

```text
Commitment → donor identity → moral norm → intention/behaviour
```

against:

```text
Brand image → attitude → intention/behaviour
```

# Recommendation

## 1. Integrate the claim set as a theory-integration candidate, not as canonical theory

The reviewed material is strong enough to support BEBA’s architecture but not all mechanisms are equally evidenced.

Recommended status:

```text
Theory integration candidate
```

not canonical ontology.

## 2. Preserve the dual-pathway structure as central to BEBA

The strongest architectural implication is:

```text
Brand equity affects donation behaviour through:
1. intention formation, and
2. behavioural enactment.
```

This should be treated as a core BEBA candidate proposition.

## 3. Elevate uncertainty and credence-good exchange to foundational status

`CG-000004` should be treated as foundational for the BEBA research question because it explains why nonprofit brands matter behaviourally.

## 4. Treat trust as the most mature mechanism

Trust has high-confidence support and should be prioritised as the first mechanism connecting nonprofit brand equity to behaviour under uncertainty.

## 5. Keep awareness and image pathways as lower-confidence mechanisms

Awareness and image pathways are theoretically plausible but currently marked low confidence. They should not be overclaimed.

## 6. Include moral norm and identity as competing explanations

BEBA should explicitly account for the possibility that moral norm and identity-based pathways outperform brand-attitude pathways.

This is important for theoretical balance and model competition.

## 7. Maintain model competition rather than selecting one brand equity structure prematurely

The internal structure of nonprofit brand equity remains unsettled. BEBA should continue to compare additive, hierarchical, relational, processual, and network-based specifications.

## 8. Do not treat empirical predictions as findings yet

Claims such as:

- brand equity explains behaviour beyond intention,
- IBM enactment conditions mediate residual brand effects,
- relational constructs outperform cognitive-evaluative constructs,
- processual/network models show superior fit,

should remain empirical predictions until verified by pipeline outputs.

## 10. Summary judgment

This claim set substantially supports BEBA’s theoretical architecture. Its strongest contribution is to connect nonprofit brand equity to behavioural effectiveness through uncertainty reduction, TPB belief formation, and IBM-style enactment conditions. It also prevents BEBA from becoming an intention-only or attitude-centric brand equity model by introducing moral norms, identity, habit, constraints, and market crowding as competing or moderating explanations.
