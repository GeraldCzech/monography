# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set supports a central BEBA premise: **brand equity should not be imported into nonprofit research as a single stable construct but reconstructed as a context-sensitive behavioural architecture.**

The claims show that brand equity research is historically broad, theoretically fragmented, and contextually diversified. Financial approaches explain brands as value-generating assets, while customer-based approaches explain brand effects through cognition, knowledge, associations, and response. However, the reviewed claims also indicate that these approaches have not converged into a single coherent model.

For BEBA, this implies that nonprofit brand equity should be theorised not primarily as a valuation object or as a fixed set of cognitive dimensions, but as a **brand-enabled behaviour architecture** linking:

1. brand-related cognition and meaning,
2. stakeholder-specific behavioural readiness,
3. organisational and market-level resource flows,
4. contextual conditions of uncertainty,
5. and nonprofit-specific behavioural outcomes such as donating, volunteering, advocating, trusting, legitimising, or remaining committed.

The claim set therefore strengthens BEBA’s justification as an integrative and reconstructive theory project rather than as a direct extension of commercial brand equity models.

The Market Layer is affected because the claim set establishes that brand equity has historically been tied to market value, financial valuation, contextual differentiation, and sector-specific adaptation. BEBA must therefore define how nonprofit brands generate behavioural and resource effects in markets or quasi-markets where financial valuation is not the only or dominant outcome.

Implication: BEBA should distinguish commercial value creation from nonprofit resource mobilisation and legitimacy generation.

## 2. Individual Layer

Strongly affected.

Relevant claims:

- `CB-000005`
- `BK-000004`
- `BE-000020`

The Individual Layer is affected because customer-based brand equity explains brand effects through brand knowledge, awareness, image, associations, and response. BEBA can adopt this as a cognitive foundation but must translate “consumer response to marketing activities” into nonprofit-relevant behaviour under uncertainty.

Implication: brand knowledge may become a behavioural enabling condition, but it is not sufficient by itself to explain nonprofit support behaviour.

## 3. Organisational Layer

Moderately to strongly affected.

Relevant claims:

- `BE-000016`
- `EB-000001`

The Organisational Layer is affected because Aaker-style brand assets and employee-based brand equity extend brand equity beyond external consumers. For BEBA, this supports the treatment of brands as organisationally embedded behavioural resources.

Implication: internal actors, especially employees and volunteers, may participate in producing, transmitting, and stabilising brand-enabled behaviour.

## 4. Digital / Signal Layer

Affected as an emerging or cross-cutting layer.

Relevant claims:

- `DB-000002`
- `BE-000021`

The reviewed claims suggest that digital indicators may matter but remain insufficiently validated as theory-driven brand equity measures.

Implication: BEBA should treat digital metrics as possible **signals**, **affordances**, or **indicators**, not automatically as brand equity constructs.

- `CB-000005`

This model would treat nonprofit brands as assets that generate separable value.

Supported by:

- `FB-000001`
- `BE-000016`

Limitation: It risks inappropriate transfer of commercial valuation logic into nonprofit settings.

- `BB-000001`

- `EB-000001`
- `DB-000002`

Limitation: It may become descriptive fragmentation unless integrated by a higher-order mechanism.

This model would explain nonprofit brand equity as a multi-level mechanism in which brand meanings, knowledge, trust, and organisational signals enable stakeholder behaviour under uncertainty.

Supported by:

- `BE-000017`

## Model Competition Conclusion

The reviewed claim set favours BEBA over direct adoption of either classical CBBE or financial brand equity models. However, BEBA should explicitly preserve those models as competitors or partial explanations rather than dismiss them.

# Recommendation

## 1. Integrate this claim set as a foundational justification for BEBA

The claim set should be used to support the argument that BEBA is necessary because brand equity research is fragmented, commercially biased, and insufficiently integrated across cognitive, financial, organisational, and contextual perspectives.

## 2. Do not treat brand equity as a canonical fixed construct without further review

The reviewed claims support a candidate status for inherited brand equity concepts. BEBA should not canonise a single universal brand equity model unless additional evidence justifies it.

## 3. Develop a BEBA-specific translation layer

Recommended candidate object: **Concept / Theory Integration Note**

Possible title:  
`Behavioural Translation of Brand Equity in BEBA`

Purpose:  
To explain how BEBA translates financial and customer-based brand equity into nonprofit behavioural effectiveness.

## 4. Preserve competing models

BEBA should explicitly compare itself against:

- classical customer-based brand equity,
- financial brand equity,
- Aaker-style asset models,
- context-specific adapted brand equity models,
- digital signal-based brand equity approaches.

This is important because the reviewed claims show that no single inherited model is sufficient.

## 5. Treat nonprofit brand equity as underdeveloped in this reviewed literature

The claim `NB-000020` notes that nonprofit brand equity is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature. Therefore, BEBA’s nonprofit claims should remain carefully framed unless supported by additional nonprofit-specific evidence.

Recommended wording:  
“The reviewed brand equity literature provides strong justification for context-sensitive reconstruction, but nonprofit-specific evidence remains insufficiently covered in this review.”
