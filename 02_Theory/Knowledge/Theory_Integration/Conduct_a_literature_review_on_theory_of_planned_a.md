# Input Reviewed

This theory integration protocol reviews the following candidate claims derived from `REVIEW-000103`:
- `TO-000008`: TPB's core constructs predict donation intention.
- `TO-000009`: Extending TPB with moral norm and past behavior increases predictive power.
- `IB-000026`: The intention-behavior gap is significant in donation contexts.
- `MN-000009`: Moral norm is a powerful predictor of donation intention.
- `PB-000029`: Past donation behavior predicts future donation.
- `SN-000008`: The effect of subjective norm is context-dependent.
- `BI-000052`: Brand image influences intention primarily through attitude.
- `DB-000010`: A model integrating DBBE into TPB is a research gap.
- `IB-000027`: The Integrated Behavioral Model (IBM) is underutilized in donation research.
- `PB-000030`: PBC's influence is heightened when behaviors have high barriers.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claims collectively establish the theoretical landscape BEBA operates in. The core implication is that the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB), especially when extended with moral norm and past behavior, provides a robust and empirically validated baseline for predicting *donation intention*. However, this baseline model is insufficient for predicting actual *behavior*, due to the well-documented intention-behavior gap (`IB-000026`).

This solidifies BEBA's primary theoretical contribution: to explain *how* nonprofit brand equity functions as a mechanism to bridge this gap. The claims strongly suggest that BEBA should be positioned as an application and extension of the Integrated Behavioral Model (IBM), focusing on the under-researched post-intentional factors like `environmental constraints` and `skills` (`IB-000027`). The role of the brand is not just to influence pre-intentional attitudes (`BI-000052`), but to structure the behavioral context to enable intended actions to occur.

# BEBA Layer Affected

The claims affect all three primary BEBA layers:
- **Individual Layer:** This is the primary locus of the reviewed claims. They define the core cognitive architecture of intention formation (Attitude, Subjective Norm, PBC, Moral Norm, Past Behavior) and identify the intention-behavior gap as a key individual-level phenomenon.
- **Organisational Layer:** The claims establish the research gap for integrating Donor-Based Brand Equity (`DB-000010`) and position the organization's brand as the key independent variable BEBA introduces. The underutilization of the IBM (`IB-000027`) points to an organizational-level opportunity: brands can actively manage `environmental constraints` and provide `skills`.
- **Societal Layer:** The inconsistent effect of Subjective Norm (`SN-000008`) highlights that the effectiveness of social influence mechanisms is moderated by societal-level factors like culture (e.g., individualism vs. collectivism).

# Existing Claims Supported

These claims provide strong empirical support for BEBA's foundational premises:
- **BEBA's Core Problem:** Claim `IB-000026` provides high-confidence evidence for the existence and significance of the intention-behavior gap, which is the central problem BEBA seeks to solve.
- **BEBA's Research Gap:** Claims `DB-000010` and `IB-000027` explicitly validate the research gap that BEBA is designed to fill, confirming that integrating brand equity with a comprehensive behavioral model like the IBM is a novel and necessary contribution.
- **BEBA's Foundational Link:** Claim `BI-000052` supports the basic mechanism that brand constructs (image, trust) link into the intention-formation process, primarily by shaping attitudes. This provides an established entry point for BEBA's more advanced claims.

# Existing Claims Challenged

The reviewed claims do not directly falsify any core BEBA tenets. Instead, they challenge any *oversimplified* version of the BEBA model. Specifically:
- They challenge a model that posits a direct Brand Equity -> Behavior link without mediating through the intention-formation process. The strength of the TPB framework (`TO-000008`) confirms the centrality of `Intention`.
- They challenge a brand model that focuses solely on rational/affective attitudes (`BI-000052`). The importance of `Moral Norm` (`TO-000009`, `MN-000009`) and `Past Behavior` (`PB-000029`) means BEBA must account for how brands interact with moral and habitual drivers.

# New Candidate Claims

Based on this synthesis, the following higher-order claims for the BEBA framework are proposed:

1.  **C-BEBA-001:** Nonprofit Brand Equity acts as a behavioral architecture that primarily bridges the intention-behavior gap by shaping post-intentional factors (e.g., skills, environmental constraints) as specified in the Integrated Behavioral Model.
2.  **C-BEBA-002:** The behavioral effectiveness of a nonprofit brand is a function of its ability to not only influence attitudes but also to activate donors' moral norms and facilitate the formation of donation habits.
3.  **C-BEBA-003:** A core function of a BEBA is to increase Perceived Behavioral Control (PBC) by reducing the actual and perceived barriers to specific supportive behaviors (e.g., simplifying the donation process).

# Boundary Conditions

The review identifies two important boundary conditions:
1.  **Cultural Context:** The influence of social influence mechanisms (Subjective Norm) is conditional on the cultural context (`SN-000008`). Therefore, a BEBA's reliance on community or social proof elements may be more or less effective in collectivist versus individualistic societies.
2.  **Behavioral Complexity:** The importance of Perceived Behavioral Control (PBC) is conditional on the complexity and perceived barriers of the target behavior (`PB-000030`). A BEBA designed for a high-barrier behavior (e.g., volunteering time) must focus more heavily on building self-efficacy and reducing barriers than one for a low-barrier behavior (e.g., a small online donation).

# Model Competition Implications

This review clearly defines the primary competing model for BEBA. The "incumbent" model is not the basic TPB, but an **Extended TPB (E-TPB)** that includes `Moral Norm` and `Past Behavior` as direct predictors of `Intention`.

Therefore, for BEBA to demonstrate theoretical and empirical superiority, it must:
1.  Replicate the findings of the E-TPB in predicting `Intention`.
2.  Demonstrate significantly better predictive power for actual `Behavior`.
3.  Show that the BEBA constructs (e.g., brand-driven reduction of environmental constraints) mediate the relationship between `Intention` and `Behavior` and explain variance beyond the E-TPB framework.

# Ontology Candidate Implications

This review solidifies the necessity of including the following concepts as first-class objects in the BEBA ontology:
- **Core TPB Constructs:** `Attitude`, `SubjectiveNorm`, `PerceivedBehavioralControl`.
- **Core Outcome Constructs:** `BehavioralIntention`, `Behavior`.
- **Key TPB Extensions:** `MoralNorm`, `PastBehavior` (as a proxy for `Habit`).
- **Core BEBA Problem:** `IntentionBehaviorGap`.
- **Core BEBA Levers (from IBM):** `EnvironmentalConstraints`, `Skills`.

These concepts form the foundational cognitive and behavioral layer upon which the brand equity constructs will be built.

# Recommendation

1.  **Adopt E-TPB as Baseline:** Formally adopt the Extended Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB + Moral Norm + Past Behavior) as the canonical baseline model for intention formation within the BEBA framework. All BEBA models must demonstrate how they engage with and extend this baseline.
2.  **Prioritize Post-Intention Mechanisms:** Focus theory development efforts on specifying the mechanisms by which Donor-Based Brand Equity (DBBE) influences the post-intention factors from the Integrated Behavioral Model (`Skills`, `EnvironmentalConstraints`). This is the most significant and well-justified research gap.
3.  **Create Concept Files:** Create or update canonical concept files for `MoralNorm` and `IntentionBehaviorGap` to reflect their high confidence and foundational importance established by this review.
4.  **Update Theoretical Map:** Update `01_THEORETICAL_MAP.md` to visually represent the E-TPB as the starting point and clearly position BEBA as the bridge across the intention-behavior gap, linking to IBM constructs.
