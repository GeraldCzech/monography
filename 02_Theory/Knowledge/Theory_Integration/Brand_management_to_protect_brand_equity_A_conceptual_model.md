# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set strengthens the organisational layer of BEBA by specifying how nonprofit brand equity can become behaviourally effective before external stakeholder response occurs.

The central implication is that brand-enabled behaviour depends on an internal organisational architecture:

```text
Brand orientation
→ explicit identity, purpose, and values
→ internal and external communication
→ internal branding and cultural embedding
→ brand-congruent behaviour
→ credibility and trust
→ protected or strengthened brand equity
```

This supports BEBA’s assumption that brand equity is not merely a stakeholder perception outcome but is partly produced through organisational systems that make brand meaning behaviourally actionable.

The claims also clarify that trust and credibility are not created by communication alone. They depend on the organisation’s perceived capability, consistency, and recovery from failures or breaches of the brand promise.

# BEBA Layer Affected

Primary affected layer:

The claim set primarily concerns internal organisational preconditions: brand orientation, identity definition, communication, internal branding, culture, and delivery capability. However, it also connects these internal mechanisms to stakeholder-facing constructs such as credibility, trust, alignment, and reputation.

# Existing Claims Challenged

No major BEBA claim is directly contradicted by the reviewed material.

However, the claim set qualifies or constrains several possible interpretations of BEBA:

## 1. External communication is insufficient

The claims challenge any simplified model in which brand equity is primarily produced through outward messaging. Communication matters, but only after identity, orientation, and organisational behaviour are aligned.

## 2. Brand trust is not merely attitudinal

The claims challenge a purely perception-based interpretation of brand trust. Trust is linked to consistent delivery and perceived organisational capability.

## 3. Brand equity is not automatically protected by strong identity

The recovery claim shows that even well-defined brands are vulnerable to breaches, failures, or stakeholder disappointment. Brand equity requires repair mechanisms.

# Boundary Conditions

The claim set introduces several important boundary conditions.

## 1. Dependence on internal strategic clarity

Brand-enabled behaviour is unlikely when identity, purpose, and values are undefined or inconsistently understood.

## 2. Dependence on organisational capability

Credibility requires more than symbolic brand expression. It depends on perceived expertise and capability to deliver the brand promise.

## 3. Dependence on consistency over time

Trust formation requires repeated and reliable fulfilment of expectations. A single act of communication is insufficient.

## 4. Vulnerability to brand-promise breaches

BEBA must account for failure conditions. Perceived inconsistency, service failure, mission failure, or unmet expectations may weaken trust and brand equity.

## 5. Recovery quality as moderator

The effect of failures on brand equity may depend on whether the organisation responds through credible recovery mechanisms.

## 6. Service and nonprofit relevance

The claims are especially relevant to service and nonprofit settings, where stakeholders often face uncertainty about intangible performance, mission fulfilment, and impact delivery.

# Model Competition Implications

The claim set has implications for competing BEBA model architectures.

## 1. Supports organisational-first models

The reviewed claims strengthen models in which brand equity emerges partly from internal organisational orientation, identity clarity, employee alignment, and delivery consistency.

A simplified pathway would be:

```text
Organisational brand orientation
→ internal alignment
→ brand-congruent behaviour
→ stakeholder credibility/trust
→ brand equity
→ behavioural effectiveness
```

## 2. Weakens communication-only models

The material weakens models that treat brand communication as the primary or sufficient driver of stakeholder brand equity.

Communication remains important, but it appears dependent on prior identity clarity and organisational enactment.

## 3. Supports trust-mediated models

The claims support models in which brand credibility and brand trust mediate the relationship between organisational behaviour and stakeholder behavioural outcomes.

Possible sequence:

```text
Capability + consistency
→ credibility
→ trust
→ behavioural willingness
```

## 4. Adds recovery-based dynamic models

The recovery claim suggests that BEBA should not be modelled only as a linear accumulation process. Brand equity may also depend on how organisations respond after expectation violations.

This introduces a possible dynamic model:

```text
Brand promise breach
→ perceived inconsistency
→ trust/reputation threat
→ recovery response
→ trust repair or further erosion
→ brand equity protection or decline
```

## 5. Supports uncertainty-reduction explanation

The claims align strongly with BEBA’s current research question:

> How do nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty?

They suggest that nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective when internal orientation, capability, consistency, and recovery reduce uncertainty for stakeholders.

## Possible new or refined ontology candidates

### 1. Brand Promise Delivery

**Object type:** Concept / Mechanism candidate  
**Suggested definition:**  
The organisational enactment of stated brand meaning through consistent actions, services, decisions, and stakeholder interactions.

**Rationale:**  
Several claims depend on delivery of the brand promise, but delivery may need to be modelled separately from communication.

### 2. Brand Recovery

**Object type:** Concept / Mechanism candidate  
**Suggested definition:**  
Organisational actions undertaken to repair stakeholder trust, credibility, or reputation after a perceived breach of the brand promise.

**Rationale:**  
`BR-000008` introduces a failure-and-repair mechanism that may be important for uncertainty management.

### 3. Brand-Congruent Employee Behaviour

**Object type:** Construct / Behavioural outcome candidate  
**Suggested definition:**  
Employee behaviour that is aligned with the organisation’s defined brand identity, values, and promise.

**Rationale:**  
`IB-000021` depends on this behavioural outcome but the construct may need explicit status in BEBA.

### 4. Organisational Brand Capability

**Object type:** Construct / Mechanism candidate  
**Suggested definition:**  
The perceived or actual organisational ability to fulfil the brand promise through expertise, resources, processes, and reliable performance.

**Rationale:**  
`BC-000022` links credibility to perceived expertise and capability.

### 5. Brand Meaning Clarity

**Object type:** Construct candidate  
**Suggested definition:**  
The degree to which an organisation’s brand identity, purpose, and values are explicitly defined and internally understandable.

**Rationale:**  
`BI-000036` and `BC-000021` imply that explicitness and shared understanding are prerequisites for coordinated behaviour.
