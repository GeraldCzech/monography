# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set supports a central BEBA proposition: **nonprofit brand effectiveness is not reducible to either internal identity or external perception, but emerges from the alignment of internal organisational architecture, enacted organisational behaviour, and stakeholder perception.**

The strongest theoretical contribution is the candidate pathway:

```text
Brand Identity
→ Organisational Cohesion
→ Brand Performance / Observable Organisational Behaviour
→ Stakeholder Trust
→ Sustained Support
→ Nonprofit Brand Effectiveness
```

This reinforces BEBA’s behaviour-first framing. Brand equity is not treated as a perception stock alone, but as an architecture that enables coordinated organisational action and converts that action into stakeholder trust and support under uncertainty.

The claim set also strengthens BEBA’s critique of perception-only and identity-only nonprofit brand models.

- vision

## 2. Individual / Stakeholder Layer

Secondary affected layer.

The claims identify stakeholder trust, awareness, support, and brand image as external perception and behaviour-related outcomes.

Important stakeholder outcomes include:

- donations
- volunteering
- media support / positive coverage
- sustained support

## 3. Market / Stakeholder-System Layer

The claims support BEBA’s assumption that nonprofit brand equity must account for multiple stakeholder pathways rather than a single customer pathway.

Relevant stakeholder groups include:

- donors
- volunteers
- beneficiaries
- media
- government, although government is explicitly not empirically covered in the reviewed study

## 4. Societal Layer

The boundary-condition claim affects the societal layer by identifying government stakeholders as an under-evidenced pathway.

This matters because government actors may shape legitimacy, funding, regulation, partnership access, and public trust differently from donors, beneficiaries, volunteers, or media.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Government stakeholder exclusion

The strongest explicit boundary condition is:

```text
The reviewed empirical study does not include government stakeholders.
```

Therefore, the claim set should not be generalized to government-related brand effects without further evidence.

Government pathways may differ because government actors can function as:

- funders
- regulators
- legitimacy providers
- partners
- policy gatekeepers
- institutional validators

The reviewed material is therefore insufficient for strong BEBA claims about governmental trust, governmental support, or policy-level brand effectiveness.

## 2. Geographic and contextual specificity

The claims are based on an empirical study of five nonprofit organisations in Northern India.

This introduces possible boundary conditions related to:

- national nonprofit ecosystem
- institutional trust environment
- donor culture
- government–nonprofit relations
- media environment
- organisational size and professionalisation
- cultural expectations of nonprofit legitimacy

The current evidence should therefore remain medium-confidence and candidate-level.

## 3. Stakeholder heterogeneity

The reviewed claims group donors, beneficiaries, volunteers, and media as stakeholders, but these groups likely differ in:

- exposure to brand performance
- trust formation
- behavioural options
- risk perception
- dependence on the nonprofit
- ability to sanction or reward the organisation

BEBA should avoid treating “stakeholder trust” as a single undifferentiated pathway unless later evidence supports this.

## 4. Mediation strength not quantified

The reviewed material proposes or supports mediation by brand performance, but the relative strength of mediation is not quantified in the provided claims.

Therefore, BEBA can use the mediation as a candidate theoretical mechanism, but not yet as a confirmed empirical effect size claim.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed claim set is useful for BEBA because it creates clear model competition.

## Model A: Image-Only Model

```text
Brand Image → Support
```

This model is challenged by `NB-000065`.

Limitation: It ignores internal identity, organisational behaviour, service quality, transparency, and internal cohesion.

## Model B: Identity-Only Model

```text
Brand Identity → Brand Effectiveness
```

This model is challenged by `NB-000066` and `BI-000038`.

Limitation: It assumes internal coherence is sufficient without explaining enactment, stakeholder observation, or behavioural conversion.

## Model C: Direct Identity-to-Image Model

```text
Brand Identity → Brand Image → Support
```

This model is qualified by `BP-000019`.

Limitation: It under-specifies the behavioural translation mechanism between internal identity and external perception.

## Model D: Performance-Mediation Model

```text
Brand Identity
→ Brand Performance
→ Brand Image / Trust
→ Sustained Support
```

This model is directly supported by `BP-000019`, `BP-000017`, `BP-000018`, and `TR-000011`.

This is currently the most BEBA-consistent model among the reviewed options.

## Model E: Alignment Model

```text
Brand Identity + Brand Performance + Brand Image alignment
→ Nonprofit Brand Effectiveness
```

This model is supported by `NB-000064`.

Its advantage is that it captures coherence across internal, behavioural, and perceptual dimensions. Its limitation is that “alignment” needs sharper operationalisation before it can become a canonical BEBA mechanism.

## Model F: Multi-Stakeholder BEBA Model

```text
Internal Identity
→ Organisational Cohesion
→ Performance Signals
→ Stakeholder-Specific Trust
→ Stakeholder-Specific Support Behaviour
→ Nonprofit Brand Effectiveness
```

This is the most complete BEBA-compatible candidate model.

It incorporates:

- internal coordination
- enacted performance
- trust formation
- stakeholder heterogeneity
- behavioural outcomes
- effectiveness beyond fundraising

Recommendation: retain this as a candidate integrative model, not yet canonical.
