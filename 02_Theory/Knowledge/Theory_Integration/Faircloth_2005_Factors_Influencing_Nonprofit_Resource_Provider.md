# Input Reviewed

Candidate claim set from `REVIEW-000022` concerning nonprofit brand equity, brand personality, brand respect, brand differentiation, brand image, awareness/familiarity, brand congruence, brand relationship, and resource-provider-based brand equity.

The evidence base is described as primarily an exploratory, single-organisation telephone survey, focused on **resource provider support intentions** rather than observed behaviour. Several claims are explicitly mechanism-oriented rather than directly tested mediation effects.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set supports BEBA’s central premise that nonprofit brands may become behaviourally effective not merely by increasing general awareness, but by structuring how resource providers interpret, evaluate, and simplify support decisions under uncertainty.

The most important theoretical implication is that **brand-enabled behaviour in nonprofit contexts appears to depend on evaluative and relational brand meanings**, not on salience alone. Brand personality, respect, differentiation, image, and perceived scale are positioned as possible behavioural enablers, whereas top-of-mind awareness and familiarity show weaker, heterogeneous, or even negative effects.

This refines BEBA by suggesting that nonprofit brand equity should be modelled less as “being known” and more as a **behavioural decision architecture** composed of:

1. evaluative meaning,
2. relational legitimacy,
3. perceived distinctiveness,
4. uncertainty-reducing heuristics,
5. fit with resource-provider identity or self-concept.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## Primary Layer: Individual

Most claims affect the **Individual** layer because they concern resource providers’ perceptions, associations, intentions, and decision processes.

Relevant individual-level mechanisms include:

- perceived nonprofit brand personality,
- perceived respectability,
- perceived differentiation,
- perceived image,
- perceived familiarity,
- brand congruence with self-concept,
- trustworthiness as a relationship partner,
- decision simplification under uncertainty.

## Secondary Layer: Organisational

`NB-000027` also affects the **Organisational** layer by suggesting that for-profit brand equity concepts can be empirically adapted to nonprofit resource-provider contexts.

However, this should remain qualified because the evidence is exploratory and single-context.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Awareness-based accounts are insufficient

Challenged or qualified by:

- `BA-000009`
- `BF-000004`
- `BA-000010`

Top-of-mind recall is not a significant predictor in the reviewed study, while familiarity is negatively associated with support intentions. This challenges any simple model in which awareness automatically increases support.

The implication for BEBA is important: **salience is not equivalent to behavioural enablement**.

## 2. Familiarity is not necessarily positive

`BF-000004` directly challenges simple familiarity-based brand equity logic.

If familiarity contains negative evaluative content, it may reduce support intentions. BEBA should therefore distinguish between:

- mere recognition,
- depth of knowledge,
- valence of associations,
- confidence in the organisation,
- relational trust,
- perceived competence/respectability.

## 3. Not all favourable associations become behavioural drivers

`BC-000008` qualifies BEBA by showing that perceived brand character was not a significant predictor in the reviewed study.

This suggests that BEBA should avoid assuming that all positive associations are behaviourally equivalent. Some associations may be symbolically favourable but behaviourally inert.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Evidence type boundary

The reviewed evidence concerns **support intentions**, not observed behaviour.

BEBA should therefore avoid treating these claims as evidence of actual donation, volunteering, advocacy, or long-term resource provision behaviour.

## 2. Organisational context boundary

The empirical evidence is based on a **single nonprofit organisation**. Findings may reflect organisation-specific perceptions rather than general nonprofit brand mechanisms.

## 3. Awareness-valence boundary

Awareness and familiarity may only enable behaviour when associated content is favourable, trustworthy, or uncertainty-reducing.

This creates a boundary condition for awareness-based brand equity:

> Awareness is behaviourally useful only when it activates favourable or confidence-producing associations.

## 4. Construct-dimensionality boundary

Brand image and awareness appear multidimensional. BEBA should avoid treating them as unitary constructs.

Relevant distinctions include:

- awareness as recall,
- awareness as familiarity,
- familiarity as neutral knowledge,
- familiarity as negatively valenced knowledge,
- image as scale,
- image as character,
- image as respectability,
- image as differentiation.

## 5. Nonprofit-specific uncertainty boundary

The heuristic role of brand image may be especially relevant when resource providers face uncertainty about:

- organisational trustworthiness,
- impact,
- legitimacy,
- competence,
- cause fit,
- appropriate recipient choice.

The reviewed document supports this as a candidate implication, but direct evidence is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

# Model Competition Implications

This claim set has significant implications for competing BEBA models.

## Model 1: Awareness-First Brand Equity Model

This model is weakened.

The evidence does not support a simple pathway:

> awareness → support intention

Top-of-mind awareness is not significant, and familiarity may be negative. Therefore, awareness-first models should be treated as insufficient unless they include association valence and evaluative content.

## Model 2: Evaluative Association Model

This model is strengthened.

Claims concerning brand personality, respect, differentiation, image, and scale suggest that evaluative brand associations are more behaviourally relevant than simple salience.

Possible pathway:

> brand associations → evaluation of organisation → support intention

## Model 3: Relational Trust / Legitimacy Model

This model is strengthened conceptually.

Claims around brand personality, respect, and relationship partner legitimacy suggest that nonprofit brands may enable behaviour by making the organisation appear trustworthy, respectable, and relationally appropriate.

Possible pathway:

> brand personality/respect → relational legitimacy → support intention

However, direct mediation evidence is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

## Model 4: Uncertainty-Reduction / Heuristic Model

This model is strengthened as a theoretical candidate.

`BI-000019` directly links brand image to decision simplification. This aligns with BEBA’s central research question. However, the evidence is conceptual rather than directly tested.

Possible pathway:

> uncertainty → reliance on brand image heuristic → support intention

## Model 5: Self-Congruence Model

This model is introduced or reinforced as a candidate mechanism.

`BC-000009` suggests that support is more likely when nonprofit brand personality matches the resource provider’s self-concept.

Possible pathway:

> brand personality-self congruence → identification / fit → support intention

But the reviewed document states that this was proposed rather than directly tested in Faircloth’s study.

## Model 6: General Prosocial Motivation Model

This model is challenged as a complete explanation.

`NB-000026` indicates that brand-related antecedents explain variance after controlling for altruistic disposition.

This supports BEBA’s position that nonprofit support behaviour cannot be fully explained by prosocial disposition alone.

# Recommendation

## 1. Integrate the claim set as support for BEBA’s behavioural architecture premise

The reviewed claims should be used to support the argument that nonprofit brand equity is behaviourally meaningful when it explains support intentions beyond altruistic disposition.

Most useful claim: `NB-000026`.

## 2. Do not treat awareness as a sufficient BEBA mechanism

The awareness findings are theoretically important because they prevent BEBA from becoming a simple salience model.

Recommended BEBA refinement:

> Awareness is a precondition for some brand effects but not a sufficient behavioural mechanism. Its effect depends on the evaluative and relational content activated by familiarity or recall.

## 3. Develop a candidate distinction between salience, familiarity, and evaluative meaning

This claim set strongly supports separating:

- brand awareness,
- top-of-mind recall,
- brand familiarity,
- brand image,
- brand respect,
- brand differentiation,
- brand personality.

This may require glossary and ontology candidate work if not already represented.

## 4. Treat brand image as a candidate uncertainty-reduction mechanism

`BI-000019` should be connected to BEBA’s core question, but with caution:

> The reviewed material supports brand image as a candidate heuristic mechanism, but direct evidence for mediation through uncertainty reduction is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

## 5. Use the findings for model competition

This claim set is especially valuable for contrasting competing explanations:

| Competing explanation | Status after review |
|---|---|

## 6. Maintain candidate status

Because the empirical basis is exploratory, single-organisation, and intention-based, these claims should remain candidate-level unless additional replicated evidence is available.

Recommended status: **candidate, medium confidence** for empirical association claims; **candidate, low confidence** for untested mechanisms.
