# Core Theoretical Implication

The main implication for BEBA is that nonprofit brand equity should not be theorised as a single perceptual asset that directly causes giving. Instead, nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective by **activating, framing, coordinating, and stabilising multiple giving mechanisms under uncertainty**.

This strongly supports the BEBA premise that nonprofit brand equity is best understood as a **Brand-Enabled Behaviour Architecture** rather than as a conventional brand-attitude or brand-preference construct.

The claim set implies that brand effectiveness in charitable giving depends on how the brand architecture shapes:

1. cognitive availability of need,
2. response to solicitation,
3. perceived costs and benefits,
4. psychological rewards,
5. reputational and signalling benefits,
6. value congruence,
7. perceived efficacy,
8. external credibility signals,
9. social context,
10. beneficiary representation,
11. interaction among mechanisms.

- `CA-000004`

- `R-000002`

## 4. Universal effects of public funding signals

Challenged by:

- `CO-000003`

BEBA should not assume that government funding automatically crowds out or crowds in private donations. The relationship appears contingent and empirically variable.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Beneficiary representation

Related claim:

- `AO-000002`

Identifiable single victims may generate stronger giving responses than statistical presentations of large numbers of victims. This suggests that need communication is not neutral. The format of beneficiary representation conditions the effectiveness of need-based brand communication.

## 2. Social observability

Related claims:

- `R-000001`
- `R-000002`

Reputational effects depend on whether giving is publicly observable and whether observers belong to the donor’s relevant social network. Brand-enabled social signalling is therefore stronger in visible, socially embedded giving contexts.

## 3. Incentive framing

Related claim:

- `CA-000004`

Economically equivalent incentives can have different behavioural effects depending on whether they are framed as matching grants, rebates, or other forms of support. BEBA should distinguish objective cost reduction from communicative framing.

## 4. External credibility signals

Related claim:

- `E-000002`

Leadership gifts or seed money can increase donations by signalling quality or efficacy. This indicates that brand equity may be strengthened by external signals rather than produced only by the focal organisation’s own communication.

## 5. Public funding context

Related claim:

- `CO-000003`

Government funding may crowd out, crowd in, or have no effect on private donations. This should be treated as a field-level contingency rather than a universal causal rule.

## 6. Strength of social ties

Related claim:

- `R-000002`

Reputational benefits are likely stronger when observers are socially relevant to the donor. This makes group size, anonymity, and tie strength important boundary conditions.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed claims have strong implications for model competition in BEBA.

## 1. Single-motive models are insufficient

Models based mainly on altruism, trust, satisfaction, awareness, or attitude are unlikely to capture the full behavioural architecture of charitable giving.

Supported by:

- `CG-000003`
- `A-000007`
- `IG-000001`

## 2. BEBA should compete against linear brand equity models

The claim set supports positioning BEBA against conventional nonprofit brand equity models that assume a direct pathway from brand awareness or brand attitude to donation intention.

BEBA’s competing explanation would be:

> Nonprofit brands produce behavioural effectiveness by configuring multiple giving mechanisms under uncertainty, not merely by increasing positive brand evaluations.

## 3. BEBA should include mechanism-based alternative models

Possible competing models include:

1. **Altruism-dominant model**  
   Giving is primarily driven by concern for beneficiaries.

2. **Warm-glow model**  
   Giving is primarily driven by internal psychological reward.

3. **Reputation/signalling model**  
   Giving is primarily driven by social visibility and reputational benefit.

4. **Efficacy model**  
   Giving is primarily driven by perceived impact and confidence that the gift matters.

5. **Solicitation-trigger model**  
   Giving is primarily driven by direct asks and behavioural prompts.

6. **Value-congruence model**  
   Giving is primarily driven by alignment between donor values and mission.

7. **Cost-framing model**  
   Giving is primarily driven by perceived economic cost and incentive framing.

8. **BEBA integrated model**  
   Giving is driven by the configuration, interaction, and salience of multiple mechanisms, enabled by nonprofit brand architecture.

## 4. BEBA’s distinct contribution is integration

The reviewed material explicitly notes that existing charitable giving research lacks formal models integrating all major mechanisms. This gives BEBA a clear theoretical opportunity:

> BEBA can contribute by explaining how nonprofit brands orchestrate multiple behavioural mechanisms rather than by proposing another isolated predictor of giving.

# Recommendation

## 1. Integrate as a high-priority theory candidate

This claim set should be treated as highly relevant to BEBA because it directly supports the core research question:

> How do nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty?

The document suggests that nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective by reducing, reframing, or exploiting uncertainty across several mechanisms: need, efficacy, value fit, social meaning, cost, and actionability.

## 2. Do not collapse the mechanisms into a single brand equity construct

BEBA should avoid absorbing all mechanisms into a broad undifferentiated “brand equity” construct. Instead, it should model brand equity as an architecture that enables and coordinates mechanisms.
