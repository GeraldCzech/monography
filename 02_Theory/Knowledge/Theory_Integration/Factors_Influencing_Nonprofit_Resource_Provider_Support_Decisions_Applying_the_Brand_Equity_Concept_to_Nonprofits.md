# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed material supports BEBA’s central premise that nonprofit brands can become behaviourally effective by shaping stakeholder perceptions that influence support intentions beyond general altruistic disposition.

However, the evidence also qualifies simple brand-equity transfer models from commercial marketing. Behavioural effectiveness is not produced by awareness or familiarity alone. Instead, specific dimensions of brand knowledge appear to matter: respect, differentiation, perceived scale/substance, and possibly relational personality cues. Conversely, positive-seeming dimensions such as character, and salience measures such as top-of-mind recall, may not be behaviourally consequential in the reviewed nonprofit setting.

The most important BEBA implication is therefore:

> Nonprofit brand equity should be theorised not as accumulated awareness or general favourability, but as a behaviour-enabling architecture in which specific, valenced brand perceptions reduce uncertainty, structure expectations, and make resource-provider action more likely.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## Primary Layer: Individual

The reviewed claims operate primarily at the individual stakeholder level. They concern perceptions, recall, familiarity, brand image, brand personality, and intentions to provide resource support.

## Secondary Layer: Relational / Interface Layer

Some claims also affect BEBA’s relational architecture, especially:

- `BP-000009`: brand personality as relational heuristic;
- `BP-000007`: respect as behaviourally relevant perception;
- `BF-000008`: valence of pre-existing perceptions as a boundary condition.

These suggest that behaviour is not only a cognitive response to brand knowledge but may also depend on whether the brand is perceived as a credible, respectable, differentiated, or relationship-worthy actor.

## Mechanism Layer

The reviewed claims affect candidate mechanisms inside BEBA, especially:

- salience mechanism;
- differentiation mechanism;
- respect/legitimacy mechanism;
- perceived substance or scale heuristic;
- valence-conditioned familiarity mechanism;
- relational heuristic mechanism.

# Existing Claims Supported

The reviewed material supports the following BEBA-compatible claim areas.

## 1. Brands can influence behaviour beyond altruism

Supported by `RP-000002`.

This is foundational for BEBA because it suggests that nonprofit support intentions are not reducible to pre-existing altruistic disposition. Brand-related perceptions appear to have behavioural relevance even when general altruistic volunteerism is controlled.

## 2. Brand equity should be defined behaviourally

Supported by `CB-000007`.

This aligns strongly with BEBA’s premise that nonprofit brand equity should not be treated merely as awareness, attitude, reputation, or symbolic value. It should be defined by its behavioural consequence: whether the brand changes stakeholder action tendencies.

## 3. Brand knowledge is multidimensional

Supported by:

- `BE-000035`
- `BI-000029`
- `BP-000010`

This supports BEBA’s refusal to treat “brand perception” as a single undifferentiated construct. Different components of brand knowledge may have different behavioural consequences.

## 4. Differentiation matters for behavioural effectiveness

Supported by `BP-000008`.

This aligns with BEBA’s assumption that nonprofit brands can become behaviourally effective by helping stakeholders distinguish one organization from alternatives under uncertainty.

## 5. Respect may be a behaviourally relevant nonprofit brand perception

Supported by `BP-000007`.

Respect appears particularly compatible with nonprofit contexts because stakeholders may evaluate organizations not only by attractiveness or familiarity, but also by moral, social, or institutional standing.

## 6. Salience is necessary but insufficient

Supported and qualified by:

- `BA-000018`
- `BA-000017`

Brand awareness may help a nonprofit enter the consideration set, but top-of-mind recall alone was not significantly associated with support intentions in the reviewed study. This supports a BEBA distinction between behavioural availability and behavioural activation.

## 7. Familiarity is not automatically beneficial

Supported by:

- `BF-000007`
- `BF-000008`

This is highly relevant for BEBA because it suggests that brand knowledge must be interpreted through valence. A familiar nonprofit brand may activate negative associations, distrust, fatigue, controversy, or perceptions of reduced need.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Awareness-based brand equity models are insufficient

Challenged by `BA-000017`.

If top-of-mind recall is not significantly associated with support intentions, BEBA should avoid modelling awareness as a direct or sufficient driver of nonprofit support behaviour.

## 2. Familiarity should not be treated as a uniformly positive antecedent

Challenged by `BF-000007`.

Greater familiarity may be negatively associated with support intentions. This challenges simple familiarity-trust-support pathways.

## 3. Positive brand image does not uniformly translate into support

Challenged or qualified by `BI-000028`.

Brand character, despite sounding normatively positive, was not significantly associated with support intentions. BEBA should therefore avoid assuming that all favourable image dimensions are behaviourally active.

## 4. Brand personality and brand image cannot be used interchangeably

Challenged by `BP-000010`.

The conceptual ambiguity between brand personality and brand image requires BEBA to maintain strict construct boundaries. Otherwise, BEBA risks importing measurement contamination from commercial brand equity theory.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Single-organization evidence

The reviewed empirical evidence is based on one nonprofit organization and should be treated as exploratory. BEBA should not generalize these effects across all nonprofit categories without further evidence.

## 2. Valence of prior perceptions

Familiarity may have positive, neutral, or negative behavioural effects depending on the content and valence of what stakeholders already know or believe about the organization.

## 3. Nonprofit category context

The salience, familiarity, and differentiation effects may vary depending on whether stakeholders perceive many interchangeable nonprofit alternatives or only a few dominant category actors.

## 4. Resource-provider role

The evidence concerns resource provider support intentions. It should not automatically be extended to beneficiaries, employees, volunteers, policymakers, or partner organizations without further review.

## 5. Intention rather than observed behaviour

The reviewed claims concern intentions to provide resource support, not necessarily verified behaviour. BEBA should distinguish behavioural intention from enacted support behaviour.

## 6. Construct ambiguity

Brand image and brand personality are not consistently separated in the broader literature. Any BEBA integration should avoid collapsing them unless the measurement model supports such treatment.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed material has important consequences for BEBA model competition.

## 1. Supports BEBA against pure altruism models

Because brand perceptions are associated with support intentions after controlling for general altruistic volunteerism, the evidence supports BEBA’s claim that nonprofit support behaviour is not explained by altruism alone.

Relevant candidate: `RP-000002`.

## 2. Challenges simple awareness models

The finding that top-of-mind recall is not significantly associated with support intentions weakens models in which awareness is assumed to directly produce nonprofit support.

Relevant candidate: `BA-000017`.

## 3. Challenges familiarity-as-trust models

The negative association between familiarity and support intentions challenges the assumption that more familiarity automatically increases trust or behavioural support.

Relevant candidates:

- `BF-000007`
- `BF-000008`

## 4. Supports multidimensional brand knowledge models

The mixed effects across respect, differentiation, scale, character, recall, and familiarity suggest that nonprofit brand equity should be modelled as a differentiated architecture rather than as a single global brand-strength construct.

Relevant candidates:

- `BE-000035`
- `BI-000029`
- `BP-000010`

## 5. Supports uncertainty-reduction and heuristic models

Differentiation, respect, and perceived scale/substance may operate as heuristics under uncertainty. This aligns with BEBA’s research question: how nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty.

## 6. Introduces bypass routes around brand equity

The reviewed material implies possible bypass routes:

### Bypass Route A: Altruism-driven support

Stakeholders may support nonprofits because of general altruistic disposition, independently of brand perceptions.

### Bypass Route B: Category salience without brand equity

A nonprofit may be recalled or recognized without that salience translating into support intention.

### Bypass Route C: Familiarity with negative valence

Familiarity may activate negative perceptions, reducing support despite high brand knowledge.

### Bypass Route D: Perceived need or organizational scale

Perceived scale may increase support through perceived capacity, but it could also, in other contexts, reduce support if stakeholders infer that a large organization already has enough resources. This latter possibility is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature and should remain a candidate boundary condition, not a confirmed claim.

# Recommendation

Integrate this claim set into BEBA as a **candidate individual-layer theory integration**, not as canonical theory yet.

Recommended treatment:

1. **Use as support for BEBA’s behavioural definition of nonprofit brand equity.**  
   The strongest theoretical contribution is that brand perceptions are associated with support intentions beyond altruistic disposition.

2. **Use as a qualification against awareness-only models.**  
   Top-of-mind recall and familiarity should not be treated as sufficient drivers of support.

3. **Create or maintain candidate mechanisms for:**
   - differentiation under uncertainty;
   - respect as behavioural legitimacy cue;
   - perceived scale/substance as decision heuristic;
   - valence-conditioned familiarity;
   - relational brand personality heuristic.

4. **Do not canonicalize the empirical effects yet.**  
   The evidence is exploratory, single-organization-based, and focused on intentions rather than observed behaviour.

5. **Flag construct-boundary work as necessary.**  
   BEBA should explicitly distinguish:
   - brand image;
   - brand personality;
   - brand awareness;
   - brand familiarity;
   - brand equity;
   - behavioural intention;
   - enacted support behaviour.

6. **Recommended integration status:**  
   `candidate / theory-supporting / requires further evidence`

7. **Recommended next step in the knowledge pipeline:**  
   Move this from claim-level review into a **Concept Workspace synthesis** on nonprofit brand perceptions as behaviour-enabling mechanisms, before any chapter-level writing.
