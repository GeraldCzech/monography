# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set strongly supports BEBA’s central theoretical move: nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective because they help donors act under uncertainty.

The claims position uncertainty as the behavioural problem, nonprofit brand equity as a multi-level enabling architecture, and brand-related trust, salience, symbolic meaning, and perceived efficacy as mechanisms that support both intention formation and intention enactment.

The most important theoretical contribution is the shift from brand equity as an attitudinal or organizational asset toward brand equity as behavioural infrastructure. This supports BEBA’s core idea that nonprofit brands do not merely create favourable evaluations; they structure the conditions under which giving becomes cognitively easier, socially meaningful, trusted, salient, and executable.

# Existing Claims Challenged

No direct contradiction to BEBA is present in the reviewed document. However, several claims create pressure to refine BEBA.

## Challenge 1: Brand equity cannot be treated as sufficient for behaviour

Raised by:

- `IB-000001`
- `IB-000002`

The intention–behaviour gap challenges any simple model in which positive brand perceptions automatically produce donations. BEBA must distinguish:

```text
brand-enabled intention formation
```

from:

```text
brand-enabled behavioural enactment
```

## Challenge 2: Brand equity may not be the only route to giving

Raised by:

- `TO-000001`
- `DD-000001`
- `GE-000001`

Giving may be explained through donor attitudes, norms, perceived control, moral identity, social pressure, situational triggers, or ecosystem-level influences without necessarily requiring brand equity as the central explanatory construct.

This does not reject BEBA, but it requires BEBA to compete against or integrate non-brand explanations.

## Challenge 3: Recursive causality complicates linear brand value chain logic

Raised by:

- `RC-000001`

If aggregate market performance reinforces individual brand perceptions, then BEBA cannot rely only on a linear sequence from brand investment to mindset to behaviour. It may need a recursive model:

```text
brand perceptions → behaviour → market signals → brand perceptions
```

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. High uncertainty contexts

BEBA is especially relevant when donors cannot directly observe nonprofit performance, impact, or trustworthiness.

Relevant claim:

- `UR-000001`

Boundary implication: Where uncertainty is low, brand-enabled uncertainty reduction may be less important.

## 2. Intention–behaviour gap contexts

BEBA is especially relevant where donors have positive intentions but face friction in enacting donation behaviour.

Relevant claims:

- `IB-000001`
- `IB-000002`
- `DK-000001`
- `EC-000001`

Boundary implication: If behaviour is automatic, habitual, or institutionally mandated, brand-enabled enactment may be less explanatory.

## 3. Trust-sensitive giving contexts

BEBA is likely stronger where trust in the organization or sector is salient.

Relevant claims:

- `TR-000002`
- `PB-000004`
- `NB-000001`

Boundary implication: If trust is already guaranteed by external institutions, brand trust may have reduced marginal explanatory value.

## 4. Mission-evaluative contexts

Nonprofit brand equity is bounded by mission-related evaluation.

Relevant claim:

- `NB-000003`

Boundary implication: Commercial brand equity dimensions cannot be transferred directly without adaptation.

## 5. Ecosystem embeddedness

Giving behaviour is shaped by micro-, meso-, and macro-level influences.

Relevant claim:

- `GE-000001`

Boundary implication: BEBA should not model donor behaviour as purely individual cognition detached from social, organizational, and market conditions.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed material has major implications for model competition.

## 1. BEBA vs. simple brand equity model

A simple model would propose:

```text
brand equity → donation intention/behaviour
```

The reviewed claims suggest this is insufficient. BEBA should instead model multiple mechanisms:

```text
brand equity → trust
brand equity → attitude
brand equity → subjective norm
brand equity → perceived behavioural control
brand equity → salience
brand equity → enactment support
brand equity → donation behaviour
```

## 2. BEBA vs. TPB-only model

TPB explains intention formation through attitude, subjective norm, and perceived behavioural control.

Relevant claim:

- `TO-000001`

BEBA can incorporate TPB but should not collapse into TPB. BEBA’s distinctive contribution is explaining how nonprofit brand equity populates or modifies TPB determinants under uncertainty.

## 3. BEBA vs. IBM-only model

IBM explains intention-to-behaviour conversion through skills, salience, environmental constraints, and enactment factors.

Relevant claims:

- `IB-000002`
- `DK-000001`
- `BS-000001`
- `EC-000001`

BEBA can use IBM to strengthen its enactment architecture. The key theoretical distinction is that BEBA explains how brand equity can function as infrastructure for those enactment conditions.

## 4. BEBA vs. donor-centric psychological models

Earlier giving models focus primarily on donor-centric psychological processes.

Relevant claim:

- `DD-000001`

BEBA extends these by integrating organizational brand-building and ecosystem-level conditions.

## 5. BEBA vs. ecosystem models

Giving ecosystem frameworks account for multi-level influences.

Relevant claim:

- `GE-000001`

BEBA should not compete with ecosystem models as a full replacement. Instead, BEBA can specify the brand-enabled behavioural pathway within the larger giving ecosystem.

## 6. Linear vs. recursive models

The adapted nonprofit brand value chain implies a linear structure:

```text
brand-building → mindsets → behaviour
```

But `RC-000001` suggests recursive reinforcement:

```text
behaviour → market performance → social proof → brand perception
```

BEBA should preserve the linear value-chain logic as one candidate model but allow recursive causality as a competing or extended model.

# Recommendation

## Integrate as candidate-level theory support

This claim set should be integrated into BEBA as candidate-level support for the central theoretical architecture, especially the idea of nonprofit brand equity as behavioural infrastructure under uncertainty.

## Do not yet canonicalize all claims

Several theoretically important claims are marked low confidence or are based on adapted/integrative reasoning rather than direct empirical evidence. These should remain candidate claims until verified against specific literature or empirical outputs.

## Prioritize three theory integration outputs

Recommended next outputs:

1. **Concept candidate file:** `Uncertainty Reduction`
2. **Concept candidate file:** `Behavioural Infrastructure`
3. **Mechanism candidate file:** `Brand-Enabled Enactment`

## Update model competition framework

The reviewed claims justify explicit comparison among:

```text
TPB-only model
IBM-only model
simple brand equity model
BEBA intention-formation model
BEBA enactment model
recursive BEBA model
ecosystem-embedded BEBA model
```

## Preserve the central BEBA formulation

The claim set supports the current research question:

> How do nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty?

The most defensible theoretical answer from this material is:

> Nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective by reducing uncertainty, structuring donor evaluations, increasing trust and salience, supporting intention formation, and providing behavioural infrastructure that helps convert intentions into donation behaviour.
