# Input Reviewed

Candidate claim set from `REVIEW-000037` concerning donor orientation, donor satisfaction, donor-nonprofit identification, donor identity salience, donor loyalty, actual donation behaviour, donation context, behavioural constraints, and donor demographics.

The claim set appears to be based primarily on Boenigk and Helmig’s nonprofit donor model across two nonprofit contexts: a money donation context and a blood donation context. The confidence level is consistently marked as `medium`, and several claims are context-specific.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set supports a differentiated identity-and-experience architecture for BEBA.

The central implication is that nonprofit brand-enabled behaviour should not be modeled as a single linear pathway from brand attitude or satisfaction to loyalty and behaviour. Instead, donor loyalty appears to emerge from multiple parallel and partly sequential mechanisms:

1. **Organisational conduct pathway**  
   Donor orientation → satisfaction / identification

2. **Experiential pathway**  
   Satisfaction → loyalty  
   Satisfaction → donor-nonprofit identification

3. **Organisational identity pathway**  
   Donor-nonprofit identification → loyalty  
   Donor-nonprofit identification → donor identity salience

4. **Role identity pathway**  
   Donor identity salience → loyalty

5. **Behavioural realisation pathway**  
   Loyalty → actual donation behaviour, moderated or constrained by contextual, regulatory, physical, demographic, and situational conditions

This strengthens BEBA’s distinction between **behavioural dispositions** and **realised behaviour**. It also supports the view that brand-enabled behaviour is not fully explained by brand equity or loyalty alone.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Identity salience should not automatically be modeled as a moderator

Challenged by:

- `DI-000007`

The reviewed claim states that donor identity salience did not significantly moderate the donor-nonprofit identification → donor loyalty relationship in the reviewed model. BEBA should therefore avoid assuming that identity salience primarily amplifies identification effects.

A better candidate specification is:

```text
Donor-nonprofit identification → donor identity salience → loyalty
```

or:

```text
Donor-nonprofit identification → loyalty
Donor identity salience → loyalty
```

rather than:

```text
Donor-nonprofit identification × donor identity salience → loyalty
```

## 2. Loyalty should not be treated as equivalent to actual behaviour

Challenged by:

- `DL-000002`
- `AD-000001`
- `BC-000019`

If the structural model explains only 4–5% of variance in actual donation behaviour, then loyalty is not sufficient as a final behavioural endpoint. BEBA should treat loyalty as a proximal disposition, not as realised behaviour.

## 3. Brand equity mechanisms may not dominate all nonprofit contexts

Challenged or qualified by:

- `DC-000005`
- `DC-000006`
- `BC-000019`

The relative importance of satisfaction, identification, and salience varies by donation context. BEBA should therefore not assume a universal hierarchy among satisfaction-based, identity-based, and brand-based mechanisms.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Donation type

The reviewed claims suggest different mechanism weights across donation contexts:

- Money donation context: identification-based constructs appear stronger than satisfaction.
- Blood donation context: satisfaction appears stronger than identification-based constructs.

This implies that BEBA should distinguish donation contexts by behavioural structure, not only by nonprofit subsector.

Possible contextual dimensions:

```text
money donation vs. bodily/material donation
low-frequency vs. high-frequency donation
service-heavy vs. communication-heavy donor relationship
high-involvement vs. low-involvement donation
regulated vs. discretionary behaviour
physically constrained vs. unconstrained behaviour
```

## 2. Behavioural constraint

Blood donation frequency can be physically or regulatorily constrained. This creates a boundary condition for the loyalty → actual behaviour relationship.

Relevant implication:

```text
Even highly loyal donors may not be able to increase actual behaviour when the behaviour has biological, regulatory, logistical, or temporal ceilings.
```

## 3. Demographics

Older donors and higher-income donors may show stronger satisfaction or identification effects in some contexts. This suggests that individual characteristics may condition the behavioural effectiveness of identity and satisfaction mechanisms.

## 4. Behavioural outcome type

The model performs weakly for actual donation behaviour. This implies that mechanisms explaining loyalty may not explain realised donation volume, frequency, or timing.

BEBA should distinguish at least:

```text
attitude
satisfaction
identification
identity salience
loyalty
intention
actual behaviour
behavioural intensity
behavioural frequency
behavioural persistence
```

# Model Competition Implications

## 1. Satisfaction model remains viable but insufficient

The claim set supports satisfaction as a direct driver of loyalty and as a possible antecedent of identification. However, satisfaction alone does not appear sufficient to explain donor loyalty or actual behaviour.

Competing model:

```text
Donor orientation → satisfaction → loyalty → behaviour
```

BEBA assessment:

- Supported as partial pathway.
- Insufficient as complete explanation.

## 2. Identification model is strongly supported but context-dependent

Donor-nonprofit identification directly influences loyalty and appears to influence donor identity salience.

Competing model:

```text
Donor orientation / satisfaction → donor-nonprofit identification → loyalty
```

BEBA assessment:

- Supported.
- Stronger in some donation contexts than others.
- Should be distinguished from donor role salience.

## 3. Role identity model is supported as direct pathway, not as moderation pathway

The reviewed material challenges a pure moderation model of donor identity salience.

Less supported model:

```text
Donor identity salience moderates identification → loyalty
```

More supported model:

```text
Donor identity salience → loyalty
```

or:

```text
Donor-nonprofit identification → donor identity salience → loyalty
```

BEBA assessment:

- Role identity salience should be modeled as a direct or mediated mechanism unless further evidence supports moderation.

## 4. Loyalty-to-behaviour model is weak

The reviewed material suggests that loyalty has limited explanatory power for actual donation behaviour under certain conditions.

Competing model:

```text
Satisfaction / identification / salience → loyalty → actual behaviour
```

BEBA assessment:

- Plausible for proximal disposition.
- Weak for realised behaviour.
- Requires additional inputs such as income, fundraising intensity, physical ability, opportunity, regulatory ceilings, timing, and situational triggers.

## 5. Bypass route around brand equity

Yes, this claim set introduces possible bypass routes around brand equity.

Potential bypass routes:

```text
Donor orientation → satisfaction → loyalty
```

```text
Donor orientation → satisfaction → actual behaviour
```

```text
Regulatory / physical constraint → actual behaviour
```

```text
Income / fundraising intensity / situational opportunity → actual donation behaviour
```

These routes suggest that behaviour can be shaped by organisational conduct, satisfaction, resource availability, and constraints without necessarily passing through brand equity as a central construct.

For BEBA, this is important: brand equity should not be assumed to mediate every route to behaviour.

# Ontology Candidate Implications

## New or refined concept candidates

### 1. Donor-Nonprofit Identification

**Object type:** Construct / Concept  
**Definition candidate:** Psychological sense of connection or self-definitional overlap with a specific nonprofit organisation.  
**Ontology implication:** Should be distinct from donor identity salience.

### 2. Donor Identity Salience

**Object type:** Construct / Concept  
**Definition candidate:** The accessibility or importance of the donor role within the individual’s self-concept.  
**Ontology implication:** Should be modeled separately from organisation-specific identification.

### 3. Donor Orientation

**Object type:** Organisational construct  
**Definition candidate:** Organisational conduct oriented toward understanding and serving donor needs, expectations, or relational concerns.  
**Ontology implication:** Candidate upstream organisational mechanism.

### 4. Behavioural Constraint

**Object type:** Boundary condition / Contextual constraint  
**Definition candidate:** Non-brand limitation that restricts the translation of behavioural disposition into realised behaviour.  
**Ontology implication:** Should be included in the BEBA behavioural realisation architecture.

### 5. Behavioural Realisation Gap

**Object type:** Candidate concept / mechanism  
**Definition candidate:** The gap between proximal behavioural disposition and realised behaviour caused by constraints, opportunities, resources, timing, regulation, or situational factors.  
**Ontology implication:** High-value candidate for BEBA because it directly addresses how brands become behaviourally effective under uncertainty.

### 6. Donation Context

**Object type:** Context / Boundary condition  
**Definition candidate:** The behavioural and institutional setting in which donation behaviour occurs, including donation type, involvement level, service intensity, regulation, and physical constraints.  
**Ontology implication:** Should condition mechanism strength.

### 7. Actual Donation Behaviour

**Object type:** Behavioural outcome  
**Definition candidate:** Observed or reported donation action, distinguishable from loyalty, intention, satisfaction, and identity.  
**Ontology implication:** Should not be collapsed into donor loyalty.

# Recommendation

## Theory recommendation

Integrate this claim set as evidence for a **multi-route donor behaviour architecture** within BEBA.

The most important theoretical refinement is the distinction between:

```text
psychological enablement
```

and

```text
behavioural realisation
```

The reviewed claims indicate that satisfaction, donor-nonprofit identification, and donor identity salience can enable loyalty, but loyalty only weakly explains actual donation behaviour in some contexts. BEBA should therefore avoid treating loyalty as the final behavioural outcome.

## Suggested BEBA mechanism structure

```text
Organisational donor orientation
→ donor satisfaction
→ donor-nonprofit identification
→ donor identity salience
→ donor loyalty
→ actual donation behaviour
```

But this should not be treated as a single mandatory chain. A better BEBA representation would allow parallel and competing pathways:

```text
Donor orientation → satisfaction → loyalty
Donor orientation → donor-nonprofit identification → loyalty
Satisfaction → donor-nonprofit identification → loyalty
Donor-nonprofit identification → donor identity salience → loyalty
Donor identity salience → loyalty
Loyalty → actual behaviour, constrained by context
```

## Required caution

The claim set has `medium` confidence and appears to rely heavily on one empirical study across two nonprofit contexts. Therefore, these implications should remain candidate-level until supported by additional reviewed literature.
