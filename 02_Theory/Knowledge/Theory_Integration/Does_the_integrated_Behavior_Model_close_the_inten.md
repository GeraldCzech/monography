# Input Reviewed

- **IB-000028**: Parsimony of reasoned-action models vs. integrated models.
- **IB-000029**: Value of integrated models is in identifying post-intentional moderators.
- **AC-000001**: 'Actual control' as a critical moderator of the intention-behavior (I-B) link.
- **AC-000002**: Methodological failure to measure 'actual control' in the literature.
- **CP-000001**: 'Coping planning' as a key mediator for persistent behaviors.
- **IB-000030**: The I-B gap as a dynamic, within-person process.
- **IB-000031**: The existence of the I-B gap as a foundational problem.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claims collectively argue that resolving the intention-behavior gap requires a decisive shift away from static, predictive models focused on intention formation. Instead, the focus must be on dynamic, post-intentional mechanisms that explain how intentions are translated into action under real-world conditions. The core value of a framework like BEBA is not to add more variables to predict intention, but to model the specific, context-dependent moderators and mediators (like 'actual control' and 'coping planning') that govern behavior enactment. The nonprofit brand's role is therefore reconceptualized: it is less a builder of intention and more an architect of the post-intentional space, providing the structure and resources for self-regulation and overcoming constraints.

# BEBA Layer Affected

- **Individual Layer**: This is the primary layer affected. Concepts such as `Intention`, `Coping Planning`, and `Self-Regulation` are individual-level cognitive and behavioral processes. The core I-B gap dynamic plays out at this level.
- **Environmental Layer**: This layer is significantly impacted through the concept of `Actual Control`, which explicitly includes environmental constraints and opportunities as a moderator of individual action.
- **Architectural/Metatheoretical Layer**: The claims reinforce BEBA's core principles, such as parsimony, focusing on explanation over prediction, and adopting a dynamic, process-based view.

# Existing Claims Supported

- **BEBA's raison d'être**: The foundational claim that a significant intention-behavior gap exists (IB-000031) validates the entire BEBA project, which is designed to address this specific problem.
- **Principle of Parsimony**: The finding that complex integrated models offer little predictive gain over simpler ones (IB-000028) strongly supports BEBA's principle of avoiding needless complexity and focusing on explanatory mechanisms.
- **Focus on Behavior Enactment**: The claims collectively shift the theoretical center of gravity from intention formation to the post-intentional phase, supporting BEBA's focus on "Brand-Enabled *Behaviour* Architecture".
- **Architectural Components**: The importance of 'Actual Control' (AC-000001), comprising skills and environmental factors, directly validates BEBA's inclusion of user capabilities and environmental affordances as first-class components of the architecture.
- **Dynamic Process View**: The characterization of the I-B gap as a dynamic, within-person process (IB-000030) supports BEBA's theoretical commitment to a process model over a static, cross-sectional one.

# Existing Claims Challenged

The reviewed claims do not directly challenge existing BEBA claims. Instead, they challenge the theoretical positions BEBA is defined *against*:
- They challenge any theory that posits a simple, unmoderated path from brand attitudes to intention to behavior.
- They challenge the research strategy of simply adding more cognitive antecedents to models like the Theory of Planned Behavior in a quest for higher R-squared values for *intention*.

# New Candidate Claims

1.  **Claim**: The primary function of a BEBA is to structure the post-intentional phase by enhancing 'actual control' and facilitating 'coping planning'.
2.  **Claim**: The effectiveness of a nonprofit brand in promoting persistent behaviors (e.g., long-term volunteering) is more dependent on its ability to support coping planning than on its ability to support action planning.
3.  **Claim**: The explanatory advantage of BEBA over traditional brand equity models lies in its ability to model the moderation of the intention-behavior relationship, not in its ability to better predict intention itself.

# Boundary Conditions

- The relative importance of **coping planning** is higher for behaviors that require **persistence against obstacles** (CP-000001). This suggests that the self-regulatory components of a BEBA are most critical for complex, long-term, or friction-filled behaviors (e.g., sustained donation, regular advocacy, lifestyle change) and less critical for simple, one-off behaviors (e.g., a single click-to-sign petition).

# Model Competition Implications

- This analysis provides a clear strategy for demonstrating BEBA's value. BEBA should not compete with models like TPB on who can better predict *intention*.
- The competition should be framed as follows:
    1.  Establish a baseline model (e.g., TPB) predicting behavior from intention.
    2.  Introduce BEBA's core components (e.g., measures of brand-provided 'actual control', facilitation of 'coping planning') as moderators of the intention -> behavior link.
    3.  BEBA demonstrates its explanatory power if these components show a significant interaction effect, explaining variance in *who acts on their intentions* and *under what conditions*.
- This also highlights a key methodological imperative for BEBA's empirical work: it *must* include robust measures of 'actual control' to address the gap identified in AC-000002.

# Ontology Candidate Implications

1.  **Actual Control**: This must be a core concept in the BEBA ontology. It should be defined as a super-concept with sub-concepts for `Individual Skills/Capabilities` and `Environmental Constraints/Affordances`.
2.  **Planning (Self-Regulation)**: The ontology must distinguish between `Action Planning` ("when, where, and how to act") and `Coping Planning` ("how to deal with anticipated barriers"). The relationship should be modeled such that Coping Planning is a key mediator for specific types of behavior.
3.  **Behavioral Phases**: The ontology should formally distinguish between a `Pre-Intentional Phase` (deliberative, intention formation) and a `Post-Intentional Phase` (implemental, intention enactment). This allows for mapping how brand functions differ across phases.

# Recommendation

1.  **Formalize Core Concepts**: Integrate `Actual Control` and `Coping Planning` as first-class concepts into the BEBA ontology and glossary. Create candidate files for them immediately.
2.  **Refine Theoretical Framing**: Explicitly position BEBA in `00_MASTER_OUTLINE.md` and related theory chapters as a framework for the **post-intentional phase**, designed to explain the moderation and mediation of the intention-behavior link.
3.  **Update Empirical Strategy**: Create a DecisionLog entry or ADR codifying the model competition strategy outlined above. The empirical pipeline must prioritize developing and validating measures for brand-related 'actual control'.
4.  **Leverage for Argumentation**: Use these claims to strengthen the monograph's argument for why a new model (BEBA) is necessary, grounding it in the well-documented failures of existing approaches to solve the intention-behavior gap.
