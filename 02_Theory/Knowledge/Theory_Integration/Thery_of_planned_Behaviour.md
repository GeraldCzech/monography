# Core Theoretical Implication

The TPB claim set establishes a strong **individual-level behavioural baseline** for BEBA.

The central implication is that BEBA should not theorise nonprofit brand equity as a direct, isolated predictor of donation behavior. Instead, nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective when they intervene in, amplify, stabilise, or bypass established behavioural mechanisms such as:

- attitude formation,
- perceived behavioral control,
- moral obligation,
- donor self-identity,
- trust,
- habit or path dependence,
- and intention-to-action translation.

This supports the BEBA framing of nonprofit brand equity as a **brand-enabled behaviour architecture**, not merely as a set of favourable brand associations.

The claims also reinforce the current BEBA research question:

> How do nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty?

The TPB integration suggests that under uncertainty, brands may become behaviourally effective by reducing perceived risk, increasing perceived control, activating moral identity, simplifying action pathways, and stabilising trust.

# Existing Claims Supported

The claim set supports several core BEBA assumptions.

## 1. Behaviour before brands

Supported by:

- `TO-000003`
- `DI-000003`
- `IB-000006`
- `IB-000007`

TPB demonstrates that charitable giving is structured by behavioural predictors before brand effects are introduced. BEBA should therefore explain how brands enter behavioural systems rather than treating brand equity as automatically behaviour-producing.

## 2. Brand equity needs behavioural translation mechanisms

Supported by:

- `IB-000006`
- `IB-000007`

The intention-behavior gap supports BEBA’s need to explain how favourable psychological states become enacted donation behavior.

This is important because brand attitudes or trust may raise intention without necessarily producing actual giving.

## 3. Trust is a central bridge between nonprofit brand equity and behavior

Supported by:

- `TR-000004`
- `TR-000005`
- `DT-000001`

Trust appears as a key mechanism through which nonprofit brands may reduce uncertainty and enable action, especially in online donation or platform-mediated environments.

## 4. Perceived behavioral control is a major action-enabling mechanism

Supported by:

- `PB-000009`

This supports the idea that nonprofit brands may become behaviourally effective not only by being liked or admired, but by making giving feel easier, more efficacious, more legitimate, and more controllable.

## 5. Moral obligation is distinct from social pressure

Supported by:

- `MN-000002`
- `MN-000003`

This supports BEBA’s need to distinguish internalised moral motivation from external social normative pressure.

## 6. Donor identity and repeated giving matter

Supported by:

- `SI-000004`
- `PB-000010`
- `LD-000002`

BEBA should not treat donation decisions as isolated events. Nonprofit brands may shape durable behavioural patterns by connecting to donor identity and past giving routines.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Any simple brand-equity-to-behavior pathway is challenged

The TPB evidence challenges any model in which nonprofit brand equity is expected to directly produce donation behavior without passing through behavioural mechanisms.

The following claims are especially relevant:

- `TO-000003`
- `DI-000003`
- `IB-000006`
- `IB-000007`

BEBA should therefore avoid a simple sequence such as:

```text
Brand equity -> donation behavior
```

A more defensible structure is:

```text
Brand-enabled mechanism -> intention/action architecture -> donation behavior
```

## 2. Intention is not sufficient as the primary outcome

Supported by:

- `IB-000006`
- `IB-000007`
- `LD-000002`

The evidence challenges models that treat donation intention as a sufficient proxy for donation behavior.

BEBA should preserve a distinction between:

- intention formation,
- intention stabilisation,
- action execution,
- repeated behavior,
- sustained giving.

## 3. Subjective norm may be less central than moral norm

Supported by:

- `MN-000002`
- `MN-000003`

This challenges overly social-pressure-based accounts of charitable giving.

BEBA should avoid collapsing moral obligation, social pressure, identity, and perceived appropriateness into one general “normative influence” construct.

## 4. Brand equity may not always be the dominant explanatory route

Supported by:

- `CC-000004`
- `PB-000010`
- `SI-000004`

Income, religiosity, past behavior, habit, and cultural context may explain donation behavior independently of nonprofit brand equity.

This creates important model competition for BEBA.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Digital donation context

Relevant claims:

- `TR-000005`
- `DT-000001`

In online donation and crowdfunding settings, trust in the organization may not be enough. Platform credibility, payment security, system reliability, and digital legitimacy may condition whether brand-enabled trust can translate into behavior.

## 2. Familiar vs. unfamiliar organizations

Relevant claim:

- `TR-000004`

Trust appears especially important when the donor has limited familiarity with the organization or donation platform. BEBA should treat uncertainty as higher under low familiarity.

## 3. Cultural context

Relevant claims:

- `CC-000003`
- `CC-000004`

TPB predictors may vary in salience across cultural contexts. BEBA should avoid assuming that brand-enabled attitude, control, or normative mechanisms operate identically across societies.

## 4. Religious and institutional giving contexts

Relevant claim:

- `CC-000004`

In some contexts, religiosity or income may be more salient than classic TPB constructs. This suggests a boundary condition where institutionalised giving norms may reduce the relative explanatory role of nonprofit brand equity.

## 5. Temporal horizon

Relevant claim:

- `LD-000002`

TPB is stronger for intention than for long-term sustained giving. BEBA should distinguish between one-time donation, repeated donation, and durable donor relationship behavior.

## 6. Existing behavioral history

Relevant claim:

- `PB-000010`

Past giving may condition future giving. In repeated donation settings, habit and prior commitment may reduce the need for active brand evaluation.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed claim set creates several important competing models for BEBA.

## Model 1: Core TPB Baseline

```text
Attitude
+ Subjective Norm
+ Perceived Behavioral Control
-> Donation Intention
-> Donation Behavior
```

Benchmark from reviewed synthesis:

- approximately 44% explained variance in charitable donation intention;
- substantially lower explained variance for actual behavior, with approximately 19% reported for behavior.

Implication:

BEBA must demonstrate explanatory value beyond this baseline.

## Model 2: Extended TPB with Moral Norms

```text
Attitude
+ Subjective Norm
+ Perceived Behavioral Control
+ Moral Norm
-> Donation Intention
```

Benchmark from reviewed synthesis:

- explained variance increases from approximately 44% to approximately 52%.

Implication:

Moral norm is a serious competitor to brand equity unless BEBA can show that brands activate, stabilise, or channel moral obligation.

## Model 3: Trust-Augmented TPB

```text
Trust
+ TPB Constructs
-> Donation Intention / Donation Behavior
```

Implication:

Trust may be both a BEBA mechanism and an alternative explanation. BEBA should clarify whether trust is:

1. a dimension of nonprofit brand equity,
2. an outcome of nonprofit brand equity,
3. a mediator between brand equity and behavior,
4. or a separate behavioural condition.

## Model 4: Identity-Based Giving Model

```text
Donor Self-Identity
-> Donation Intention
-> Repeated Donation Behavior
```

Implication:

Identity may explain durable giving independently of brand evaluations. BEBA should examine whether nonprofit brands support donor identity or merely benefit from pre-existing identity.

## Model 5: Habit / Past Behavior Model

```text
Past Donation Behavior
-> Future Donation Behavior
```

Implication:

Repeated giving may become routinised. This creates a bypass around active brand evaluation and even around explicit intention.

## Model 6: Cultural-Institutional Model

```text
Income
+ Religiosity
+ Cultural Norms
+ Institutional Context
-> Donation Behavior
```

Implication:

In some contexts, cultural and institutional predictors may override brand-enabled psychological mechanisms.

## Model 7: Digital Trust Infrastructure Model

```text
Platform Credibility
+ System Trust
+ Organization Trust
-> Online Donation Behavior
```

Implication:

In digital donation environments, BEBA must account for infrastructure-level credibility, not only organization-level brand equity.

# Recommendation

## 1. Integrate TPB as a foundational behavioural baseline for BEBA

TPB should be treated as a foundational individual-level theory against which BEBA must position itself.

Recommended status:

```text
Theory of Planned Behavior = foundational competing / complementary behavioural architecture
```

## 2. Do not treat brand equity as a direct behavioral cause

BEBA should model nonprofit brand equity as operating through mechanisms such as:

- trust,
- perceived behavioral control,
- moral norm activation,
- donor identity stabilisation,
- action simplification,
- uncertainty reduction.

## 3. Treat the intention-behavior gap as central

The TPB evidence strongly supports making the intention-behavior gap a core BEBA problem.

BEBA should explicitly distinguish:

```text
brand perception -> intention
brand perception -> behavior
brand perception -> repeated behavior
```

## 4. Use TPB benchmarks in model competition

The reviewed synthesis provides useful comparative benchmarks:

- core TPB: approximately 44% variance in intention;
- extended TPB with moral norms: approximately 52%;
- actual behavior: substantially lower, approximately 19% reported.

These should be used cautiously and only with the reviewed source trail preserved.

## 5. Create ontology candidates before canonical changes

Recommended candidate ontology entries:

- `Perceived Behavioral Control`
- `Moral Norm`
- `Donor Self-Identity`
- `Intention-Behavior Gap`
- `Organizational Trust`
- `Platform Trust`
- `Digital Trust`
- `Past Donation Behavior`
- `Sustained Giving`
- `Cultural Context`

## 6. Flag possible ADR need

If BEBA’s core model architecture is revised to include TPB as a formal baseline or layer within the theory architecture, this should likely require an ADR because it affects theoretical architecture.

## 7. Keep low-confidence claims non-canonical

The following should not be promoted to canonical theory without direct source verification:

- digital trust as a necessary precondition;
- income and religiosity overriding TPB in non-Western contexts;
- long-term TPB limitations beyond the stated research gap.

## Bottom Line

The TPB claim set strengthens BEBA by giving it a clear behavioural baseline and a precise theoretical problem:

> Nonprofit brand equity becomes behaviourally effective when it modifies, stabilises, or enables the psychological and contextual mechanisms that translate charitable motivation into actual giving under uncertainty.

At the same time, the claim set challenges BEBA to compete against non-brand explanations, especially moral norms, perceived behavioral control, trust, past behavior, religiosity, income, and cultural context.
