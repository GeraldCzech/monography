# Core Theoretical Implication

The claim set strengthens BEBA by specifying **which perceptual components of nonprofit brand image may enable supportive behaviour under uncertainty**.

The most important theoretical contribution is that nonprofit brand image should not be treated as a generic positive evaluation. Instead, it may function as a **multidimensional behavioural architecture** composed of perceptions that perform different enabling functions.

In particular:

- **Reliability** appears to function as an uncertainty-reducing perception.
- **Ethicality** appears to function as a trust-relevant and legitimacy-relevant perception.
- **Usefulness, Efficiency, Affect, Dynamism, Reliability, and Ethicality** together provide a candidate perceptual structure through which nonprofit brands become behaviourally consequential.

This supports BEBA’s general argument that nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective not merely by being liked, known, or salient, but by enabling action under uncertainty.

However, the evidence remains measurement-focused and intention-based. It does not yet establish that these image dimensions causally reduce uncertainty or generate observed supportive behaviour.

Relevant measurement implications:

- Nonprofit brand image should be treated as multidimensional.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Generic nonprofit brand image models are insufficient

The claim set challenges any BEBA formulation that treats nonprofit brand image as a single undifferentiated perception.

If accepted, BEBA should avoid representing nonprofit brand image only as:

- general favourability,
- attractiveness,
- reputation,
- affective attachment,
- symbolic association,
- or overall attitude.

Instead, nonprofit brand image should be treated as a structured set of behaviourally relevant perceptions.

## 2. Earlier four-dimensional nonprofit brand image models may not be portable

`NB-000058` challenges the assumption that the Michel and Rieunier four-factor model can function as a general nonprofit brand image measurement standard.

This does not invalidate the earlier model universally, but it does suggest:

- cross-context instability,
- possible omitted dimensions,
- and the need for context-sensitive validation.

## 3. Affect-centred explanations may be incomplete

Because reliability and ethicality improve explanatory power for donation intentions, explanations that foreground emotional liking alone appear insufficient.

This matters for BEBA because nonprofit support may involve morally loaded, trust-sensitive, and risk-sensitive decisions rather than purely affective brand preference.

## 4. Intention-based evidence should not be overextended to behaviour

The claim set supports intention-level behavioural relevance, but it challenges any stronger BEBA claim that nonprofit brand image has been shown here to produce actual donation or volunteering behaviour.

The current evidence does not support claims about:

- observed donations,
- repeated giving,
- actual volunteering,
- retention,
- donor lifetime value,
- advocacy behaviour,
- or behavioural conversion.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Sector Boundary

The evidence is based on the **UK children’s charity sector**.

Therefore, transferability to other nonprofit subsectors is not established, including:

- international development organizations,
- religious charities,
- environmental nonprofits,
- health nonprofits,
- cultural organizations,
- advocacy organizations,
- foundations,
- membership associations,
- humanitarian emergency organizations.

## 2. National Context Boundary

The evidence is UK-based. Cross-national validity is not sufficiently covered.

The six-factor structure should not yet be assumed to hold across:

- German-speaking nonprofit contexts,
- continental European welfare systems,
- US philanthropy,
- Global South nonprofit contexts,
- high-trust versus low-trust institutional environments.

## 3. Behavioural Outcome Boundary

The evidence concerns **self-reported intentions**, not observed behaviour.

Supported outcomes:

- donation intention,
- volunteering intention.

Not yet supported by this evidence:

- actual donation,
- donation amount,
- recurring donation,
- donor retention,
- volunteer registration,
- volunteer hours,
- advocacy,
- bequest intention,
- membership renewal.

## 4. Mechanism Boundary

Uncertainty reduction is theoretically plausible but not directly established.

The reviewed claim set does not yet demonstrate that:

```text
Reliability / Ethicality
→ Reduced Uncertainty
→ Donation or Volunteering Behaviour
```

Instead, the evidence appears closer to:

```text
Brand Image Dimensions
→ Donation / Volunteering Intention
```

with uncertainty reduction inferred rather than directly tested.

## 5. Measurement Boundary

The 18-item six-factor scale is a **candidate instrument**, not yet canonical.

Before canonical adoption, BEBA would require evidence on:

- replication across nonprofit sectors,
- replication across countries,
- measurement invariance,
- predictive validity for actual behaviour,
- discriminant validity against trust, reputation, attitude, authenticity, legitimacy, and credibility,
- temporal stability,
- performance in BEBA’s own empirical context.

# Model Competition Implications

The claim set has important implications for competing models within BEBA.

## 1. Six-Factor Image Model vs Four-Factor Image Model

The evidence favours the six-factor model over the earlier four-factor model in the UK children’s charity context.

Competing models:

```text
Four-factor nonprofit brand image model
vs.
Six-factor nonprofit brand image model
```

Current implication:

- The six-factor model appears more suitable as a candidate measurement model.
- The four-factor model should not be treated as canonical without contextual replication.

## 2. Global Image Model vs Multidimensional Image Model

The claim set supports a multidimensional model over a global single-factor model.

Competing models:

```text
Global nonprofit brand image
vs.
Multidimensional nonprofit brand image
```

BEBA implication:

- A global score may obscure the distinct behavioural roles of reliability, ethicality, efficiency, affect, usefulness, and dynamism.
- BEBA should preserve the possibility that different image dimensions enable different behaviours.

## 3. Affect-Based Model vs Trust-Relevant Model

The evidence challenges affect-only explanations.

Competing models:

```text
Affect / liking → intention
vs.
Reliability + ethicality + other image dimensions → intention
```

BEBA implication:

- Trust-relevant dimensions may be especially important when stakeholders cannot observe nonprofit quality directly.
- Affective brand preference may be insufficient under uncertainty.

## 4. Direct Image Effect Model vs Mediated Uncertainty-Reduction Model

The evidence currently supports a direct association between image and intention, but BEBA’s theory suggests a possible mediated model.

Competing models:

```text
Brand Image → Donation / Volunteering Intention
```

versus

```text
Reliability / Ethicality → Reduced Uncertainty → Donation / Volunteering Intention
```

The second model is more theoretically aligned with BEBA but requires additional empirical testing.

## 5. Donation-Specific vs Multi-Behaviour Model

The inclusion of both donation intention and volunteering intention supports BEBA’s multi-behaviour orientation.

Competing models:

```text
Brand image as donation-enabling only
vs.
Brand image as enabling multiple supportive behaviours
```

BEBA implication:

- Nonprofit brand image may enable both monetary and non-monetary support.
- Future models should test whether the same dimensions predict donation and volunteering equally, or whether different behaviours depend on different image dimensions.

## Recommended BEBA Interpretation

The reviewed material should be used to support the following BEBA-compatible proposition:

> Nonprofit brand image may enable supportive behaviour by organizing stakeholder perceptions into behaviourally relevant dimensions, especially reliability and ethicality, which are likely to matter under uncertainty.

However, BEBA should avoid stronger claims such as:

> Reliability and ethicality have been proven to reduce uncertainty and cause donation or volunteering behaviour.

That stronger claim is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

## Recommended Next Steps

1. **Create or update a candidate concept file** for `Nonprofit Brand Image` if not already present.
2. **Add Reliability and Ethicality as candidate dimensions** of nonprofit brand image.
3. **Flag the 18-item six-factor scale as a candidate measurement object**, not canonical.
4. **Record a measurement caution** in the ConstructLedger if the scale is considered for empirical use.
5. **Do not update canonical ontology yet** without further validation.
6. **Model competition should preserve at least three alternatives**:
   - global nonprofit brand image model,
   - four-factor nonprofit brand image model,
   - six-factor nonprofit brand image model.
7. **Future empirical testing should examine whether uncertainty reduction mediates** the relationship between reliability/ethicality perceptions and supportive behaviour.
8. **Observed behaviour should be distinguished from behavioural intention** in all downstream writing.

## Concise Theory Integration Verdict

This claim set strengthens BEBA by identifying **Reliability** and **Ethicality** as potentially central nonprofit brand image dimensions for explaining behavioural effectiveness under uncertainty. It supports a multidimensional and context-sensitive view of nonprofit brand image, but its evidence remains limited to intention outcomes and a specific UK children’s charity context. Therefore, the material should enter BEBA as a **candidate construct and measurement integration**, with uncertainty reduction treated as a plausible but not yet empirically confirmed mechanism.
