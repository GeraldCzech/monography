# Core Theoretical Implication

This claim set strengthens the BEBA proposition that nonprofit brand equity can operate as an **internal behavioural architecture**, not only as an external perception asset.

The central implication is that nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective for volunteers through at least two linked but distinguishable pathways:

1. **Cognitive-structural pathway**  
   Internal brand management → role clarity → brand-consistent behaviour, satisfaction, and retention.

2. **Affective-relational pathway**  
   Internal brand management → volunteer brand commitment → brand citizenship behaviour, satisfaction, retention, and positive word-of-mouth.

The evidence suggests that, in volunteer contexts, brand-enabled behaviour is not only a matter of brand knowledge or communication. It is also shaped by relational organisational conditions such as socialisation, management support, and the “H Factor” noted in the source claim set.

# Existing Claims Challenged

The claim set does not directly falsify a core BEBA proposition, but it qualifies several possible assumptions.

## 1. Challenge to simple transfer from corporate employee brand equity

`VB-000002` challenges any assumption that employee brand equity measures can be directly transferred to nonprofit volunteer contexts. Terms such as job training, goals, and intention-to-stay horizons require adaptation.

Implication: BEBA should treat volunteer brand equity as a context-specific construct candidate rather than as a direct reuse of employee brand equity.

## 2. Challenge to purely informational brand management models

`BC-000014` suggests that relational internal brand management dimensions are more strongly associated with volunteer brand commitment than informational or involvement-based dimensions.

Implication: brand communication alone may be insufficient. The behavioural effectiveness of nonprofit brands may depend strongly on relational and socialisation processes.

## 3. Challenge to role clarity as a universal dominant mechanism

`RC-000005` indicates that role clarity is weaker than volunteer brand commitment for positive word-of-mouth.

Implication: cognitive clarity may be especially relevant for task execution, participation, and retention, while affective commitment may be more relevant for advocacy.

## 4. Challenge to single-pathway BEBA models

The evidence suggests that role clarity and brand commitment each predict several outcomes. This challenges any model in which brand-enabled behaviour is explained only through one mechanism.

Implication: BEBA should retain a multi-pathway architecture.

# Boundary Conditions

The claim set introduces several important boundary conditions.

## 1. Stakeholder type: formal volunteers

The evidence concerns formal volunteers in nonprofit organizations. It should not automatically be generalized to:

- informal volunteers;
- episodic volunteers;
- donors;
- beneficiaries;
- paid nonprofit employees;
- board members;
- activists;
- community supporters.

## 2. Organisational context: nonprofit organizations

The findings concern nonprofit contexts. They should not be treated as direct evidence for corporate employee brand equity.

## 3. Cultural/contextual scope: Portuguese formal volunteers

The study context appears to involve Portuguese formal volunteers. Cross-cultural generalisation remains uncertain.

## 4. Evidence type: exploratory and cross-sectional

Several notes indicate cross-sectional evidence. Therefore, causal claims should be avoided unless supported by additional research.

Recommended wording:

- “is associated with”
- “predicts in cross-sectional models”
- “is consistent with the mechanism that”
- “supports the proposition that”

Avoid:

- “causes”
- “leads to”
- “produces”
- “determines”

unless confirmed by stronger empirical designs.

## 5. Measurement adaptation requirement

The evidence depends on adaptation of corporate employee brand equity measures. This creates a construct-validity boundary condition: results depend on whether the adapted volunteer measures are theoretically and empirically defensible.

# Model Competition Implications

The claim set affects BEBA model competition in several ways.

## 1. Supports a dual-mechanism model

The evidence supports a model in which nonprofit brand-enabled behaviour operates through both:

- role clarity;
- brand commitment.

This is stronger than a single-mechanism model.

## 2. Introduces competition between cognitive and affective mechanisms

Role clarity and brand commitment both predict behavioural outcomes, but their relative importance may vary.

Possible competing models:

### Model A: Cognitive Clarity Model

Internal brand management → role clarity → behavioural outcomes.

Best suited for explaining:

- role-consistent behaviour;
- satisfaction linked to reduced ambiguity;
- intention to stay through clearer expectations.

### Model B: Affective Commitment Model

Internal brand management → brand commitment → behavioural outcomes.

Best suited for explaining:

- brand citizenship behaviour;
- positive word-of-mouth;
- retention through attachment;
- satisfaction through identification or emotional connection.

### Model C: Relational Internal Brand Management Model

Relational internal brand management → brand commitment → discretionary behaviour.

This model competes with communication-heavy or information-heavy internal branding models.

### Model D: Volunteer Management / HRM Alternative

Volunteer outcomes may be explained by general volunteer management, role design, socialisation, support, or organisational climate rather than brand equity specifically.

BEBA should therefore avoid over-attributing all effects to “brand” unless the brand-specific mechanism is empirically separated from broader management practices.

## 3. Possible bypass route around Brand Equity

Yes, the claim set creates a possible bypass route.

Role clarity may influence behaviour, satisfaction, and retention without necessarily requiring brand commitment or broader brand equity. This suggests a pathway:

Internal management practices → role clarity → volunteer outcomes

This could operate as a brand-enabled route if role clarity is explicitly brand-based, but it could also be a general organisational management route. BEBA should distinguish:

- **brand-specific role clarity**: clarity about how to enact the nonprofit brand;
- **general role clarity**: clarity about tasks and responsibilities independent of brand.

This distinction is important for model competition.
