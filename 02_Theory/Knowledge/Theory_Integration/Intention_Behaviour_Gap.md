# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set strongly supports BEBA’s central premise that nonprofit brand equity cannot be treated merely as favourable attitudes, intentions, trust, satisfaction, commitment, or relational evaluations. Behavioural effectiveness depends on whether brand-enabled mechanisms help convert motivational states into actual charitable action under conditions of uncertainty, friction, limited self-regulation, cue exposure, and social norm alignment.

The strongest theoretical implication is that BEBA requires at least two behaviour pathways:

1. **Intention-driven pathway**  
   Brand-related evaluations, trust, satisfaction, commitment, and perceived legitimacy may contribute to donation intention, but intention requires additional execution support to become behaviour.

2. **Cue-driven pathway**  
   Emotional activation, cause salience, and action opportunities may trigger donation behaviour even without prior formed intention.

This means BEBA should not model nonprofit brand equity as a simple upstream antecedent of intention. Instead, brand equity should be conceptualised as a **behaviour-enabling architecture** that can operate before, during, and after intention formation.

## Secondary layer: Societal Layer

`NA-000001` affects the societal/contextual layer by identifying social norms as a moderator of intention-to-behaviour conversion.

Normative alignment appears to function as a **conversion condition**: when injunctive and descriptive norms support donation, intentions are more likely to become behaviour.

## Possible interface layer: Brand–Behaviour Interface

The claims also affect the interface between nonprofit brand equity and behaviour. They suggest that brands may become behaviourally effective not only by producing favourable evaluations, but by:

- reducing action friction,
- making causes salient,
- activating emotion,
- simplifying execution,
- legitimising behaviour,
- aligning donation with social expectations,
- supporting self-regulation.

# Existing Claims Supported

The reviewed claims support several likely BEBA assumptions:

## 1. Behaviour is the appropriate endpoint of nonprofit brand effectiveness

Supported by `IB-000004`.

A measurable gap between stated donation intention and actual donation behaviour supports BEBA’s insistence that nonprofit brand equity must ultimately be evaluated against behaviour, not only attitudinal or intentional outcomes.

## 2. Intention is insufficient as a behavioural proxy

Supported by `IB-000005`, `RB-000001`, `SR-000001`, and `PI-000001`.

If TPB models explain substantially less variance in actual giving than in intention, and if relational brand drivers have much stronger effects on intention than behaviour, then intention cannot be treated as equivalent to behavioural effectiveness.

## 3. Nonprofit brand equity requires post-intentional mechanisms

Supported by `SR-000001` and `PI-000001`.

Self-regulation failure and practical barriers weaken intention enactment. This supports the BEBA proposition that brand equity must include mechanisms that facilitate behavioural completion after intention has formed.

## 4. Brand-enabled behaviour may be cue-driven

Supported by `EA-000001` and `CS-000001`.

Emotional activation and cognitive salience can trigger donation behaviour without prior intention. This supports a BEBA-compatible cue-driven pathway in which brands can activate behaviour at moments of opportunity.

## 5. Context conditions influence behavioural conversion

Supported by `NA-000001`.

The effect of donation intention on donation behaviour is stronger when injunctive and descriptive norms align in favour of giving. This supports BEBA’s need to include boundary conditions rather than assume universal brand-to-behaviour effects.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Relational brand evaluations are sufficient indicators of behavioural effectiveness

Challenged by `RB-000001`.

If satisfaction, trust, and commitment have much stronger effects on donation intentions than on actual donations, then models relying on these variables as behavioural proxies are theoretically incomplete.

## 2. Intention-based models are adequate explanations of charitable giving

Challenged by `IB-000005`.

The reported difference in explained variance between giving intentions and actual giving suggests that intention-based theories explain motivation better than behavioural enactment.

## 3. Brand equity operates mainly through attitude and intention formation

Challenged by `EA-000001`, `CS-000001`, `SR-000001`, and `PI-000001`.

The claims suggest that nonprofit brands may also operate through salience, emotion, friction reduction, self-regulatory support, and behavioural cueing.

## 4. Donation behaviour requires prior explicit intention

Challenged by `EA-000001` and `CS-000001`.

Acute emotional activation and cause salience may trigger donation behaviour without prior donation intention. BEBA should therefore not rely exclusively on sequential cognition-based models.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Self-regulatory capacity

Donation intentions are less likely to become donations when individuals experience self-regulation failure. BEBA should treat self-regulation as a post-intentional boundary condition.

## 2. Practical friction

Limited time, effort requirements, lack of resources, and difficulty of execution reduce behavioural conversion. Brand effects may therefore depend on how easy the organisation makes the donation behaviour.

## 3. Emotional activation

Acute emotional states such as empathy or guilt may bypass prior intention and directly trigger action. This suggests that affective intensity can condition whether behaviour follows a deliberate or cue-driven route.

## 4. Cognitive salience

Donation behaviour is more likely when a cause becomes salient at a moment of action opportunity. Brand effects may therefore depend on timing, attention, and situational cueing.

## 5. Normative alignment

Intention-to-behaviour conversion is stronger when both injunctive norms and descriptive norms support donation. Norm conflict may weaken conversion even when individual intention is present.

# Model Competition Implications

The claim set has major implications for BEBA model competition.

## 1. Intention-only models are insufficient

Models that explain donation behaviour primarily through attitudes, subjective norms, perceived behavioural control, and intention are challenged because they explain intentions better than actual behaviour.

## 2. Relational brand equity models are incomplete

Models that treat satisfaction, trust, and commitment as central indicators of brand strength are insufficient if they do not explain why these evaluations often fail to produce actual donations.

## 3. BEBA should compete against three model families

### a. Intention-formation models

These models explain how people come to intend to donate. BEBA should accept their relevance but position them as incomplete for behavioural explanation.

### b. Relational evaluation models

These models explain favourable nonprofit brand relationships. BEBA should test whether relational variables predict behaviour directly, indirectly through intention, or only weakly after friction and conversion mechanisms are included.

### c. Cue-driven behaviour models

These models explain donation behaviour through salience, emotion, and situational triggers. BEBA should incorporate them as a competing or complementary behavioural route.

## 4. BEBA should distinguish behavioural pathways

The reviewed claims suggest at least two competing pathways:

```text
Pathway A: Intention-driven route

Brand evaluations / trust / satisfaction / commitment
→ donation intention
→ post-intentional conversion mechanisms
→ donation behaviour
```

```text
Pathway B: Cue-driven route

Emotional activation / cause salience / action opportunity
→ immediate donation behaviour
```

A possible extended BEBA model should evaluate whether nonprofit brand equity works primarily by:

- forming intentions,
- converting intentions,
- reducing friction,
- activating cues,
- strengthening normative alignment,
- or combining these mechanisms.

# Recommendation

## 1. Integrate this claim set into BEBA as a foundational behavioural justification

The Intention–Behavior Gap should be treated as a foundational reason for BEBA’s behavioural architecture. It directly supports the move from nonprofit brand equity as favourable perception to nonprofit brand equity as behaviour-enabling architecture.

## 2. Create or maintain a candidate concept file for Intention–Behavior Gap

Recommended target object:

```text
Object type: Concept
Candidate title: Intention–Behavior Gap
Status: Candidate
Layer: Individual
```

This concept should not yet be promoted to canonical ontology unless the existing ontology review confirms definitional alignment.

## 3. Add post-intentional conversion as a BEBA mechanism candidate

The strongest mechanism implication is that BEBA needs a distinct post-intentional conversion mechanism. This should be represented separately from intention formation.

## 4. Treat relational brand drivers as insufficient but not irrelevant

Satisfaction, trust, and commitment should remain relevant to BEBA, but their theoretical role should be narrowed. They may support intention formation, perceived legitimacy, or relational readiness, but they should not be treated as sufficient evidence of behavioural effectiveness.

## 5. Add cue-driven behaviour as an alternative pathway

Emotional activation and cognitive salience require BEBA to include a route to behaviour that does not depend on prior explicit intention. This is important for model competition because it prevents BEBA from becoming merely an extended TPB model.

## 6. Add normative alignment as a boundary condition

Normative alignment should be treated as a societal-level moderator of intention-to-behaviour conversion.

## 7. Do not make empirical claims beyond the reviewed material

The claim set reports that TPB explains 19% of actual giving and 44% of donation intentions, and that relational effects on intentions are 3 to 8 times larger than effects on behaviour. These numbers should remain attached to the reviewed evidence source and should not be generalised beyond that review without checking the original literature and pipeline evidence.

## 8. Suggested integration summary

The reviewed claim set supports BEBA by showing that nonprofit brand equity must explain not only why people intend to give, but why they actually give. It strengthens the behavioural orientation of BEBA, introduces post-intentional conversion as a necessary mechanism, identifies self-regulation failure and practical friction as barriers, adds emotional and salience-based cueing as a bypass route around prior intention, and positions normative alignment as a boundary condition for behavioural enactment.
