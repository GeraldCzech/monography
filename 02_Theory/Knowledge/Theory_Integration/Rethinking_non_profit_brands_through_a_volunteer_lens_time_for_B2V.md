# Input Reviewed

-   **BK-000011**: Volunteer knowledge of a nonprofit brand is accumulated from national (Macro), local community (Micro), and personal (Mego) sources.
-   **BE-000060**: A potential volunteer's level of Brand Engagement with a nonprofit determines their subsequent brand choice process (Brand Discovery).
-   **BE-000061**: High Brand Engagement ("Brand Wise") leads to an automatic, non-compensatory volunteer choice process.
-   **DM-000002**: High-involvement decisions, such as choosing a regular volunteer role, do not necessarily entail a complex, rational, or comparative evaluation.
-   **VC-000003**: Volunteer choice is often automatic and non-compensatory when the brand is perceived as the category leader.
-   **VC-000004**: A strong personal ("Mego") connection to a nonprofit or its cause triggers an automatic and non-compensatory volunteer choice process.
-   **NB-000084**: A strong nonprofit brand reduces a potential volunteer's perceived risk and cognitive effort during the choice process.
-   **BA-000071**: For volunteers who engage in an explicit search, higher brand awareness increases the probability of inclusion and selection from a consideration set.
-   **VT-000001**: Potential volunteers can be classified into distinct typologies based on their brand-driven decision-making process.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claims provide strong qualitative evidence for the central thesis of BEBA: that strong nonprofit brands function as a **cognitive efficiency mechanism** or a **behavioural heuristic**. They enable potential volunteers to bypass complex, high-effort deliberation, even for high-involvement decisions. The brand architecture works by reducing perceived risk and cognitive load, triggering automatic, non-compensatory choice processes when specific conditions—high brand engagement, category leadership, or personal connection—are met.

# BEBA Layer Affected

-   **Individual Layer**: This is the primary layer affected, as the claims detail the cognitive processes, engagement states, and choice heuristics of potential volunteers.
-   **Meso (Community) & Macro (Societal) Layers**: These layers are implicated as sources of brand knowledge ("Micro" and "Macro" sources in claim BK-000011) that shape the individual's perceptions and subsequent behaviour. This provides a multi-level structure for the antecedents of brand knowledge.

# Existing Claims Supported

These claims provide direct, mechanism-level support for BEBA's core premise that brands structure behaviour.
-   The fundamental idea that brands can serve as a "behavioural architecture" is strongly supported by NB-000084 (reducing effort) and BE-000061 (enabling automaticity).
-   The role of brand awareness as a gateway to consideration (BA-000071) aligns with foundational brand equity models and confirms its place in BEBA.
-   The importance of identity alignment and personal meaning is directly supported by VC-000004 ("Mego" connection), reinforcing the symbolic function of nonprofit brands within BEBA.

# Existing Claims Challenged

The claims do not challenge BEBA's internal logic. Instead, they provide powerful evidence to challenge **competing theoretical explanations** that BEBA positions itself against.
-   **DM-000002** directly challenges standard rational choice models (e.g., Theory of Planned Behavior, Multi-Attribute Utility Theory) that assume high-involvement decisions necessitate high-effort, compensatory evaluation. This strengthens BEBA's justification for focusing on heuristics and automaticity.

# New Candidate Claims

This body of evidence suggests several new, more specific claims for integration into BEBA:

1.  **Claim (Moderation)**: The effect of NPO Brand Equity on the choice process is moderated by the individual's level of Brand Engagement. For the "Brand Wise," Brand Equity triggers an automatic, non-compensatory process; for the "Brand Aware," it primarily influences consideration set formation.
2.  **Claim (Mechanism)**: The brand-as-architecture effect is activated through at least two distinct mechanisms: (a) perception of the brand as a category leader (a cognitive heuristic) and (b) strong personal identity alignment with the brand (an affective/identity heuristic).
3.  **Claim (Antecedent Structure)**: The brand knowledge that underpins the BEBA effect is formed through a multi-level accumulation of information from Macro (e.g., national campaigns), Micro (e.g., community presence), and Mego (e.g., personal experience) sources.

# Boundary Conditions

-   **Brand Engagement**: The claims introduce `Brand Engagement` (conceptualised as Brand Wise, Aware, Ignorant) as a critical boundary condition. The BEBA model, particularly its emphasis on automaticity, may only apply to the "Brand Wise" segment. The behaviour of the "Brand Aware" or "Brand Ignorant" may be better explained by different models (e.g., search-based rational choice).

# Model Competition Implications

These findings strongly support a **dual-process theory** perspective on volunteer choice. This is a significant refinement for BEBA. Instead of a single model, BEBA can propose (and test) a competing model framework:
1.  **BEBA Heuristic Model**: For the highly engaged ("Brand Wise"), brand equity acts as a heuristic, leading to a direct, non-compensatory choice. This is BEBA's core explanatory domain.
2.  **Deliberative Choice Model**: For the less engaged but aware ("Brand Aware"), the choice process is more traditionally rational. Brand equity's role is limited to increasing the probability of being included in a consideration set, which is then evaluated more deliberatively.

This reframes the research from "does BEBA work?" to "**for whom and under what conditions** does the BEBA mechanism dominate a more traditional, deliberative mechanism?"

# Ontology Candidate Implications

This review necessitates the creation or refinement of several concepts in the BEBA ontology:

1.  **New Concept Candidate: `Volunteer Decision Typology`**: Create a new ontology object based on VT-000001, detailing the proposed types (e.g., "Leader-lover," "Personally connected") as distinct actor profiles.
2.  **Refinement of `Brand Engagement`**: The existing concept should be updated to include the three-tiered typology of "Brand Wise," "Brand Aware," and "Brand Ignorant" as specific states or levels.
3.  **New Concept Candidate: `Brand Knowledge Sources`**: Create a new ontology object to formalise the "Macro-Micro-Mego" framework as the structural antecedent to `Brand Knowledge`. This provides a clear link between the environmental layers and the individual cognitive layer.
4.  **Refinement of `Choice Process`**: The ontology should explicitly distinguish between `Automatic/Non-Compensatory Choice` and `Deliberative/Compensatory Choice` as distinct process types triggered by different conditions.

# Recommendation

1.  **Adopt the Claims**: Formally accept these nine candidate claims as providing strong qualitative support for the core BEBA theory.
2.  **Introduce a Moderating Variable**: Position `Brand Engagement` as a central moderator in the BEBA theoretical model, determining the subsequent decision pathway.
3.  **Formalise Model Competition**: Explicitly frame BEBA's contribution as a dual-process model. Develop a `BEBA Heuristic Model` to compete with a `Deliberative Choice Model`, and define the boundary conditions (Brand Engagement level) under which each is expected to hold.
4.  **Update Ontology**: Initiate the process for creating ontology candidates for `Volunteer Decision Typology` and `Brand Knowledge Sources`. Begin the process to refine the `Brand Engagement` and `Choice Process` concepts as described above. This will likely require an entry in the `DecisionLog` and updates to the `glossary`.
