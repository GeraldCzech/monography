# Input Reviewed

This integration memo assesses the theoretical implications of a claim set derived from `REVIEW-000115`. The claims focus on the segmentation of monetary non-donors, identifying distinct reasons for their non-support and their demographic characteristics. The input consists of 13 claims (`SS-000007`, `TR-000030`, `PB-000033`, `BI-000053`, `S-000013`, `B-000002`, `SE-000006`, `VP-000001`, and `D-000001` through `D-000005`).

Object type reviewed: **Claim set**
Status of input: **candidate**
Primary BEBA relevance: **Market-level heterogeneity and its implications for individual behavioural enablement.**

# Core Theoretical Implication

The central theoretical implication is that a universal, one-size-fits-all Brand-Enabled Behaviour Architecture (BEBA) is likely to be ineffective. The population of non-donors is not a monolith but a collection of distinct segments, each with specific behavioural barriers. Therefore, BEBA must be conceptualized as a **portfolio of segment-specific architectures** rather than a single universal model. The brand's function shifts from merely enabling behaviour to enabling the *right* behaviour for the *right* segment by overcoming that segment's dominant behavioural friction (e.g., lack of trust, perceived cost, inertia, or reactance).

# BEBA Layer Affected

This claim set has multi-layer implications for the BEBA framework:

-   **Market (Primary):** The foundational claim (`SS-000007`) establishes that stakeholder groups (like non-donors) are heterogeneous. This positions segmentation as a critical market-level activity that precedes and informs any organisational or individual-level strategy.
-   **Individual (Primary):** The claims identify specific psychological barriers at the individual level that prevent behaviour:
    -   **Trust deficit** (`TR-000030`)
    -   **Low Perceived Behavioural Control** due to financial constraints (`PB-000033`)
    -   **Behavioural Inertia** or lack of cognitive activation (`BI-000053`)
    -   **Reactance** to certain solicitation methods (`S-000013`)
-   **Organisational (Secondary):** The findings directly inform organisational strategy by implying the need for tailored BEBA designs (`B-000002`), including multi-modal engagement pathways (monetary and non-monetary) (`SE-000006`) and segment-specific value propositions (`VP-000001`).
-   **Societal (Contextual):** Demographic patterns (`D-000001` to `D-000005`) provide societal-level context, linking non-donation to broader social factors like age, gender, income, education, and religiosity.

# Existing Claims Supported

These claims provide strong support for several core BEBA principles:

1.  **Behaviour before brands:** The focus on *reasons for non-behaviour* reinforces the principle that understanding the behavioural landscape is the necessary first step before designing brand-based interventions.
2.  **Brand equity as friction reduction:** The claims give specific content to the idea of "behavioural friction." A BEBA must reduce friction related to trust, cost, and effort. For example, `TR-000030` supports the idea that trust-building brand signals are crucial for overcoming the barrier of perceived inefficiency. `PB-000033` supports the need for BEBAs that lower the perceived cost of action.
3.  **The centrality of Trust:** The finding that perceived high administrative costs is a primary barrier (`TR-000030`) elevates trust and perceived efficiency from a "nice-to-have" brand attribute to a foundational prerequisite for any BEBA to function.

# Existing Claims Challenged

The claim set does not directly contradict any core BEBA claims but strongly challenges any overly simplistic or universalist application of the theory.

1.  **Challenge to Universal Models:** The most significant challenge is to any implicit assumption that a single BEBA model can be effective across all stakeholders. Claim `SS-000007` makes a universalist approach untenable.
2.  **Challenge to Activation-Only Models:** Claim `BI-000053` suggests that a large portion of non-behaviour is due to passivity, not opposition. This challenges models that focus solely on persuasion or changing negative attitudes, highlighting the need for BEBA to also incorporate mechanisms for maintaining cognitive availability and providing timely, low-effort action cues.

# New Candidate Claims

Based on this integration, the following higher-level claims can be formulated for the BEBA theory:

## Candidate Claim 1: BEBA Contingency

-   **Statement:** The behavioural effectiveness of a nonprofit's Brand-Enabled Behaviour Architecture is contingent upon its alignment with the dominant behavioural barriers (e.g., trust deficits, cost perceptions, inertia) of its target stakeholder segment.
-   **Status:** Candidate
-   **Layer:** Organisational / Market
-   **Relationship type:** Foundational

## Candidate Claim 2: The Role of Non-Monetary Pathways

-   **Statement:** For stakeholder segments characterized by positive attitudes but high perceived behavioural costs (e.g., financial constraints), a BEBA that offers non-monetary engagement pathways is more effective for building long-term relational capital than one focused exclusively on monetary support.
-   **Status:** Candidate
-   **Layer:** Organisational / Individual
-   **Relationship type:** Mechanism candidate

## Candidate Claim 3: Overcoming Inertia

-   **Statement:** To overcome behavioural inertia in attitudinally indifferent stakeholder segments, a BEBA must prioritize maintaining cognitive availability (brand salience) and providing salient, low-friction action affordances.
-   **Status:** Candidate
-   **Layer:** Individual / Organisational
-   **Relationship type:** Mechanism candidate

# Boundary Conditions

The primary boundary condition introduced is **Stakeholder Segment**. The claims suggest that the mechanisms and effectiveness of any BEBA are bounded by the characteristics of the target segment. For example:

-   For the **"Restricted"** segment (financially constrained), the boundary condition is **economic capacity**. A BEBA focused on large donations will fail.
-   For the **"Categorical"** or **"Unsolicited"** segments (high distrust/reactance), the boundary condition is **trust and perceived legitimacy**. A BEBA based on aggressive fundraising will backfire.
-   For the **"Indifferent"** segment (passive), the boundary condition is **cognitive activation**. A BEBA that requires proactive, high-effort engagement will be ineffective.

# Model Competition Implications

This review has significant implications for model competition within the BEBA research program:

1.  **Universal vs. Segmented BEBA:** It sets up a direct competition between a single, universal structural model of BEBA and a multi-group or moderated model where stakeholder segment is the key moderating variable. The evidence from `REVIEW-000115` strongly favors the **segmented model**.
2.  **Competing Mechanisms by Segment:** It implies that different mediating pathways will be dominant for different segments. This leads to competing models:
    -   **Model (Trust-Dominant):** For distrustful segments, Brand Equity -> Trust -> Intention.
    -   **Model (PBC-Dominant):** For resource-constrained segments, Brand Equity -> Non-Monetary Affordances -> Perceived Behavioural Control -> Intention.
    -   **Model (Salience-Dominant):** For indifferent segments, Brand Awareness -> Action Cue -> Behaviour.

Empirical work should test for the differential importance of these pathways across segments.

# Ontology Candidate Implications

This integration suggests several refinements and additions to the BEBA ontology:

-   **Stakeholder Segmentation:** Must be elevated to a foundational, first-class concept in the ontology. It is not merely a marketing tactic but a structural feature of the market layer that conditions all other BEBA functions.
-   **Behavioural Barrier:** A new concept candidate should be created to formally capture the reasons for non-behaviour. This concept could have subtypes derived from the review, such as:
    -   `Trust Barrier` (related to perceived inefficiency/misconduct)
    -   `Cost Barrier` (related to perceived lack of resources)
    -   `Activation Barrier` (related to inertia/indifference)
    -   `Reactance Barrier` (related to aversion to solicitation)
-   **BEBA (Concept Refinement):** The canonical definition of BEBA should be reviewed to ensure it incorporates the idea of contingency and segment-specificity. It is an architecture that must be *adapted*, not just deployed.
-   **Non-Monetary Engagement:** This should be added as a key concept, representing a class of behaviours (e.g., volunteering, advocacy, sharing information) that a comprehensive BEBA must enable.
