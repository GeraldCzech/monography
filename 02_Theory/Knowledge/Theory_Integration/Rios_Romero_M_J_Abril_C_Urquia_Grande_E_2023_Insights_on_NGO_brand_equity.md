Reviewed candidate claim set from `REVIEW-000024`, covering:

- Moral Capital (`MC-000002`)
- Donor-Based Brand Equity (`DB-000004`, `DB-000005`, `DB-000006`)

The evidence base is described as relying primarily on a PLS-SEM study using a Spanish convenience sample of 131 respondents focused on childhood-related NGOs, alongside associated conceptual sources listed in the claim metadata.

# Core Theoretical Implication

This claim set supports a BEBA interpretation in which nonprofit brand effectiveness depends less on mere visibility or familiarity and more on morally credible, evaluatively rich, and relationally binding meaning structures.

The strongest integration point is that nonprofit brands appear behaviourally effective when they help donors resolve uncertainty through:

1. morally credible organisational identity,
2. value-laden brand associations,
3. emotional and attitudinal commitment,
4. identification with organisational values.

This reinforces BEBA’s central move away from awareness-based or recognition-based brand equity models toward a behavioural architecture model in which brand meaning enables supportive behaviour under uncertainty.

However, the empirical basis is contextually limited. The claims should remain candidate-level because the reviewed evidence is based on a specific NGO category, country context, and convenience sample.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## Primary Layer: Individual

Most claims operate at the individual donor level:

- donor-based brand equity,
- brand associations,
- brand familiarity,
- brand commitment,
- donor identification,
- emotional attachment,
- attitudinal commitment.

These claims affect how BEBA conceptualises the psychological mechanisms through which a nonprofit brand becomes behaviourally effective.

## Secondary Layer: Societal

`MC-000002` affects the societal layer because moral capital concerns the wider legitimacy and moral credibility expectations placed on NGOs. It suggests that NGOs may face a stronger demand for moral justification than other nonprofit organisations.

## Cross-Layer Implication

The claim set implies a cross-layer bridge:

```text
Societal moral expectations
→ perceived moral credibility / reputation
→ value-laden brand associations
→ donor commitment
→ donor-based brand equity
→ supportive behavioural intentions
```

This is important for BEBA because it links societal-level uncertainty and moral scrutiny to individual-level brand-enabled behaviour.

# Existing Claims Supported

## 1. Brand effectiveness depends on meaning, not mere awareness

Supported by:

- `DB-000004`: brand associations positively affect donor-based brand equity.
- `BF-000005`: brand familiarity has no significant direct effect on donor-based brand equity in the tested sample.
- `BA-000011`: brand associations include reputation, differentiation, and value identification.

The evidence supports the BEBA position that being known is insufficient. Nonprofit brands must be meaningfully interpreted by donors.

## 2. Nonprofit brand equity is relational and behavioural, not only perceptual

Supported by:

- `DB-000005`: brand commitment positively affects donor-based brand equity.
- `BC-000010`: commitment includes attitudinal and emotional dimensions.
- `BL-000001`: commitment may be more appropriate than loyalty for donor-NGO relationships.

This supports BEBA’s emphasis on psychological and behavioural architecture rather than simple repeat-behaviour models.

## 3. Moral credibility is central to nonprofit brand effectiveness

Supported by:

- `MC-000002`: NGOs are differentiated by donors’ heightened demand for moral capital.
- `BA-000011`: brand associations include perceived reputation and identification with NGO values.

This supports the view that nonprofit brand equity is partly grounded in moral trustworthiness, legitimacy, and value congruence.

## 4. BEBA should model brand equity as an enabling architecture

Supported by:

- `DB-000006`: a model using familiarity, associations, and commitment explains 56.7% of the variance in donor-based brand equity in the tested sample.

This provides a comparator model that can be used to position BEBA against narrower donor-based brand equity models.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Awareness-first brand equity assumptions are challenged

`BF-000005` challenges models that assume familiarity or awareness directly produces donor-based brand equity.

The implication is not that familiarity is irrelevant, but that its role may be indirect, conditional, or insufficient without evaluative and relational content.

## 2. Authenticity as a distinct equity driver is challenged

`BA-000012` states that brand authenticity was not retained as a valid measurement component in the final donor-based NGO brand equity model.

This cautions BEBA against treating authenticity as an automatically distinct mechanism. Authenticity may need to be:

- empirically distinguished from reputation,
- separated from moral credibility,
- tested as a boundary condition,
- or treated as part of broader moral/evaluative association structures.

## 3. Loyalty-based donor models are challenged

`BL-000001` suggests that brand commitment is more appropriate than brand loyalty for capturing donor-NGO relationships.

This supports BEBA’s move away from commercial loyalty analogies where repeat purchase or behavioural retention is central. In nonprofit contexts, behavioural support may be episodic, morally motivated, identity-relevant, and commitment-based.

# New Candidate Claims

## Candidate Claim 1: Familiarity is insufficient without evaluative meaning

Nonprofit brand familiarity may not directly generate donor-based brand equity unless accompanied by value-laden, reputational, or identity-relevant brand associations.

Status: candidate  
Confidence: medium, based on the reviewed sample limitation.

NGO brand effectiveness depends partly on perceived moral capital because donors expect NGOs to justify trust, legitimacy, and value alignment more strongly than other organisation types.

Status: candidate  
Confidence: low to medium, because the claim is conceptually important but requires validation across NGO and non-NGO nonprofit settings.

## Candidate Claim 3: Commitment is a behavioural architecture mechanism

Donor brand commitment functions as a psychological mechanism connecting brand meaning to supportive donor intentions through attitudinal and emotional bonds.

Status: candidate  
Confidence: medium.

## Candidate Claim 4: Nonprofit brand associations are value-laden evaluative structures

In nonprofit settings, brand associations may include reputation, differentiation, and identification with organisational values rather than only cognitive associations or awareness-based memory structures.

Status: candidate  
Confidence: medium.

## Candidate Claim 5: Authenticity should not be assumed as a distinct construct

Brand authenticity should not be treated as a standalone driver of donor-based nonprofit brand equity unless discriminant validity and empirical contribution are established.

Status: candidate  
Confidence: medium for the caution; low for any broader generalisation beyond the reviewed study.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Organisational type

The moral capital claim may apply more strongly to NGOs than to all nonprofit organisations. The reviewed material explicitly notes the need for validation across NGO and non-NGO nonprofit settings.

## 2. Cause category

The empirical evidence concerns childhood-related NGOs. Findings may not generalise to:

- humanitarian aid,
- environmental NGOs,
- religious nonprofits,
- cultural organisations,
- advocacy organisations,
- health charities,
- member-based associations.

## 3. National context

The study uses a Spanish sample. Donor expectations concerning trust, moral legitimacy, reputation, and commitment may vary across countries and institutional environments.

## 4. Sampling limitation

The evidence is based on a convenience sample of 131 respondents. Generalisation should remain cautious.

## 5. Measurement structure

The reviewed model found that identification items loaded with brand associations, and brand authenticity items were eliminated. This indicates possible construct instability and the need for further measurement validation.

## 6. Direct versus indirect effects

Brand familiarity did not show a significant direct effect, but the reviewed notes indicate it may still operate indirectly or conditionally. BEBA should not remove familiarity entirely; rather, it should avoid assigning it a simple direct-effect role without evidence.

# Model Competition Implications

This claim set strengthens BEBA’s model competition agenda.

## Comparator Model Identified

The reviewed donor-based brand equity model uses:

```text
Brand Familiarity
Brand Associations
Brand Commitment
→ Donor-Based Brand Equity
```

It reportedly explains 56.7% of variance in donor-based brand equity in the tested sample.

This model can serve as a comparator against BEBA.

## BEBA Differentiation

BEBA should distinguish itself from this model by explaining not only variance in donor-based brand equity, but the behavioural architecture through which nonprofit brands become effective under uncertainty.

A BEBA-oriented model would likely ask:

```text
How do moral credibility, evaluative meaning, identification, and commitment enable donor behaviour under uncertainty?
```

rather than only:

```text
Which brand variables predict donor-based brand equity?
```

## Awareness-Based Model Weakened

The lack of a significant direct familiarity effect weakens simple awareness-first brand equity models.

## Authenticity-First Model Weakened

The elimination of authenticity items weakens models that assume authenticity is always a distinct nonprofit brand equity driver.

## Commitment-Based Model Strengthened

The positive effect of brand commitment supports relational and commitment-based explanations of donor support.

## Moral Capital Model Introduced but Not Yet Established

The moral capital claim introduces an important societal-level explanation, but its evidence status remains low. It should be treated as theoretically promising but not yet canonical.
