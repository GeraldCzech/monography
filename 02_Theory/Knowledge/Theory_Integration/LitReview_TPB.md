# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set strongly supports BEBA’s central move away from simple nonprofit brand-liking explanations toward a behavioural architecture view of nonprofit brand equity.

The main implication is:

> Nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective not primarily by producing favourable brand attitudes, but by shaping the behavioural conditions under which giving becomes intended, possible, normatively supported, morally salient, trustworthy, and contextually appropriate.

This supports the BEBA premise that nonprofit brand equity should be conceptualised as a **brand-enabled behaviour architecture**, not merely as a set of brand associations or attitudinal evaluations.

The claim set also strengthens the case for using an **extended TPB / IBM-like behavioural chassis** as the behavioural foundation of BEBA, while adding brand equity constructs as uncertainty-reducing, agency-enabling, norm-shaping, and context-sensitive mechanisms.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## 1. Individual Layer

Strongly affected.

The following individual-level behavioural mechanisms are reinforced:

- Attitude toward donating
- Perceived Behavioral Control
- Donation intention
- Moral norms
- Past behaviour / behavioural habit formation
- Organizational trust as perceived risk reduction
- Brand image and NPO brand attitude as possible but not consistently direct predictors
- Descriptive and subjective norms as context-sensitive influences
- Audience involvement and pre-existing beliefs as moderators of communication effects

The individual layer should therefore remain the primary behavioural-processing layer in BEBA.

## 2. Organisational Layer

Moderately to strongly affected.

Organisational reputation and trust appear as mechanisms that shape the behavioural architecture indirectly. In particular:

- Negative CEO reputation suppresses the influence of attitudes and moral norms.
- Under reputational uncertainty, subjective norms and organisational identification may become more salient.
- Organisational trust functions as an informational cue reducing perceived risk.

This supports modelling the nonprofit brand not only as an image object, but as an institutional uncertainty-reduction structure.

## 3. Market / Channel Layer

Moderately affected.

Donation channel claims suggest that behavioural architecture varies by donation environment:

- Online crowdfunding donation contexts make social presence and platform trust more important.
- Traditional subjective norms may be weaker in online monetary donation contexts.
- Normative influence depends on interaction between brand, channel, and social visibility.

This implies that BEBA should not model donation behaviour independently of the interaction setting.

## 4. Societal Layer

Moderately affected.

Cultural, religious, and economic context alters the relative predictive strength of core behavioural predictors.

This supports treating BEBA parameters as context-sensitive rather than universal.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Direct brand attitude → donation intention models

Challenged by:

- `NB-000018`: Attitude toward the nonprofit organisation is not consistently significant when other behavioural predictors are included.

Implication:

> BEBA should not rely on a simple “favourable nonprofit attitude causes donation intention” model.

Instead, NPO brand attitude should be treated as:

- potentially indirect,
- context-dependent,
- mediated through trust, identification, perceived effectiveness, perceived control, or moral salience,
- possibly weaker than behaviour-specific attitudes.

## 2. Universal social proof assumptions

Challenged by:

- `DN-000001`: Descriptive norms have positive, null, or negative effects.
- `DC-000003`: Subjective norms may be weaker in online money donation contexts.
- `CF-000001`: Injunctive norm appeals depend on involvement and prior beliefs.

Implication:

> BEBA should not assume that visible popularity, peer behaviour, or normative messaging automatically increases donation behaviour.

## 3. Stable brand equity effect assumptions

Challenged by:

- `OR-000002`: Negative CEO reputation suppresses attitude and moral norm effects.
- `OR-000003`: Under negative CEO reputation, subjective norms and organisational identification become more salient.

Implication:

> Brand equity effects may be structurally reconfigured under reputational threat.

## 4. Original TPB sufficiency

Challenged by:

- `MN-000005`: Adding moral norms increases explanatory power.
- `PD-000002` and `PD-000003`: Past behaviour predicts future intention and behaviour.
- `IB-000009`: Extended TPB constructs overlap with IBM components.

Implication:

> BEBA should not use original TPB alone as its behavioural foundation. An extended TPB or IBM-like architecture is more appropriate.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed material has strong implications for competing BEBA model specifications.

## Model 1: Simple Brand Attitude Model

**Structure:**  
NPO brand attitude → donation intention → donation behaviour

**Status:** Weakened.

**Reason:** `NB-000018` indicates that NPO brand attitude is not consistently significant when behavioural predictors are included.

**Implication:** This model should be treated as an insufficient baseline or competitor, not as BEBA’s preferred model.

---

## Model 2: Original TPB Model

**Structure:**  
Attitude + subjective norms + perceived behavioural control → intention → behaviour

**Status:** Partially supported but insufficient.

**Reason:** Attitude, subjective norms, PBC, intention, and behaviour are all relevant, but moral norms, past behaviour, trust, reputation, and channel context extend the model.

**Implication:** Original TPB can serve as a baseline behavioural model, but BEBA should use an extended behavioural architecture.

---

## Model 3: Extended TPB Model

**Structure:**  
Attitude + subjective norms + PBC + moral norms + past behaviour → intention → behaviour

**Status:** Strongly supported.

**Reason:** Moral norms and past behaviour add explanatory relevance in donation contexts.

**Implication:** Extended TPB is a strong intermediate competitor and may provide the empirical bridge toward IBM.

---

## Model 4: IBM-like Behavioural Architecture

**Structure:**  
Experiential attitude + instrumental attitude + perceived norms + personal agency + moral obligation + knowledge/salience + environmental constraints + habit/past behaviour → intention/behaviour

**Status:** Theoretically strengthened.

**Reason:** The reviewed donation literature does not explicitly use IBM terminology, but several constructs overlap with IBM-style components.

**Implication:** BEBA can justify IBM as a formal integration chassis, but should avoid claiming that the reviewed literature already adopts IBM unless independently verified.

---

## Model 5: BEBA Brand-Enabled Behaviour Architecture

**Structure:**  
Brand equity dimensions → behavioural mechanisms → donation intention → donation behaviour, moderated by uncertainty, reputation, channel, audience state, and context.

**Status:** Strongly supported as the distinctive theoretical contribution.

**Reason:** The reviewed literature supports behavioural mechanisms and some brand-related predictors, but lacks systematic integration of multidimensional brand equity with an explicit behavioural model.

**Implication:** BEBA should be positioned as an integrative theory that explains when and how nonprofit brand equity becomes behaviourally effective.

# Recommendation

## 1. Integrate this claim set into BEBA as a theory-integration candidate

The claim set is highly relevant to the BEBA architecture and should be treated as a candidate integration between donation-behaviour theory and nonprofit brand equity theory.

Recommended object type:

- `Claim Set`
- `Theory Integration`
- possible later `Ontology Candidate`

## 2. Do not immediately canonicalise all claims

Several claims have no attached sources in the provided document. They should remain candidate claims until source verification is completed through the literature system.

Especially cautionary claims:

- `IB-000008`
- `IB-000009`
- `BE-000012`

These are synthesis-level or interpretation-level claims and should not be treated as empirical findings.

## 3. Use this integration to strengthen BEBA’s behavioural chassis

The document supports positioning BEBA on an extended TPB / IBM-like foundation.

Preferred framing:

> BEBA uses an IBM-like behavioural architecture to explain how nonprofit brand equity becomes behaviourally effective under uncertainty.

Avoid overclaiming:

> The reviewed literature already uses IBM.

The reviewed material explicitly states that IBM terminology is not commonly used.

## 4. Preserve distinction between brand attitude and behavioural attitude

This is theoretically important.

BEBA should distinguish:

- attitude toward donating,
- attitude toward the nonprofit organisation,
- brand image,
- organisational trust,
- organisational reputation.

Collapsing these would weaken the explanatory precision of the architecture.

## 5. Add reputational uncertainty as a boundary condition

Negative CEO or organisational reputation appears to alter the behavioural mechanism structure.

Recommended BEBA addition:

> Reputational uncertainty moderates the behavioural effectiveness of nonprofit brand equity by changing which behavioural predictors become salient.

## 6. Add channel-specific trust as a market-layer mechanism

Online giving contexts introduce platform trust and social presence.

Recommended BEBA addition:

> In platform-mediated donation contexts, brand-enabled behaviour depends on both organisational trust and channel trust.

## 7. Develop a model competition section

This claim set is useful for explicitly comparing:

1. Simple brand attitude model  
2. Original TPB model  
3. Extended TPB model  
4. IBM-like behavioural architecture  
5. BEBA model  

This would support BEBA’s principle of competing explanations over single models.
