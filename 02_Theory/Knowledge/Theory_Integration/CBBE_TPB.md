# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set supports a central BEBA premise: brand equity should not be treated merely as a marketing asset or downstream outcome, but as an upstream behavioural architecture that can shape behavioural intention through established behavioural mechanisms.

Most importantly, the claim set supports integrating brand equity with behavioural theory rather than modelling brand equity and behaviour separately. In BEBA terms, brand equity becomes behaviourally effective when it influences evaluative, motivational, or decision-relevant structures such as attitude, intention, satisfaction, perceived sacrifice, or possibly other TPB-related constructs.

The strongest implication is that **attitude functions as a behavioural translation mechanism between brand equity and intention**. However, the reviewed material also suggests that direct pathways from brand equity to intention may exist, meaning BEBA should not assume full mediation through attitude only.

# Existing Claims Challenged

The reviewed material does not directly falsify BEBA, but it challenges several overly simple versions of the theory.

## 1. Challenge to a brand-only explanation of behaviour

Because integrated CBBE-TPB models explain more than isolated CBBE models, BEBA should avoid claiming that brand equity alone explains stakeholder action.

Implication: BEBA should remain a behavioural architecture, not a brand-determinism model.

## 2. Challenge to a purely mediated model

`BI-000008` and `NB-000010` suggest that brand equity may sometimes affect intention directly, beyond attitude.

Implication: BEBA should not assume that attitude is the only route from brand equity to behavioural intention.

## 3. Challenge to universal brand-equity dimension effects

`CB-000004`, `BA-000003`, and `MF-000001` suggest that awareness, associations, perceived quality, loyalty, or nonprofit-specific dimensions may not have uniform effects across contexts.

Implication: BEBA should allow dimension-specific, field-specific, and behaviour-specific variation.

## 4. Challenge to direct nonprofit generalisation

The nonprofit-specific claims `NB-000007` to `NB-000010` are low-confidence because they are extrapolated from for-profit evidence.

Implication: BEBA may use these as candidate propositions, but not yet as canonical nonprofit claims.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed claim set has strong implications for BEBA model competition.

## 1. CBBE-only model vs. TPB-only model vs. integrated model

The integrated model appears theoretically preferable because it treats brand equity as an upstream antecedent of behavioural determinants.

Candidate comparison:

1. Brand-equity-only model  
2. TPB-only model  
3. Integrated brand-equity + TPB model  
4. Integrated model with mediation through attitude  
5. Integrated model with both mediated and direct paths  
6. Integrated model with moderators such as perceived sacrifice, involvement, demographics, or mission field

## 2. Full mediation vs. partial mediation

BEBA should explicitly compare:

- brand equity → attitude → intention,
- brand equity → intention,
- brand equity → attitude → intention plus direct brand equity → intention.

The reviewed evidence suggests that partial mediation may be necessary in some contexts.

## 3. Initial-support model vs. repeat-support model

For initial supportive behaviour, attitude may be the central mediator.

For repeat supportive behaviour, satisfaction may become more important.

This suggests that BEBA may need different behavioural submodels for:

- first-time donation,
- repeat donation,
- one-time volunteering,
- recurring volunteering,
- advocacy participation,
- loyalty-like nonprofit support.

## 4. Universal model vs. contingent model

The reviewed material challenges a single universal BEBA pathway. BEBA should allow model comparison across:

- mission fields,
- stakeholder groups,
- involvement levels,
- perceived sacrifice conditions,
- behaviour types.

# Recommendation

## Theoretical recommendation

Integrate this claim set into BEBA as a **candidate theoretical bridge between brand equity and behavioural intention**, with attitude as the primary translation mechanism and direct brand-to-intention effects retained as a competing pathway.

## Status recommendation

Keep the nonprofit-specific propositions as **candidate claims**, not canonical claims, until nonprofit-specific evidence is available.

## Model recommendation

BEBA should test at least three competing structures:

1. **TPB-only model**  
   Behavioural intention explained by standard TPB constructs.

2. **Brand-equity antecedent model**  
   Nonprofit brand equity dimensions influence attitude and possibly other TPB constructs.

3. **Partial mediation model**  
   Nonprofit brand equity influences intention both indirectly through attitude and directly.

Additional moderated versions should be considered where theoretically justified:

- mission field,
- perceived sacrifice,
- involvement,
- stakeholder group,
- initial vs. repeat behaviour.

## Ontology recommendation

Create or retain ontology candidates for:

- `Brand-Enabled Attitude Formation`
- `Direct Brand-Intention Pathway`
- `Mission Field Contingency`
- `Perceived Sacrifice`
- `Repeat-Support Satisfaction Mediation`

Do not update the canonical ontology solely from this review.

## Evidence recommendation

For nonprofit BEBA, the current evidence should be labelled:

`Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.`

Specifically, nonprofit-specific validation is still needed for:

- whether nonprofit brand equity increases explanatory power beyond TPB,
- whether nonprofit brand equity dimensions predict stakeholder attitude,
- whether attitude mediates nonprofit brand equity effects,
- whether a direct nonprofit brand equity → intention pathway exists,
- whether mission field moderates brand-equity effects.
