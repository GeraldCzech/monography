# Input Reviewed

Claim set on **Brand Attachment**, **Brand–Self Connection**, **Brand Prominence**, **Brand Attitude Strength**, and **Behavioural Difficulty** from review `REVIEW-000042`, sources `SRC-000213` and `SRC-000214`.

Core claim family:

- Brand Attachment and Brand Attitude Strength are empirically distinct.
- Brand Attachment is constituted by the strength of the brand–self bond.
- Brand Attachment is indicated by Brand–Self Connection and Brand Prominence.
- Brand Attachment has stronger predictive relevance than Brand Attitude Strength for difficult, resource-intensive, enacted, and allocation-based behaviours.
- Brand Attitude Strength may be sufficient for easy, low-cost behaviours.
- Behavioural Difficulty moderates the relative importance of attachment-based versus attitude-based pathways.
- Brand Attachment is limited to positive brand bonds and should not be used directly to explain aversive brand relations.

# Core Theoretical Implication

This claim set strengthens BEBA’s core theoretical move from **brand evaluation** to **brand-enabled behaviour architecture**.

The main implication is that nonprofit brand effectiveness should not be modelled as a single evaluative pathway from favourable brand perceptions to support behaviour. Instead, BEBA should distinguish at least two individual-level explanatory routes:

1. **Attitude-based route**  
   Positive, stable, or strong evaluations may explain low-cost, easy, or weakly involving behaviours.

2. **Attachment-based route**  
   Self-connected, salient, emotionally meaningful brand bonds may explain costly, difficult, identity-relevant, or resource-intensive behaviours such as donating, volunteering, advocating, defending, or choosing one nonprofit over alternatives.

This supports the idea that nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective when they organise supporter behaviour under uncertainty by becoming personally meaningful, cognitively accessible, and motivationally consequential.

The strongest implications are at the individual supporter level:

- self-concept linkage

## Secondary layer: Behavioural / Action layer

The claims affect how BEBA should classify target behaviours:

- easy versus difficult behaviours
- low-resource versus high-resource behaviours
- intention versus enacted behaviour
- one-off behaviour versus relationship-maintaining behaviour
- direct support versus allocation among alternatives

## Secondary layer: Market / Competitive allocation layer

Several claims extend BEBA beyond dyadic supporter–brand relationships:

- purchase/share allocation against direct competitors
- need-share allocation against substitutable alternatives
- supporter choice among comparable nonprofit organisations
- supporter choice among alternative ways of addressing a cause or need

For nonprofit brands, this suggests that attachment may not only increase support for one organisation, but also influence how supporters allocate limited money, time, attention, and advocacy across multiple possible causes and organisations.

# Existing Claims Challenged

No direct contradiction to BEBA is visible from the provided material. However, the claim set challenges or qualifies several possible simplifications.

## 1. Challenge to single-path brand equity models

If BEBA currently treats brand equity as a general latent force leading uniformly to support behaviour, this claim set challenges that architecture. It suggests that different psychological mechanisms may explain different behaviours.

Specifically:

- easy behaviours may be explained by attitude strength;
- difficult behaviours may require attachment;
- actual allocation behaviour may require more than stated preference.

## 2. Challenge to awareness-centric salience models

Brand Prominence should not be reduced to basic brand awareness. The document frames prominence as accessibility of brand-related thoughts and feelings within an attachment architecture.

This challenges any BEBA interpretation where salience is treated merely as recognition, recall, or top-of-mind awareness.

## 3. Challenge to immediate persuasion models

`BA-000041` suggests that attachment may strengthen with relationship length more than attitude strength does. Although this claim is low-confidence and speculative, it challenges short-term persuasion models of nonprofit brand effectiveness.

If accepted cautiously, it implies that nonprofit brand attachment may develop through repeated relational experience rather than one-time communication exposure.

## 4. Challenge to applying positive attachment theory to negative brand relations

`BA-000040` qualifies the scope of Brand Attachment. BEBA should not use positive attachment theory to explain rejection, antagonism, distrust, resentment, or moral opposition toward nonprofit brands without additional theory.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Behavioural difficulty

The strongest boundary condition is **Behavioural Difficulty**.

Attachment appears more theoretically relevant when behaviours require:

- money;
- time;
- social capital;
- personal effort;
- reputational exposure;
- emotional commitment;
- sustained involvement.

This is especially important for nonprofit contexts because many target behaviours are not simple purchases but costly support acts.

## 2. Easy versus difficult behaviour

The claim set distinguishes easy-to-perform behaviours from difficult behaviours.

Implication:

- Brand Attitude Strength may be sufficient for easy behaviours.
- Brand Attachment may become necessary or more predictive for difficult behaviours.

This prevents BEBA from overstating the role of attachment in all forms of nonprofit support.

## 3. Positive relationships only

The Park et al. attachment model, as represented in the reviewed synthesis, applies to positive brand bonds. It should not be extended to negative nonprofit brand relationships without additional conceptual work.

## 4. Identity-based versus instrumental self-connection

`BP-000016` suggests that Brand Prominence may be especially important when Brand–Self Connection is instrumental rather than identity-based.

Possible distinction:

- **Identity-based attachment:** the nonprofit brand expresses who the supporter is.
- **Instrumental attachment:** the nonprofit brand helps the supporter achieve valued goals.

This distinction may matter for different nonprofit categories, such as humanitarian aid, environmental advocacy, religious charities, cultural institutions, or health organisations.

## 5. Relationship length

`BA-000041` suggests that attachment may develop over time. Confidence is low, but the implication is important: attachment-based nonprofit brand equity may require repeated experience, accumulated trust, or longitudinal relationship formation.

# Model Competition Implications

This claim set has strong implications for BEBA model competition.

## 1. Attitude model versus attachment model

BEBA should explicitly compare:

- **Attitude Strength Model:** favourable, stable, confident evaluations predict support behaviour.
- **Attachment Model:** self-connected, prominent, emotionally meaningful brand bonds predict support behaviour.

Expected pattern:

- attitude strength may perform well for easy behaviours;
- attachment may outperform attitude strength for difficult, costly, or identity-relevant behaviours.

## 2. Awareness/salience model versus attachment-prominence model

Brand Prominence should be compared against simpler awareness or recall constructs.

Possible competing explanations:

- supporters act because the nonprofit is known or salient;
- supporters act because the nonprofit is attached to the self and prominent within memory.

BEBA should avoid treating all salience as equivalent.

## 3. Intention model versus enacted-behaviour model

The reviewed claims distinguish intentions, actual behaviour, purchase share, and need share. For BEBA, this means that models predicting stated support may not be sufficient.

Model competition should distinguish:

- stated donation intention;
- actual donation;
- volunteering behaviour;
- advocacy;
- repeat support;
- allocation across multiple nonprofits;
- substitution between nonprofit and non-nonprofit need satisfaction.

## 4. Direct competition versus substitutable alternatives

`BA-000036` and `BA-000037` imply that attachment may explain behaviour not only against direct competitors but also against substitutable alternatives.

For nonprofit brands, this is theoretically important. A supporter may choose between:

- two humanitarian organisations;
- donating versus volunteering;
- supporting a nonprofit versus helping directly;
- supporting one cause versus another cause;
- giving money versus spending time elsewhere.

This expands BEBA’s competitive logic from brand choice to behavioural allocation under constraint.

## 5. Short-term persuasion model versus relational-development model

If attachment strengthens with relationship length, BEBA should compare short-term communication effects against relationship-development explanations.

Possible competing models:

- immediate message-induced attitude change;
- cumulative attachment formation;
- trust and familiarity accumulation;
- identity integration over time.

# Recommendation

## Integrate as candidate theory material, not yet canonical ontology

This material should be integrated into BEBA as a **candidate mechanism cluster** around attachment-based nonprofit brand effectiveness.

Recommended status:

- Brand Attachment: candidate construct/concept
- Brand–Self Connection: candidate component construct
- Brand Prominence: candidate component construct
- Behavioural Difficulty: candidate boundary condition
- Attachment-based pathway: candidate mechanism
- Attitude-based pathway: competing explanatory pathway

## Recommended theoretical move

BEBA should explicitly distinguish:

```text
Low-difficulty behaviour:
Brand Attitude Strength may be sufficient.

High-difficulty behaviour:
Brand Attachment becomes more theoretically important.

High-difficulty nonprofit support under uncertainty:
Brand Attachment may enable supporters to invest resources despite cost, ambiguity, and competing alternatives.
```

## Recommended model competition framing

Future BEBA model architecture should compare at least three models:

1. **Attitude-only model**  
   Nonprofit support is explained by favourable or strong evaluations.

2. **Attachment-only model**  
   Nonprofit support is explained by self-connected, prominent brand bonds.

3. **Difficulty-contingent dual-pathway model**  
   Attitude Strength explains easy support behaviours; Brand Attachment explains difficult, costly, or identity-relevant support behaviours.

The third model appears most consistent with the reviewed claims.

## Recommended caution

Do not overgeneralise Brand Attachment.

The reviewed material does **not** justify using positive attachment theory to explain:

- nonprofit brand rejection;
- distrust;
- antagonism;
- moral outrage;
- donor resentment;
- negative brand relationships.

Those require additional theory.

## Suggested BEBA integration summary

Brand Attachment should be treated as a distinct individual-level mechanism through which nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective. Its relevance is strongest when support behaviour is difficult, costly, identity-relevant, or requires sustained resource investment. Brand Attitude Strength remains relevant, especially for easy behaviours, but should not be treated as a sufficient explanation for high-commitment nonprofit support. Behavioural Difficulty should be modelled as a key boundary condition determining when attachment-based brand equity matters most.
