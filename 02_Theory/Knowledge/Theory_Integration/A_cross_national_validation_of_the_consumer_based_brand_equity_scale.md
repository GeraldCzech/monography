# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set supports using **multidimensional consumer-based brand equity** as a provisional starting architecture for BEBA, but it also strongly qualifies its transferability.

For BEBA, the most important theoretical implication is not simply that brand equity can be measured through awareness, perceived quality, associations, and loyalty. Rather, the implication is that **brand equity must be treated as a differentiated perception architecture whose dimensions may or may not remain stable across contexts**.

This matters because BEBA asks:

> How do nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty?

The reviewed claims suggest that nonprofit brand equity cannot be assumed to operate as a single global favourable attitude. Instead, behavioural effectiveness may depend on which specific brand-equity components become activated under uncertainty, for example awareness, perceived quality, organisational associations, trust-related associations, or loyalty.

The claims therefore support a BEBA position in which brand equity is not the behaviour itself, but a **latent architecture of brand-enabled behavioural readiness**.

---

# BEBA Layer Affected

## Primary layer: Individual layer

The strongest effect is at the **Individual** layer because the claims concern consumer perceptions, associations, and latent brand equity dimensions.

Relevant individual-level BEBA concerns:

- brand awareness as cognitive availability,
- perceived quality as evaluative confidence,
- brand associations as meaning structures,
- organisational associations as trust-relevant representations,
- brand loyalty as behavioural predisposition or prior relational attachment.

## Secondary layer: Market layer

The measurement invariance claims affect the **Market** layer because they concern comparison across countries and populations.

Relevant market-level implications:

- cross-national comparison requires measurement invariance,
- BEBA cannot compare nonprofit brand equity scores across contexts unless configural and metric invariance are established,
- market-level claims about stronger or weaker nonprofit brand equity require validated measurement equivalence.

## Possible organisational relevance

The distinction between organisational associations and other brand associations has implications for nonprofit contexts because nonprofit brands are often strongly identified with the organisation itself. However, the reviewed evidence comes from consumer product contexts, so this remains a candidate implication rather than a validated BEBA claim.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Any assumption that commercial CBBE scales are directly generalisable to nonprofit contexts

Challenged by:

- CB-000011

The reviewed claim set explicitly notes that the CBBE scale has not been shown to be invariant in nonprofit, service, B2B, or non-Western contexts.

BEBA consequence:

> BEBA should not adopt commercial consumer-brand-equity scales as canonical nonprofit measures without testing dimensionality, reliability, validity, and invariance in nonprofit settings.

## 2. Any unidimensional treatment of brand associations

Challenged by:

- BA-000029
- OA-000002

The evidence suggests that brand associations may require internal differentiation.

BEBA consequence:

> A BEBA model that treats brand associations as a single undifferentiated perception risks losing explanatory precision.

## 3. Any direct comparison of nonprofit brand equity across groups without measurement equivalence

Challenged by:

- MI-000005
- CB-000010

BEBA consequence:

> Apparent differences in nonprofit brand equity between countries, cultures, donor groups, or stakeholder categories may reflect measurement artefacts unless invariance is established.

## 4. Any assumption that brand equity dimensions have the same behavioural function across contexts

Partially challenged by:

- CB-000011

The reviewed material does not establish whether the same dimensions predict behaviour similarly across nonprofit, service, B2B, or non-Western contexts.

BEBA consequence:

> Even if dimensions are structurally similar, their behavioural role may differ under nonprofit uncertainty conditions.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Sector boundary

The reviewed evidence is from consumer product contexts, not nonprofit contexts.

BEBA implication:

> Transfer to nonprofit brands is theoretically plausible but empirically unconfirmed.

## 2. Geographic boundary

The evidence is limited to UK and Spanish consumer samples.

BEBA implication:

> The findings should not be generalised to non-Western, global South, or culturally distinct nonprofit contexts without further evidence.

## 3. Category boundary

The tested model concerns commercial consumer categories.

BEBA implication:

> Nonprofit contexts may activate different evaluative criteria, such as mission legitimacy, moral trust, perceived impact, transparency, or value congruence.

## 4. Measurement boundary

The reviewed claims support configural and metric invariance in the studied context, but do not establish full scalar invariance or behavioural equivalence.

BEBA implication:

> Even if factor structure and loadings are equivalent, direct mean comparisons or behavioural interpretations may require additional invariance evidence.

## 5. Behavioural boundary

The evidence concerns measurement structure, not direct behavioural effectiveness.

BEBA implication:

> The claims support BEBA’s measurement architecture but do not yet explain how brand equity becomes behaviourally effective under uncertainty.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed claim set affects model competition in several ways.

## 1. Supports multidimensional BEBA over global attitude models

The evidence favours a model in which brand equity contains multiple dimensions rather than a single global evaluation.

Model competition implication:

> BEBA should compete against simpler models that explain nonprofit behaviour through general attitude, liking, or satisfaction alone.

## 2. Supports association-specific models over undifferentiated image models

The differentiation of brand associations suggests that models treating “brand image” as one broad construct may be under-specified.

Model competition implication:

> BEBA should test whether specific association types, especially organisational associations, explain behaviour better than general brand image.

## 3. Supports measurement-sensitive comparison models

The measurement invariance claim means that cross-group BEBA models must compete not only on predictive fit but also on measurement equivalence.

Model competition implication:

> A model that predicts behaviour across groups but lacks measurement invariance may be theoretically weaker than an invariant model with clearer construct interpretation.

## 4. Does not yet establish superiority over alternative nonprofit behaviour models

The evidence does not show that brand equity explains nonprofit behaviour better than:

- trust-only models,
- legitimacy models,
- commitment models,
- donor identity models,
- perceived impact models,
- social norm models,
- habit or convenience models.

Model competition implication:

> The reviewed claims support BEBA’s measurement foundation but do not yet demonstrate BEBA’s explanatory superiority.

# Recommendation

## Recommended integration status

Integrate the reviewed claims as **candidate support for BEBA’s multidimensional measurement architecture**, not as canonical nonprofit theory.

## Recommended actions

1. **Retain CB-000009 and CB-000010 as candidate foundational measurement claims.**  
   They support multidimensional CBBE structure and cross-national metric equivalence, but only in the reviewed consumer contexts.

2. **Promote MI-000005 as a strong methodological candidate.**  
   Measurement invariance should become a BEBA methodological requirement for cross-context comparisons.

3. **Use BA-000029 and OA-000002 to justify differentiated brand association modelling.**  
   These claims support rejecting a unidimensional association construct, but nonprofit-specific association dimensions require further evidence.

4. **Keep CB-000011 as an explicit boundary-condition claim.**  
   This is important because it prevents overextension of commercial CBBE into nonprofit BEBA.

5. **Create or update an ontology candidate for Organisational Associations if not already present.**  
   This concept appears especially relevant to nonprofit brand equity because nonprofit brands are likely to be evaluated through organisational credibility, legitimacy, and trust. However, those nonprofit-specific mechanisms require additional reviewed evidence.

6. **Do not treat this claim set as evidence of behavioural effectiveness.**  
   The reviewed material supports construct structure and measurement comparability, not the behavioural mechanism by which nonprofit brands generate action under uncertainty.

## Bottom-line theoretical integration

The reviewed claims strengthen BEBA’s foundation as a **multidimensional brand-enabled perception architecture**, but they also impose a major constraint:

> Commercial consumer-based brand equity models may inform BEBA, but nonprofit brand equity must be theoretically adapted, empirically validated, and tested for measurement invariance before it can support cross-context behavioural claims.
