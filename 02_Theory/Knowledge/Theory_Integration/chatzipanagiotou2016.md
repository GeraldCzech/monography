# Core Theoretical Implication

This claim set supports a central BEBA move: brand equity should not be treated as a static, monolithic outcome or asset, but as an architecture through which brand-related conditions enable stakeholder behaviour.

The strongest contribution is configurational. The reviewed material suggests that brand equity can emerge through multiple pathways, that those pathways are not necessarily symmetrical, and that relational and meaning-based mechanisms may be more theoretically central than simple awareness or reputation effects.

For BEBA, this strengthens the interpretation of nonprofit brand equity as a behaviour-enabling architecture operating under uncertainty, rather than as a single latent construct whose effects can be fully captured through linear additive models.

# Existing Claims Challenged

No direct contradiction to BEBA is evident from the provided material. However, the claim set challenges or qualifies several possible simplified interpretations.

## 1. Challenges a single-pathway model of brand equity

`E-000003` suggests that no single causal path should be assumed as the universal route to high brand equity.

Implication: BEBA should avoid presenting one canonical linear pathway as sufficient across contexts.

## 2. Challenges symmetric causal assumptions

`A-000008` challenges models that assume the predictors of high brand equity also explain low brand equity in reverse.

Implication: BEBA should distinguish enabling configurations from disabling, absent, or failed configurations.

## 3. Qualifies the centrality of awareness and reputation

`BU-000001` notes that awareness and reputation are peripheral rather than core contributors within the Brand Understanding Block.

Implication: BEBA should not overstate awareness or reputation as primary behavioural mechanisms unless supported by nonprofit-specific evidence.

## 4. Challenges purely additive measurement logic

`BT-000008`, `E-000003`, and `A-000008` suggest configurational causality.

Implication: BEBA should preserve space for configurational model competition alongside SEM-style linear modelling.

# Boundary Conditions

Several boundary conditions should be retained because the evidence is not automatically generalisable to BEBA’s nonprofit context.

## 1. Sector boundary

The reviewed claim set concerns consumer-based brand equity and consumers’ favourite brands. It may not generalise directly to nonprofit stakeholders, donors, volunteers, beneficiaries, members, or advocacy publics.

## 2. Valence boundary

The findings concern favourite brands. Brand mechanisms may differ for:

- unfamiliar brands
- neutral brands
- low-trust brands
- controversial nonprofits
- emergency-response organisations
- advocacy organisations with polarising missions

## 3. Behavioural boundary

The reviewed claims concern high overall consumer-based brand equity. They do not directly establish effects on specific nonprofit behaviours such as:

- donating
- volunteering
- recurring giving
- advocacy
- membership
- compliance
- beneficiary engagement
- word-of-mouth

## 4. Methodological boundary

The sequence described in `CB-000006` is modelled from cross-sectional evidence. It should not be treated as directly observed temporal development.

## 5. Cultural and market boundary

The tested context involved consumers’ favourite brands in urban centres in the United Kingdom. Cross-cultural, sectoral, and organisational generalisation remains uncertain.

## 6. Configurational boundary

The fs/QCA-based findings imply causal configurations but should not be translated into simple additive linear effects without further evidence.

# Model Competition Implications

This claim set has substantial implications for BEBA model competition.

## 1. Linear latent-variable models are insufficient alone

The evidence does not invalidate SEM/CFA approaches, but it suggests that additive models may miss configurational effects.

BEBA should therefore not rely exclusively on models where brand equity dimensions are assumed to have independent, symmetric, additive effects.

## 2. Configurational models become theoretically relevant

The claims support considering fs/QCA or other configurational approaches as competing or complementary models.

Relevant claims:

- `E-000003`
- `A-000008`
- `BT-000008`

## 3. Sequential architecture models remain plausible but need temporal caution

`CB-000006` and `BE-000025` support a staged architecture, but because the evidence is cross-sectional, BEBA should treat sequence as theoretical or modelled rather than empirically demonstrated over time.

## 4. Separate models may be needed for success and failure

`A-000008` implies that high behavioural effectiveness and low behavioural effectiveness should not automatically be modelled as opposite poles of the same explanation.

## 5. Nonprofit-specific model competition is required

The reviewed consumer-brand evidence should be treated as input for candidate theory, not as final evidence for nonprofit BEBA. Competing nonprofit models should test whether the same mechanisms hold for donors, volunteers, beneficiaries, and other stakeholder groups.
