# Core Theoretical Implication

Warm glow should be integrated into BEBA as a **candidate non-brand motivational mechanism** that can make charitable behaviour rewarding independently of perceived beneficiary impact or nonprofit brand equity.

Its strongest implication is that nonprofit behaviour cannot be explained only through:

- perceived impact,
- altruistic concern,
- brand attitude,
- trust,
- identification,
- or donation intention.

Instead, some giving may be driven by the donor’s own affective, self-image, or social-image reward from the act of giving itself.

For BEBA, this matters because warm glow creates a possible **behavioural activation route that does not require brand equity as the primary explanatory source**. A donor may give because giving feels good, confirms moral identity, or produces social-image benefits, even when the nonprofit brand itself is weakly differentiated.

Therefore, warm glow should not be absorbed into brand equity. It should be treated as a competing, complementary, or moderating motivational pathway.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Challenge to brand-equity primacy

Warm glow creates a possible route to giving that does not require nonprofit brand equity as the main cause.

Relevant claims:

- WG-C01
- WG-C03
- WG-C05
- WG-C27

If donors give because the act of giving itself is rewarding, then brand equity may be:

- a facilitator,
- a cue,
- a boundary condition,
- a trust filter,
- or a target-selection mechanism,

but not necessarily the primary motivational source.

## 2. Challenge to impact-centred explanations of giving

Relevant claims:

- WG-C02
- WG-C03
- WG-C04
- WG-C09

Warm glow challenges any BEBA formulation that treats perceived impact or perceived beneficiary benefit as sufficient for explaining charitable behaviour.

## 3. Challenge to undifferentiated affective constructs

Relevant claims:

- WG-C12
- WG-C13
- WG-C14
- WG-C15
- WG-C23

The reviewed material suggests that private affect, self-image, social image, and prestige may be empirically entangled but conceptually distinct. BEBA should not collapse them into one construct without measurement justification.

## 4. Challenge to linear communication-effect assumptions

Relevant claims:

- WG-C16
- WG-C17
- WG-C21

Nudges, donor-benefit framing, and cause-related claims do not automatically increase motivation. Poorly designed interventions may crowd out motivation or trigger skepticism.

## 5. Challenge to assuming generalisability across giving contexts

Relevant claims:

- WG-C19
- WG-C28
- WG-C29

Warm glow may not operate identically in religious giving, repeated giving, Austrian contexts, or obligation-driven charitable systems.

# Model Competition Implications

Warm glow has substantial implications for BEBA model competition.

## 1. Brand equity model versus warm-glow model

A brand-equity-centred model would explain donation intention through brand awareness, brand trust, brand associations, perceived quality, authenticity, or identification.

A warm-glow model would explain donation behaviour through private emotional utility, self-image reward, or social-image reward from giving.

These models may compete where donors give despite weak brand differentiation.

## 2. Impact model versus warm-glow model

An impact model predicts that giving increases when donors believe their contribution produces beneficiary benefit.

A warm-glow model predicts that giving may persist even when marginal impact is low or absent.

This is especially relevant to claims WG-C02 and WG-C03.

## 3. Altruism model versus impure-altruism model

Pure altruism predicts sensitivity to total charitable output. Impure altruism predicts continued giving because donors value the act of giving itself.

Relevant claims:

- WG-C01
- WG-C02
- WG-C04
- WG-C11

## 4. Trust/authenticity model versus skepticism-suppression model

Brand trust and authenticity may enable warm glow by making giving credible. However, skepticism may suppress warm-glow responses.

Relevant claims:

- WG-C17
- WG-C21
- WG-C25
- WG-C26

## 5. Participation model versus intensity model

Self-image may primarily affect whether people give, while social image may affect how much existing donors give.

Relevant claims:

- WG-C12
- WG-C13
- WG-C15

This implies that BEBA should avoid treating donation intention, donation participation, donation amount, and repeated giving as interchangeable behavioural outcomes.

## 6. Acquisition versus retention model

Warm glow may operate differently in first-time giving and repeated giving, but this is not sufficiently covered.

Relevant claim:

- WG-C28

This creates a potential future distinction between:

- warm glow as acquisition trigger;
- warm glow as retention reinforcement;
- warm glow as habituating affect;
- brand commitment as longer-term stabiliser.

# Recommendation

## Recommended integration status

Integrate warm glow as a **candidate motivational mechanism and competing explanation**, not as a canonical BEBA construct yet.

## Recommended theoretical handling

Warm glow should be used to sharpen BEBA’s central question:

> How do nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty?

Warm glow suggests one answer:

> Nonprofit brands may become behaviourally effective not only by reducing uncertainty about impact or trustworthiness, but also by making giving feel emotionally, morally, or socially rewarding for the donor.

However, current evidence does not yet justify the stronger claim that nonprofit brand equity causes warm glow or that warm glow mediates the brand-equity-to-donation-intention pathway.

## Recommended caution for manuscript use

Use warm glow in manuscript text only with candidate-level caution until source verification is complete.

Safe formulation:

> Prior literature on impure altruism suggests that charitable giving may generate private emotional utility for donors, sometimes independently of the donation’s marginal impact. In BEBA, this indicates a potential non-brand motivational pathway that may complement or compete with brand-equity-based explanations of donation behaviour.

Avoid stronger formulations such as:

> Brand equity increases warm glow.

or:

> Warm glow mediates the effect of nonprofit brand equity on donation intention.

Those are currently **not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature**.
