# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set strengthens BEBA’s interpretation of nonprofit brand equity as a layered psychological and behavioural architecture rather than as a single evaluative asset.

The most important theoretical contribution is the distinction between:

1. **Foundational cognitive availability**  
   Brand awareness, categorization, and associative brand knowledge make the nonprofit brand identifiable and mentally usable.

2. **Psychological meaning formation**  
   Stakeholders identify, experience, integrate, signify, and connect with the brand through multiple processes.

3. **Depth of engagement**  
   Engagement may remain object-centered, become self-centered, or become socially embedded.

4. **Behavioural enablement under uncertainty**  
   Brands may reduce uncertainty by functioning as informational cues, identity signals, and cultural or communal symbols.

For BEBA, this supports a processual architecture: nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective not merely by being known or evaluated positively, but by becoming meaningful, identity-relevant, and socially actionable under uncertainty.

# Existing Claims Challenged

No direct contradiction to BEBA is evident from the provided material. However, the reviewed claims create several cautions for BEBA.

## 1. Challenge to linear funnel assumptions

Challenged by:

- `BR-000006`

If brand-related psychological processes do not necessarily occur in a fixed sequence, BEBA should avoid a rigid progression such as:

`awareness → knowledge → attitude → attachment → behaviour`

Instead, BEBA should allow recursive, parallel, or context-dependent pathways.

## 2. Challenge to universal construct structures

Challenged by:

- `BP-000011`

The cultural variability of brand personality structures cautions against assuming that nonprofit brand meanings are universally stable across societies, countries, or cultural groups.

## 3. Challenge to brand-centric causal explanations

Challenged by:

- `BE-000038`

If stakeholder characteristics moderate the salience of engagement levels, BEBA should not attribute behaviour only to brand architecture. Behavioural effectiveness may depend on person-brand-context fit.

## 4. Challenge to simple brand-equity-as-asset models

Challenged by:

- `BR-000007`
- `BR-000005`
- `BE-000036`

The reviewed claims imply that brand equity cannot be adequately explained as a single accumulated asset. It requires differentiation between knowledge, experience, signification, connection, attachment, community, and behaviour.

# Boundary Conditions

The reviewed claims introduce several boundary conditions for BEBA.

## 1. Cultural Context

Brand personality and symbolic brand meaning may not generalize across cultures.

Implication: BEBA should distinguish between potentially general brand mechanisms and culturally specific brand meanings.

## 2. Stakeholder Self-Construal

Stakeholders with different self-construals may engage with nonprofit brands differently.

Possible implication:

- independent self-construal may strengthen self-centered engagement
- interdependent self-construal may strengthen social or communal engagement

This requires empirical validation before being treated as canonical.

## 3. General Brand Engagement Tendency

Some stakeholders may be more disposed to engage with brands as meaningful objects than others.

Implication: nonprofit brand effectiveness may depend partly on stakeholder receptivity to brands as psychological or social objects.

## 4. Engagement Depth

Object-centered engagement may support evaluation or preference, while self-centered and social engagement may be more relevant for stronger behavioural forms such as volunteering, advocacy, identification, or long-term commitment.

## 5. Process Non-Linearity

BEBA should not assume that all stakeholders move through the same psychological pathway. Some may begin with experience, others with social connection, identity relevance, or informational trust.

## 6. Evidence Status

Several claims have medium or low confidence and derive from an integrative theoretical framework rather than fully validated empirical taxonomies.

Implication: these should remain candidate-level until supported by additional literature or empirical outputs.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed claim set affects BEBA model competition in important ways.

## 1. Linear Hierarchy Models Are Weakened

Models assuming a fixed hierarchy from awareness to attitude to behaviour are challenged by `BR-000006`.

BEBA should compare linear, recursive, and parallel-process models.

## 2. Attitude-Only Models Are Insufficient

`BC-000012` implies that attitude captures only object-centered engagement. Stronger behavioural effects may require attachment or community.

This weakens models where brand attitude is treated as the main or sole mediator of brand-enabled behaviour.

## 3. Knowledge-Structure Models Are Strengthened

`BK-000005` strengthens models where brand equity operates through associative stakeholder knowledge.

This supports BEBA models that treat brand equity as structured cognition rather than as a unidimensional perception.

## 4. Signification Models Are Strengthened

`BS-000007` supports models in which brands enable behaviour by reducing uncertainty through cues, identity signals, and cultural symbols.

This is especially relevant for nonprofit contexts, where stakeholders often act under moral, informational, and outcome uncertainty.

## 5. Social-Community Models Are Strengthened

`BC-000013` supports competing models in which behaviour is enabled not only through individual cognition but also through social embedding and community interpretation.

This may be especially relevant for volunteering, advocacy, movement participation, and peer-based donation behaviour.

## 6. Moderator Models Become Necessary

`BE-000038` implies that BEBA should test or theorize moderated pathways. Brand mechanisms may differ across stakeholder groups.

Possible moderators include:

- self-construal
- prior engagement disposition
- cultural context
- stakeholder role
- uncertainty level
- cause involvement

Only moderators supported by reviewed literature or empirical outputs should be formalized.

The claim set should be integrated into BEBA as candidate-level support for the individual and social mechanisms of brand-enabled behaviour.

## Use for BEBA Mechanism Development

This material is especially useful for developing the following BEBA mechanisms:

1. **Cognitive availability mechanism**  
   Awareness, categorization, and associative knowledge make the nonprofit brand mentally available.

2. **Meaning-formation mechanism**  
   Identifying, experiencing, integrating, signifying, and connecting make the brand psychologically meaningful.

3. **Uncertainty-reduction mechanism**  
   Brands work as cues, identity signals, and cultural symbols.

4. **Engagement-depth mechanism**  
   Behavioural effectiveness depends on whether engagement remains object-centered or deepens into self-centered or social forms.

5. **Collective-action mechanism**  
   Social engagement and brand community may enable nonprofit brands to coordinate behaviour beyond individual preference.

BEBA should avoid representing Schmitt’s five psychological processes as a fixed sequence. A candidate architecture should allow multiple pathways into behaviour.
