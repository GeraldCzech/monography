# Input Reviewed

**Object type reviewed:** Claim set / theory-integration input  
**Concept focus:** Donation Intention and related antecedents/extensions  
**Reviewed candidate claims:**

- **DI-000002** — Donation intention is positively predicted by attitude toward donating, perceived norms, and perceived behavioural control.
- **MN-000001** — Moral norms improve explanation beyond subjective social norms.
- **IB-000003** — Donation intention predicts donation behaviour but does not fully explain actual donation behaviour.
- **NT-000001** — Trust in a charitable organization is a distinct positive antecedent of donation intention.
- **PD-000001** — Past donation behaviour improves explanation of future donation intention and behaviour.

- **PT-000001** — In digital donation contexts, platform trust predicts donation intention in addition to organizational trust.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The claim set supports a view of donation intention as a central but insufficient behavioural mechanism in BEBA.

It confirms that nonprofit brands should not be theorised merely as image or awareness assets. Instead, they can become behaviourally effective by shaping the motivational, normative, trust-based, and control-related conditions under which individuals form and enact donation intentions.

However, the claim set also qualifies BEBA’s architecture in three important ways:

1. **Donation intention is not equivalent to donation behaviour.**  
   BEBA must preserve the distinction between intention formation and behavioural enactment.

2. **Brand equity does not act alone.**  
   Trust, moral obligation, perceived behavioural control, cultural context, platform trust, and past behaviour may enable, strengthen, weaken, or bypass brand effects.

3. **Uncertainty is central.**  
   Trust in the nonprofit organization and, in digital settings, trust in the platform operate as uncertainty-reduction mechanisms that help convert favourable dispositions into donation intention.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## Primary layer: Individual

Most claims affect the **Individual** layer because they concern psychological antecedents of donation intention and behaviour:

- Attitude toward donating
- Subjective/social norms
- Moral norms
- Perceived behavioural control
- Trust in the nonprofit organization
- Past donation behaviour
- Donation intention
- Donation behaviour

## Secondary layer: Societal

**CC-000002** affects the **Societal** layer because it introduces cultural context as a moderator of intention-based donation models.

## Secondary layer: Technological / Infrastructure-relevant context

**PT-000001** implies that BEBA may need a distinct digital-context or infrastructure-sensitive component, especially for online giving and crowdfunding. Platform trust should not be collapsed into nonprofit brand trust.

# Existing Claims Supported

The reviewed claim set supports several core BEBA assumptions.

## 1. Behaviour before brands

The claims reinforce that nonprofit brand equity should be embedded in a behavioural architecture rather than treated as a standalone marketing outcome.

Donation intention is shaped by behavioural determinants such as attitude, perceived norms, perceived behavioural control, moral norms, trust, and past behaviour. This supports BEBA’s principle that brand effects must be explained through behaviourally meaningful mechanisms.

## 2. Brands as behavioural enablers under uncertainty

**NT-000001** supports modelling the nonprofit brand as a trust-generating mechanism.

If donors face uncertainty about organizational competence, integrity, use of funds, or beneficiary impact, nonprofit brand trust may help reduce perceived risk and support donation intention.

## 3. Intention is necessary but insufficient

**IB-000003** supports BEBA’s focus on the intention-behaviour gap.

This strengthens the argument that BEBA should not stop at predicting donation intention. The architecture must explain how intentions become enacted giving, especially under uncertainty, friction, competing demands, or weak situational cues.

## 4. Internalised obligation matters

**MN-000001** supports the need to distinguish subjective social norms from moral norms.

This is important for BEBA because nonprofit giving is often morally charged. A donor may give not only because others expect it, but because the act is internalised as the right thing to do.

## 5. Behavioural history matters

**PD-000001** supports the inclusion of past donation behaviour as a stabilising or habit-like behavioural mechanism.

This suggests that brand-enabled behaviour may depend partly on prior behavioural traces, donor routines, learned trust, or repeated interaction with the organization.

# Existing Claims Challenged

The claim set does not directly falsify BEBA, but it challenges several possible overextensions.

## 1. Challenge to brand-centric explanations

The evidence cautions against explaining donation intention primarily through brand equity.

Attitude, norms, moral norms, perceived behavioural control, trust, and past behaviour may all predict intention or behaviour. Therefore, BEBA should avoid a model in which nonprofit brand equity is treated as the dominant or universal antecedent of giving.

## 2. Challenge to intention-only models

**IB-000003** challenges any BEBA formulation that treats donation intention as the final behavioural outcome.

Donation intention should be modelled as an intermediate behavioural state, not as equivalent to donation behaviour.

## 3. Challenge to universal model stability

**CC-000002** challenges the assumption that standard intention-based models operate similarly across cultural settings.

BEBA should not assume that brand-enabled pathways are culturally invariant. Cultural context may alter the strength or salience of attitude, norms, control, moral obligation, and trust.

## 4. Challenge to single-trust models

**PT-000001** challenges any model that collapses all trust into nonprofit brand trust.

In digital donation contexts, platform trust may operate independently from organizational trust. A donor may trust the nonprofit but not the payment platform, crowdfunding platform, or digital intermediary.

## Candidate Claim 3: Moral norm as internalised behavioural pressure

**BEBA relevance:**  
Prevents BEBA from reducing normative influence to external social pressure.

**Statement:**  
Past donation behaviour contributes to future donation intention and behaviour by capturing behavioural continuity, familiarity, learned trust, or habit-like repetition.

## Candidate Claim 5: Platform trust as digital giving condition

**Statement:**

**BEBA relevance:**  
Adds a digital infrastructure condition to brand-enabled giving.

## Candidate Claim 6: Cultural context as pathway moderator

**Statement:**  
The relative predictive strength of attitude, norms, perceived behavioural control, moral norms, trust, and brand-related mechanisms varies across cultural contexts.

**BEBA relevance:**  
Introduces cultural boundary conditions for BEBA’s behavioural pathways.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Intention-behaviour gap

Donation intention does not automatically produce donation behaviour.

BEBA must account for conditions under which intention fails to translate into giving, including friction, uncertainty, lack of opportunity, weak perceived control, competing priorities, or insufficient trust.

## 2. Digital donation context

Platform trust becomes relevant specifically in online giving, crowdfunding, and digitally mediated donation environments.

This boundary condition implies that the behavioural architecture may differ between offline and online donation contexts.

## 3. Cultural context

The reviewed material suggests that standard intention-based donation models may be less stable across collectivist, developing, or non-Western contexts.

The claim is currently low-confidence and should remain a candidate boundary condition rather than a canonical BEBA assumption.

## 4. Prior donor experience

Past donation behaviour may condition future giving. BEBA should therefore distinguish between:

- first-time donors,
- occasional donors,
- repeated donors,
- habitual or committed donors.

Brand effects may differ across these behavioural histories.

## 5. Moral salience of the cause

Moral norms may be especially important when the cause is perceived as ethically urgent, personally relevant, or socially consequential.

This is not directly established in the provided claim set but follows as a plausible candidate boundary condition requiring further evidence.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed claims strengthen the need for competing explanatory models within BEBA.

## 1. Standard intention model

A baseline model would include:

- attitude toward donating,
- subjective norms,
- perceived behavioural control,
- donation intention,
- donation behaviour.

This reflects the foundational behavioural scaffold in **DI-000002**.

## 2. Moral norm extension model

A competing or extended model would add:

- moral norm,
- possibly separated from subjective social norm.

This is supported by **MN-000001**.

## 3. Trust-augmented BEBA model

A BEBA-specific model would add:

- trust in the nonprofit organization,
- nonprofit brand trust,
- perceived uncertainty reduction.

This is supported by **NT-000001** and is particularly central to BEBA’s core research question.

## 4. Past-behaviour continuity model

Another competing model would include:

- past donation behaviour,
- future donation intention,
- future donation behaviour.

This is supported by **PD-000001** and may compete with or qualify brand-based explanations.

## 5. Digital trust infrastructure model

For online giving contexts, the model should distinguish:

- trust in nonprofit organization,
- trust in platform,
- donation intention,
- possibly perceived transaction security or digital confidence.

This is supported by **PT-000001**.

## 6. Culturally moderated model

A further model would test whether intention-based pathways vary by cultural context.

This is suggested by **CC-000002**, but the current confidence level is low.

# Recommendation

## Theory integration recommendation

Integrate the reviewed claim set into BEBA as an **individual-level behavioural intention architecture**, but do not treat donation intention as the final behavioural endpoint.

The claims should be used to refine BEBA in the following way:

1. **Position donation intention as a central intermediate mechanism.**  
   It is a key behavioural state through which nonprofit brands may influence giving, but it is not equivalent to donation behaviour.

2. **Model nonprofit brand trust as an uncertainty-reduction mechanism.**  
   Trust is one of the clearest bridges between nonprofit brand equity and behavioural effectiveness under uncertainty.

3. **Separate moral norms from subjective norms.**  
   BEBA should distinguish internalised moral obligation from perceived external social pressure.

4. **Include past donation behaviour as a behavioural continuity mechanism.**  
   This prevents BEBA from over-attributing repeated giving to current brand perceptions alone.

5. **Treat platform trust as a distinct construct in digital giving.**  
   In online contexts, brand trust may be insufficient if the technological donation infrastructure is not trusted.

6. **Keep cultural context as a candidate boundary condition.**  
   The evidence is currently low-confidence and should not yet be made canonical.

## Suggested theory status

The integration should be treated as a **candidate theory-integration object**, not yet as a canonical ontology update.
