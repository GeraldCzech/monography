# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set supports an identity-based refinement of BEBA at the individual layer.

Nonprofit brand equity dimensions may become behaviourally effective through different behavioural-enablement routes:

1. **Direct behavioural cue route**  
   Brand personality, brand image, and brand awareness may directly influence giving intention.

2. **Identity/self-concept route**  
   Brand personality and brand awareness may influence giving intention partly by activating or aligning with the donor’s self-concept.

3. **Non-identity signalling route**  
   Brand image may influence giving intention directly, but not necessarily through self-concept.

This is theoretically important because it suggests that “brand equity” is not a single uniform behavioural mechanism. Different brand dimensions may enable behaviour through different psychological pathways.

# Existing Claims Challenged

The reviewed claim set qualifies or potentially challenges any BEBA formulation that treats nonprofit brand equity dimensions as functionally equivalent.

Specifically:

1. **Challenge to a uniform brand equity mechanism assumption**  
   If BEBA currently implies that all nonprofit brand equity dimensions operate through the same psychological route, this evidence challenges that position.

2. **Challenge to a general brand-image-as-identity assumption**  
   `NB-000044` and `NB-000047` suggest that nonprofit brand image may not activate donor self-concept, at least in the reviewed empirical context.

3. **Challenge to treating awareness as purely cognitive salience**  
   `NB-000043` and `NB-000046` suggest that awareness may also have identity-related implications, not only availability or recall effects.

However, these challenges should remain provisional because the evidence is cross-sectional, context-specific, and based on intention rather than observed giving.

# Boundary Conditions

The following boundary conditions should be attached to this integration:

1. **Behavioural outcome boundary**  
   The evidence concerns giving intention, not observed donation behaviour.

2. **Design boundary**  
   The evidence is based on cross-sectional survey data. Causal interpretation should remain cautious.

3. **Cultural/contextual boundary**  
   Evidence is based on data from China. Generalisability to other nonprofit markets, welfare regimes, religious contexts, or philanthropic cultures is not established in the supplied material.

4. **Operationalisation boundary**  
   The non-significant effect of nonprofit brand image on self-concept may depend on how brand image and self-concept were operationalised.

5. **Construct-level boundary**  
   “Nonprofit brand equity” should not be treated as a homogeneous construct in this evidence set. Effects differ across brand personality, brand awareness, and brand image.
