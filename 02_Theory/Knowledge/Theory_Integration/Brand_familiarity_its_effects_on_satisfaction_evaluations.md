# Input Reviewed

Candidate claim set from `REVIEW-000048` concerning **Brand Familiarity**, **Satisfaction**, **Expectations**, **Perceived Performance**, and **Expectancy-Disconfirmation**, based on sources `SRC-000229` to `SRC-000233`.

Core reviewed claims:

- Brand familiarity moderates expectation–performance disconfirmation effects on satisfaction.
- Low familiarity strengthens the role of disconfirmation and post-consumption expectation updating.
- High familiarity strengthens the satisfaction → behavioural intention pathway.
- Perceived performance, satisfaction, expectations, and expectancy-disconfirmation remain foundational individual-level mechanisms.
- Familiarity is treated as an accumulated direct and indirect experience-based knowledge resource.

Evidence context: restaurant-service setting, longitudinal survey data, structural equation modeling, with limitations for direct transfer to nonprofit contexts.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set strengthens BEBA’s individual-level explanation of how brands become behaviourally effective under uncertainty by showing that **brand familiarity changes the cognitive pathway through which experiences become behavioural intentions**.

The central implication is that familiarity does not merely increase positive brand evaluation. It restructures behavioural processing:

- Under **low familiarity**, stakeholders appear to rely more heavily on expectation comparison, performance disconfirmation, and post-experience learning.
- Under **high familiarity**, stakeholders rely less on single-encounter disconfirmation and more directly translate satisfaction into behavioural intention.

For BEBA, this suggests that brand-enabled behaviour can operate through at least two individual-level routes:

1. **Novice learning route**  
   Experience → disconfirmation → satisfaction → revised expectations → behavioural intention

2. **Familiarity-enabled shortcut route**  
   Experience/satisfaction → behavioural intention, with reduced dependence on effortful disconfirmation processing

This is theoretically important because it links brand familiarity to uncertainty reduction, learning, and behavioural readiness.

# Existing Claims Challenged

No direct contradiction to BEBA is introduced, but the claim set qualifies several likely BEBA assumptions.

## 1. Expectation-disconfirmation is not uniformly central

The claims challenge any simple model in which expectation-disconfirmation is always the dominant route from experience to satisfaction.

Relevant claims:

- `BF-000011`
- `E-000006`
- `BF-000013`

The disconfirmation route appears stronger under low familiarity and weaker under high familiarity.

Implication: BEBA should avoid treating disconfirmation as a universal mechanism. It is conditionally important.

## 2. Satisfaction does not always translate equally into behavioural intention

Relevant claim:

- `S-000009`

The satisfaction → behavioural intention relationship is stronger under high familiarity than low familiarity.

Implication: Satisfaction alone may be insufficient to produce behavioural readiness among unfamiliar stakeholders. Additional mechanisms such as expectation updating, trust formation, legitimacy, or risk reduction may be needed.

## 3. Familiarity is not merely awareness

Relevant claim:

- `BF-000012`

Brand familiarity is rooted in accumulated direct and indirect experience.

Implication: BEBA should distinguish familiarity from simple brand awareness, recognition, or salience. Familiarity has a processual and experiential basis.

# Boundary Conditions

Several boundary conditions should be recorded.

## 1. Sector boundary

The reviewed evidence comes from a restaurant-service context. Transfer to nonprofit brands is theoretically plausible but not empirically established by the reviewed material.

Nonprofit-specific mechanisms such as mission identification, moral trust, perceived legitimacy, donor confidence, beneficiary vulnerability, and value congruence may alter the pathways.

## 2. Experience-dominant service boundary

The claims may be strongest in settings where stakeholders evaluate the brand through direct service encounters.

Applicability may be weaker for:

- purely symbolic nonprofit brands,
- advocacy organizations,
- emergency relief brands,
- low-contact donation contexts,
- institutional funder relationships.

## 3. Familiarity level boundary

The claims explicitly depend on stakeholder familiarity.

BEBA should distinguish:

- unfamiliar stakeholders,
- moderately familiar stakeholders,
- highly familiar stakeholders,
- experientially familiar but attitudinally ambivalent stakeholders.

## 4. Measurement boundary

Tam operationalised brand familiarity with a single self-reported item, according to the provided notes.

This limits construct precision. BEBA should not treat this as sufficient for canonical measurement without further validation.

## 5. Causal boundary

Some evidence is correlational or SEM-based. The reviewed material should not be overinterpreted as proving causal mechanisms.

## 6. Stakeholder-type boundary

The evidence concerns customers. In nonprofit contexts, behavioural intentions may include:

- donating,
- volunteering,
- recommending,
- advocating,
- re-engaging,
- complying,
- participating,
- trusting,
- granting legitimacy.

The pathway may differ by stakeholder role.

# Model Competition Implications

The claim set has important implications for competing BEBA models.

## 1. Supports a moderated mechanism model

Brand familiarity should be considered as a moderator of the pathways from:

- expectations to satisfaction,
- disconfirmation to satisfaction,
- satisfaction to behavioural intention,
- post-consumption expectations to behavioural intention.

This supports BEBA models that treat behavioural effectiveness as conditional rather than linear.

## 2. Challenges simple direct brand equity models

A model where brand equity directly produces behavioural intention may be underspecified.

The reviewed claims suggest that the path from brand knowledge to behaviour depends on familiarity and experience-processing mechanisms.

## 3. Supports competing pathway architecture

BEBA should allow at least two competing or complementary routes:

### Route A: Expectation-disconfirmation learning route

Most relevant under low familiarity.

```text
Prior expectations
→ perceived performance
→ disconfirmation
→ satisfaction
→ updated expectations
→ behavioural intention
```

### Route B: Familiarity-enabled satisfaction shortcut

Most relevant under high familiarity.

```text
Accumulated familiarity
→ satisfaction evaluation
→ behavioural intention
```

## 4. Introduces possible bypass route around brand equity

Yes, the claim set suggests a partial bypass route.

For low-familiarity stakeholders, behavioural intention may be shaped less by established brand equity and more by:

- perceived performance,
- disconfirmation,
- post-consumption expectation updating.

This means that behaviour can emerge through experiential learning even before strong brand equity exists.

However, this is not a complete bypass of brand mechanisms. Rather, it may describe how brand equity begins to form through experience.

## 5. Supports dynamic rather than static brand equity models

The claims support treating brand equity as dynamic and recursively updated through stakeholder experience.

This is aligned with BEBA’s behavioural architecture logic.

# Recommendation

## Integrate as candidate theory support, not as canonical BEBA claim yet

The claim set should be integrated into BEBA as a **candidate individual-level mechanism** because it supports the architecture of uncertainty reduction, experience-based learning, and behavioural readiness. However, it should not yet be treated as canonical for nonprofit brands because the empirical context is commercial restaurant service.

## Recommended theoretical integration

Create or update a candidate theory note around:

**Brand Familiarity as a Moderator of Behavioural Architecture**

Core proposition:

> Brand familiarity changes how stakeholders convert experience into behavioural readiness. Under low familiarity, behavioural intention depends more on expectation-disconfirmation and expectation updating. Under high familiarity, satisfaction becomes a stronger behavioural shortcut.

## Recommended ontology candidates

Add or maintain candidate entries for:

1. **Brand Familiarity**
2. **Familiarity-Enabled Behavioural Shortcut**
3. **Novice Learning Loop**
4. **Expectation Updating**
5. **Expectancy-Disconfirmation Pathway**

## Recommended BEBA model implication

BEBA should represent brand familiarity as a moderator that shifts stakeholder processing between:

- a **learning-heavy route** under uncertainty,
- and a **shortcut/reinforcement route** under familiarity.

## Recommended caution

Do not generalize directly from customer repurchase and word-of-mouth to nonprofit stakeholder behaviours without additional evidence. For nonprofit brands, the model should be tested or theoretically compared against alternative mechanisms such as trust, moral identification, legitimacy, perceived impact, and mission fit.

## Bottom line

This claim set strengthens BEBA by showing that brand familiarity is not merely an antecedent of positive evaluation. It is a **behavioural architecture condition** that determines whether stakeholders process brand experience through learning, disconfirmation, expectation updating, or satisfaction-based shortcuts.
