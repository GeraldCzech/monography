# Input Reviewed

The input consists of a set of six related claims (BE-000056, BI-000043, BM-000005, BR-000012, BR-000013, BE-000057) derived from an empirical study of a nonprofit, health-promotion organization (ParticipACTION in Canada). These claims collectively test and affirm the validity of Keller's Brand Equity Pyramid (CBBE model) in this context. The key findings are:
1.  The overall sequential structure (Identity → Meaning → Responses → Resonance) holds.
2.  Each step in the sequence is a statistically significant positive predictor of the next.
3.  The final stage, Brand Resonance, positively predicts behavioural intentions.
4.  The influence of early stages on the final stage is fully mediated by the intermediate steps, highlighting the necessity of the complete sequence.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The core implication is that a sequential, hierarchical brand-building process, as articulated by the CBBE model, is not just a commercial-sector phenomenon but represents a viable and empirically supported mechanism for building a **Brand-Enabled Behaviour Architecture** in a nonprofit context. It strongly suggests that for a nonprofit brand to influence stakeholder behaviour, it must guide stakeholders through a structured psychological journey from basic awareness to a deep, resonant relationship. Skipping steps is not only inefficient but may be ineffective, as demonstrated by the full mediation finding.

# BEBA Layer Affected

-   **Organisational Layer:** Claim BE-000056 validates the use of the pyramid as a strategic model for the nonprofit organization.
-   **Individual Layer:** Claims BI-000043, BM-000005, BR-000012, and BE-000057 describe the cognitive and affective processing sequence within the individual stakeholder. This is the core psychological mechanism of the architecture.
-   **Behavioural Layer:** Claim BR-000013 directly connects the psychological state of Brand Resonance (Individual Layer) to behavioural intentions, thus bridging to the Behavioural Layer.

# Existing Claims Supported

This body of evidence provides strong support for the central tenets of BEBA, specifically:
-   Claims that brand equity is a multi-stage, developmental construct.
-   Claims that brand equity serves as a causal mechanism linking organizational brand strategy to individual behavioural outcomes.
-   Claims positioning higher-order brand relationships (i.e., Resonance) as the most proximate psychological drivers of desired behaviours.

# Existing Claims Challenged

No core BEBA claims are directly challenged. However, this evidence challenges any simpler, non-hierarchical, or "direct effect" models that might exist as competing explanations within BEBA. Specifically, it challenges the notion that lower-level brand constructs like awareness or identity can directly influence complex behavioural outcomes without the mediating work of building meaning and fostering positive responses.

# New Candidate Claims

Based on this synthesis, the following refined claims should be considered for formal inclusion in the BEBA theory:

1.  **C-BEBA-SEQ-01:** The influence of a nonprofit brand on stakeholder behavioural intention is mediated by a sequential psychological progression from (1) Identity Salience, to (2) Brand Meaning, to (3) Positive Brand Responses, to (4) Brand Resonance.
2.  **C-BEBA-MED-01:** The effect of Brand Identity on Brand Resonance is fully mediated by the intervening stages of Brand Meaning and Brand Responses, making the complete architectural sequence a necessary condition for building a behaviourally effective brand relationship.
3.  **C-BEBA-LINK-01:** In the nonprofit context, Brand Resonance is a direct and positive predictor of stakeholder intentions to adopt brand-advocated behaviours.

# Boundary Conditions

The applicability of these claims is subject to several important boundary conditions derived from the source study:
-   **Context:** The evidence comes from a single nonprofit in the **health-promotion** sector. Its applicability to other nonprofit sectors (e.g., advocacy, humanitarian aid, arts) needs further testing.
-   **Brand Maturity:** The study subject (ParticipACTION) is a **well-established national brand**. The model may not apply in the same way to new, niche, or low-awareness nonprofits.
-   **Data Structure:** The findings are based on **cross-sectional survey data**, which establishes correlation and supports the hypothesised causal sequence but does not prove causation over time.
-   **Outcome Measure:** The dependent variable was **self-reported behavioural intention**, not objectively observed behaviour. The intention-behaviour gap remains a potential moderator.

# Model Competition Implications

This evidence significantly strengthens the position of **Sequential Hierarchical Models** (like Keller's CBBE) within the BEBA framework. It provides a strong empirical benchmark against which competing models must be compared. Specifically:
-   It disadvantages **"Direct Effect" models** that posit a direct link from Brand Awareness to Behaviour.
-   It challenges **"Independent Contributor" models** where different brand equity dimensions (e.g., imagery, quality) are assumed to influence behaviour in parallel rather than in a structured sequence.
-   The full mediation result (BE-000057) provides a specific, falsifiable hypothesis for future model comparisons: any competing model should demonstrate superior fit to the data while accounting for this strong mediation effect.

# Ontology Candidate Implications

This evidence provides strong justification for elevating the following concepts to canonical status within the BEBA ontology for the nonprofit context:
-   `Concept: Brand Identity (or Salience)`
-   `Concept: Brand Meaning`
-   `Concept: Brand Responses`
-   `Concept: Brand Resonance`

Furthermore, the `relationship_type` between these concepts should be defined as `isPrecursorTo` in a strict sequence, reflecting the empirical findings. The ontology should capture the hierarchical structure explicitly.
