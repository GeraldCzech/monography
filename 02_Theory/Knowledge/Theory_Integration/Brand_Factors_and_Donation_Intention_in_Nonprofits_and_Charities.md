Candidate claim set from `REVIEW-000005`, covering the following concepts:

- Brand Image

- Organisational Reputation

Primary behavioural outcome in the reviewed claims: **donation intention**.  
Secondary supportive behaviours mentioned: **volunteering** and **referrals**.  
Important limitation: the reviewed synthesis focuses mainly on **intention**, not verified longitudinal giving behaviour.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The claim set strongly supports the BEBA premise that nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective by reducing uncertainty and enabling supportive behavioural readiness.

However, the evidence does **not** support a simple “strong brand → donation” model. Instead, it suggests that nonprofit brand equity functions through several behavioural mechanisms:

1. **Trust formation**
2. **Positive attitude formation**
3. **Identity or self-concept congruence**
4. **Authenticity perception**
5. **Salience and credibility heuristics**
6. **Behaviour-specific cue relevance**

The integration implication is that BEBA should treat brand equity not as a direct behavioural force, but as a **behaviour-enabling architecture** that operates through mediating mechanisms and under boundary conditions.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## Primary Layer: Individual Layer

Most claims affect the individual donor/supporter layer because they concern:

- perception
- trust
- attitude
- involvement
- identity congruence
- donation intention
- behavioural readiness

## Secondary Layer: Organisational Layer

The organisational layer is affected through:

- organisational reputation
- scandal information
- perceived operational effectiveness
- credibility of the nonprofit actor

## Cross-Layer Implication

The claim set implies a cross-layer mechanism:

```text
Organisational signals / brand architecture
→ individual perception and uncertainty reduction
→ trust, attitude, salience, identification
→ supportive behavioural intention
```

This supports BEBA’s architecture logic: nonprofit brands are not merely communication assets but structured uncertainty-reduction systems connecting organisational characteristics to individual behavioural readiness.

# Existing Claims Supported

Based on the provided material, the following BEBA-type claims are supported.

## 1. Nonprofit brand perceptions can enable behavioural intention under uncertainty

Supported by:

- `BI-000005`
- `BA-000001`
- `BF-000002`

Positive brand image, authenticity, familiarity, and credibility-relevant associations appear to help potential donors form intentions under uncertainty.

## 2. Trust is a central behavioural mechanism

Supported by:

- `TR-000003`

Trust appears as a key mediator between brand perceptions and donation intention. This is highly consistent with BEBA’s uncertainty logic: when donors cannot fully observe nonprofit quality, impact, integrity, or future use of funds, trust becomes a behavioural bridge.

## 3. Attitude formation is a relevant pathway from brand perception to intention

Supported by:

- `AT-000001`

A positive attitude toward the charity mediates the effect of nonprofit brand perceptions on donation intention. This supports a cognition-affect-intention pathway within BEBA.

## 4. Identity congruence can enable pro-social behaviour

Supported by:

- `BP-000001`

Brand personality and donor self-concept congruence suggest that brands may become behaviourally effective by connecting the nonprofit to the donor’s identity, values, or self-expression.

## 5. Familiarity operates as a heuristic, not as a sufficient cause

Supported by:

- `BF-000001`
- `BF-000002`

Brand familiarity increases salience and may enhance perceived credibility, but familiarity alone is insufficient without trust or favourable associations.

## 6. Reputation acts as a boundary condition

Supported by:

- `OR-000001`

Negative organisational reputation can weaken the positive effect of nonprofit brand factors. This supports BEBA’s assumption that brand-enabled behaviour is conditional, not automatic.

## 7. Behaviour-specific modelling is necessary

Supported by:

- `SB-000001`

Different supportive behaviours may depend on different brand dimensions. Monetary donation and volunteering should not be treated as identical behavioural outcomes.

## 8. Donor involvement moderates brand cue effectiveness

Supported by:

- `DI-000001`

Highly involved donors may rely more on attitudes or evaluative judgments, while less-involved donors may rely more on salience, familiarity, or heuristic cues.

# Existing Claims Challenged

The reviewed claim set challenges or qualifies several possible simplified BEBA assumptions.

## 1. Challenged: Brand equity directly produces supportive behaviour

The evidence suggests mediation and moderation rather than a simple direct-effect model.

More defensible formulation:

```text
Nonprofit brand perceptions influence supportive behavioural intention through trust, attitude, authenticity, salience, and identity-related mechanisms, conditional on donor and organisational context.
```

## 2. Challenged: Familiarity is sufficient for donation intention

`BF-000001` explicitly qualifies familiarity. Familiarity may increase consideration but does not reliably increase donation intention without trust or positive associations.

## 3. Challenged: One generic brand-to-support pathway applies to all behaviours

`SB-000001` suggests that monetary donation and volunteering may depend on different brand cues. BEBA should therefore avoid modelling “supportive behaviour” as a single homogeneous outcome unless theoretically justified.

## 4. Challenged: Positive brand factors are robust against negative reputation

`OR-000001` indicates that reputational damage can weaken brand effects. This means brand equity may be fragile under negative organisational information.

## 5. Challenged: Brand image alone explains donor intention

Although `BI-000005` supports brand image as positively associated with donation intention, the broader claim set indicates that image likely works alongside or through trust, attitude, credibility, authenticity, and identity congruence.

# Boundary Conditions

The claim set introduces several important boundary conditions for BEBA.

## 1. Behavioural Outcome Type

The effect of brand dimensions differs depending on whether the outcome is:

- donation intention
- monetary giving
- volunteering
- referral
- broader support intention

BEBA should distinguish between **intention**, **one-time behaviour**, **repeat behaviour**, and **non-monetary support**.

## 2. Donor Involvement

Donor involvement likely changes the cue-processing route.

Possible distinction:

```text
Low involvement
→ familiarity, salience, heuristic credibility

High involvement
→ attitude, trust, value congruence, evaluative judgement
```

## 3. Organisational Reputation

Negative reputation may override or suppress positive brand associations. This is especially important under uncertainty because scandals or low-effectiveness information directly attack the trust mechanism.

## 4. Trust Availability

Where trust is absent, familiarity and image may be insufficient. Trust appears to be a necessary or near-necessary mediator for stronger behavioural intention.

## 5. Evidence Scope: Intention Rather Than Behaviour

The reviewed claims mainly concern intention. Therefore, BEBA should avoid overextending these claims to actual donation behaviour unless verified by empirical pipeline outputs or longitudinal evidence.

# Model Competition Implications

This claim set has strong implications for BEBA model competition.

## 1. Direct Brand Effect Model

Simple model:

```text
Brand Image → Donation Intention
```

Supported only partially. `BI-000005` supports association, but the broader claim set suggests this model is underspecified.

## 2. Trust-Mediated BEBA Model

Model:

```text
Brand Perceptions → Trust → Donation Intention
```

Strongly supported by `TR-000003`. This model is highly consistent with BEBA’s uncertainty-reduction premise.

## 3. Attitude-Mediated Model

Model:

```text
Brand Perceptions → Attitude Toward Charity → Donation Intention
```

Supported by `AT-000001`. This model should compete with or complement the trust-mediated model.

## 4. Dual-Mediation Model

Model:

```text
Brand Perceptions
→ Trust
→ Attitude
→ Donation Intention
```

or:

```text
Brand Perceptions
→ Trust and Attitude in parallel
→ Donation Intention
```

This should be considered as a plausible BEBA candidate model, but the reviewed material does not settle the relative ordering of trust and attitude.

## 5. Identity-Congruence Model

Model:

```text
Brand Personality / Symbolic Meaning
→ Donor-Brand Congruence
→ Donation Intention
```

Supported by `BP-000001`. This model may be particularly relevant for emotionally or value-expressive causes.

## 6. Familiarity-Heuristic Model

Model:

```text
Brand Familiarity
→ Salience / Consideration
→ Perceived Credibility
→ Donation Intention
```

Supported but qualified by `BF-000001` and `BF-000002`. Familiarity should not be modelled as a sufficient direct driver.

## 7. Reputation-Moderated Model

Model:

```text
Brand Perceptions → Trust / Attitude → Donation Intention
                 moderated by Organisational Reputation
```

Supported by `OR-000001`. Negative reputation should be treated as a suppressor or boundary condition.

## 8. Behaviour-Specific Model

Model:

```text
Brand Dimensions → Behaviour-Specific Support Intention
```

with different pathways for:

- donating
- volunteering
- referral
- advocacy
- repeat support

Supported by `SB-000001`.

# Recommendation

## Integration Recommendation

Integrate this claim set into BEBA as a **candidate theory-integration module on individual-level brand-enabled behavioural intention under uncertainty**.

Recommended framing:

```text
Nonprofit brand equity becomes behaviourally effective when brand perceptions reduce uncertainty, generate trust, shape favourable attitudes, provide identity congruence, and increase salience or credibility. These effects are conditional on donor involvement, organisational reputation, and the type of supportive behaviour considered.
```

## Do Not Yet Canonise As Final Theory

The claims should remain candidate-level until:

1. source coverage is verified,
2. construct definitions are checked against the ConstructLedger and glossary,
3. evidence strength is assessed per source rather than only at synthesis level,
4. empirical pipeline outputs are consulted for actual BEBA measurement implications,
5. intention-based claims are separated from behaviour-based claims.
