**Object type:** Theory Integration Synthesis  
**Input:** Candidate claim set from `REVIEW-000006` concerning nonprofit brand image, personality, awareness/salience/familiarity, trust, commitment, digital presence, heritage, incentives, transparency, and self-congruence.

**Main empirical/theoretical focus:** Individual-level donation intention, propensity to donate, contribution likelihood, sustained support, and bequest intention, with selected organisational brand inputs.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set strongly supports the BEBA premise that nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective by shaping individual-level evaluative, cognitive, relational, and uncertainty-reduction mechanisms.

The strongest integration point is that nonprofit brand equity should not be treated as a single attitudinal asset. Instead, the reviewed claims imply a **multi-path behavioural architecture** in which brand perceptions enable behaviour through several distinct routes:

1. **Cognitive availability route**  
   Brand awareness, salience, and familiarity increase the likelihood that the nonprofit enters the donor’s consideration set.

2. **Evaluative image route**  
   Positive brand image and brand personality improve donor evaluations and donation intention.

3. **Uncertainty-reduction route**  
   Brand trust, trustworthiness, transparency, heritage, and digital credibility reduce perceived uncertainty around giving.

4. **Identity-congruence route**  
   Self-congruence links donation to actual or ideal self-expression.

5. **Relational durability route**  
   Brand commitment supports sustained support behaviours rather than only one-time donation intention.

6. **Organisational signal route**  
   Digital brand presence, social media marketing, and brand heritage operate as organisational inputs that shape individual perceptions such as image and trust.

The claims therefore support BEBA as a behavioural theory of nonprofit brand effectiveness under uncertainty, but they also challenge any overly simple trust-only or awareness-only account.

Several claims identify organisational brand inputs that shape individual-level mechanisms.

Affected organisational-level constructs include:

- Digital brand presence
- Social media marketing
- Brand heritage
- Brand-building activities

These organisational inputs do not appear to operate as final behavioural mechanisms themselves. Instead, they influence behaviour through individual perceptions such as trust, image, or transparency.

## Behavioural Outcome Layer

The reviewed claims distinguish several behavioural outcomes:

- Donation intention
- Propensity to donate
- Likelihood of contribution
- Donation of money
- Donation of time
- Sustained support behaviour
- Bequest intention

This matters for BEBA because different behaviours may be enabled by different brand mechanisms. One-time donation intention may depend more on image, familiarity, salience, or trust, while sustained support and bequest intention may depend more on commitment, transparency, and relationship depth.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. A universal trust-centred account is challenged

Challenged by:

- `T-000001`: Perceived trustworthiness is not a significant predictor of propensity to donate when familiarity is accounted for in Katz’s national study.

**Theoretical implication:**  
Trust may not always dominate nonprofit giving decisions. The effect of trust-related constructs may depend on:

- Measurement choice: trust vs. trustworthiness
- Inclusion of familiarity or awareness
- Donor lifecycle stage
- Behavioural outcome
- Study context
- Model specification

This prevents BEBA from adopting a simple claim such as: “trust is always the primary driver of donation behaviour.”

A more precise BEBA-compatible claim would be:

> Trust is a major uncertainty-reduction mechanism in nonprofit brand-enabled behaviour, but its effect depends on measurement, behavioural context, donor familiarity, and competing cognitive accessibility mechanisms.

## 2. Brand personality effects may be motivationally fragile

Challenged or qualified by:

- `IM-000001`: Extrinsic monetary incentives weaken the positive effect of nonprofit brand personality on donation intention, especially when pre-existing trust is high.

**Theoretical implication:**  
Brand-enabled behaviour can be disrupted by external motivational structures. This suggests that brand personality may work partly through intrinsic, expressive, or affective pathways that can be crowded out or weakened by extrinsic incentives.

## 3. Brand salience may not affect all donors equally

Qualified by:

- `BS-000002`: Salience effects are stronger among new donors than established donors.

**Theoretical implication:**  
BEBA should avoid assuming that awareness or salience has uniform behavioural effects across the supporter lifecycle.

# Boundary Conditions

The reviewed claims introduce several important boundary conditions for BEBA.

## 1. Donor lifecycle stage

Relevant claim:

- `BS-000002`

Brand salience appears stronger for new donors than established donors. This implies that BEBA should distinguish between:

- New donors
- Familiar but inactive potential donors
- Existing donors
- Volunteers
- Long-term supporters
- Legacy/bequest prospects

## 2. Behavioural outcome type

Relevant claims:

- `BI-000006`
- `BT-000001`
- `BC-000001`
- `T-000002`

Donation intention, time donation, sustained support, and bequest intention may not follow the same brand mechanism.

Possible distinction:

| Behavioural outcome | Likely dominant mechanism |
|---|---|
| First-time donation intention | Awareness, salience, image, trust |
| Propensity to donate | Familiarity, image, trust |
| Time donation / volunteering | Image, identity, commitment |
| Sustained support | Commitment, trust, relationship quality |
| Bequest intention | Transparency, trust, long-term relationship |

## 3. Familiarity as a competing explanatory variable

Relevant claims:

- `T-000001`
- `BF-000003`

When familiarity is accounted for, trustworthiness may lose explanatory power in some models. This suggests that familiarity may either:

- Precede trust,
- Substitute for trust in some contexts,
- Capture part of what trust measures,
- Or dominate trustworthiness under certain model specifications.

## 4. Measurement of trust versus trustworthiness

Relevant claim:

- `T-000001`

The reviewed material distinguishes brand trust from perceived trustworthiness. BEBA should not collapse these constructs without conceptual review.

Possible distinction:

- **Brand trust:** Willingness to rely on the nonprofit brand under uncertainty.
- **Perceived trustworthiness:** Evaluation that the nonprofit has attributes making it worthy of trust.

This distinction may require glossary and construct-ledger attention if not already canonical.

## 5. Extrinsic incentive context

Relevant claim:

- `IM-000001`

Extrinsic monetary incentives can weaken brand personality effects. This introduces a motivational boundary condition: brand effects may be strongest when giving remains internally, relationally, morally, or identity motivated.

## 6. Existing supporter or volunteer status

Relevant claim:

- `T-000002`

Transparency mediates brand strength effects on bequest intention among existing supporters or volunteers. This suggests that transparency may become more important in high-commitment, high-consequence, or long-horizon behaviours.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed claims have strong implications for BEBA model competition.

## 1. Direct-effects brand equity model

**Model logic:**  
Brand image, personality, awareness, familiarity, and trust directly predict donation intention or propensity.

**Supported by:**

- `BI-000006`
- `BP-000002`
- `BA-000002`
- `BT-000001`
- `BF-000003`

**Limitation:**  
This model may be too simple because several claims indicate mediation and moderation.

## 2. Trust-mediated model

**Model logic:**  
Brand-building activities influence donation intention through brand trust.

**Supported by:**

- `BT-000002`
- `BH-000002`
- `SM-000001`

**Challenged by:**

- `T-000001`

**Implication:**  
A trust-mediated model is theoretically important but should compete against models that include familiarity, image, and lifecycle stage.

## 3. Cognitive availability model

**Model logic:**  
Awareness, salience, and familiarity influence giving by increasing recall, recognition, and consideration-set inclusion.

**Supported by:**

- `BA-000002`
- `BF-000003`
- `BS-000002`

**Implication:**  
This model may explain early-stage donor behaviour better than relational or commitment-based models.

## 4. Identity-congruence model

**Model logic:**  
Giving is more likely when the nonprofit brand expresses or confirms the donor’s actual or ideal self-concept.

**Supported by:**

- `SC-000001`

**Implication:**  
This model extends BEBA beyond uncertainty reduction into identity expression.

## 5. Relationship-commitment model

**Model logic:**  
Brand commitment enables durable support behaviour over time.

**Supported by:**

- `BC-000001`
- `T-000002`

**Implication:**  
This model may be more appropriate for retention, volunteering, bequests, and sustained giving than for first-time donation intention.

## 6. Organisational signal-processing model

**Model logic:**  
Organisational brand activities such as digital presence, social media marketing, and heritage influence behaviour through perceived image, trust, or transparency.

**Supported by:**

- `DB-000001`
- `BH-000002`
- `SM-000001`

**Implication:**  
BEBA should distinguish organisational brand inputs from individual-level brand equity mechanisms.

## 7. Incentive-disruption model

**Model logic:**  
External incentives can weaken brand-enabled motivational pathways.

**Supported by:**

- `IM-000001`

**Implication:**  
This model competes with simple additive models in which incentives and brand equity are assumed to independently increase donation intention.

## Possible new ontology candidate: Donor Lifecycle Stage

The claim set strongly suggests the need for a lifecycle construct.

**Candidate definition:**  
Donor lifecycle stage refers to the donor’s current relationship status with the nonprofit, ranging from non-donor awareness to first-time donation, repeated support, volunteering, long-term commitment, and legacy/bequest consideration.

**Reason for inclusion:**  
Several mechanisms appear stage-dependent. Salience may matter more for new donors, while commitment and transparency may matter more for existing supporters.

## Possible refinement: Trust versus Trustworthiness

The reviewed claims indicate that BEBA should not automatically merge brand trust and trustworthiness.

**Candidate distinction:**

- **Brand Trust:** A donor’s willingness to rely on the nonprofit brand under uncertainty.
- **Trustworthiness:** A donor’s perception that the nonprofit possesses qualities that make it deserving of trust.

**Recommendation:**  
Treat as separate candidate constructs until measurement and theory review confirm whether they should be merged, nested, or distinguished.

## Possible refinement: Awareness, Salience, and Familiarity

The reviewed claims use awareness, salience, and familiarity in closely related but not identical ways.

**Candidate distinction:**

- **Brand Awareness:** Recognition or recall of the nonprofit brand.
- **Brand Salience:** The likelihood that the nonprofit comes to mind in a giving situation.
- **Brand Familiarity:** Accumulated subjective knowledge or experience with the nonprofit brand.

**Recommendation:**  
Do not collapse these constructs prematurely. They may represent different forms of cognitive availability.

# Recommendation

## Overall recommendation

Integrate this claim set as a **candidate theory integration file**, not as canonical ontology yet.

The claims provide strong support for BEBA’s central architecture, but they also introduce important qualifications. The most important theoretical conclusion is that nonprofit brand equity should be modelled as a **multi-mechanism behavioural architecture** rather than a single construct or simple direct predictor.

## Recommended BEBA architecture refinement

BEBA should represent nonprofit brand-enabled behaviour through at least five mechanism families:

1. **Cognitive availability**  
   Awareness, salience, familiarity.

2. **Evaluation and meaning**  
   Brand image, brand personality.

3. **Uncertainty reduction**  
   Brand trust, trustworthiness, transparency, heritage-based credibility.

4. **Identity and self-expression**  
   Self-congruence, actual-self fit, ideal-self fit.

5. **Relational durability**  
   Commitment, sustained support, bequest-related relationship depth.

8. **Update glossary only if new canonical definitions are adopted.**

## Final integration judgment

This claim set **supports BEBA strongly**, but it also requires BEBA to remain mechanism-plural and context-sensitive. The reviewed material supports the proposition that nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under uncertainty by making organisations more cognitively available, more positively evaluated, more trusted, more identity-relevant, and more relationally durable. However, these mechanisms vary by donor stage, behaviour type, measurement context, and incentive environment.
