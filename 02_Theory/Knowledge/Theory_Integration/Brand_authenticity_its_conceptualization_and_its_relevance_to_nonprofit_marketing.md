Candidate claim set on **Brand Authenticity / Nonprofit Brand Authenticity / Brand Strength**:

- BA-000043 to BA-000050

The reviewed claim set conceptualises brand authenticity primarily as **perceived category exemplarity**: the degree to which a brand object is seen by a relevant audience as the quintessential exemplar of its category.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The claim set strengthens BEBA by introducing **perceived category exemplarity** as a parsimonious uncertainty-reduction mechanism.

For BEBA, brand authenticity should not be treated primarily as internal organisational sincerity, ethical purity, heritage, or consistency. Instead, authenticity becomes a **stakeholder-perceived judgement** that a nonprofit brand is an especially fitting, representative, or prototypical example of a recognised category.

This has two major implications:

1. **Authenticity can operate as a behavioural enablement mechanism under uncertainty.**  
   If stakeholders are uncertain about which nonprofit to trust, support, donate to, volunteer for, or advocate for, perceived authenticity may simplify behavioural choice by making one organisation appear as the most category-defining or category-appropriate option.

2. **Authenticity should be separated from adjacent constructs.**  
   BEBA should avoid multidimensional authenticity definitions that mix the construct with antecedents such as heritage, familiarity, consistency, credibility, or consequences such as loyalty, identification, or behavioural support.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## Primary Layer: Market Layer

The strongest implication concerns the **Market layer**, because the claim set defines authenticity relative to a recognised category and comparative brand field.

Affected claims:

- BA-000043: authenticity as perceived quintessential exemplarity.
- BA-000044: multidimensional authenticity risks construct contamination.
- BA-000046: authenticity is not equivalent to pioneer status.
- BA-000047: authenticity requires a recognisable category.
- BA-000050: authenticity’s nomological role remains unresolved.

## Secondary Layer: Individual Layer

The claim set also affects the **Individual layer**, because authenticity is audience-conferred rather than internally possessed.

Affected claims:

- BA-000045: authenticity is conferred by audience perception.
- BA-000048: perceptions of the category exemplar may differ across stakeholder audiences.

## Tertiary Layer: Organisational Layer

The claim set affects the **Organisational layer** only indirectly. It does not define authenticity as an internal organisational property, but it allows nonprofit organisations to be treated as brand objects capable of being perceived as authentic.

Affected claim:

- NB-000063: exemplar-based brand authenticity applies to nonprofit organisations.

# Existing Claims Supported

The claim set supports several likely BEBA commitments.

## 1. Brands operate through stakeholder perception

Supported by:

- BA-000045  
- BA-000048  

Authenticity is not determined solely by internal values, consistency, ethics, or organisational intent. It is conferred by audiences. This supports BEBA’s emphasis on perception as the behavioural bridge between brand systems and stakeholder action.

## 2. Brand mechanisms reduce uncertainty

Supported by:

- BA-000043  
- BA-000047  

If authenticity is perceived category exemplarity, then it can reduce uncertainty by helping stakeholders identify which organisation best represents a relevant nonprofit category, cause domain, or behavioural option.

## 3. Brand equity should not be collapsed into behaviour

Supported by:

- BA-000044  
- BA-000049  

The claim set warns against authenticity measures that include outcomes, antecedents, or adjacent constructs. This supports BEBA’s need to distinguish brand perceptions, behavioural dispositions, and actual behavioural outcomes.

## 4. Nonprofit brand theory does not require nonprofit exceptionalism

Supported by:

- NB-000063  

The reviewed claim set allows authenticity to apply to nonprofit organisations without redefining authenticity through nonprofit-specific moral purity, altruism, or mission sincerity. This is useful for BEBA because it permits nonprofit specificity without abandoning general brand-theoretical discipline.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Internally focused authenticity definitions

Challenged by:

- BA-000045  

Any BEBA formulation that defines nonprofit authenticity as internal consistency, ethical purity, or fidelity to organisational values would be challenged. These may be antecedents or cues, but not the core construct if authenticity is defined as perceived category exemplarity.

## 2. Multidimensional authenticity measures

Challenged by:

- BA-000044  
- BA-000049  

Measures that combine authenticity with credibility, familiarity, heritage, personal utility, trust, identification, or behavioural intention risk construct contamination. BEBA should therefore avoid importing multidimensional authenticity scales without construct-level review.

## 3. Pioneer-status interpretations of authenticity

Challenged by:

- BA-000046  

Authenticity should not be equated with being the original, oldest, founding, or first-moving organisation in a cause category. A nonprofit may be perceived as authentic without being historically first.

## 4. Universal-audience assumptions

Challenged by:

- BA-000048  

BEBA should not assume that authenticity is uniform across stakeholder groups. Donors, volunteers, beneficiaries, policymakers, professional peers, and local communities may differ in which organisation they perceive as the category exemplar.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Recognisable category condition

Authenticity, as defined here, requires a category against which the organisation can be evaluated. It is less applicable where:

- the category is nascent,
- stakeholders do not recognise the cause domain,
- the organisation crosses multiple ambiguous categories,
- the stakeholder lacks category knowledge.

Relevant claim:

- BA-000047

## 2. Audience-specific perception condition

Authenticity cannot be assumed to be universal. Stakeholder groups may differ in their category prototypes and therefore in their authenticity judgements.

Relevant claim:

- BA-000048

## 3. Measurement validity condition

Authenticity should not be operationalised using heterogeneous dimensions that include antecedents or outcomes.

Relevant claims:

- BA-000044  
- BA-000049

## 4. Non-pioneer condition

Being first in a category is neither necessary nor sufficient for authenticity.

Relevant claim:

- BA-000046

## 5. Unresolved nomological condition

The role of authenticity as mediator, moderator, antecedent, or consequence of brand strength remains unresolved.

Relevant claim:

- BA-000050

# Model Competition Implications

The claim set creates important implications for competing BEBA model specifications.

## Model A: Brand Strength → Authenticity → Behaviour

This model treats authenticity as a mediator. Strong brands become behaviourally effective because stakeholders come to perceive them as category exemplars.

Supported tentatively by:

- BS-000009  
- BA-000050

Status: plausible but unresolved.

## Model B: Brand Strength × Authenticity → Behaviour

This model treats authenticity as a moderator. Brand strength may translate into behavioural outcomes more effectively when the brand is also perceived as authentic.

Supported tentatively by:

- BA-000050

Status: plausible but unresolved.

## Model C: Authenticity → Trust/Credibility → Behaviour

This model treats authenticity as an upstream perceptual judgement that may support trust, credibility, or commitment. However, the reviewed claim set warns that trust and credibility should not be folded into the definition of authenticity.

Supported indirectly by:

- BA-000044  
- BA-000049

Status: possible, but requires separate evidence.

## Model D: Category Recognition → Authenticity → Behaviour

This model adds category recognisability as a prerequisite. Authenticity only functions when stakeholders can evaluate the nonprofit against a known category.

Supported by:

- BA-000047

Status: strong candidate boundary model.

## Model E: Stakeholder Group → Authenticity Perception → Behaviour

This model treats authenticity as audience-specific. Different stakeholder groups may show different authenticity perceptions and behavioural consequences.

Supported by:

- BA-000048

Status: strong candidate segmentation or multigroup model.

## Model Competition Summary

The claim set does not settle whether authenticity is:

- an antecedent,
- mediator,
- moderator,
- outcome of brand strength,
- or audience-specific perception layer.

Therefore, BEBA should treat authenticity as a **candidate mechanism in model competition**, not as a settled canonical pathway.

# Recommendation

## 1. Integrate as candidate mechanism, not canonical theory

Brand authenticity should be added to the BEBA theory architecture as a **candidate perceptual mechanism of uncertainty reduction**, not yet as a canonical causal pathway.

## 2. Preserve a narrow definition

Use the exemplar-based definition:

> Brand authenticity is the perceived degree to which a brand object is regarded by a relevant audience as the quintessential exemplar of a recognised category.

Avoid broader multidimensional definitions unless the repository separately evaluates and justifies them.

## 3. Add audience and category boundary conditions

Any BEBA use of authenticity should specify:

- relevant stakeholder audience,
- recognised category of evaluation,
- behavioural context,
- whether authenticity is expected to mediate, moderate, or independently influence behaviour.

## 4. Treat measurement as unresolved

BEBA should not adopt multidimensional authenticity scales without review. Measurement should focus on perceived category exemplarity. Existing scales should be assessed for contamination with credibility, familiarity, heritage, trust, identification, personal utility, or behavioural outcomes.

## 5. Link to model competition

Authenticity should be included in model competition as a candidate mechanism in at least three possible roles:

1. mediator between brand strength and behaviour,
2. moderator of brand strength effects,
3. audience-specific perceptual condition affecting behavioural enablement.
