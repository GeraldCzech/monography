# Core Theoretical Implication

The claim set strongly supports BEBA’s central premise that nonprofit brand equity should not be treated merely as a perceptual asset or image construct, but as a **behaviour-enabling architecture under uncertainty**.

The most important integration point is:

> Charitable giving is behaviourally difficult because donors face uncertainty about organisational reliability, use of funds, and impact. Nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective when they function as signals that reduce uncertainty, support trust, enable intention formation, and help bridge the path from intention to realised support.

This reinforces the BEBA research question:

> How do nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty?

The reviewed claims also support a layered BEBA architecture:

1. **Market / uncertainty layer**: charitable giving as credence-like exchange.
2. **Brand signalling layer**: brand awareness, familiarity, image, personality, and legitimacy as signals.
3. **Individual psychological layer**: trust, commitment, attitudes, norms, perceived behavioural control, and intention.
4. **Behavioural enactment layer**: habit, salience, knowledge, constraints, and intention–behaviour gap.
5. **Societal / institutional layer**: legitimacy and mission authenticity.

The material therefore supports BEBA as a multi-layer explanatory architecture rather than a single brand-equity measurement model.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## 1. Market Layer

Affected by:

- `CG-000005`
- `BS-000005`
- `BS-000006`

The reviewed claims establish the behavioural problem that BEBA addresses: donor uncertainty and information asymmetry. Charitable giving is framed as a context in which donors cannot fully verify service quality, fund use, or impact. This gives the brand a signalling function.

**Integration implication:**  
The Market Layer should explicitly contain the proposition that nonprofit brands matter because charitable giving occurs under uncertainty, not because branding is intrinsically persuasive.

# Existing Claims Challenged

No reviewed claim directly falsifies BEBA’s core architecture. However, several claims qualify or constrain possible BEBA assumptions.

Challenged by:

- `BT-000013`
- `RD-000001`
- `RD-000002`
- `BE-000033`

If trust mediates brand-signal effects and trust fosters commitment, then nonprofit brand equity may be better represented as a relational sequence than as an additive index of awareness, image, trust, and loyalty-like constructs.

Challenged by:

**Implication:**  
The donor pathway remains central, but BEBA should preserve a societal and institutional extension.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed claim set has major implications for BEBA model competition.

## 1. Aggregate Brand Equity Model

The aggregate model receives partial support because multiple brand constructs are positively associated with donation intention:

- awareness,
- familiarity,
- image,
- personality,
- trust,
- commitment.

However, the reviewed material does not establish that these dimensions should be collapsed into one additive index.

**Implication:**  
The aggregate model remains plausible but theoretically under-specified.

---

## 2. Relational / Sequential Model

The relational model receives strong conceptual support from:

- `RD-000001`
- `RD-000002`
- `BT-000013`
- `CO-000008`

These claims suggest a sequence:

```text
Brand awareness / familiarity / image / personality
→ trust
→ commitment
→ intention / recurring support
→ behaviour
```

**Implication:**  
The relational model should be treated as a serious competitor, not merely as an extension of an aggregate brand-equity model.

---

## 3. Disaggregated Signal Model

The disaggregated model is also supported because different brand signals may perform different functions:

- awareness may enable recognition and salience,
- familiarity may reduce uncertainty through repeated exposure,
- image may provide evaluative meaning,
- personality may provide symbolic identification,
- trust may reduce perceived risk,
- commitment may stabilise future support.

**Implication:**  
The disaggregated model may be especially useful for explaining which brand signals matter under which uncertainty conditions.

---

## 4. Behavioural Enactment Model

The reviewed claims support adding or preserving a post-intentional model layer:

```text
Brand-enabled intention
+ knowledge
+ salience
+ environmental opportunity
+ habit
− constraints
→ realised donation/support behaviour
```

**Implication:**  
A model predicting only donation intention is insufficient for BEBA’s full behavioural explanation.

---

## 5. Legitimacy / Institutional Extension Model

The legitimacy claim suggests a broader model in which nonprofit brands affect stakeholder support through perceived legitimacy, not only donor trust.

**Implication:**  
This may become either:

1. a boundary layer around donor behaviour, or  
2. a separate BEBA extension for stakeholder and institutional behaviour.

# Recommendation

## 1. Integrate the claim set as candidate-level support for BEBA’s uncertainty architecture

The strongest contribution of the reviewed claims is that they clarify why nonprofit brand equity matters:

> Nonprofit brand equity becomes behaviourally relevant because charitable giving occurs under uncertainty and brand signals can reduce perceived risk, support trust, and enable action.

This should be integrated into BEBA’s theoretical map as candidate support for the Market → Individual mechanism.

---

## 2. Preserve model competition

Do not canonise a single nonprofit brand-equity structure yet. The reviewed claims support continued comparison among:

1. aggregate brand-equity model,
2. relational/sequential model,
3. disaggregated signal model,
4. post-intentional enactment model,
5. legitimacy/institutional extension model.

`BE-000033` directly justifies this.

---

## 3. Elevate trust as a central mechanism, but not as the whole theory

Trust should be treated as a central BEBA mechanism because it links brand signals to reduced uncertainty and donation intention. However, BEBA should avoid reducing all brand effects to trust alone. Awareness, familiarity, image, personality, commitment, legitimacy, and enactment conditions may retain distinct explanatory roles.

---

## 4. Keep intention and behaviour analytically separate

The reviewed claims strongly justify BEBA’s distinction between intention formation and behavioural enactment. Donation intention should remain an intermediate outcome unless verified behavioural data are available.

No empirical behavioural claims should be made without consulting verified pipeline outputs.

---

## 5. Add or maintain Brand–Mission Tension as a low-confidence boundary condition

`BM-000001` is theoretically important but low-confidence. It should be retained as a candidate boundary condition, not treated as established.

Suggested wording:

> Professional nonprofit branding may weaken behavioural effectiveness when it is perceived as commercialisation, inauthenticity, or mission drift.

---

## 6. Consider ontology candidates, but do not update canonical ontology without review

The following should be considered for ontology candidate files:

- Charitable Giving as Credence Good
- Brand Signalling
- Brand Trust as uncertainty-reduction mechanism
- Donor Commitment as relational stabilisation mechanism
- Intention–Behaviour Gap
- Brand–Mission Tension
- Organisational Legitimacy Signal

Canonical ontology, glossary, and ConstructLedger changes would require manual review and, where relevant, ADR or DecisionLog updates.

---

## 7. Suggested integration summary

The reviewed claim set supports the following BEBA formulation:

> Nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective when they operate as credible signals under donor uncertainty. Awareness, familiarity, image, and symbolic associations provide initial cues; trust reduces perceived risk; commitment stabilises relational support; and behavioural enactment depends on post-intentional conditions such as salience, habit, knowledge, and environmental opportunity. The effectiveness of this architecture is constrained by legitimacy concerns and potential brand–mission tension.
