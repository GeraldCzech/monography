# Input Reviewed

This review integrates a set of eight candidate claims (BC-000025 to BC-000032) derived from the Hassay & Peloza (2009) conceptual model on brand communities in the nonprofit sector. The claims collectively propose two distinct, cyclical pathways for developing supporter commitment: a cognitive-learning pathway initiated by `Identification` and an experiential-learning pathway initiated by `Behavioral Involvement`.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The core implication is the formalization of two competing, yet potentially complementary, mechanisms for building supporter commitment, which is a proxy for behavioral brand equity. This directly addresses the BEBA research principle of "Competing explanations over single models." It moves the theory beyond a simple linear model (Brand -> Behaviour) to a dynamic, cyclical system where behaviour also shapes brand perceptions and identity.

Specifically, it operationalizes the "Behaviour before brands" principle via the experiential-learning pathway (BC-000032), providing a testable model for how action can precede and build attitudinal brand equity.

# BEBA Layer Affected

-   **Primary:** `Individual` layer. The claims model the cognitive and behavioural processes of an individual supporter (Identification, Commitment, Involvement).
-   **Secondary:** `Social` layer. The construct `Perceived Sense of Community` is a social-psychological construct that acts as a key mediator. It bridges the individual's experience and a collective identity, making it a critical link between the Individual and Social layers of BEBA.

# Existing Claims Supported

-   **Behaviour shapes brand perceptions:** The experiential pathway, particularly claims BC-000028 (`Involvement -> Sense of Community`) and BC-000029 (`Sense of Community -> Identification`), provides strong theoretical support for the BEBA principle that behaviour is not just an outcome of brand equity but also an input to it.
-   **Cognitive pathways drive behaviour:** The cognitive pathway, particularly claim BC-000025 (`Identification -> Involvement`), supports the traditional view that internal, brand-related cognitions are direct antecedents to supportive behaviours.
-   **Feedback loops are central:** Claim BC-000027 (`Commitment -> Involvement`) supports the concept of BEBA as a self-reinforcing architecture where established attitudinal states drive further action.

# Existing Claims Challenged

These claims do not directly challenge existing BEBA principles; rather, they enrich and specify them. They challenge a simplistic, unidirectional view of `Brand Equity -> Behaviour` by introducing a reciprocal, cyclical model. The primary "challenge" is constructive: it forces the BEBA framework to explicitly model and test the causal priority of cognition versus behaviour in the formation of supporter commitment.

# New Candidate Claims

The input documents *are* a set of candidate claims. The most significant new meta-claims for the BEBA architecture are:

1.  **Duality of Commitment Pathways:** Supporter commitment can be generated through two distinct causal sequences: one cognitive-led (`Identification` -> `Involvement` -> `Community` -> `Commitment`) and one experiential-led (`Involvement` -> `Community` -> `Identification` -> `Commitment`).
2.  **Mediating Role of Community:** `Perceived Sense of Community` is a key mediating variable that translates both cognitive states (`Identification`) and behavioural acts (`Involvement`) into durable `Commitment`.

# Boundary Conditions

The introduction of two pathways immediately raises questions about their boundary conditions. Candidate boundary conditions for investigation include:

-   **NPO Maturity/Awareness:** The cognitive pathway (starting with Identification) may be more prevalent for well-known, high-equity NPOs, where supporters can form an identity prior to deep involvement. The experiential pathway may be more critical for new or lesser-known NPOs that must first engage supporters in activities to build awareness and identity.
-   **Type of Involvement:** The nature of the "behavioral involvement" may matter. Low-cost, low-effort actions (e.g., signing a petition online) may be less likely to trigger the experiential loop than high-effort, socially visible actions (e.g., participating in a fundraising run).
-   **Supporter Motivation:** The initial motivation of the supporter (e.g., value-expressive vs. social-adjustive) could determine which pathway is activated first.

# Model Competition Implications

This set of claims is a major asset for the BEBA principle of model competition. It provides the basis for a direct empirical test between:

-   **Model A (Cognitive-Led):** A structural model specifying the causal chain `Identification -> Involvement -> Sense of Community -> Commitment`.
-   **Model B (Experiential-Led):** A structural model specifying the causal chain `Involvement -> Sense of Community -> Identification -> Commitment`.

Further, it allows for testing a more complex, non-recursive model where both pathways operate simultaneously. This moves the empirical agenda from "what factors predict commitment?" to "what is the dominant causal architecture of commitment formation?".

# Ontology Candidate Implications

This integration requires that the following concepts are rigorously defined and added to the BEBA ontology, if not already present.

1.  **`Supporter Identification`**: Needs a canonical definition distinguishing it from related concepts like brand identity or organizational identity.
2.  **`Behavioral Involvement`**: Requires clear definition. Is it a formative construct composed of different actions (donating, volunteering, advocating)? How does it differ from specific behavioural outcomes in the BEBA model?
3.  **`Perceived Sense of Community`**: Must be defined as a psychological construct at the individual level, distinct from the objective existence of a community at the social level.
4.  **`Supporter Commitment`**: Needs to be defined, likely as a multi-dimensional construct (e.g., affective, continuance commitment) and positioned relative to brand loyalty.

# Recommendation

1.  **Adopt the Dual-Pathway Model:** Formally incorporate the cognitive-learning and experiential-learning pathways from Hassay & Peloza (2009) into the `01_THEORETICAL_MAP.md` as a central mechanism for model competition within the BEBA framework.
2.  **Create Concept Files:** Initiate new concept workspace files for `Supporter Identification`, `Behavioral Involvement`, and `Perceived Sense of Community` to establish canonical definitions within the BEBA ontology.
3.  **Update Construct Ledger:** Review `ConstructLedger.md` to map existing or new scales to these concepts, noting their conceptual origin in brand community theory.
4.  **Design Empirical Test:** Formulate a research question for the empirical pipeline that explicitly tests the competing causal structures of the cognitive-led vs. experiential-led models. This should become a core component of the dissertation's empirical strategy.
