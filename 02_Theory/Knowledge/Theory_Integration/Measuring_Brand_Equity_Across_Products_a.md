# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set supports BEBA’s position that nonprofit brand equity should not be reduced to financial outcomes, fundraising volume, or aggregate support behaviour. Instead, brand equity should be understood as a configuration of behaviour-enabling assets: awareness, associations, perceived competence or quality, trust-related organisational associations, loyalty, and supportive behavioural tendencies.

The strongest implication for BEBA is that classical brand equity measurement can be used as a **translation scaffold**, but not as a ready-made nonprofit measurement model. Commercial constructs such as price premium, perceived quality, market share, and loyalty require nonprofit-specific adaptation before they can validly represent behaviourally effective nonprofit brands.

In BEBA terms, the claim set strengthens the idea that brand equity is not merely an outcome variable. It is an enabling architecture that shapes whether supporters notice, understand, trust, prefer, repeat, recommend, donate, volunteer, or advocate under uncertainty.

# Existing Claims Supported

Based on the provided BEBA implications, the claim set supports the following BEBA-aligned theoretical positions.

## 1. Brand equity should be understood behaviourally, not only financially

Supported by:

- `BE-000040`
- `BL-000002`
- `MS-000002`

Reason:

Short-term financial or market outcomes can obscure the underlying condition of brand equity. Loyalty, repeat support, willingness to recommend, willingness to give more, and durable behavioural commitment are more directly aligned with BEBA’s behavioural orientation.

## 2. Nonprofit brand equity requires adapted measurement rather than direct import from commercial branding

Supported by:

- `MA-000002`
- `PP-000003`
- `PQ-000003`
- `PV-000001`
- `OA-000001`

Reason:

Commercial constructs such as price premium, quality, market share, and value cannot be assumed to map directly onto nonprofit support contexts. They require translation into concepts such as support premium, perceived impact, donation efficacy, perceived competence, organisational credibility, and trust.

## 3. Awareness is a necessary but insufficient condition for brand-enabled behaviour

Supported by:

- `BA-000020`
- `BA-000022`
- `BA-000023`

Reason:

Awareness is a foundational brand equity dimension, but the appropriate form of awareness differs by brand maturity. Recognition may matter more for new or niche nonprofits, whereas recall and top-of-mind awareness may be more relevant for established organisations.

## 4. Brand meaning and associations are central to behaviour enablement

Supported by:

- `BA-000021`
- `OA-000001`
- `BP-000012`

Reason:

Supporter behaviour is not enabled by awareness alone. Associations, organisational credibility, values, personality, and perceived identity cues shape whether a nonprofit brand becomes meaningful enough to support, recommend, or defend.

## 5. Loyalty segmentation is important for interpreting nonprofit brand equity

Supported by:

- `LS-000001`
- `BL-000002`

Reason:

Aggregate brand equity scores may conceal important differences between committed supporters, occasional supporters, switchers, and non-supporters. BEBA should therefore avoid treating “the public” or “donors” as homogeneous categories.

## 6. Perceived competence, quality, and impact may operate as behaviour-enabling evaluations

Supported by:

- `PQ-000003`
- `PV-000001`
- `OA-000001`

Reason:

In nonprofit contexts, perceived quality likely needs to be translated into perceived organisational competence, effectiveness, trustworthiness, or impact quality. Perceived value may need to be translated into perceived donation efficacy or perceived contribution meaningfulness.

## 7. A single aggregate brand equity index is theoretically risky

Supported by:

- `BE-000041`

Reason:

A single score requires decisions about construct inclusion, weighting, and aggregation. For BEBA, premature aggregation could hide the architecture of behavioural enablement and weaken model competition.

# Existing Claims Challenged

The reviewed claim set does not directly challenge BEBA’s behavioural orientation. It does, however, challenge several possible simplifications that BEBA should avoid.

## 1. Challenge to treating financial or fundraising outcomes as direct indicators of brand equity

Relevant claims:

- `BE-000040`
- `MS-000002`

Implication:

Donation volume, campaign income, market share of giving, or supporter growth cannot automatically be interpreted as evidence of durable nonprofit brand equity. They may reflect short-term tactics, temporary salience, external events, emergency effects, or low-effort acquisition strategies.

## 2. Challenge to using commercial brand equity dimensions without adaptation

Relevant claims:

- `MA-000002`
- `PP-000003`
- `PQ-000003`
- `PV-000001`

Implication:

BEBA should not simply relabel commercial constructs. “Price premium” should not be imported literally; it requires translation into willingness to donate more, volunteer more, tolerate inconvenience, advocate more strongly, or continue support despite alternatives.

## 3. Challenge to assuming perceived quality and perceived value are clearly separable

Relevant claim:

- `PV-000001`

Implication:

The claim has low confidence and notes overlap between value and quality. BEBA should treat perceived nonprofit impact, donation efficacy, perceived competence, and perceived quality as potentially distinct but empirically contestable constructs.

## 4. Challenge to treating brand leadership as an established nonprofit brand equity dimension

Relevant claims:

- `BL-000003`
- `BL-000004`

Implication:

Perceived cause-area leadership may be theoretically interesting, but the evidence status is weak. It should remain a candidate construct, not a canonical BEBA dimension.

## 5. Challenge to universal relevance of brand personality

Relevant claim:

- `BP-000012`

Implication:

Brand personality should not be treated as universally central. Its relevance depends on boundary conditions such as low physical differentiation, high symbolic value, and social visibility of support.

# Model Competition Implications

The claim set strengthens the need for competing models rather than one assumed brand equity structure.

## 1. BEBA versus financial-outcome model

The claims support BEBA over a model that treats financial outcomes, fundraising success, or market share as sufficient indicators of brand equity.

Relevant claims:

- `BE-000040`
- `MS-000002`

Implication:

A nonprofit may show strong short-term support but weak brand equity if support is driven by urgency, discounts, aggressive acquisition, crisis salience, or temporary media attention.

## 2. BEBA versus aggregate-index model

The claims caution against a single brand equity index.

Relevant claim:

- `BE-000041`

Implication:

BEBA should preserve multi-dimensional architecture unless there is theoretical and empirical justification for aggregation.

## 3. BEBA versus awareness-only model

The claims imply that awareness is foundational but insufficient.

Relevant claims:

- `BA-000020`
- `BA-000021`
- `PQ-000003`
- `OA-000001`
- `BL-000002`

Implication:

A nonprofit brand can be known but not behaviourally effective if it lacks trust, relevance, perceived competence, positive associations, or loyalty.

## 4. BEBA versus loyalty-only model

The claim set supports loyalty as behaviourally central but not sufficient as a complete explanation.

Relevant claims:

- `BL-000002`
- `S-000008`
- `BA-000020`
- `BA-000021`
- `PQ-000003`

Implication:

Loyalty may be an outcome of satisfaction, perceived quality, associations, trust, and prior behaviour rather than the whole brand equity architecture.

## 5. BEBA versus service-satisfaction model

The satisfaction claim introduces a possible competing or bypass route.

Relevant claim:

- `S-000008`

Implication:

Supporter satisfaction may drive loyalty independently of brand equity. This creates a model competition question: does satisfaction operate through brand equity, alongside brand equity, or as a partial bypass around brand equity?

## 6. BEBA versus cause-driven behaviour model

Although not directly stated, the nonprofit translation implies another competing explanation: supporters may act because of cause commitment rather than brand equity.

Implication:

BEBA should distinguish behaviour enabled by the nonprofit brand from behaviour driven primarily by cause salience, moral identity, emergency need, or social pressure.

# Recommendation

## Overall recommendation

Integrate this claim set as a **candidate measurement architecture input**, not as a canonical BEBA model.

The claims are useful for translating classical brand equity theory into BEBA, but they remain commercial-brand-derived and require nonprofit-specific theoretical adaptation and empirical validation.

## Recommended action

1. Keep the claims in candidate status.
2. Use them to inform a nonprofit brand equity measurement adaptation file.
3. Do not collapse BEBA into Aaker-style dimensions without sector translation.
4. Treat awareness, associations, perceived quality, organisational associations, and loyalty as strong candidate dimensions.
5. Treat perceived value, differentiation, leadership, and brand personality as conditional or lower-confidence candidates.
6. Add “support premium” as a promising BEBA-specific translation of price premium.
7. Add “brand maturity” and “loyalty segment” as measurement boundary conditions.
8. Avoid creating a single nonprofit brand equity index unless construct inclusion, weighting, and aggregation logic are explicitly justified.
9. Mark satisfaction as a possible antecedent or bypass route that requires model competition against brand-equity-mediated explanations.

## Final theoretical interpretation

This claim set supports BEBA’s central move from brand equity as market valuation to brand equity as behavioural enablement. Its main value is not that it provides a ready nonprofit model, but that it clarifies which commercial brand equity dimensions can be translated, which must be adapted, and which should remain contested.
