# Core Theoretical Implication

The claim set strongly supports BEBA’s central move: **nonprofit brand equity should not be treated as an organizational financial asset or as behavioural intention itself, but as a stakeholder-level cognitive, relational, and evaluative architecture that can enable behaviour under uncertainty.**

The most important theoretical implication is the need to separate:

1. **Brand-enabled states**  
   e.g. awareness, associations, trust, commitment, identification, value congruence.

2. **Behavioural dispositions or intentions**  
   e.g. willingness to donate, preference, planned support.

3. **Observed behaviour**  
   e.g. actual donation, repeated giving, volunteering, advocacy, partnership action.

This distinction is crucial because the reviewed claims indicate that nonprofit brand equity research often stops at intention or preference and lacks an empirically tested bridge to actual observed behaviour.

For BEBA, this strengthens the argument that nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective not merely because stakeholders “like” or “know” them, but because brand structures reduce uncertainty, support moral or relational alignment, and create readiness for action.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## Primary Layer: Individual

Most claims operate at the **individual stakeholder level**. They concern how donors or members of the general public perceive, evaluate, trust, identify with, and commit to nonprofit organizations.

Affected individual-level mechanisms include:

- cognitive accessibility: awareness;
- evaluative simplification: image, reputation, personality;
- uncertainty reduction: trust, perceived integrity, perceived competence;
- relational attachment: commitment;
- identity-based motivation: moral identification, value congruence;
- behavioural readiness: intention, loyalty, support disposition.

## Secondary Layer: Organisational

The organisational layer is affected through:

- nonprofit type as a moderator;
- perceived accountability, transparency, effectiveness, and quality;
- scale validity across nonprofit types;
- differences between advocacy NGOs, service providers, hospitals, arts organizations, and other nonprofit categories.

## Secondary Layer: Societal

The societal layer is affected through the possible role of:

- cultural context;
- cross-national differences;
- culturally variable importance of trust, identification, and brand personality.

However, the reviewed claim set explicitly notes that cultural boundary conditions are **not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature**.

# Existing Claims Supported

The reviewed material supports several core BEBA assumptions.

## 1. Brand equity is stakeholder-level, not organization-owned

Supported by `NB-000021`.

Nonprofit brand equity resides in stakeholders’ minds rather than in the organization as a financial asset. This supports BEBA’s treatment of brand equity as a **stakeholder-level cognitive-relational architecture**.

## 2. Brand equity must be separated from behavioural intention

Supported by `NB-000022`, `BI-000011`, and `NB-000024`.

The reviewed claims indicate that nonprofit brand equity becomes conceptually ambiguous when operationalised as intention. BEBA is therefore correct to distinguish:

```text
brand-enabled state ≠ behavioural intention ≠ observed behaviour
```

## 3. Awareness enables but does not determine support

Supported by `BA-000005` and `BA-000006`.

Brand awareness is necessary but insufficient. It can support action only when associated meanings are sufficiently positive or behaviourally relevant. Awareness may even reduce support when accessible associations are negative.

This supports modelling awareness as an **enabling condition**, not as a direct behavioural driver.

## 4. Brands guide behaviour under uncertainty through heuristics

Supported by `BI-000010`, `BP-000003`, and `BR-000001`.

Brand image, brand personality, and reputation function as cognitive shortcuts or evaluative heuristics under incomplete information. This directly supports the BEBA research question:

> How do nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty?

## 5. Trust is central to nonprofit brand-enabled behaviour

Supported by `BT-000003`, `BT-000004`, and `BT-000005`.

Trust reduces perceived donation risk when nonprofit performance and resource use are hard to observe. Trust also signals integrity, competence, and ethical conduct.

This supports positioning **brand trust** as a central uncertainty-reduction mechanism in BEBA.

## 6. Commitment supports durable behavioural readiness

Supported by `CO-000001` and `CO-000002`.

Commitment reflects an enduring psychological desire to maintain a valued nonprofit relationship. It transforms support from an isolated transaction into a durable relational bond.

This supports BEBA’s focus on behavioural architecture rather than isolated support acts.

## 7. Moral identification and value congruence extend BEBA beyond risk reduction

Supported by `MI-000001` and `VC-000001`.

Donors may support nonprofit brands because these brands express moral values and align with their self-concept. This supports adding an **identity-expressive mechanism** alongside the uncertainty-reduction mechanism.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Any model treating nonprofit brand equity as equivalent to behavioural intention is challenged

`NB-000022` and `BI-000011` challenge intention-based operationalisations of brand equity.

If brand equity is measured as intention, preference, or support willingness, the explanation risks becoming circular:

```text
brand equity explains donation intention
but brand equity is measured as donation intention
```

BEBA should avoid this by keeping brand-state constructs separate from outcome constructs.

## 2. Static brand equity models are challenged

`DB-000003` challenges static and cross-sectional models.

If nonprofit brand equity is supposed to explain behaviour over time, then BEBA cannot rely only on one-time perception → intention models. It needs to allow:

- feedback from behaviour to brand state;
- repeated interaction effects;
- trust accumulation or erosion;
- commitment development;
- reputation change;
- behavioural reinforcement.

## 3. Simple awareness-driven models are challenged

`BA-000005` and `BA-000006` challenge any model where awareness directly or automatically increases support.

Awareness must be qualified by:

- association valence;
- association content;
- stakeholder relevance;
- nonprofit category;
- situational context.

## 4. Uncritical transfer of commercial brand constructs is challenged

`M-000002` challenges the assumption that commercial brand image or personality scales can be transferred unchanged into nonprofit contexts.

BEBA measurement work should therefore treat commercial brand constructs as candidate sources, not as ready-made nonprofit measurement instruments.

## 5. Trust as a catch-all construct is challenged

`BT-000005` and `PQ-000001` challenge the use of trust as a proxy for perceived quality, effectiveness, transparency, and accountability.

BEBA should avoid collapsing these constructs into one undifferentiated trust factor without theoretical justification.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Stakeholder type

Supported by `BC-000002`.

Existing nonprofit brand equity models are primarily developed for individual donors or the general public. BEBA should therefore avoid assuming direct transfer to:

- corporate partners;
- foundations;
- public agencies;
- institutional funders;
- volunteers;
- beneficiaries;
- employees;
- activists.

The behavioural architecture may differ by stakeholder role.

## 2. Behaviour type

Supported by `BC-000003`.

Existing theories may be better suited to deliberative planned giving than to impulsive or emergency-driven donations.

BEBA should distinguish between:

```text
planned brand-enabled support
vs.
situationally triggered emergency giving
```

Emergency giving may bypass parts of the brand equity architecture if situational urgency, media exposure, or social influence dominates.

## 3. Cultural context

Supported weakly by `BC-000004`.

The relative importance of trust, identification, and personality may vary across cultures.

Status: **Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.**

This should remain a candidate boundary condition, not a canonical claim.

## 4. Nonprofit type

Supported by `BC-000005`.

Moral identification may be more relevant for cause-based advocacy NGOs than for local service providers, hospitals, or arts organizations.

Status: inferred and requiring further empirical testing.

BEBA should model nonprofit type as a potential moderator.

## 5. Association valence

Supported by `BA-000006`.

Awareness can reduce support if accessible associations are negative. Therefore, brand accessibility is only beneficial under favourable association conditions.

## 6. Construct maturity

Supported by `PQ-000001`, `L-000001`, and `M-000002`.

Several constructs remain underdeveloped in nonprofit brand equity research, especially:

- loyalty;
- perceived quality;
- effectiveness;
- transparency;
- accountability;
- nonprofit-specific brand personality;
- nonprofit-specific brand image.

BEBA should treat these as candidate constructs requiring conceptual clarification before canonical inclusion.

# Model Competition Implications

The reviewed material has substantial implications for model competition inside BEBA.

## 1. Intention model vs. behaviour model

The claim set challenges models that stop at intention or preference. BEBA should explicitly compare:

```text
Brand perceptions → intention
```

against models that include actual behaviour:

```text
Brand perceptions → brand-enabled state → behavioural readiness → observed behaviour
```

The reviewed literature does not provide sufficient evidence for a tested path to actual observed donation behaviour. Therefore, empirical pipeline outputs would be required before making behavioural-effect claims.

## 2. Direct effect model vs. mediated architecture model

The claim set favours a mediated architecture model over simple direct effects.

Rather than assuming:

```text
brand awareness/image/trust → donation
```

BEBA should consider:

```text
brand knowledge
→ evaluative heuristic / trust / identification / commitment
→ behavioural readiness
→ observed behaviour
```

## 3. Trust-reduction model vs. identity-expression model

Two mechanisms appear to compete or complement each other:

### A. Trust / uncertainty reduction route

```text
brand reputation / image / transparency cues
→ trust
→ reduced perceived risk
→ support readiness
```

### B. Moral identification / value congruence route

```text
mission meaning / symbolic associations
→ value congruence / moral identification
→ self-expressive motivation
→ support readiness
```

BEBA should not collapse these into one general “positive brand attitude” pathway.

## 4. Relational commitment model vs. transactional support model

The commitment claims suggest a distinction between:

```text
one-time support
```

and

```text
durable relational support
```

BEBA should model commitment as especially relevant for repeated giving, membership, advocacy, or long-term engagement.

## 5. Static model vs. dynamic feedback model

The reviewed claims challenge static cross-sectional models. BEBA should consider a dynamic architecture:

```text
brand-enabled state → behaviour → experience/confirmation → updated brand state
```

This would allow trust, commitment, and reputation to change over time.

## 6. Brand equity route vs. bypass route

The boundary condition on emergency giving suggests possible bypass routes.

In emergency or crisis contexts, behaviour may be triggered by:

- urgency;
- emotional arousal;
- media salience;
- peer influence;
- situational compassion;
- disaster proximity.

These may partially bypass deliberative brand equity mechanisms, although trusted brands may still shape which organization receives support.

## 3. Prioritise conceptual separation before measurement

The most urgent theoretical task is to prevent construct collapse among:

```text
brand equity
trust
intention
loyalty
commitment
observed behaviour
```

This should be reflected in the ConstructLedger if measurement mappings are affected.

## 4. Treat trust as central but not all-inclusive

Brand trust should be central in BEBA because it directly addresses uncertainty. However, BEBA should distinguish trust from its antecedents and signals.

Recommended distinction:

```text
transparency / accountability / perceived effectiveness
→ trust
→ reduced perceived risk
→ behavioural readiness
```

rather than:

```text
transparency = accountability = effectiveness = trust
```

## 5. Add identity-expression as a second major route

BEBA should include at least two major candidate mechanisms:

```text
Uncertainty-reduction route:
reputation / image / accountability cues → trust → reduced perceived risk → support

Identity-expression route:
mission meaning / values → value congruence / moral identification → self-expressive support
```

## 6. Include bypass routes in model competition

Emergency giving and situationally triggered donations may not require the full BEBA architecture. This should be represented as a competing or boundary model:

```text
situational trigger → emotional urgency → donation
```

possibly moderated by brand trust or awareness.

## 7. Require empirical verification for observed-behaviour claims

The reviewed material states that existing nonprofit brand equity models do not provide an empirically tested path from brand perceptions to actual observed donation behaviour.

Therefore, BEBA should not claim empirical confirmation of this path unless verified pipeline outputs support it.

## 8. Recommended integration judgement

Overall judgement:

```text
High theoretical relevance.
Strong support for BEBA’s architecture.
Strong challenge to intention-based brand equity models.
Requires candidate ontology development before canonical integration.
```

Most important BEBA refinement:

> Nonprofit brand equity should be modelled as a stakeholder-level architecture that enables behaviour through uncertainty reduction, relational commitment, and moral self-expression, while remaining analytically distinct from intention, loyalty, and observed behaviour.
