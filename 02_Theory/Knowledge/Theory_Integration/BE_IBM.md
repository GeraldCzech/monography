# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claim set strengthens the BEBA position that nonprofit brand equity should not be treated merely as an attitudinal outcome, communication asset, or aggregate brand strength indicator. Instead, brand equity can be theorised as a **behaviour-enabling architecture** that shapes stakeholder action through multiple pathways:

1. **Deliberative pathway**: brand perceptions influence attitudes, perceived behavioural control, and subjective norms.
2. **Direct intention pathway**: overall brand equity may directly influence behavioural intention.
3. **Heuristic pathway**: strong brands reduce cognitive burden under uncertainty.
4. **Relational/satisfaction pathway**: satisfaction may mediate sustained behavioural outcomes.
5. **Context-conditioned pathway**: culture, stakeholder characteristics, value orientations, channel integration, and perceived cost/sacrifice moderate effectiveness.

The claim set therefore supports BEBA’s central research question:

> How do nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty?

It suggests that nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective when brand equity dimensions function as cognitive, relational, normative, and feasibility-enabling structures that help stakeholders decide, justify, and perform pro-social behaviours under uncertainty.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Any claim that brand equity is always necessary for behaviour

Challenged or qualified by:

- `BE-000006`

Brand equity is not a mandatory component of IBM. BEBA should therefore avoid implying that nonprofit behaviour requires brand equity in all cases.

Recommended formulation:

> Brand equity is an enabling or enhancing architecture for behaviour, not a universal prerequisite for behaviour.

## 2. Any overly causal claim based on cross-sectional evidence

Challenged or qualified by:

- `LV-000001`

The evidence base is predominantly cross-sectional. BEBA should avoid strong causal language unless supported by longitudinal, experimental, or verified pipeline evidence.

Recommended wording:

- Use “is associated with,” “may influence,” “can enable,” or “is theorised to support.”
- Avoid “causes,” “determines,” or “proves” unless stronger evidence is available.

## 3. Any assumption that positive brand equity is the only relevant brand condition

Challenged or qualified by:

- `NB-000006`

Negative and ambivalent brand perceptions are not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature. BEBA should not build a one-sided positive-only model of brand-enabled behaviour.

Relevant missing or underdeveloped constructs include:

- Brand skepticism
- Distrust
- Brand avoidance
- Brand ambivalence
- Brand hate
- Perceived hypocrisy
- Reputation damage

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Cultural context

Claim:

- `CC-000001`

Cultural dimensions such as individualism and collectivism may moderate the relevance and behavioural translation of brand equity dimensions.

BEBA implication:

Nonprofit brand effectiveness may vary across cultural settings. A brand cue that enables behaviour in one context may be less effective or differently interpreted in another.

## 2. Perceived cost, sacrifice, or affordability

Claim:

- `PP-000001`

The original claim concerns price perception in consumer purchase contexts. For BEBA, this should be adapted carefully.

Possible nonprofit equivalents:

- Donation amount
- Time commitment
- Emotional burden
- Reputational risk
- Opportunity cost
- Effort required to volunteer
- Perceived inefficiency or waste
- Administrative friction

BEBA implication:

Even strong nonprofit brand equity may not translate into action if the perceived cost or sacrifice of support is too high.

## 3. Stakeholder demographics

Claim:

- `DM-000001`

BEBA implication:

Demographic moderation should remain a candidate boundary condition rather than a canonical claim until more precise evidence is available.

# Recommendation

## 1. Integrate the claim set as support for BEBA’s central architecture

The reviewed claims should be used to support the argument that nonprofit brand equity can operate as a behaviour-enabling architecture rather than merely as a marketing outcome.

Recommended theoretical formulation:

> BEBA conceptualises nonprofit brand equity as a multidimensional behavioural architecture that may enable pro-social action by shaping stakeholder attitudes, perceived feasibility, social expectations, satisfaction, and decision heuristics under uncertainty.

## 2. Preserve brand equity as optional/enabling, not mandatory

Do not define brand equity as a universal requirement for behaviour.

Recommended wording:

> Brand equity enhances, enables, or stabilises behavioural pathways but does not replace the core behavioural mechanisms of established behavioural theories.

## 3. Add or refine a heuristic mechanism candidate

The strongest direct connection to BEBA’s uncertainty question is `BH-000001`.

Recommended candidate mechanism:

> Under uncertainty, brand equity may function as a heuristic decision architecture that reduces cognitive burden and increases confidence in pro-social action.

## 4. Maintain model competition

Do not collapse the claims into one fixed SEM model. The reviewed material supports competing model forms:

- Brand equity as upstream antecedent
- Brand equity as direct predictor
- Brand equity as heuristic shortcut
- Brand equity as configural architecture
- Brand equity as satisfaction-mediated persistence mechanism
- Brand equity as context-moderated effect
- Behaviour through non-brand bypass routes

## 5. Treat nonprofit transfer carefully

Several claims originate from consumer/purchase contexts. Transfer to nonprofit contexts requires adaptation, especially for:

- Price perception → perceived cost/sacrifice/effort
- Purchase intention → donation, volunteering, advocacy, support
- Customer satisfaction → donor/volunteer/stakeholder satisfaction
- Consumer ecological consciousness → value-based stakeholder orientation

## 6. Avoid strong causal claims

Because longitudinal validation is limited, BEBA should use cautious causal language unless verified empirical outputs are available.

Recommended wording:

- “may enable”
- “is associated with”
- “can support”
- “is theorised to influence”
- “may function as”

Avoid unless empirically verified:

- “causes”
- “determines”
- “proves”
- “leads to” in a strong causal sense
