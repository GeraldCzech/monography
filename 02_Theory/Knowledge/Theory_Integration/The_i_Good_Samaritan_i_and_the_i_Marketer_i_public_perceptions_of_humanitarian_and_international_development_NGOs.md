# Input Reviewed

The reviewed input consists of eight related candidate claims derived from a study on UK public perception of nonprofit brands (REVIEW-000086). The claims collectively introduce a central perceptual dichotomy: the "Good Samaritan" (altruistic, mission-driven) versus the "Marketer" (corporate, self-serving) archetypes. They link these archetypes to foundational outcomes like public trust, commitment, and motive attribution, and identify organisational communication as a potential trigger. A key finding is that the "Marketer" perception generates a profound distrust based on perceived motives, which cannot be easily remedied by standard transparency measures.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The core theoretical implication for BEBA is that in the nonprofit sector, the *perceived motive* of the brand, crystallized in public archetypes, is a more powerful determinant of trust and behavioural enablement than perceptions of operational competence or effectiveness. An effective Brand-Enabled Behaviour Architecture (BEBA) must therefore be designed primarily to align with the normative "Good Samaritan" ideal. Failure to do so (i.e., being perceived as a "Marketer") represents a critical failure state that undermines the entire architecture, as it cannot be easily repaired by instrumental signals of accountability.

# BEBA Layer Affected

These claims affect all three primary layers of the BEBA theory:

1.  **Societal Layer:** The "Good Samaritan" archetype is established as a dominant *normative ideal* (BA-000074), setting the context in which all nonprofit brands are judged.
2.  **Organisational Layer:** Organisational choices, specifically brand communication strategies (BC-000040) and accountability mechanisms like transparency reports (TR-000028), are identified as key inputs that shape public perception.
3.  **Individual Layer:** The core mechanism plays out at this level. The perception of archetypes (BA-000073) directly influences individual-level constructs like Trust (TR-000026, TR-000027), Motive Attribution (MA-000006), and Commitment (CO-000010).

# Existing Claims Supported

These findings strongly support existing foundational claims within the BEBA framework, likely including:

*   Claims that link brand perception to stakeholder trust. These findings specify the *mechanism* for this link (archetype alignment).
*   Claims that stakeholder trust is a critical enabler of supportive behaviours (e.g., commitment).
*   Claims that organisational communication shapes brand meaning. These findings identify a specific, high-risk outcome of certain communication styles.

# Existing Claims Challenged

The claims challenge or add critical nuance to several potential BEBA assumptions:

*   Any claim suggesting that demonstrating *effectiveness* or *competence* is the primary driver of nonprofit brand equity. MA-000006 explicitly states the issue is perceived motive, not incompetence.
*   Claims that frame distrust as a problem solvable primarily through increased *transparency* and *accountability*. TR-000028 suggests that when distrust is rooted in motive attribution, such remedies are insufficient.
*   Any linear, attribute-based model of brand perception. The archetype concept suggests a holistic, gestalt-level judgment that can override individual positive performance signals.

# New Candidate Claims

Based on this synthesis, the following higher-level claims should be considered for integration into BEBA:

1.  **C-BEBA-ARCH-01:** The perceived alignment of a nonprofit brand with the societally dominant 'Good Samaritan' archetype is a primary antecedent to stakeholder trust.
2.  **C-BEBA-ARCH-02:** Perception of a nonprofit brand as a 'Marketer' archetype signifies a critical failure in motive alignment, leading to a profound form of distrust that is resistant to standard accountability-based remedies.
3.  **C-BEBA-COMM-01:** The adoption of corporate or competitive communication frames by a nonprofit increases the risk of activating the 'Marketer' archetype in public perception, thereby undermining trust.

# Boundary Conditions

The findings introduce a significant boundary condition:

*   **Cultural Context:** The archetypes were derived from UK focus groups. Their specific form ("Good Samaritan" vs. "Marketer") and salience may be specific to Western, market-oriented societies with a particular cultural history of charity. The model's applicability in other cultural contexts is an open question.

# Model Competition Implications

These claims provide strong grounds for model selection and competition:

*   They favor models of brand equity where *brand meaning*, *normative alignment*, and *motive attribution* are central constructs, over more traditional models focused on awareness, quality signals, or performance metrics.
*   They create a clear competitive test: a model predicting trust from `Perceived Effectiveness` + `Transparency` should be outperformed by a model predicting trust from `Perceived Archetype Alignment`.
*   This challenges the direct applicability of signaling theory (which often focuses on signals of quality/competence) and supports the integration of concepts from attribution theory and institutional theory (normative legitimacy).

# Ontology Candidate Implications

This integration requires updates to the BEBA ontology:

1.  **New Concept:** `Brand Archetype` should be formally adopted as a key perceptual construct.
2.  **New Sub-Concepts/Instances:** `Good Samaritan Archetype` and `Marketer Archetype` should be created as specific instances relevant to the nonprofit sector.
3.  **New Concept:** `Normative Ideal` should be introduced as a Societal-level concept that defines the expected archetype for a class of organisations.
4.  **New Relationship:** A formal relationship should be specified: `Organisational Communication Style` -> `triggers` -> `Brand Archetype Perception`.

# Recommendation

1.  **Integrate Archetype Mechanism:** The "Good Samaritan" vs. "Marketer" archetype dichotomy should be integrated as a core mechanism within the BEBA theory, explaining how brand perceptions are formed and how they translate into trust or distrust.
2.  **Prioritize Motive over Competence:** The BEBA model must be updated to reflect the primacy of *perceived motive alignment* over *perceived competence* as the key mediator between brand activities and stakeholder trust in the nonprofit context.
3.  **Create Ontology Candidates:** Proceed with creating formal ontology candidate files for `Brand Archetype`, `Good Samaritan Archetype`, `Marketer Archetype`, and `Normative Ideal`.
4.  **Flag Boundary Condition:** The UK cultural context must be explicitly noted as a boundary condition for the current formulation of these claims, highlighting the need for cross-cultural replication.
