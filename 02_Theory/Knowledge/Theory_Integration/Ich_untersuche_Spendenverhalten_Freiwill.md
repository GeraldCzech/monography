# Input Reviewed

This review integrates a set of seven candidate claims (H-000004, CC-000009, SI-000010, BC-000043, H-000005, RB-000005, DP-000003) derived from literature on practice theory, specifically Bourdieu's concepts of Habitus and Capital, as applied to nonprofit supporter behaviour. The claims collectively argue that supporter behaviour is not purely a result of conscious, rational deliberation but is deeply embedded in an individual's social position, embodied dispositions (habitus), and routine practices. Key themes include the role of habitus in generating behaviour, the conversion of economic to symbolic capital through donation, the reproduction of social inequality via volunteering, brand choice as an expression of 'taste', and the shift from intentional to routine drivers for recurring behaviours.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The core implication is that BEBA must evolve from a single-process, cognitive-centric model to a **dual-process architecture**. This architecture must account for both a deliberative, intention-driven pathway (dominant in novel situations) and a pre-reflexive, habit-driven pathway (dominant for established, routine behaviours). This introduces a sociological, practice-based foundation that precedes and shapes the psychological constructs often found in brand equity models.

# BEBA Layer Affected

The claims primarily affect the **Individual** layer by introducing pre-reflexive and habit-driven mechanisms. However, they also have significant implications for:
- **Societal Layer**: Claim `SI-000010` directly addresses how supporter practices reproduce societal structures like inequality.
- **Market Layer**: Claim `CC-000009` reframes supporter behaviour within a market of capital conversion, not just a market of mission-support.

# Existing Claims Supported

Without access to the full set of existing BEBA claims, this assessment is provisional. However, these new claims would support any existing BEBA claims that:
- Emphasise the importance of sustained, long-term supporter behaviour (loyalty, retention). Claim `RB-000005` provides a mechanism (shift to routine) for this.
- Posit that brand meaning extends beyond functional attributes. Claim `BC-000043` supports this by framing brand choice as a manifestation of 'taste' and social identity.
- Acknowledge that demographic or psychographic segmentation is important. The concept of habitus provides a deeper, generative theoretical basis for such segmentation.

# Existing Claims Challenged

These claims fundamentally challenge any BEBA claims that are based on a purely rational-choice, utility-maximising, or universally applicable cognitive-deliberative model of supporter behaviour. Specifically, they challenge claims that:
- Model supporter behaviour as a consistently conscious and deliberative process (challenged by `H-000004` and `RB-000005`).
- Assume brand equity drivers (e.g., perceived quality, awareness) operate uniformly across all social strata (challenged by `SI-000010` and `BC-000043`).
- Treat supporter behaviour as an isolated act, disconnected from the individual's broader life and social position (challenged by the entire set of claims).

# New Candidate Claims

Based on the integration of the input claims, the following higher-order claims for BEBA are proposed:

1.  **Claim (BEBA-Dual-Process):** The behavioural effectiveness of a nonprofit brand is determined by its ability to operate within two distinct pathways: a deliberative pathway for behaviour acquisition and a habit-driven pathway for behaviour retention.
2.  **Claim (BEBA-Brand-as-Practice-Mediator):** A nonprofit brand's equity is, in part, its capacity to serve as a socially recognised tool for supporters to engage in practices of taste-making and capital conversion.
3.  **Claim (BEBA-Social-Antecedents):** The psychological antecedents of brand equity (e.g., attitudes, norms, perceived control) are themselves shaped and constrained by an individual supporter's habitus and position in social space.

# Boundary Conditions

This body of theory introduces critical boundary conditions for the BEBA model:

-   **Novelty vs. Routine:** The relative importance of cognitive-deliberative versus habit-driven pathways is conditional on the supporter's history with the behaviour. Deliberation dominates for novel behaviours (e.g., a first-time donation), while habitus/routine dominates for recurring ones (e.g., regular monthly giving) (`DP-000003`).
-   **Social Position:** The mechanisms of brand engagement and their social meaning are not uniform across the population. They are conditioned by an individual's volume and composition of capital (cultural, social, economic) (`SI-000010`, `CC-000009`). For example, the 'capital conversion' mechanism may be far more salient for elite donors.

# Model Competition Implications

This integration fundamentally enriches the principle of 'competing explanations' for the BEBA project. It moves beyond comparing variations of cognitive models to a competition between theoretical traditions. BEBA must now formally specify and test:

1.  **Model A (Cognitive-Intentional):** A model where brand equity constructs (e.g., awareness, associations, loyalty) directly influence intentions (e.g., via TPB), which in turn drive behaviour.
2.  **Model B (Practice-Based):** A model where social structure (Capital) shapes Habitus, which directly generates patterns of behaviour ('taste'), with brand choice being an outcome.
3.  **Model C (Integrated Dual-Process):** A synthesised model where Habitus acts as an antecedent to the components of the cognitive model (e.g., attitudes, norms, PBC), creating a moderated/mediated structure that can account for both routine and novel behaviour. Claim `H-000005` provides the direct theoretical link for this model.

The research should aim to determine the conditions under which each model provides superior explanatory power.

# Ontology Candidate Implications

This integration requires significant expansion of the BEBA ontology. The following concepts must be developed as formal ontology candidates:

-   **`Habitus`**: A core concept representing a system of durable, transposable dispositions that generates practices and perceptions. This is a foundational, non-reflexive individual-level construct.
-   **`Capital`**: To be defined and differentiated into its forms: `Economic Capital`, `Social Capital`, `Cultural Capital`, and `Symbolic Capital`. This is essential for modelling the social space in which supporters operate.
-   **`Practice`**: To conceptualise supporter behaviours not as isolated acts but as socially meaningful, recurring practices (e.g., 'the practice of volunteering').
-   **`Taste`**: A concept linking Habitus to specific consumption choices, including nonprofit brand selection.
-   **`Field`**: A necessary contextual concept from Bourdieu's theory. To be defined as the 'nonprofit field' or 'philanthropic field'—a structured space of positions and competition where capital has value.

# Recommendation

1.  **Adopt a Dual-Process Architecture:** Formally adopt a dual-process framework as a core theoretical commitment for BEBA. This is a major architectural decision. **An ADR should be created** to document this shift.
2.  **Initiate Ontology Development:** Begin creating candidate files for `Habitus`, `Capital` (and its forms), `Practice`, `Taste`, and `Field`. These are foundational and must be defined canonically for the project.
3.  **Update Theoretical Map:** Revise `01_THEORETICAL_MAP.md` to explicitly include Practice Theory (Bourdieu) as a core theoretical pillar alongside existing psychological theories.
4.  **Specify Competing Models:** Draft formal specifications for the three competing models (Cognitive, Practice-Based, Integrated) to guide the next phase of empirical investigation. This will directly address the 'Competing explanations over single models' principle.
5.  **Tag for Future Research:** The claim `H-000005` (Habitus shapes TPB) has low confidence and is noted as a synthesis. This connection is a critical link for the integrated model and must be a priority for further literature review and empirical testing.
