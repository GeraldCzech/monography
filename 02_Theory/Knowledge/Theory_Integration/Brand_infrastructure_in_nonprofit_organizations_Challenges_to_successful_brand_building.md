# Input Reviewed

Candidate claim set from `REVIEW-000021`, focused on nonprofit brand infrastructure, internal brand socialization, leadership-driven value translation, brand orientation, experiential branding, brand infrastructure failure, and resource reallocation.

Reviewed candidate claims:

- `BI-000014` — Leadership support, clear vision, and employee buy-in as prerequisites for nonprofit branding.
- `BI-000015` — Limited marketing budgets not perceived as the primary obstacle.
- `IB-000011` — Employee buy-in as necessary for consistent brand-aligned stakeholder experiences.
- `LD-000003` — Leadership support as necessary for translating brand values into actionable guidance.
- `BI-000016` — Internal implementation as the primary branding challenge.
- `BO-000001` — Nonprofit brand orientation as behavioural construct expressed through activities, structures, and practices.
- `EB-000002` — Nonprofits as suited to experiential and emotional branding through mission/value resonance.
- `BI-000017` — Staff cynicism and lack of shared understanding as barriers.
- `RR-000001` — Substitution of paid communication through internal culture, mission strength, employee passion, PR, and low-cost communication.

Evidence base: exploratory qualitative study with 20 senior nonprofit marketers in the UK. Confidence is mostly medium, with experiential branding and resource reallocation marked low.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The claim set strengthens BEBA’s organisational foundation: nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective not primarily through communication expenditure, but through internal organisational architecture that translates mission, values, and brand meaning into coordinated behaviour.

The strongest theoretical implication is that **brand equity should not be treated only as an external stakeholder perception asset**. In nonprofit settings, it also depends on whether the organisation has the internal infrastructure to make brand meaning actionable for employees and other internal actors.

This supports BEBA’s central logic:

> Nonprofit brand effectiveness emerges when brand meanings are embedded in organisational structures, leadership practices, employee understanding, and stakeholder-facing behaviour.

The reviewed claims therefore reinforce the idea of brand as a **behaviour-enabling architecture**, not merely as a communication symbol, identity statement, or reputational outcome.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## Primary Layer: Organisational Layer

The organisational layer is most directly affected. The claims concern:

- leadership support,
- internal vision clarity,
- employee buy-in,
- staff cynicism,
- shared understanding,
- internal implementation,
- brand-oriented structures and practices,
- resource substitution through organisational practices.

## Secondary Layer: Behavioural Layer

The behavioural layer is affected indirectly because the claims explain how internal conditions shape brand-aligned action:

- employee enactment of brand meaning,
- consistency of stakeholder experience,
- behavioural translation of mission and values,
- implementation of brand promises through conduct.

## Tertiary Layer: Experiential / Stakeholder Interface Layer

The experiential layer is also implicated, especially through `EB-000002` and `IB-000011`, because internal brand alignment is positioned as a condition for consistent, emotionally resonant stakeholder experience.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Communication-output explanations of brand effectiveness are challenged

The claim set challenges any BEBA-adjacent model that treats nonprofit brand effectiveness primarily as the result of external communication volume, campaign quality, or media spend.

The evidence does not show that communication is irrelevant. Rather, it suggests that communication-only explanations are incomplete because internal implementation and organisational alignment are perceived as more critical by senior nonprofit marketers.

## 2. Resource-dependence explanations are qualified

`BI-000015` and `RR-000001` challenge a simple resource-based explanation in which limited marketing budgets are assumed to be the primary obstacle to nonprofit brand building.

However, the challenge should remain cautious. The reviewed evidence concerns practitioner perception, not measured causal effects of budget constraints. Therefore, BEBA should not conclude that budgets do not matter. A more defensible formulation is:

> Financial resources may constrain nonprofit branding, but internal organisational architecture may be a more proximal condition for behavioural brand effectiveness.

## 3. Purely symbolic understandings of nonprofit brands are challenged

`BO-000001`, `IB-000011`, and `LD-000003` challenge the view that nonprofit brands are mainly symbolic identity markers. Instead, they support a behavioural conception of brand orientation as something expressed through practices, structures, and internal activities.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Evidence context: UK senior nonprofit marketers

The evidence is based on perceptions of 20 senior nonprofit marketers in the UK. Therefore, the claims should be bounded to:

- nonprofit organisational settings,
- UK context unless further literature expands scope,
- senior marketer perceptions,
- qualitative exploratory evidence.

The claims should not yet be generalised to all nonprofit organisations, countries, subsectors, or stakeholder groups.

## 2. Perception-based evidence

The claims concern what senior nonprofit marketers perceive as important. They do not demonstrate causal effects on brand equity, donations, volunteering, advocacy, trust, or stakeholder behaviour.

## 3. Internal implementation may matter more where brand delivery depends on employees

The claims are especially relevant for nonprofits where stakeholder experience is mediated by staff, volunteers, service encounters, advocacy work, fundraising interactions, or community relationships.

## 4. Resource substitution may depend on mission strength and organisational culture

The resource reallocation claim is plausible but low-confidence. It may apply most strongly where the nonprofit has:

- a compelling mission,
- emotionally resonant values,
- committed employees or volunteers,
- strong community relationships,
- reputational legitimacy,
- access to earned media or public relations channels.

It may apply less strongly where public visibility, competition for attention, or professional communication demands require higher financial investment.

## 5. Staff cynicism as a failure condition

Internal brand infrastructure may fail where employees perceive branding as superficial, managerial, commercialised, or misaligned with mission. This introduces a possible boundary condition:

> Brand architecture becomes behaviourally effective only when internal actors perceive it as legitimate and mission-consistent.

# Model Competition Implications

## 1. BEBA vs. communication-output model

The claim set favours BEBA over a model in which nonprofit brand equity is primarily generated through communication campaigns, advertising, or external messaging.

A competing communication-output model would predict that stronger brand outcomes mainly follow from more or better external communication. These claims suggest that communication may fail if internal actors do not understand or enact the brand.

## 2. BEBA vs. resource-input model

The claim set qualifies resource-input models. If limited budgets are not perceived as the primary barrier, then financial resources alone cannot explain nonprofit brand effectiveness.

BEBA can incorporate resources, but should model them as enabling or constraining conditions rather than as the central mechanism.

## 3. BEBA vs. identity-symbol model

The claims support BEBA against a purely symbolic identity model. Brand meaning matters, but it becomes effective through behaviour, internal practices, and stakeholder experience.

## 4. BEBA and brand orientation models

`BO-000001` creates a bridge between BEBA and brand orientation theory. It suggests that brand orientation can be interpreted behaviourally: not merely as a strategic posture, but as a pattern of internal activities and routines that sustain brand meaning.

## 5. Potential bypass route around brand equity

The resource reallocation and mission-strength claims may introduce a possible bypass route:

> Mission strength, employee passion, and organisational culture may influence stakeholder behaviour directly, without passing through conventional brand equity constructs.

This is important for BEBA model competition. It suggests that some nonprofit behavioural outcomes may be explained by mission identification, organisational trust, moral commitment, or relational embeddedness rather than by brand equity as traditionally measured.

BEBA should therefore allow comparison between:

- brand equity-mediated pathways,
- mission/values direct-effect pathways,
- internal culture-driven behavioural pathways,
- stakeholder trust or identification pathways.

# Recommendation

## Integrate into BEBA as organisational mechanism evidence

This claim set should be integrated as support for the organisational layer of BEBA. It strengthens the theory’s claim that nonprofit brand equity is behaviourally enabled by internal infrastructure.

## Do not yet treat as causal empirical evidence

Because the reviewed evidence is exploratory and perception-based, the claims should not be stated as proven causal effects. Use cautious language such as:

- “is perceived as necessary,”
- “supports the interpretation that,”
- “suggests that,”
- “indicates a possible mechanism,”
- “qualifies resource-based explanations.”

Avoid stronger formulations such as:

- “leadership support causes brand equity,”
- “budgets do not matter,”
- “employee buy-in guarantees stakeholder behaviour.”

## Preserve separate mechanisms

Do not collapse all claims into a single broad “brand infrastructure” concept. BEBA should distinguish:

- infrastructure conditions,
- leadership translation,
- employee socialization,
- implementation failure,
- resource substitution,
- experiential stakeholder resonance.

## Add model competition note

This review should inform model competition by distinguishing BEBA from:

1. communication-output models,
2. resource-input models,
3. symbolic identity models,
4. direct mission-effect models.

## Candidate integration statement

A suitable integration statement would be:

> The reviewed evidence supports BEBA’s organisational layer by suggesting that nonprofit brand effectiveness depends on internal brand infrastructure: leadership support, shared vision, employee buy-in, and organisational practices that translate mission and brand values into behaviour. The evidence also qualifies communication- and budget-centred explanations by indicating that senior nonprofit marketers perceive internal implementation, rather than financial marketing inputs alone, as the central challenge of nonprofit brand building. These claims should be retained as candidate organisational mechanisms because the evidence is exploratory, perception-based, and contextually limited to senior nonprofit marketers in the UK.
