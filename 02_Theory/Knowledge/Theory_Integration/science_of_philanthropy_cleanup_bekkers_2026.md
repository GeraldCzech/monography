# Input Reviewed

This integration protocol assesses a set of 15 candidate claims derived from `REVIEW-000119`. The claims cover two broad areas:
1.  **Meta-Scientific Findings:** The state of philanthropy research, including the prevalence of replications, retractions, and statistical reporting errors (RQ-000002, RQ-000003, R-000012).
2.  **Robustness of Giving Mechanisms:** An evaluation of which common charitable giving mechanisms are supported by replication studies and which are not.
    -   **Robust Mechanisms:** Direct solicitation, cost reduction (tax/price effects), specific psychological framing ("matching grant", "unit asking"), and perceived efficiency (low overhead).
    -   **Non-Robust Mechanisms:** The identifiable victim effect (in the field), situational reputation cues ("watching eyes"), and public recognition.
    -   **Qualifying/Boundary Conditions:** Motivational crowding-out from material benefits, solicitation context (avoidance cost, fatigue), and cultural/national context for incentives and social cues.

Object type reviewed: **Claim set**
Status of input: **candidate**
Primary BEBA relevance: **Establishing an evidence-based foundation for BEBA by distinguishing robust behavioural mechanisms from non-robust ones, and justifying BEBA's meta-scientific principles.**

# Core Theoretical Implication

The core implication is that the evidence base for nonprofit behaviour is systemically uncertain and prone to error. This elevates BEBA's "Replication before novelty" and "Evidence before preference" principles from methodological preferences to **foundational theoretical necessities**.

Theoretically, this means a BEBA cannot be built on an eclectic mix of popular psychological effects. Instead, it must be constructed from a core set of mechanisms that have demonstrated robustness in field settings. The claims suggest this core includes:
1.  **Action Initiation:** Direct solicitation is a reliable trigger.
2.  **Cost-Benefit Perception:** Perceived cost and efficacy are key levers.
3.  **Communication Framing:** The language used to structure a choice matters significantly.

Conversely, BEBA must be explicitly skeptical of mechanisms based on transient social cues or lab-based effects that fail to replicate in the field. This positions BEBA as a **realist theory of brand-enabled behaviour**, focused on durable structures rather than situational nudges.

# BEBA Layer Affected

-   **Societal (Primary):** The claims on research quality (RQ-*) and cultural moderation (MC-000004, R-000015) directly impact this layer. They define the "uncertainty" of the scientific field in which BEBA operates and establish the cultural contingency of behavioural mechanisms.
-   **Organisational (Secondary):** Claims on framing (PF-*), perceived efficacy (PE-000003), and solicitation fatigue (S-000020) provide direct guidance for the design of an NPO's brand and communication architecture.
-   **Individual (Secondary):** All claims related to specific giving mechanisms (S-*, MC-*, PF-*, PE-*, IV-*, R-*, MB-*) operate at this layer, defining what reliably influences stakeholder behaviour.

# Existing Claims Supported

This claim set provides strong support for several of BEBA's core research principles and assumptions:

1.  **Replication before Novelty & Evidence before Preference:** Strongly supported by RQ-000002, RQ-000003, and R-000012, which quantify the unreliability of the existing literature.
2.  **Brand as Uncertainty-Reduction Mechanism:** Supported by PE-000003, which shows that signals of efficiency (low overhead) increase giving. This is a direct instance of a brand-related perception reducing stakeholder uncertainty about impact, thus enabling behaviour.
3.  **Contextual Contingency of BEBA:** Strongly supported by the claims on cultural moderation (R-000015), national context for incentives (MC-000004), and solicitation context (S-000019). This validates BEBA's avoidance of universalist claims.
4.  **BEBA as a Designed Architecture:** Supported by the robustness of direct solicitation (S-000018) and specific framing tactics (PF-000002, PF-000003). These are not just perceptions; they are designed interventions that facilitate behaviour, fitting the "architecture" concept.
5.  **Focus on Behaviour:** The claims are grounded in behavioural outcomes (giving), aligning with BEBA's "Behaviour before brands" principle.

# Existing Claims Challenged

The reviewed claims challenge several naive or simplistic assumptions that could be incorporated into BEBA if not for this evidence.

1.  **Challenge to Naive Reputation Models:** The failure of situational reputation cues ("watching eyes", R-000013) and public recognition (R-000014) to replicate robustly challenges any BEBA model that equates "brand" with simple, transient social signaling. It forces a more sophisticated model based on durable trust and perceived competence.
2.  **Challenge to Over-reliance on "Classic" Psychological Effects:** The failure of the identifiable victim effect to replicate in the field (IV-000003) is a powerful warning against building a theory on well-known but potentially fragile lab findings. BEBA must prioritize field-validated evidence.
3.  **Challenge to Simple Incentive Models:** The finding that unconditional material benefits can *reduce* giving (MB-000003) introduces the concept of motivational crowding-out, challenging a simple economic model where all incentives are additive. A BEBA must account for the psychological meaning of its components.

# New Candidate Claims

Based on this synthesis, the following higher-level claims are proposed for BEBA:

-   **Claim C-BEBA-04 (Principle of Evidential Conservatism):** The behavioural effectiveness of a Brand-Enabled Behaviour Architecture must be constructed from mechanisms with demonstrated replicability in field contexts, as the baseline rate of non-replication and hypothesis-favouring error in the reference literature is high.
-   **Claim C-BEBA-05 (Durable vs. Situational Brand Effects):** BEBA's effectiveness relies on durable stakeholder perceptions of trust and competence rather than on transient situational cues for social reputation, which are not robustly effective.
-   **Claim C-BEBA-06 (Motivational Crowding-Out Risk):** A BEBA that incorporates material incentives or gifts must be designed to mitigate the risk of motivational crowding-out, where extrinsic rewards undermine intrinsic prosocial motivation.

# Boundary Conditions

This review introduces several crucial boundary conditions for BEBA:

1.  **Field vs. Lab Context:** The validity of a psychological mechanism cannot be assumed to transfer from laboratory to field settings (e.g., identifiable victim effect). BEBA's claims should be conditioned on the context of the supporting evidence.
2.  **Cultural Context:** The effectiveness of social signaling and reputational mechanisms is moderated by cultural norms (R-000015).
3.  **National/Economic Context:** The impact of financial incentives is moderated by national policy and economic context (MC-000004).
4.  **Solicitation Context:** The effectiveness of a direct "ask" is moderated by the stakeholder's ability to avoid the request and their history of prior solicitations (S-000019, S-000020).

# Model Competition Implications

This evidence set provides a powerful filter for model competition.

1.  **BEBA vs. "Nudge"-Based Models:** It positions a robust BEBA model as superior to a model based on a collection of popular but potentially non-replicable "nudges" (e.g., watching eyes, identifiable victim). It forces the competition onto the ground of field-validated evidence.
2.  **Durable Trust vs. Situational Reputation Models:** It sets up a competition between:
    -   **Model A (BEBA/Durable Trust):** Brand enables behaviour through stable perceptions of competence, trust, and well-framed, direct solicitations.
    -   **Model B (Situational Reputation):** Brand enables behaviour through transient social cues that heighten reputational concern in the moment of decision.
    The reviewed claims suggest Model B is likely to fail, strengthening the case for Model A.
3.  **Simple Incentive vs. Motivational Crowding-Out Models:** BEBA must incorporate crowding-out effects, making it more psychologically realistic than purely economic models of giving that treat all incentives as positive.

# Ontology Candidate Implications

This integration suggests the following additions and refinements to the BEBA ontology:

-   **New Concept: `Motivational Crowding-Out`**: Must be added as a candidate concept representing the risk that extrinsic incentives can damage intrinsic motivation. This is a critical mechanism for BEBA to manage.
-   **New Concept: `Evidence Quality`**: A meta-concept or property to be attached to claims and evidence, distinguishing between, for example, `pre-registered-replication`, `field-experiment`, and `lab-study`. This operationalizes BEBA's "Evidence before preference" principle.
-   **Refined Concept: `Reputation`**: The concept must be refined to distinguish between `Durable Reputation` (based on perceived competence, trust, and history) and `Situational Reputation Cues` (e.g., "watching eyes"), with the theory explicitly de-emphasizing the latter.
-   **Refined Concept: `Solicitation`**: This should be modelled not as a simple trigger but as a process whose effectiveness is moderated by `Solicitation Context` (cost of avoidance, frequency/fatigue).
