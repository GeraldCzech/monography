# Input Reviewed

Candidate claim set from `REVIEW-000051` concerning nonprofit brand salience, brand attitude, donor decision involvement, income, and market-position differences in international aid charity choice:

- `BS-000010` — Brand salience positively predicts donor attitude.
- `BA-000061` — Brand attitude positively predicts donation intention.
- `BS-000011` — Brand salience directly predicts brand choice intention independent of brand attitude.
- `BA-000062` — Brand attitude partially mediates the salience–choice intention relationship.
- `DD-000005` — Donor decision involvement strengthens the attitude–choice intention pathway.
- `DD-000006` — Low donor decision involvement strengthens the salience–choice intention pathway.
- `I-000003` — Income moderates pathway reliance: higher-income donors rely more on attitude; lower-income donors rely more on salience.
- `BS-000012` — Prominence and distinctiveness contribute differently to salience depending on nonprofit market position.

Evidence context: new donor acquisition, international aid charities, Australian disaster-relief scenario, intention/choice intention outcomes rather than observed donation behaviour.

# Core Theoretical Implication

This claim set strongly supports BEBA’s central idea that nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective by structuring donor behaviour under uncertainty through multiple mechanisms.

The most important theoretical implication is that brand equity should not be treated as a single linear evaluative pathway. Instead, nonprofit brand effectiveness appears to operate through at least two partially distinct individual-level routes:

1. **Evaluative route**  
   Brand salience increases favourable brand attitude, and favourable brand attitude increases donation or brand choice intention.

2. **Heuristic accessibility route**  
   Brand salience directly affects brand choice intention independently of attitude, especially when donors are less involved or have lower income.

This is highly relevant for BEBA because it suggests that nonprofit brands do not only persuade donors by improving evaluations; they can also reduce decision friction by becoming mentally available, easy to retrieve, and behaviourally accessible in uncertain choice situations.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## Primary layer: Individual

The strongest implications concern the individual donor decision process:

- mental availability,
- attitude formation,
- heuristic choice,
- involvement-dependent processing,
- income/resource-dependent processing,
- intention formation.

## Secondary layer: Market

`BS-000012` affects the market layer by suggesting that salience formation differs according to nonprofit market position:

- larger market-leading organizations may build salience more through prominence,
- smaller organizations may rely more on distinctiveness.

## Possible bridge layer: Individual–Market interface

The reviewed claims also imply an interface between market structure and individual cognition. Market position may shape which salience cues are available to donors, while individual involvement and income shape how those cues are used in choice.

# Existing Claims Challenged

## 1. Purely attitudinal models of nonprofit brand equity

The direct effect of salience on brand choice intention in `BS-000011` challenges any model in which brand attitude is treated as the necessary mediator between brand knowledge and behavioural intention.

BEBA should therefore avoid defining brand equity only as favourable evaluation. A brand may become behaviourally effective because it is accessible, familiar, prominent, or easy to choose, even when evaluative processing is limited.

## 2. Uniform donor-processing assumptions

`DD-000005`, `DD-000006`, and `I-000003` challenge any assumption that all donors process nonprofit brands through the same psychological route.

High-involvement and higher-income donors appear more likely to rely on evaluative attitude-based processing, while low-involvement and lower-income donors appear more likely to rely on salience-based heuristic processing.

## 3. One-size-fits-all salience-building strategies

`BS-000012` challenges the assumption that all nonprofit brands build salience in the same way. Market leaders and smaller organizations may require different routes to salience:

- market leaders: prominence,
- smaller organizations: distinctiveness.

# New Candidate Claims

The following candidate claims could be introduced into the BEBA claim architecture.

## Candidate Claim 1: Dual-route brand-enabled behaviour

Nonprofit brand salience can enable donor choice through both an evaluative route mediated by brand attitude and a direct heuristic route based on mental accessibility.

## Candidate Claim 2: Involvement-dependent route dominance

Under high donor decision involvement, evaluative brand attitude becomes a stronger predictor of nonprofit brand choice intention; under low involvement, brand salience becomes a stronger predictor.

## Candidate Claim 3: Resource-conditioned route dominance

Donor income may condition the dominant brand-enabled behaviour pathway, with higher-income donors relying more strongly on brand attitude and lower-income donors relying more strongly on salience.

## Candidate Claim 4: Salience as more than awareness

In nonprofit donor acquisition, salience should be conceptualised as behaviourally operative mental availability rather than passive awareness.

## Candidate Claim 5: Market-position-specific salience formation

Nonprofit organizations may build brand salience through different routes depending on market position, with prominence mattering more for large market leaders and distinctiveness mattering more for smaller organizations.

## Candidate Claim 6: Partial mediation implies architectural pluralism

Because brand attitude only partially mediates the salience–choice relationship, BEBA should represent nonprofit brand effectiveness as an architecture of parallel and conditional mechanisms rather than a single causal chain.

# Boundary Conditions

## 1. Donor type

The evidence concerns **new donor acquisition**, not necessarily retention, loyalty, volunteering, advocacy, bequests, or recurring giving.

Boundary condition:

> The salience–attitude–choice architecture is currently best supported for new donor acquisition contexts.

## 2. Sector/category

The evidence is bounded to **international aid charities**, specifically in a disaster-relief scenario.

Boundary condition:

> Findings may not generalise automatically to arts, education, health, religious, environmental, or local community nonprofits.

## 3. Behavioural outcome

The reviewed evidence concerns **intention** or **brand choice intention**, not observed donation behaviour.

Boundary condition:

> Behavioural effectiveness is inferred at the intention level; observed behavioural validation remains required.

## 4. Decision involvement

The mechanism varies by involvement:

- high involvement: stronger attitude pathway,
- low involvement: stronger salience pathway.

Boundary condition:

> Brand-enabled behaviour depends on the depth of donor decision processing.

## 5. Income/resource capacity

Income moderates pathway reliance.

Boundary condition:

> Donor socioeconomic resources may shape whether brand choice is primarily evaluative or heuristic.

## 6. Market position

Salience-building route varies by organization size or market position.

Boundary condition:

> Prominence and distinctiveness may not contribute equally to salience across nonprofit brands.

## 7. Geographic context

The reviewed claims are based on an Australian setting.

Boundary condition:

> Cultural, institutional, and giving-market differences may affect generalisability.

# Model Competition Implications

This claim set is important for BEBA model competition because it distinguishes at least three competing or complementary explanations of donor brand choice.

## Model A: Attitude-mediated brand equity model

Basic structure:

```text
Brand Salience → Brand Attitude → Donation / Brand Choice Intention
```

Supported by:

- `BS-000010`
- `BA-000061`
- `BA-000062`

Interpretation:

> Nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective by becoming salient, which improves attitude, which then increases intention.

Limit:

> This model is insufficient alone because salience also has a direct effect on choice intention.

## Model B: Heuristic salience model

Basic structure:

```text
Brand Salience → Brand Choice Intention
```

Supported by:

- `BS-000011`
- `DD-000006`
- `I-000003`

Interpretation:

> Nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective by being mentally available and easy to choose, especially under low involvement or lower resource conditions.

Limit:

> Does not explain cases where evaluative attitude dominates.

## Model C: Conditional dual-route model

Basic structure:

```text
                 → Brand Attitude → Choice Intention
Brand Salience
                 → Choice Intention

Moderators:
- decision involvement
- income
- market position / salience route
```

Supported by:

- `BA-000062`
- `DD-000005`
- `DD-000006`
- `I-000003`
- `BS-000012`

Interpretation:

> Nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective through both evaluative and heuristic routes, with route dominance depending on involvement, donor resources, and market context.

Recommendation:

> Model C is most compatible with BEBA because it treats brand equity as a behaviour-enabling architecture rather than as a single attitudinal construct.

# Ontology Candidate Implications

This claim set suggests several ontology candidate implications.

## 1. Brand Salience should be represented as a mechanism-capable construct

Brand salience should not be treated only as a descriptive awareness construct. In BEBA it likely requires links to:

- mental availability,
- memory accessibility,
- heuristic choice,
- prominence,
- distinctiveness,
- donor choice intention.

Possible ontology relation:

```text
Brand Salience enables Heuristic Brand Choice
```

## 2. Brand Attitude should be represented as an evaluative pathway construct

Brand attitude should be connected to:

- evaluative judgment,
- donation intention,
- brand choice intention,
- high-involvement processing.

Possible ontology relation:

```text
Brand Attitude mediates Salience-to-Donation Intention Pathway
```

## 3. Donor Decision Involvement should be a moderator construct

Decision involvement appears to determine which pathway dominates.

Possible ontology relations:

```text
Donor Decision Involvement strengthens Attitude-Based Choice
Donor Decision Involvement weakens or reduces relative reliance on Salience-Based Choice
Low Donor Decision Involvement strengthens Heuristic Brand Choice
```

## 4. Income should be treated cautiously as a boundary/moderator candidate

Income is potentially important but theoretically under-specified. It may indicate:

- donor capacity,
- perceived donation risk,
- cognitive/resource constraints,
- socioeconomic position,
- affordability of giving,
- lower margin for error in charitable choices.

Possible ontology relation:

```text
Income moderates Brand-Enabled Behaviour Pathway Dominance
```

However, income should not be overinterpreted without further theoretical grounding.

## 5. Market Position should be linked to salience formation

`BS-000012` suggests that market-level brand position affects how salience is built.

Possible ontology relations:

```text
Market-Leading Nonprofit Brand builds Salience through Prominence
Smaller Nonprofit Brand builds Salience through Distinctiveness
```

## 6. Possible new or refined concepts

The claim set may justify candidate concepts such as:

- **Heuristic Brand Choice**
- **Evaluative Brand Choice**
- **Route Dominance**
- **Mental Availability**
- **Prominence-Based Salience**
- **Distinctiveness-Based Salience**
- **Donor Decision Involvement**

These should remain candidates unless already canonical in the repository.

# Recommendation

## Integration recommendation

Integrate this claim set into BEBA as support for a **conditional dual-route architecture of nonprofit brand-enabled behaviour**.

The most important theoretical move is to distinguish:

```text
Evaluative pathway:
Brand Salience → Brand Attitude → Choice / Donation Intention

Heuristic pathway:
Brand Salience → Choice / Donation Intention

Moderators:
Decision involvement, income/resource capacity, market position
```

## Status recommendation

Keep the claims as **candidate** for now because:

- confidence is medium,
- evidence concerns intention rather than observed behaviour,
- evidence is bounded to new donors and international aid charities,
- the empirical context is scenario-based,
- income requires further theoretical interpretation.

## Architecture recommendation

BEBA should explicitly avoid treating brand attitude as the only mechanism of nonprofit brand equity. The theory should allow brand salience to function as a direct behavioural enabler under uncertainty.

## Ontology recommendation

Create or update ontology candidates for:

1. `Brand Salience → Brand Attitude`
2. `Brand Attitude → Donation Intention`
3. `Brand Salience → Brand Choice Intention`
4. `Brand Attitude mediates Brand Salience → Brand Choice Intention`
5. `Donor Decision Involvement moderates route dominance`
6. `Income moderates route dominance`
7. `Market Position moderates salience-building route`

## Caution

Do not generalise these claims to observed donation behaviour, donor retention, or all nonprofit categories without additional evidence. For now, the safest BEBA formulation is:

> In new donor acquisition for international aid charities, nonprofit brand salience appears to support behavioural intention through both evaluative and heuristic pathways, with the relative importance of these pathways depending on donor involvement, income, and nonprofit market position.
