# Input Reviewed

Candidate claim set from `REVIEW-000044` concerning Consumer-Based Brand Equity, Brand Name, Brand Name as Heuristic, and Brand Associations:

- `CB-000013`: Long-term advertising investment is positively associated with consumer-based brand equity among objectively similar brands.
- `CB-000014`: Higher consumer-based brand equity generates greater consumer preference than functionally similar lower-equity brands.
- `CB-000015`: Higher consumer-based brand equity generates stronger purchase intentions than functionally similar lower-equity brands.
- `BN-000001`: Brand name significantly influences consumer preference as a distinct attribute in multi-attribute choice contexts.
- `BN-000002`: Brand name is relatively more important in low-involvement product categories than in higher-involvement service categories.
- `BA-000042`: Higher advertising investment is associated with more favourable brand association structures among objectively similar brands.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The reviewed claims support a central BEBA assumption: brand-related communication can create or strengthen cognitive brand structures, and these structures can shape behavioural precursors such as preference and intention.

However, the claims also qualify BEBA. They suggest that brand-enabled behaviour is not uniform across contexts. The behavioural effectiveness of brand structures appears to depend on involvement, perceived risk, and category type. Brand name effects may be stronger when decision stakes are low and weaker when the decision requires more deliberation or perceived risk reduction.

For BEBA, this means that brand equity should not be treated simply as a universal behavioural driver. It should be theorised as a behaviour-enabling architecture whose influence varies by decision context, uncertainty structure, and the availability of alternative decision cues.

# BEBA Layer Affected

## Primary Layer: Individual Layer

The strongest implications concern the individual decision-making layer:

- brand associations shape preference;
- brand name functions as a heuristic;
- consumer-based brand equity affects intention;
- decision context moderates the role of brand-based cues.

## Secondary Layer: Market Layer

`CB-000013` and `BA-000042` affect the market layer because they connect long-term communication investment to the development of brand equity and association structures. This supports the idea that market-level investment can configure individual-level cognitive architectures.

## Possible Bridge Layer: Communication-to-Cognition Mechanism

The claims imply a cross-layer pathway:

```text
Communication investment
→ brand associations / consumer-based brand equity
→ preference / purchase intention
→ behavioural readiness
```

For nonprofit BEBA, this pathway may become:

```text
Communication / symbolic investment
→ trust-relevant, mission-relevant, identity-relevant associations
→ reduced uncertainty / increased salience / perceived legitimacy
→ donation, volunteering, advocacy, or support intention
```

# Existing Claims Challenged

The claims do not directly falsify core BEBA assumptions, but they challenge overly general formulations.

## 1. Challenge to universal brand-equity effects

If BEBA contains or implies a general claim such as:

> Stronger brand equity reliably produces stronger behavioural outcomes across contexts.

Then `BN-000002` qualifies this. Brand effects appear stronger in low-involvement product contexts than in higher-involvement service contexts.

## 2. Challenge to simple communication-effect causality

`CB-000013` and `BA-000042` use advertising expenditure as a proxy for communication investment and do not definitively establish causality. Therefore, BEBA should avoid claiming that communication investment necessarily causes brand equity unless stronger evidence is available.

More careful formulation:

> Communication investment is associated with stronger consumer-based brand equity and more favourable association structures, but the causal pathway requires further evidence.

## 3. Challenge to direct transfer from consumer goods to nonprofit settings

The reviewed evidence concerns objectively similar brands and includes product/service comparison. It does not directly establish the same mechanism for nonprofit organisations, donation behaviour, volunteering, advocacy, or trust under mission uncertainty.

For BEBA, the evidence is theoretically useful but not sufficient as direct nonprofit evidence.

# Boundary Conditions

The claim set introduces or reinforces several boundary conditions for BEBA.

## 1. Involvement

Brand name effects may be stronger in low-involvement contexts. In high-involvement contexts, individuals may rely more on substantive information, perceived performance, trust, accountability, impact evidence, or social proof.

## 2. Perceived decision risk

The reviewed claims suggest that higher-risk decisions may reduce the relative importance of brand name. In nonprofit contexts, perceived risk may include:

- uncertainty about donation use;
- uncertainty about organisational effectiveness;
- fear of moral misallocation;
- reputational risk of public support;
- uncertainty about mission legitimacy.

## 3. Category type

Effects observed in product categories may not transfer directly to services or nonprofit organisations. Nonprofit brands often involve intangible outcomes, moral claims, trust, and delayed or unobservable impact.

## 4. Functional similarity

Several claims depend on objectively or functionally similar alternatives. Brand equity may become especially relevant when alternatives are difficult to distinguish through objective attributes.

## 5. Communication investment as proxy

Advertising expenditure is used as a proxy for communication investment. This is a boundary condition because communication quality, consistency, credibility, channel, and message content may matter as much as expenditure.

# Model Competition Implications

The claim set affects BEBA’s model competition logic by strengthening brand-equity models while also requiring comparison with alternative explanations.

## 1. Supports brand-equity-mediated models

The claims support models in which communication investment affects behavioural precursors through brand equity or brand associations:

```text
Communication investment
→ brand associations / CBBE
→ preference / intention
```

## 2. Requires comparison with direct communication models

Because the evidence does not definitively establish causality, BEBA should compare mediated and direct models:

```text
Communication investment
→ preference / intention
```

versus

```text
Communication investment
→ brand equity
→ preference / intention
```

## 3. Requires heuristic models

The brand-name claims support a heuristic pathway:

```text
Brand name
→ decision simplification
→ preference
```

This may compete with or complement fuller brand-equity models.

## 4. Requires involvement/risk moderation models

`BN-000002` implies that BEBA should test or theorise moderated models:

```text
Brand cue effect on preference/intention
is moderated by involvement and perceived risk
```

In nonprofit terms:

```text
Brand equity → support intention
is moderated by perceived donation risk, trust uncertainty, or mission involvement
```

## 5. Raises possible bypass route around full brand equity

Brand name may operate independently as a cue without requiring full consumer-based brand equity. This suggests a possible bypass route:

```text
Brand name familiarity / salience
→ heuristic preference
→ behavioural intention
```

This route may bypass richer brand-equity structures such as associations, trust, identification, and perceived quality.

For BEBA, this is important because nonprofit support behaviour may sometimes be triggered by name recognition or salience rather than deep brand meaning.

# Recommendation

Integrate the reviewed claims as a candidate theory input, not as canonical BEBA ontology yet.

Recommended next steps:

1. Preserve the claims as candidate claims linked to Consumer-Based Brand Equity, Brand Name, Brand Associations, Involvement, and Perceived Risk.

2. Create or update a concept workspace for **Brand Name as Heuristic**, because the claims imply a distinct mechanism that may bypass full brand equity.

3. Treat **involvement** and **perceived decision risk** as candidate boundary conditions for BEBA’s behavioural effectiveness logic.

4. Avoid causal wording for advertising investment unless supported by stronger evidence. Use “is associated with” rather than “causes.”

5. Do not directly generalise from consumer goods or service categories to nonprofit behaviour. Mark the nonprofit transfer as theoretically plausible but empirically unverified.

6. Add this claim set to model competition as support for at least three competing pathways:

```text
A. Communication investment → Brand equity → Preference/intention

B. Communication investment → Preference/intention

C. Brand name/salience → Heuristic preference → Intention
```

7. For nonprofit BEBA, reformulate the implication around uncertainty:

```text
Under uncertainty, nonprofit brand cues may become behaviourally effective by reducing decision burden, increasing perceived trustworthiness, and structuring preference; however, this effect is likely moderated by involvement, perceived risk, and the availability of alternative evaluative evidence.
```
