# Input Reviewed

This analysis integrates claims NP-000002, NP-000003, NP-000004, NP-000005, and NP-000006. These claims collectively define a six-dimensional structure for Non-profit Brand Image (NBI) and assess its measurement invariance across UK, Indian, and Bosnian-Herzegovinian cultures.

# Core Theoretical Implication

The core implication is that Non-profit Brand Image is a stable, multidimensional cognitive structure whose conceptual framework and relational meaning are consistent across diverse cultures. However, the expression of this image on measurement scales is subject to cultural response biases. This provides BEBA with a robust, structured concept for brand image while simultaneously imposing strict methodological constraints on cross-cultural comparative research, shifting the focus from "which country has a better brand image" to "do the relationships between brand image and behaviour hold across countries".

# BEBA Layer Affected

-   **Individual Layer:** The foundational claim (NP-000002) defines the six-dimensional cognitive structure of brand image as it exists in an individual's mind.
-   **Societal Layer:** The invariance claims (NP-000003 to NP-000006) operate at the societal level, examining how the individual-level structure behaves and is measured across different cultural groups. It also introduces culture as a potential moderator of measurement.

# Existing Claims Supported

-   Any existing BEBA claim positing a stable, universal architecture for brand perception. The findings of configural (NP-000003) and metric invariance (NP-000004) provide strong evidence that the fundamental building blocks of nonprofit brand image and their relative importance are understood similarly across these diverse cultures.
-   This supports the generalisability of the BEBA framework, suggesting that core mechanisms involving brand image can be modelled and tested in different national contexts.

# Existing Claims Challenged

-   This evidence challenges any naive or implicit claim within BEBA that assumes perceptual constructs like brand image can be directly compared (e.g., via mean scores) across cultures without rigorous invariance testing.
-   The lack of scalar invariance (NP-000005) acts as a direct refutation of simplistic cross-cultural comparisons of brand strength, forcing a more sophisticated modelling approach.

# New Candidate Claims

1.  **Claim:** The BEBA architecture must model Non-profit Brand Image as a second-order construct composed of six first-order dimensions: usefulness, efficiency, affect, dynamism, reliability, and ethicality.
2.  **Claim:** The causal relationships *between* Non-profit Brand Image and other BEBA constructs (e.g., Trust, Commitment, Behavioural Intentions) are theoretically comparable across cultures where metric invariance has been established.
3.  **Claim:** Direct comparisons of mean levels of Non-profit Brand Image across national cultures are invalid unless scalar invariance is demonstrated or appropriate adjustments for response bias are made.
4.  **Claim:** Cultural-level factors (e.g., uncertainty avoidance, power distance, religiosity) moderate the reporting of brand perceptions, introducing systematic bias in measurement that must be accounted for in the Societal layer of BEBA.

# Boundary Conditions

The findings introduce a critical methodological and theoretical boundary condition: **culture moderates the measurement of brand image**. While the conceptual structure is stable (configural invariance) and the meaning of the scale is shared (metric invariance), the way individuals use the response scale is culturally dependent (lack of scalar invariance). Therefore, the generalisability of BEBA's empirical findings regarding *levels* of brand image is bounded by culture.

# Model Competition Implications

-   This six-dimensional model of NBI becomes the benchmark standard within the BEBA framework. Any competing model that uses a unidimensional, ad-hoc, or different multidimensional operationalisation of brand image is now at a theoretical and empirical disadvantage.
-   It forces model competition to move beyond simple path coefficients and to include tests of measurement invariance as a standard for evaluating the robustness and generalisability of a proposed model. A model that is not cross-culturally invariant in its measurement structure would be considered inferior.

# Ontology Candidate Implications

This review provides a strong, evidence-based candidate for the canonical definition of a core BEBA concept.

-   **Concept:** `Non-profit Brand Image`
-   **Type:** Second-order latent construct.
-   **First-order Dimensions (sub-concepts):**
    1.  `Usefulness`: Perceived utility and effectiveness of the NPO's services.
    2.  `Efficiency`: Perception of the NPO as well-managed, non-wasteful, and professional.
    3.  `Affect`: Feelings and emotional responses evoked by the NPO brand.
    4.  `Dynamism`: Perception of the NPO as modern, innovative, and forward-looking.
    5.  `Reliability`: Perception of the NPO as dependable, consistent, and trustworthy.
    6.  `Ethicality`: Perception of the NPO as adhering to high moral and ethical standards.

This structure should be formalized in a new ontology candidate file.
