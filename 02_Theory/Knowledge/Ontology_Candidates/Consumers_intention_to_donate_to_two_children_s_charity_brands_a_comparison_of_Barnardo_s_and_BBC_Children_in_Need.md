# Concept

Brand Typicality

## Canonical Definition

A stakeholder's cognitive judgment of how well a nonprofit brand represents, or serves as an exemplar for, its perceived category (e.g., "children's charity," "environmental organization"). It reflects the degree to which the brand matches the stakeholder's mental schema for that category.

## Alternative Definitions

-   **Prototypicality (Cognitive Psychology)**: The degree to which an item is a good example of its category (Rosch, 1975). Brand Typicality is the application of this concept to branding.
-   **Representativeness (Branding)**: The extent to which a brand is seen as representative of its product class.

## BEBA Layer

Individual Layer. Brand Typicality is a cognitive construct representing a stored mental schema or judgment.

## Parent Concepts

-   `Brand Cognition`
-   `Brand Schema`

## Child Concepts

-   None identified in the current BEBA model.

## Related Concepts

-   `Brand Image`: Brand Typicality can be seen as one dimension of Brand Image, or as a mediator of its effects. The source document suggests a mediating role.
-   `Brand Awareness`: A necessary but not sufficient precondition. A brand must be known to be judged for its typicality.
-   `Intention to Donate Time`: Brand Typicality is a strong positive predictor of this intention.
-   `Intention to Donate Money`: Brand Typicality is a weaker predictor of this intention compared to time donation.
-   `Category Schema`: The mental model of a nonprofit category against which a specific brand is judged.

## Core Claims

-   **BT-000019**: Brand Typicality is a stronger predictor for intention to donate time than money.
-   **BT-000020**: Brand Typicality mediates the relationship between Brand Image and intention to donate time.
-   **Candidate Claim**: The cognitive pathway from brand perception to behaviour is resource-dependent. The pathway for lower-cost support (time) relies more on fast, frugal heuristics like typicality than the pathway for higher-cost support (money).

## Evidence Base

-   The primary evidence comes from the analysis of the paper `Consumers_intention_to_donate_to_two_children_s_charity_brands_a_comparison_of_Barnardo_s_and_BBC_Children_in_Need` (as per `REVIEW-000059`). The findings were observed in a UK-based study comparing an established, high-income charity with an event-driven, lower-income one.

## Boundary Conditions

-   **Behaviour Type**: The predictive power of Brand Typicality is contingent on the specific supportive behaviour being considered. It is more influential for time-based support than for financial support.
-   **Market Context**: The evidence is derived from the children's charity sector in the UK. Its generalisability to other nonprofit sectors (e.g., advocacy, environmental) or different cultural contexts is not yet established.

## Competing Explanations

-   **Brand Familiarity**: It is possible that what is being measured is simply familiarity or salience. Highly typical brands are often also highly familiar, and the unique effect of "typicality" as a category-matching process may be confounded with simple ease-of-retrieval. A model competition should test `Brand Typicality` against `Brand Familiarity` as predictors.
-   **Representativeness Heuristic**: The effect could be a specific instance of the broader representativeness heuristic (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974), where individuals make judgments based on how much a stimulus resembles a prototype. This is less a competing explanation and more of a deeper theoretical embedding.

## Operationalisations

-   Typically operationalised using multi-item Likert scales. Respondents rate the brand on items such as:
    -   "This brand is a good example of a [category] charity."
    -   "This brand is typical of charities in this category."
    -   "When I think of a [category] charity, this brand comes to mind."

## Recommended Glossary Entry

-   **Brand Typicality**: A cognitive judgment of how well a brand represents the "typical" example of its category (e.g., how typical a nonprofit is of the "children's charity" category). It is a key cognitive shortcut that influences behaviour, especially the donation of time.

**Moderate-Low**. The concept is well-established in consumer psychology, but its specific role and boundary conditions in the nonprofit sector are based on a single study within the BEBA evidence base. The finding is theoretically plausible and strong within its context, but requires replication.
