# Concept

Brand Archetype (Nonprofit)

## Canonical Definition

A Brand Archetype in the nonprofit context is a holistic, gestalt-level mental model held by stakeholders to categorise and evaluate an organisation's fundamental character, perceived motives, and normative legitimacy. These archetypes often manifest as a dichotomy between a societally-endorsed ideal (e.g., the 'Good Samaritan') and a delegitimising, market-oriented alternative (e.g., the 'Marketer'). The perceived archetype is a primary determinant of trust and is triggered by organisational behaviour, particularly communication style.

## Alternative Definitions

While the concept of brand archetypes is well-established in commercial marketing (e.g., Jungian archetypes in Mark & Pearson, 2001), the reviewed literature posits a sector-specific application. In the nonprofit context, the archetypes are less about personality and more about the perceived fundamental *motive* of the organisation: whether it is mission-driven and altruistic or self-serving and commercial.

## BEBA Layer

This concept is multi-level and acts as a crucial link between BEBA layers:
- **Societal:** The normative ideal (e.g., 'Good Samaritan') is a societal-level construct that provides the benchmark for judgment.
- **Organisational:** Organisational actions, such as communication strategies, act as the primary triggers for activating a specific archetype.
- **Individual:** The perception of the archetype and its subsequent effects on trust, motive attribution, and commitment occur at the individual stakeholder level.

## Core Claims

1.  **C-BEBA-ARCH-01:** The perceived alignment of a nonprofit brand with the societally dominant 'Good Samaritan' archetype is a primary antecedent to stakeholder trust.
2.  **C-BEBA-ARCH-02:** Perception of a nonprofit brand as a 'Marketer' archetype signifies a critical failure in motive alignment, leading to a profound form of distrust that is resistant to standard accountability-based remedies.
3.  **C-BEBA-COMM-01:** The adoption of corporate or competitive communication frames by a nonprofit increases the risk of activating the 'Marketer' archetype in public perception, thereby undermining trust.
4.  Distrust rooted in the perception of a 'Marketer' archetype cannot be effectively mitigated by signals of operational transparency or effectiveness, as the core issue is perceived motive, not competence.

## Evidence Base

The primary evidence for this concept is derived from `REVIEW-000086`, a qualitative study of UK public perceptions of humanitarian and international development NGOs. The claims emerge from thematic analysis of focus group data.

## Boundary Conditions

- **Cultural Context:** The specific 'Good Samaritan' vs. 'Marketer' dichotomy is empirically derived from a UK sample. Its form and salience may be specific to Western, market-oriented societies. The general mechanism may be more broadly applicable, but the specific archetypes require cross-cultural validation.
- **Sector Context:** This mechanism is hypothesised to be particularly salient for organisations like nonprofits, where a presumption of altruistic motive is foundational to their social license to operate.

## Competing Explanations

This concept competes with models of nonprofit brand equity that prioritise instrumental or competence-based signals. Specifically, it challenges the view that stakeholder trust is primarily a function of:
- `Perceived Effectiveness`: The belief that the organisation achieves its programmatic goals.
- `Perceived Efficiency`: The belief that the organisation uses resources wisely (e.g., low overheads).
- `Transparency`: The availability of operational and financial data.
The Brand Archetype concept posits that a holistic, normative judgment of motive can precede and override these instrumental evaluations.

## Operationalisations

The source literature uses qualitative methods (focus groups) to identify the archetypes. For quantitative research, the concept could be operationalised as a multi-item scale measuring the degree to which an organisation is perceived as aligning with one archetype versus the other.
*Example items:*
- "This organisation seems more like a true charity than a business."
- "I am suspicious of this organisation's underlying motives."
- "This organisation's communication feels authentic and mission-driven."
- "This organisation's advertising feels like corporate marketing."

## Existing Scales

Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature. A new scale would likely need to be developed and validated to measure this construct quantitatively.

## Confidence

- **Theoretical Utility:** High. The concept offers a powerful, parsimonious explanation for a complex phenomenon (motive-based distrust) and directly addresses the core BEBA research question.
- **Empirical Support:** Medium-Low. The concept is well-grounded in qualitative data from a single study. It requires quantitative validation and cross-cultural replication before it can be considered empirically robust.
