# Concept

Theory of Planned Behavior

## Canonical Definition

The Theory of Planned Behavior is an individual-level behavioural theory explaining intention and behavior as functions of attitude toward the behavior, subjective norm, and perceived behavioral control.

Within BEBA, TPB should be treated as a foundational behavioural baseline for explaining charitable donation intention and as a competing/complementary architecture for assessing how nonprofit brand equity becomes behaviourally effective.

## Alternative Definitions

- TPB as a general intention-formation model.
- TPB as a behavioural prediction model for charitable giving.
- TPB as a baseline model against which nonprofit brand-enabled mechanisms must demonstrate additional explanatory value.
- Extended TPB as a flexible framework incorporating moral norms, trust, donor self-identity, past behavior, and contextual moderators.

## Core Claims

1. TPB predicts charitable donation intention through attitude, subjective norm, and perceived behavioral control.

2. Core TPB provides a strong behavioural baseline for donation intention, with reviewed synthesis indicating approximately 44% explained variance in charitable donation intention.

3. TPB explains actual donation behavior less strongly than donation intention, leaving the intention-behavior gap as a central theoretical issue.

4. Perceived behavioral control is frequently a strong predictor in charitable giving contexts.

5. Moral norms improve TPB-based explanation and may outperform subjective norms in some charitable giving contexts.

6. Donor self-identity contributes to donation intention and may support future or repeated donation behavior.

7. Past donation behavior influences both intention and future behavior, creating a possible habit or path-dependence route.

8. Trust strengthens extended TPB models, especially under uncertainty and in online or platform-mediated donation contexts.

9. Digital giving contexts may require distinguishing organizational trust from platform/system trust.

10. Cultural, religious, institutional, and income-related contexts may moderate the salience of TPB predictors.

11. BEBA should not model nonprofit brand equity as a simple direct cause of donation behavior.

12. Nonprofit brands may become behaviourally effective by shaping, amplifying, stabilising, or bypassing TPB-related mechanisms such as trust, perceived control, moral norm activation, donor identity, and intention-to-action translation.

## Boundary Conditions

1. **Intention versus behavior**  
   TPB is stronger for explaining donation intention than actual donation behavior.

2. **One-time versus sustained giving**  
   Long-term donation behavior remains less established than intention prediction.

3. **Digital donation context**  
   Online donation and crowdfunding contexts may depend on platform credibility, system trust, payment security, and digital legitimacy.

4. **Familiar versus unfamiliar organizations**  
   Trust mechanisms may be especially important when donors have limited familiarity with the nonprofit organization or platform.

5. **Cultural context**  
   TPB pathways may vary across cultural settings.

6. **Religious and institutional giving contexts**  
   In some contexts, religiosity, income, or institutionalised giving norms may be more salient than classic TPB constructs.

7. **Past behavior and habit**  
   Repeated giving may become routinised, reducing reliance on active brand evaluation or explicit intention formation.

```text

-> Donation Intention

```

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Theory of Planned Behavior**  
An individual-level behavioural theory explaining intention and behavior through attitude toward the behavior, subjective norm, and perceived behavioral control. In BEBA, TPB functions as a foundational behavioural baseline for charitable giving and as a competing/complementary framework for explaining how nonprofit brand equity becomes behaviourally effective through mechanisms such as trust, perceived control, moral obligation, identity, and intention-to-action translation.

## Confidence

Medium-high.

Rationale:

- Confidence is high for TPB as a behavioural baseline for donation intention.
- Confidence is medium for extensions involving moral norms, trust, self-identity, and past behavior.
- Confidence is lower for strong claims about digital trust as a necessary precondition, cultural dominance of income/religiosity, and long-term sustained giving because these require further source verification.

## Review Recommendation

Promote TPB to a reviewed ontology candidate as a foundational behavioural baseline for BEBA.

Do not yet promote all extended mechanisms to canonical ontology status.

Recommended next steps:

1. Create separate ontology candidates for:
   - Perceived Behavioral Control
   - Moral Norm
   - Donor Self-Identity
   - Intention-Behavior Gap
   - Organizational Trust
   - Platform Trust
   - Digital Trust
   - Past Donation Behavior
   - Sustained Giving
   - Cultural Context

2. Consider an ADR if TPB is integrated formally into BEBA’s core model architecture.

3. Preserve the distinction between:
   - intention formation,
   - intention stabilisation,
   - action execution,
   - repeated giving,
   - sustained donor relationship behavior.

4. Treat nonprofit brand equity not as a direct cause of donation behavior, but as a possible modifier, stabiliser, or enabler of behavioural mechanisms under uncertainty.
