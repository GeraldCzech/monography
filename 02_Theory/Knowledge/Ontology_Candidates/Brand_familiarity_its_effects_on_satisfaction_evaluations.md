# Concept

Brand Familiarity

## Canonical Definition

**Brand familiarity** is an individual stakeholder’s accumulated direct and indirect experience-based knowledge of a brand, which shapes how new brand encounters are interpreted and translated into behavioural readiness.

## Alternative Definitions

- Accumulated direct and indirect brand experience that informs later evaluation and intention formation.
- Stakeholder-level knowledge of a brand derived from prior encounters, exposure, or indirect information.
- A familiarity-based cognitive resource that reduces uncertainty in interpreting brand-related experiences.

## Core Claims

1. **Brand familiarity moderates how stakeholders process experience.**  
   Low familiarity increases reliance on expectation-disconfirmation and post-experience learning, while high familiarity reduces dependence on single-encounter disconfirmation.

2. **Brand familiarity can reduce uncertainty.**  
   Familiar stakeholders possess accumulated experience-based knowledge that makes new encounters easier to interpret.

3. **Low familiarity activates a learning-heavy route.**  
   Under low familiarity, stakeholders are more likely to form behavioural intentions through perceived performance, disconfirmation, satisfaction, and updated expectations.

4. **High familiarity activates a satisfaction shortcut.**  
   Under high familiarity, satisfaction may translate more directly into behavioural intention.

5. **Familiarity is distinct from simple awareness.**  
   Familiarity is grounded in accumulated direct and indirect experience, not merely recognition, salience, or name awareness.

## Boundary Conditions

1. **Sector boundary**  
   The reviewed evidence comes from commercial restaurant-service contexts. Direct transfer to nonprofit brands is theoretically plausible but not empirically established here.

2. **Stakeholder boundary**  
   The original evidence concerns customers. Nonprofit stakeholder roles may include donors, volunteers, beneficiaries, members, advocates, institutional funders, or supporters.

3. **Behavioural outcome boundary**  
   Restaurant repurchase and recommendation intentions may not directly map onto nonprofit behaviours such as donating, volunteering, trusting, participating, advocating, or granting legitimacy.

4. **Experience-intensity boundary**  
   The mechanism may be strongest where stakeholders have direct service encounters. It may be weaker in low-contact, symbolic, emergency-relief, advocacy, or institutional contexts.

5. **Measurement boundary**  
   The reviewed notes indicate that brand familiarity was operationalised with a single self-reported item in at least one source. This is insufficient for canonical measurement without further validation.

6. **Causal boundary**  
   SEM-based evidence should not be overinterpreted as definitive causal proof.

## Competing Explanations

Alternative mechanisms may explain stakeholder behavioural readiness in nonprofit contexts:

- Trust formation
- Perceived legitimacy
- Mission identification
- Value congruence
- Moral obligation
- Perceived impact
- Donor confidence
- Beneficiary vulnerability
- Social proof
- Institutional endorsement
- Prior category experience rather than brand-specific familiarity

BEBA should therefore treat brand familiarity as one candidate moderator, not as the sole explanation for behaviour under uncertainty.

## Operationalisations

Possible operational indicators:

- Self-reported familiarity with the nonprofit brand
- Prior direct experience with the organization
- Prior indirect exposure through media, word-of-mouth, campaigns, or networks
- Perceived knowledge about the organization
- Frequency of prior interactions
- Length of relationship with the brand or organization
- Confidence in understanding what the organization does
- Ability to predict the organization’s behaviour or service quality

Recommended distinction:

- **Brand awareness:** recognition or recall of the brand
- **Brand familiarity:** accumulated experience-based knowledge
- **Brand trust:** willingness to rely on the brand under vulnerability
- **Brand experience:** direct or indirect encounters with the brand

## Existing Scales

Reviewed material indicates use of a single self-reported familiarity item in the restaurant-service context.

- Existing measurement is **not sufficient for canonical adoption**.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Brand Familiarity**  
An individual stakeholder’s accumulated direct and indirect experience-based knowledge of a brand. In BEBA, brand familiarity is treated as a candidate individual-level condition that may reduce uncertainty and alter how stakeholders convert experience, satisfaction, and expectation-disconfirmation into behavioural readiness. It is distinct from simple brand awareness because it implies experiential or knowledge-based accumulation rather than mere recognition.

## Confidence

**Medium** for the general individual-level mechanism in service settings.

**Low to medium** for direct transfer to nonprofit brand contexts.

Rationale:

- The reviewed claims coherently support familiarity as a moderator of satisfaction, disconfirmation, and behavioural intention pathways.
- Evidence is not yet nonprofit-specific.
- Measurement precision is limited.
- Competing nonprofit mechanisms such as trust, legitimacy, moral identification, mission fit, and perceived impact remain plausible.
