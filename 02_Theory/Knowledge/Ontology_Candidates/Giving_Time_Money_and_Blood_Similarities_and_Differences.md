# Concept

Pro-Social Role-Identity

## Canonical Definition

A self-conception tied to the performance of a specific, institutionalized pro-social role. It represents the extent to which an individual integrates a role, such as 'blood donor', 'volunteer', or 'financial supporter', into their personal identity.

## Alternative Definitions

Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature. However, this concept is a specific application of broader identity theories, such as Structural Identity Theory, where identity is defined by the set of meanings one attributes to oneself in a social role. It is distinct from more general self-congruence with a brand, as it is tied directly to the performance of a behavior.

## Core Claims

1.  Pro-Social Role-Identity is a significant and direct predictor of future pro-social behavioral intention. (Derived from RI-000001)
2.  Past pro-social behavior is a primary antecedent to the formation and strengthening of a corresponding Pro-Social Role-Identity. (Derived from RI-00002)
3.  Pro-Social Role-Identity mediates the influence of antecedents like personal norms and social expectations on behavioral intention. (Derived from RI-00005)
4.  The formation of a Pro-Social Role-Identity creates a self-reinforcing feedback loop where behavior strengthens identity, which in turn motivates sustained future behavior. (Derived from Core Theoretical Implication in the input)

## Evidence Base

The evidence is synthesized in `REVIEW-000068`, which covers claims RI-000001 to RI-000005. The primary evidence comes from studies comparing the drivers of donating time, money, and blood. These studies establish that role-identity is a key psychological mechanism for sustained, institutionalized helping behaviors.

## Boundary Conditions

-   **Institutionalized Behaviour:** The model is most applicable to formal, institutionalized pro-social behaviors (e.g., volunteering for an NPO, donating blood to a blood bank) rather than informal, spontaneous helping.
-   **Repeated Behaviour:** The concept has the most explanatory power for repeated or sustained behaviors, where a feedback loop between past action and identity can be established. Its relevance is lower for novel or one-off actions.

## Competing Explanations

-   **Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB):** Simpler TPB models explain intention primarily through attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control, without a central role for identity. The Pro-Social Role-Identity model posits identity as a more proximate and powerful predictor of intention for this class of behaviors.
-   **Habit-Only Models:** Models that treat repeated behavior purely as an automatic, non-conscious habit (`Past Behaviour -> Intention`) fail to capture the reflective, meaning-making function of identity that helps sustain behavior, particularly when circumstances change.

## Operationalisations

The concept is typically operationalized using multi-item self-report scales. Participants are asked to rate their agreement with statements that reflect the extent to which a specific pro-social role is central to their self-concept (e.g., "I see myself as a volunteer," "Being a blood donor is an important part of who I am").

## Existing Scales

Specific scales exist for child concepts, such as the Volunteer Identity scale. The exact scales used in the source literature for `REVIEW-000068` should be consulted for canonical measurement models.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Pro-Social Role-Identity:** A component of an individual's self-concept derived from performing a specific pro-social role, such as 'volunteer' or 'blood donor'. It is formed through past behavior and serves as a key psychological driver for sustained future engagement in that role.

## Confidence

High. The concept is well-established in the social psychology of pro-social behavior, and the evidence base reviewed provides strong support for its role as a key mediator and predictor of intention.

## Review Recommendation

Propose for inclusion in the canonical ontology. This concept is critical for operationalizing the "behaviour-first" principle of BEBA. It provides the core mechanism linking initial actions to sustained commitment. This candidate should also be used to inform an ADR regarding the necessity of behavior-specific models within the BEBA framework.
