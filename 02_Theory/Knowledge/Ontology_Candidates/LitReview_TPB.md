# Concept

Brand Equity–Behavior Integration

## Canonical Definition

Brand Equity–Behavior Integration refers to the proposed BEBA model-level relation through which nonprofit brand equity becomes behaviourally effective by shaping the behavioural conditions of giving, including perceived agency, trust, moral obligation, normative salience, intention formation, and contextual feasibility under uncertainty.

## Alternative Definitions

- The integration of nonprofit brand equity constructs with behavioural decision mechanisms.
- A theory-integration model explaining how brand-related perceptions influence donation intention and donation behaviour through behavioural architecture rather than through brand liking alone.
- A BEBA candidate model linking nonprofit brand assets to extended TPB / IBM-like behavioural determinants.

## Core Claims

1. Nonprofit brand equity is behaviourally effective when it shapes the behavioural conditions under which giving becomes intended, possible, normatively supported, morally salient, trustworthy, and contextually appropriate.

2. Behaviour-specific attitudes toward donating should be distinguished from general favourable attitudes toward the nonprofit organisation.

3. General NPO brand attitude should not be assumed to directly predict donation intention when behavioural predictors are included.

4. Perceived Behavioral Control / perceived donation agency is central to the behavioural enablement logic of BEBA.

5. Moral norms are important nonprofit-specific behavioural determinants and extend the original TPB model.

6. Donation intention remains the proximal behavioural outcome preceding realised donation behaviour.

7. Past donation behaviour creates behavioural path dependence by shaping future donation intention and future giving behaviour.

8. Organisational trust functions as an uncertainty-reduction mechanism by reducing perceived risk and increasing confidence in donation action.

9. Organisational and leadership reputation can reconfigure the behavioural architecture of giving, especially under reputational uncertainty.

10. Normative influence in nonprofit giving is conditional and should not be modelled as a single undifferentiated construct.

11. Digital and platform-mediated donation contexts require attention to channel trust, platform trust, and social presence.

12. BEBA should be positioned as an integrative theory connecting multidimensional nonprofit brand equity with an IBM-like behavioural architecture.

## Boundary Conditions

1. Reputational uncertainty  
Negative organisational or leadership reputation may weaken attitude-based and moral-norm-based pathways while increasing reliance on subjective norms or identification.

2. Donation channel  
Online, crowdfunding, and platform-mediated donation environments may increase the relevance of platform trust, channel credibility, and social presence.

3. Norm type  
Subjective norms, descriptive norms, injunctive norm appeals, and social presence should remain analytically distinct.

4. Audience state  
Audience involvement and pre-existing beliefs may moderate communication-framing and norm-appeal effects.

5. Cultural, religious, and economic context  
Macro-context may change the relative strength of attitude, norm, moral obligation, and perceived control pathways.

6. Behavioural history  
Past donation behaviour may influence future intention and future behaviour through habit, behavioural memory, or path dependence.

## Competing Explanations

1. Simple Brand Attitude Model  
Structure:  
NPO brand attitude → donation intention → donation behaviour

Assessment: Weakened.  
Reason: General NPO brand attitude appears not consistently significant when behavioural predictors are included.

2. Original TPB Model  
Structure:  
Attitude + subjective norms + perceived behavioural control → intention → behaviour

Assessment: Partially supported but insufficient.  
Reason: It captures important behavioural determinants but omits moral norms, past behaviour, trust, reputation, and channel context.

3. Extended TPB Model  
Structure:  
Attitude + subjective norms + perceived behavioural control + moral norms + past behaviour → intention → behaviour

Assessment: Strong intermediate competitor.  
Reason: Moral norms and past behaviour appear important in charitable giving contexts.

4. IBM-like Behavioural Architecture  
Structure:  
Attitudes + perceived norms + personal agency + knowledge/salience + environmental constraints + habit/past behaviour → intention/behaviour

Assessment: Theoretically strengthened.  
Reason: Several reviewed constructs overlap with IBM-style components, though the reviewed literature should not be described as already adopting IBM unless independently verified.

5. BEBA Model  
Structure:  
Brand equity dimensions → behavioural mechanisms → donation intention → donation behaviour, moderated by uncertainty, reputation, channel, audience state, and context.

Assessment: Preferred candidate integration model.  
Reason: It explains how nonprofit brand equity becomes behaviourally effective rather than assuming direct effects of brand liking.
