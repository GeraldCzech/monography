# Concept

Nonprofit Brand Authenticity

## Canonical Definition

Nonprofit brand authenticity is the perceived degree to which a nonprofit organisation is regarded by a relevant stakeholder audience as the quintessential exemplar of a recognised nonprofit, cause, service, or mission category.

## Alternative Definitions

- Brand authenticity as internal organisational sincerity.
- Brand authenticity as fidelity to founding values or heritage.
- Brand authenticity as moral purity or ethical consistency.
- Brand authenticity as credibility, trustworthiness, or reliability.
- Brand authenticity as first-mover, pioneer, or original status.
- Brand authenticity as a multidimensional composite including familiarity, heritage, credibility, identification, or behavioural loyalty.

These alternatives should not be treated as canonical within BEBA unless separately justified by reviewed literature.

## BEBA Layer

Primary layer: Market Layer

Secondary layer: Individual Layer

Tertiary layer: Organisational Layer

Rationale: Authenticity is category-relative and audience-conferred. It depends on how stakeholders perceive a nonprofit within a recognised category or comparative field.

## Core Claims

1. Nonprofit brand authenticity is best treated as perceived category exemplarity rather than internal sincerity, moral purity, or heritage.

2. Authenticity is conferred by stakeholder audiences and should not be treated as an intrinsic organisational property.

3. Authenticity requires a recognisable category against which the nonprofit can be evaluated.

4. Authenticity may reduce stakeholder uncertainty by making one nonprofit appear especially fitting, representative, or category-defining.

5. Authenticity perceptions may differ across stakeholder groups.

6. Authenticity should be separated from antecedents, cues, correlates, and outcomes such as trust, credibility, familiarity, heritage, identification, loyalty, and behavioural support.

7. Being first, oldest, or pioneering in a category is neither necessary nor sufficient for authenticity.

8. The nomological role of authenticity in BEBA remains unresolved. It may operate as antecedent, mediator, moderator, or outcome in different model specifications.

Evidence summary:

- The reviewed claim set supports an exemplar-based understanding of authenticity.
- It supports treating authenticity as perception-based, audience-conferred, and category-relative.
- It challenges multidimensional authenticity definitions that combine authenticity with antecedents or outcomes.
- It supports applying exemplar-based brand authenticity to nonprofit organisations.
- It does not settle whether authenticity is an antecedent, mediator, moderator, or outcome of brand strength.

## Boundary Conditions

1. Recognisable category condition  
   Authenticity requires a recognised category. It is weaker or less applicable when the cause domain is nascent, ambiguous, hybrid, or unknown to stakeholders.

2. Audience-specific perception condition  
   Donors, volunteers, beneficiaries, policymakers, professionals, and local communities may differ in which organisation they perceive as authentic.

3. Measurement validity condition  
   Authenticity should not be measured using scales that include trust, credibility, familiarity, heritage, identification, personal utility, or loyalty unless these are explicitly modelled as separate constructs.

4. Non-pioneer condition  
   Pioneer status is not equivalent to authenticity.

5. Unresolved nomological condition  
   The causal role of authenticity in relation to brand strength and behaviour remains unsettled.

## Competing Explanations

1. Brand Strength → Authenticity → Behaviour  
   Authenticity may mediate the relationship between brand strength and behavioural support. Status: plausible but unresolved.

2. Brand Strength × Authenticity → Behaviour  
   Authenticity may moderate the effect of brand strength on behaviour. Status: plausible but unresolved.

3. Authenticity → Trust/Credibility → Behaviour  
   Authenticity may support trust or credibility, which then enable behaviour. Status: possible, but requires separate evidence.

4. Category Recognition → Authenticity → Behaviour  
   Authenticity may only operate when stakeholders recognise the relevant category. Status: strong candidate boundary model.

5. Stakeholder Group → Authenticity Perception → Behaviour  
   Authenticity may vary across stakeholder audiences and therefore produce different behavioural effects. Status: strong candidate segmentation or multigroup model.

## Operationalisations

Recommended operational focus:

- Perceived degree to which the nonprofit represents the category.
- Perceived fit with the cause, service, mission, or nonprofit type.
- Perceived quintessentiality or category exemplarity.
- Audience-specific judgement of being the most fitting or representative organisation in a category.

Avoid operationalisations that directly include:

- trust,
- credibility,
- familiarity,
- heritage,
- loyalty,
- behavioural intention,
- identification,
- personal utility,
- ethical purity,
- internal consistency,
- organisational sincerity.

## Existing Scales

Existing multidimensional brand authenticity scales require construct-level review before use.

Scales should be assessed for contamination with:

- credibility,
- trust,
- familiarity,
- heritage,
- consistency,
- identification,
- personal relevance,
- behavioural loyalty,
- behavioural intention.

No existing scale should be adopted as canonical from this review alone.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

Nonprofit Brand Authenticity: The perceived degree to which a nonprofit organisation is regarded by a relevant stakeholder audience as the quintessential exemplar of a recognised nonprofit, cause, service, or mission category. In BEBA, this should be treated as an audience-conferred, category-relative perception, not as internal sincerity, moral purity, heritage, pioneer status, trust, or loyalty.

## Confidence

Medium for the conceptual distinction and exemplar-based definition.

Medium for the audience-specific and category-recognition boundary conditions.

Medium-low for authenticity as a behavioural enablement mechanism.

Low for Brand Strength as an antecedent of authenticity.

## Review Recommendation

Integrate as an ontology candidate and candidate BEBA mechanism, not as a canonical construct yet.

Recommended review actions:

1. Manually compare the exemplar-based definition with existing BEBA brand perception constructs.
2. Review existing brand authenticity scales for construct contamination.
3. Decide whether the primary canonical concept should be `Brand Authenticity` or `Nonprofit Brand Authenticity`.
4. Treat authenticity as a model-competition construct rather than a settled pathway.
5. Do not update the canonical ontology until the relationship between authenticity, brand strength, trust, credibility, and behaviour has been further reviewed.
