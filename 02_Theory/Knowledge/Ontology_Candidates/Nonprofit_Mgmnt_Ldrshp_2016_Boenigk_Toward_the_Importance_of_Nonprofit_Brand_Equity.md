# Concept

Stakeholder-Based Nonprofit Brand Equity

## Canonical Definition

Stakeholder-based nonprofit brand equity is a candidate individual-level behaviour-enabling architecture composed of nonprofit brand awareness, nonprofit brand trust, and nonprofit brand commitment, through which a nonprofit brand may reduce stakeholder uncertainty and stabilize supportive orientation toward the organization.

## Alternative Definitions

- A multidimensional stakeholder-side construct consisting of nonprofit brand awareness, nonprofit brand trust, and nonprofit brand commitment.
- A nonprofit-specific form of brand equity in which awareness enables cognitive access, trust reduces perceived risk, and commitment stabilizes the stakeholder relationship.
- A stakeholder-side heuristic or quality signal that may simplify nonprofit support decisions under uncertainty.

## Core Claims

1. Stakeholder-based nonprofit brand equity can be conceptualized as a multidimensional architecture composed of awareness, trust, and commitment.

2. Nonprofit brand awareness functions as an access condition because stakeholders must recognize or recall the nonprofit before trust and commitment can develop.

3. Nonprofit brand trust is central in nonprofit contexts because stakeholders often face uncertainty about ethical conduct, resource use, and mission fidelity.

4. Nonprofit brand commitment stabilizes the stakeholder relationship by supporting an enduring willingness to maintain a valued relationship with the nonprofit brand.

5. Nonprofit brand equity requires nonprofit-specific theorisation and should not be directly imported from commercial brand equity models without adaptation.

6. High nonprofit brand equity may function as a stakeholder-side heuristic that simplifies decision-making when nonprofit performance or internal conduct cannot be directly observed.

7. The link from awareness, trust, and commitment to actual supportive behaviour remains candidate-level unless supported by additional reviewed literature or verified empirical pipeline outputs.

## Evidence Base

Based on reviewed claim set `NB-000028` to `NB-000034` from `REVIEW-000023`, concerning Boenigk et al. 2016 in *Nonprofit Management & Leadership*.

The reviewed claims support the view that nonprofit brand equity consists of:

1. nonprofit brand awareness,
2. nonprofit brand trust,
3. nonprofit brand commitment.

The evidence is strongest for the perceptual and relational architecture of nonprofit brand equity. Behavioural conversion into donation, volunteering, advocacy, retention, or other supportive behaviours is **not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature**.

## Boundary Conditions

- The reviewed framework appears to be linked to well-known nonprofit brands.
- The reviewed context appears to be German; institutional and cultural generalisability should not be assumed.
- Stakeholder groups may differ, including donors, volunteers, beneficiaries, members, employees, institutional funders, advocacy supporters, and campaign participants.
- The mechanism is most relevant under uncertainty, information asymmetry, and limited observability of nonprofit conduct.
- The behavioural pathway from brand equity to actual support behaviour remains insufficiently established.
- The framework may operate differently for small, unknown, new, local, grassroots, crisis-response, or unfamiliar international nonprofits.

## Competing Explanations

1. **Flat multidimensional model**

```text
Nonprofit Brand Equity
= Awareness + Trust + Commitment
```

2. **Sequential enabling model**

```text
Awareness
→ Trust
→ Commitment
→ Behaviour
```

3. **Trust-dominant model**

```text
Awareness
→ Trust
→ Behaviour
Commitment as stabilizer or moderator
```

4. **Bypass route**

Stakeholders may support a nonprofit without fully developed brand equity through:

- direct personal experience,
- peer recommendation,
- institutional endorsement,
- emergency need salience,
- moral obligation,
- religious or political identity,
- situational urgency.

Candidate bypass formulation:

```text
External cue / direct experience / moral urgency
→ trust or action
without fully developed nonprofit brand equity
```

## Operationalisations

Candidate operationalisation should treat stakeholder-based nonprofit brand equity as either:

1. a multidimensional construct composed of awareness, trust, and commitment, or  
2. a sequential enabling architecture in which awareness precedes trust, trust supports commitment, and commitment may increase likelihood of supportive behaviour.

Possible dimensions:

- recognition or recall of the nonprofit brand,
- belief that the nonprofit acts ethically,
- belief that the nonprofit uses resources appropriately,
- belief that the nonprofit remains faithful to its mission,
- willingness to maintain a valued relationship with the nonprofit,
- intention to support, if behavioural outcomes are included.

Behavioural indicators should not be inferred unless supported by verified literature or empirical pipeline outputs.
