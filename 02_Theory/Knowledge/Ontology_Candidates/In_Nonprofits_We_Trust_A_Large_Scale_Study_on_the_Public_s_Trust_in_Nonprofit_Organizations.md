# Concept
`Stable Individual Context`

## Canonical Definition
The set of stable, pre-existing individual characteristics, including demographics and general dispositions (e.g., generalized trust), that serve as a primary antecedent and moderator in the formation of an individual's trust in an organization.

## Alternative Definitions
Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature. This concept is a synthesis derived from empirical findings within the BEBA research programme.

## BEBA Layer
Individual Layer

## Parent Concepts
- `Individual Layer Antecedents`
- `Stakeholder Characteristics`

## Child Concepts
- `Demographic Characteristics` (e.g., Age, Gender, Income)
- `Generalized Trust` (Disposition to Trust)
- `Socio-Cultural Identity` (e.g., Religious affiliation, Political orientation)

## Related Concepts
- `Trust`: `Stable Individual Context` is a primary antecedent of Trust.
- `Stakeholder Evaluation`: Acts as a co-equal antecedent of Trust alongside `Stable Individual Context`.
- `Brand Signal`: The effect of `Brand Signal` on Trust is moderated by `Stable Individual Context`.

## Core Claims
1.  A majority of the explainable variance in public trust in NPOs is attributable to the `Stable Individual Context`, not to the objective characteristics of the organization.
2.  The `Stable Individual Context` serves as a significant filter or moderator, conditioning the (weak) effect of organizational brand signals on trust formation.
3.  The overall trust judgment is a function of both the `Stable Individual Context` and a more dynamic, target-specific `Stakeholder Evaluation`.

## Evidence Base
The primary evidence comes from the large-scale, multi-level analysis in "In_Nonprofits_We_Trust_A_Large_Scale_Study_on_the_Public_s_Trust_in_Nonprofit_Organizations", as synthesized from claims TR-000014 through TR-000023. Specifically:
-   `TR-000015`: Establishes that variance in trust is roughly equally split between a stable "truster effect" (captured by this concept) and a specific person-organization evaluation.
-   `TR-000014` and `TR-000016`: Demonstrate the dominance of individual-level variance over organizational-level variance in explaining trust.
-   `TR-000020`, `TR-000021`, `TR-000022`: Provide direct evidence for the role of specific demographic child concepts (age, gender, income) in shaping trust.

## Boundary Conditions
-   **Cultural Context**: The evidence is derived from a largely secular German context. The composition and relative importance of this context may vary in other socio-cultural environments.
-   **Stakeholder Type**: The evidence pertains to the general public. The influence of `Stable Individual Context` might be lower for expert stakeholders (e.g., foundation managers) who rely more on systematic organizational due diligence.

## Competing Explanations
This concept challenges brand-centric models that posit organizational signals as the primary, direct drivers of stakeholder trust. It positions those models as explaining, at best, a minor portion of the variance in trust, and suggests their effects are conditional upon this stable individual context.

## Operationalisations
As a higher-order concept, `Stable Individual Context` is not measured directly. It is operationalized by a set of indicators measuring its child concepts:
-   Standard demographic survey questions (for age, gender, income, etc.).
-   Validated psychometric scales for generalized trust or disposition to trust.

## Existing Scales
-   Scales for Generalized Trust are widely available, for example, from the World Values Survey or established social psychology research.

## Recommended ConstructLedger Updates
-   Create a new entry for `Stable Individual Context`.
-   Classify it as a second-order or aggregate construct.
-   Link it to its first-order child constructs: `Age`, `Gender`, `Income`, `Generalized Trust`.
-   Define its relationship to `Trust` as `is_antecedent_of` and `moderates_effect_on`.

## Recommended Glossary Entry
**Stable Individual Context**: The set of an individual's enduring characteristics (e.g., demographics, generalized trust) that act as a baseline filter for perceiving and trusting organizations.

## Confidence
High. The concept is directly and strongly supported by large-scale empirical evidence that challenges simpler, brand-centric models of trust formation. It is a necessary theoretical construct to account for the observed variance structure in the data.

## Review Recommendation
Promote to canonical ontology. This concept is critical for recalibrating the BEBA theory of trust formation. It establishes the individual, not the brand, as the primary locus of explanation. Further work should focus on its precise interaction with the `Stakeholder Evaluation` concept.
