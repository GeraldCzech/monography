# Concept

Institutional Brand Equity

## Canonical Definition

A stakeholder-specific form of brand equity representing the value accrued to a nonprofit brand in the perception of institutional actors (e.g., government agencies, public funders, policymakers). This value is based on perceptions of the organisation's administrative competence, compliance, reliability, and political alignment, which in turn enables behaviours such as securing public contracts, maintaining legitimacy, and acquiring institutional resources.

## Alternative Definitions

-   Contrast with **Public Brand Equity**: While public brand equity is oriented towards the general public and based on dimensions like awareness, image, and loyalty to enable donations or volunteering, institutional brand equity is oriented towards state actors to enable resource acquisition through political and administrative processes.
-   Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature under this specific term. The concept is synthesized from observations of state-nonprofit interdependence.

## BEBA Layer

-   **Organisational Layer**: It is an intangible asset of the nonprofit organisation.
-   **Market Layer**: It functions within the "institutional resource market" where nonprofits compete for grants and contracts, as opposed to the "public resource market" for donations.
-   **Societal Layer**: Its relevance and function are determined by the societal context, specifically the nature of the state-nonprofit relationship (e.g., corporatism).

## Core Claims

1.  In institutional environments with high state dependency, Institutional Brand Equity is a more significant driver of nonprofit resource acquisition and resilience than Public Brand Equity.
2.  The primary behavioural function of Institutional Brand Equity is to signal fitness as a state partner, thereby securing public funding and contracts.
3.  The dimensions of Institutional Brand Equity (e.g., perceived compliance, competence) are distinct from and may even trade off against the dimensions of Public Brand Equity (e.g., perceived warmth, grassroots authenticity).

## Evidence Base

This concept is a theoretical inference derived from the synthesis of claims regarding the Austrian nonprofit sector, which is characterized by a corporatist state-nonprofit partnership. The primary evidence is the logical need for a mechanism to explain brand function in a context where public funders, not private donors, are the key resource providers.

Supporting claims from the BEBA system:
-   `IE-000002`: Corporatist welfare partnership defines the environment.
-   `PF-000001`: High dependency on public funding makes funders the primary audience.
-   `A-000014`: Accountability pressures from funders shape organisational behaviour.
-   `CR-000008`: State partnerships, not public support, are a key source of crisis resilience.

## Boundary Conditions

-   **High Relevance**: In corporatist welfare states or any nonprofit sector with high dependency on government funding (e.g., social and health services in Austria).
-   **Low Relevance**: In liberal market-oriented nonprofit sectors where private donations and individual giving are the primary sources of funding (e.g., the US model for certain sub-sectors).

## Competing Explanations

-   **Public Brand Equity spillover**: The idea that a strong public brand simply creates a positive halo that influences institutional actors, without a distinct form of equity being necessary.
-   **Political Capital / Lobbying**: The idea that resource acquisition is purely a function of political connections and lobbying efforts, independent of any brand-related perceptions.
-   **Objective Performance**: The idea that contract allocation is based solely on objective, verifiable performance metrics, with no role for intangible brand perceptions.

## Operationalisations

-   **Survey-based**: Measurement through surveys of key institutional stakeholders (e.g., public administrators in funding agencies) assessing perceptions of NPO competence, reliability, etc.
-   **Behavioural**: Analysis of administrative data, such as funding application success rates, controlling for project quality and organisational size.
-   **Textual Analysis**: Content analysis of official reports, funding proposals, and government evaluations for language related to trust, competence, and reliability.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Institutional Brand Equity**: A form of brand equity based on the perceptions of institutional stakeholders, such as government funders. It reflects the brand's value in signaling administrative competence, reliability, and compliance, enabling the organisation to secure public contracts and legitimacy.
