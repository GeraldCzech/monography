# Concept

Perceived Authenticity

## Canonical Definition

The extent to which stakeholders judge an organization's stated prosocial intentions and actions to be sincere, genuine, and aligned with its core values, rather than driven by opportunistic or selfish motives.

## Alternative Definitions

Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature. However, related literature on brand authenticity often includes dimensions such as continuity (brand being true to itself), credibility (being true to its consumers), and integrity (being true to its values). These could inform alternative conceptualisations.

## BEBA Layer

-   **Individual Layer:** As a core perceptual and attributional construct, it resides at the individual stakeholder level. It functions as a psychological filter through which organisational actions are interpreted.
-   **Organisational Layer:** It is a critical mediator of the outcomes of organisational-level strategies (e.g., Cause-Related Marketing, partnerships, social initiatives), directly impacting their success or failure.

## Parent Concepts

-   Stakeholder Perception
-   Attribution Theory

## Child Concepts

-   Motive Attribution (Prosocial vs. Selfish)
-   Perceived Sincerity
-   Value Congruence

## Related Concepts

-   **Brand Reputation:** A distinct but related concept. Reputation is a cumulative, historical judgment about the organization, while perceived authenticity is often a more immediate judgment about a specific action or campaign in light of that reputation.
-   **Stakeholder Skepticism:** A general disposition that likely moderates the formation of authenticity perceptions. Higher skepticism makes a positive authenticity judgment less likely.
-   **Brand-Cause Fit:** A likely antecedent. A high degree of fit between the brand and the social cause can increase the likelihood of an action being perceived as authentic.
-   **Trust:** A likely outcome of sustained perceptions of authenticity.

## Core Claims

-   The effectiveness of a Brand-Enabled Behaviour Architecture is mediated by stakeholder perception of the organization's authenticity (C-BEBA-NEW-01).
-   Brand-led social initiatives perceived as inauthentic or selfish can negatively impact Brand Equity and suppress, rather than enable, prosocial behaviour (C-BEBA-NEW-02, derived from CR-000006).
-   The failure to establish perceived authenticity represents a critical failure condition for any BEBA-driven initiative.

## Evidence Base

The primary evidence comes from `REVIEW-000078`, which synthesizes literature on the social value of marketing. Specifically, claim `CR-000006` highlights that stakeholders can have negative perceptions of initiatives like Cause-Related Marketing (CRM) if they attribute selfish motives to the firm. This potential for a "backfire effect" necessitates a mediating construct like Perceived Authenticity.

## Boundary Conditions

-   **Stakeholder Skepticism:** In high-skepticism environments, establishing perceived authenticity is more difficult and its role as a mediator becomes more pronounced.
-   **Pre-existing Brand Reputation:** A poor reputation may create an insurmountable barrier to achieving perceived authenticity for any new initiative. Conversely, a strong reputation may create a "halo effect," increasing initial perceptions of authenticity.
-   **Communication Framing:** The way an initiative is communicated can heavily influence attributions of motive, and therefore, perceptions of authenticity.

## Competing Explanations

A simpler, competing model would posit a direct, unmediated effect of brand-led social initiatives on stakeholder behaviour. For example, a "Direct Effects Model" might claim that a CRM campaign directly increases purchase intent. The "Perceived Authenticity Model" competes with this by claiming the effect of the CRM campaign is indirect, mediated by whether stakeholders see the action as genuine or manipulative.

## Operationalisations

The construct can be operationalised through survey-based measures asking stakeholders to rate their agreement with statements regarding an organization's specific prosocial action, such as:
-   "The organization's support for this cause is genuine."
-   "They are doing this primarily to help the cause, not just to improve their image."
-   "This initiative is consistent with the organization's core values."

## Existing Scales

Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature (`REVIEW-000078`). A targeted review of literature on brand authenticity, CSR, and CRM is required to identify and validate existing scales.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Perceived Authenticity:** A stakeholder's judgment of the sincerity and genuineness of an organization's prosocial actions and communications. It reflects the degree to which an action is seen as value-driven versus opportunistic.

## Confidence

Moderate. The concept is strongly implied as a critical mediator by the reviewed literature summary (`REVIEW-000078`). However, its precise definition, operationalisation, and distinction from related constructs require a dedicated literature search.

## Review Recommendation

Accept as a high-priority candidate for the BEBA ontology. The next steps should be:
1.  Conduct a targeted literature search to establish a canonical definition and identify validated scales.
2.  Incorporate `Perceived Authenticity` as a key mediating variable in theoretical models within the BEBA framework, especially those dealing with partnerships and social marketing campaigns.
3.  Design empirical tests to compare the explanatory power of a model mediated by Perceived Authenticity against a simpler, direct-effects model.
