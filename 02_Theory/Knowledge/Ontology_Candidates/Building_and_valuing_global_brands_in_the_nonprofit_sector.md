# Concept

Building and Valuing Global Brands in the Nonprofit Sector

## Canonical Definition

A candidate concept describing how international nonprofit brands create behavioural value by enabling stakeholder trust, mission legitimacy, internal cohesion, resource mobilisation, and multi-stakeholder action under uncertainty, while remaining constrained by mission coherence, decentralised governance, and limits of financial brand valuation.

## Alternative Definitions

- The strategic development and assessment of nonprofit brands as trust-based, mission-linked, and behaviour-enabling organisational assets.
- The use of global nonprofit brands to coordinate stakeholder behaviour, mobilise resources, and sustain legitimacy across diverse organisational and cultural contexts.
- A nonprofit-specific approach to brand equity in which brand value is evaluated through financial and non-financial behavioural effects rather than financial value alone.

## BEBA Layer

Primary layers:

- Individual Layer: trust, uncertainty reduction, behavioural willingness.
- Organisational Layer: mission coherence, internal cohesion, brand governance, resource mobilisation.
- Market / Field Layer: stakeholder complexity, public legitimacy, advocacy, partnerships, policymaker attention.

## Core Claims

1. Nonprofit brands can function as trust proxies when stakeholders cannot directly observe service delivery, impact, beneficiary outcomes, or organisational integrity.

2. Nonprofit brand equity is not only donor preference or financial value; it includes the capacity to enable multiple stakeholder behaviours.

3. Mission-brand coherence is central to nonprofit brand legitimacy because branding may be resisted when perceived as commercially inappropriate or detached from mission.

4. In international and decentralised nonprofits, brands may support internal cohesion, identity, and coordination across staff, volunteers, teams, and country sections.

5. Different nonprofit types may require different brand architectures, for example crisis-oriented relief/development brands versus cause-centred advocacy brands.

6. Nonprofit brand valuation is incomplete if it captures only financial mobilisation and excludes volunteering, advocacy, partnerships, internal cohesion, policy influence, and other non-financial behaviours.

## Boundary Conditions

- Organisational type: Relief/development and advocacy organisations may rely on different brand architectures and behavioural pathways.
- Stakeholder role: Donors, volunteers, staff, partners, policymakers, beneficiaries, and publics may rely on different brand signals.
- Decentralisation: Global consistency may conflict with local autonomy in international nonprofit networks.
- Internal legitimacy: Branding may be constrained when perceived as commercialised, resource-diverting, or mission-inappropriate.
- Mission coherence: Brand effects depend on perceived authenticity and connection to mission.
- Valuation scope: Financial valuation methods may understate nonprofit brand value when relevant outcomes are non-financial.

## Competing Explanations

- Awareness-based brand equity models: explain behaviour through visibility or recognition, but understate trust and uncertainty reduction.
- Donor-choice-only models: focus on donation behaviour but fail to account for volunteers, staff, partners, policymakers, and beneficiaries.
- Financial asset models: treat brand value mainly as monetary mobilisation and may miss non-financial behavioural value.
- Corporate branding transfer models: assume commercial brand logic can be transferred to nonprofits, but may ignore mission primacy and internal resistance.
- Universal nonprofit branding models: assume one common brand architecture, whereas evidence suggests variation by organisational type and communication logic.
