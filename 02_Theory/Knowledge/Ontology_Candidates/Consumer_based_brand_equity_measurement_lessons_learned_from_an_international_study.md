# Concept

Consumer-Based Brand Equity (Aaker, 1991)

## Canonical Definition

Consumer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) is a conceptual framework proposed by Aaker (1991) that defines brand equity as a set of brand assets and liabilities linked to a brand, its name, and symbol, that add to or subtract from the value provided by a product or service. In its common operationalisation, it is treated as a multidimensional construct consisting of four core components: Brand Awareness, Brand Associations, Perceived Quality, and Brand Loyalty. These dimensions are typically modelled as reflective indicators of a higher-order latent CBBE construct.

## Alternative Definitions

-   **Keller (1993):** Defines CBBE as the differential effect of brand knowledge on consumer response to the marketing of the brand. This definition is more process-oriented, focusing on a causal sequence from awareness to brand image (associations), which contrasts with Aaker's set of co-equal assets.

## BEBA Layer

-   **Individual Layer:** The framework attempts to model the structure of brand knowledge and its effects at the individual consumer level.
-   **Market Layer:** When aggregated, individual-level CBBE is assumed to represent a market-level asset for the organisation.

## Core Claims

1.  The hypothesised four-factor structure of Aaker's (1991) CBBE model lacks discriminant validity, specifically between the Brand Awareness and Brand Associations dimensions. (`BK-000009`)
2.  The relationship between Brand Awareness and Brand Associations is not reflective but causally dependent; awareness is a necessary precondition for the formation of associations. (`BK-000010`)
3.  The structural validity of the Aaker (1991) model is negatively moderated by brand intangibility; the model's validity issues are more severe for services (and by extension, nonprofits) than for tangible goods. (`CB-000018`)
4.  The Aaker (1991) model structure is not universally applicable and fails tests of measurement invariance across different national and cultural contexts. (`CB-000017`, `CB-000019`)

## Evidence Base

The core claims challenging the model's validity are derived from an international study on CBBE measurement, captured in `REVIEW-000057` and the resulting atomic claims: `CB-000017`, `BK-000009`, `CB-000018`, `BK-000010`, `CB-000019`. This evidence base provides a strong empirical rationale for rejecting the model's structure as a foundation for BEBA.

## Boundary Conditions

The applicability and structural validity of Aaker's (1991) model are bounded by:
-   **Sector / Intangibility:** The model performs poorly in highly intangible contexts, such as services and nonprofits.
-   **National / Cultural Context:** Measurement and structural invariance cannot be assumed across different countries.
-   **Macro-Economic Environment:** Generalisability may be limited by macro-level uncertainty.

## Competing Explanations

-   **Process-Based Models:** A competing explanation, supported by the evidence (`BK-000010`), is that brand knowledge is structured as a causal sequence (e.g., Awareness -> Associations -> Affect/Preference) rather than a set of co-equal reflective dimensions. Keller's (1993) framework is more aligned with this view.
-   **BEBA Model:** The BEBA framework itself posits a behaviour-centric architecture that does not begin with Aaker's dimensions, representing a fundamental competing explanation.

## Operationalisations

The construct is typically operationalised using multi-item scales for each of the four dimensions, loaded onto a second-order latent factor in a Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) or Structural Equation Model (SEM).

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Consumer-Based Brand Equity (Aaker, 1991):** A foundational framework defining brand equity as a set of four key dimensions: brand awareness, brand associations, perceived quality, and brand loyalty. **BEBA Status:** The structural assumptions of this model (i.e., a four-factor reflective structure) have been empirically challenged (`REVIEW-000057`). It is not used as the theoretical basis for the BEBA model due to a lack of discriminant validity between its core components and its failure to hold in highly intangible contexts.
