# Concept

Configurational Brand Equity Formation

## Canonical Definition

Configurational Brand Equity Formation refers to the process by which brand equity emerges through multiple, potentially non-equivalent combinations of brand-building, meaning-based, and relational conditions, rather than through a single universal, additive, or strictly linear pathway.

## Alternative Definitions

- Brand equity creation as a staged process involving Brand Building, Brand Understanding, and Brand Relationship conditions.
- Consumer-based brand equity formation as a multi-stage architecture connecting brand inputs, stakeholder meaning structures, and relational mechanisms.
- Brand equity emergence through equifinal and asymmetric causal configurations.

## Core Claims

1. Brand equity should not be treated only as a static or monolithic construct; it may be better understood as an architecture of enabling conditions.

2. Consumer-based brand equity may develop through staged or causally related processes involving Brand Building, Brand Understanding, and Brand Relationship.

3. Relational mechanisms such as trust, intimacy, relevance, and perceived partner quality are candidate pathways through which brands become behaviourally effective.

4. Meaning-based mechanisms such as brand associations and brand self-connection may connect brand perception to stakeholder behaviour.

5. High brand equity may arise through multiple distinct configurations of antecedent conditions.

6. Conditions explaining high brand equity may not be the simple inverse of conditions explaining low brand equity.

7. Brand trust may depend on configurations involving reputation and strong brand associations rather than on either condition independently.

## Boundary Conditions

1. Sector boundary  
The evidence is consumer-brand based and should not be directly generalised to nonprofit donors, volunteers, beneficiaries, members, or advocacy publics without further support.

2. Valence boundary  
The findings concern favourite brands. Mechanisms may differ for unfamiliar, controversial, low-trust, or polarising nonprofit brands.

3. Behavioural boundary  
The reviewed claims concern high overall consumer-based brand equity. They do not directly establish effects on specific nonprofit behaviours such as donating, volunteering, advocacy, recurring giving, membership, compliance, beneficiary engagement, or word-of-mouth.

4. Methodological boundary  
Sequential claims appear to be based on cross-sectional modelling and should not be interpreted as directly observed temporal development.

5. Cultural and market boundary  
The reviewed context involved consumers’ favourite brands in urban centres in the United Kingdom. Cross-cultural and sectoral transfer remains uncertain.

6. Configurational boundary  
The configurational findings should not be reduced to simple additive linear effects without additional evidence.

## Competing Explanations

1. Linear additive explanation  
Brand equity may be explained as the additive effect of independent dimensions. The reviewed claim set challenges the sufficiency of this explanation but does not invalidate it.

2. Sequential-stage explanation  
Brand equity may develop through a staged sequence from Brand Building to Brand Understanding to Brand Relationship and then to equity. This remains plausible but requires temporal caution.

3. Relational-mechanism explanation  
Brand equity may become behaviourally effective primarily through trust, intimacy, relevance, and perceived partner quality.

4. Meaning-based explanation  
Brand equity may operate through stakeholder meaning structures, especially brand associations and self-connection.

5. Configurational explanation  
High brand equity may emerge from different combinations of conditions, implying equifinality.

6. Asymmetric explanation  
High and low brand equity may require different causal explanations rather than mirror-image models.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

Configurational Brand Equity Formation: A candidate BEBA concept describing brand equity formation as the emergence of high brand equity through multiple possible combinations of brand-building, meaning-based, and relational conditions. The concept implies equifinality and possible asymmetry between the causes of strong and weak brand equity. Current evidence is consumer-brand based and should be treated as transferable but not yet canonical for nonprofit stakeholder behaviour.
