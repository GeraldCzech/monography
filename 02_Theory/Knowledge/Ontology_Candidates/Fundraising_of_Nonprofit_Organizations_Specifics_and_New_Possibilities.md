# Concept

Stakeholder-Architectural Fit

## Canonical Definition

The degree to which a nonprofit organization's internal systems, processes, and professional posture (its architecture) align with the expectations, evaluation criteria, and cultural norms of a specific stakeholder segment.

## Alternative Definitions

Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature. This is a proposed BEBA-specific concept. Related concepts include:
- **Market Orientation:** The organizational culture that most effectively and efficiently creates the necessary behaviours for the creation of superior value for buyers and, thus, continuous superior performance for the business (Narver & Slater, 1990). `Stakeholder-Architectural Fit` is more specific, focusing on the *internal architecture* as the antecedent and its alignment with *specific stakeholder segments* rather than a general market.
- **Person-Organization Fit:** The compatibility between people and organizations that occurs when: (a) at least one entity provides what the other needs, or (b) they share similar fundamental characteristics, or (c) both (Kristof, 1996). `Stakeholder-Architectural Fit` adapts this logic from the individual to the stakeholder group level.

## BEBA Layer

This concept is relational and bridges multiple layers:
- **Organisational Layer:** The 'Architectural' component of the fit originates here.
- **Market Layer:** The 'Stakeholder' component and their expectations reside here.
- The concept itself describes the quality of the interface between these two layers.

## Core Claims

1.  A higher degree of `Stakeholder-Architectural Fit` with a target stakeholder segment positively influences brand equity perceptions and supportive behaviours from that segment.
2.  There can be a trade-off in fit: an architecture optimized for one stakeholder segment (e.g., institutional funders) may result in a lower degree of fit with another segment (e.g., grassroots donors).
3.  The effect of specific architectural components (e.g., Organisational Professionalism) on NPO outcomes is moderated by stakeholder type, mediated through the mechanism of `Stakeholder-Architectural Fit`.

## Evidence Base

The primary evidence is indirect and derived from `REVIEW-000067`, which synthesizes claims from Hommerová & Severová (2018).
- **Claim OP-000002:** Organisational Professionalism is a positive predictor of reliance on public funding. This suggests a high degree of fit between professionalism and the expectations of public funders.
- **Claim OP-000003:** Organisational Professionalism is a negative predictor of reliance on individual donations. This suggests a poor fit (or even misfit) between high professionalism and the expectations of individual donors, who may value perceived authenticity or grassroots character more.

## Boundary Conditions

The initial evidence base for the differential effect of professionalism is from a study of NPOs in the Czech Republic. The specific expectations of stakeholder groups (and thus what constitutes 'fit') are likely culturally and contextually bounded.

## Competing Explanations

- **Universalist Model of Organisational Capacity:** This model posits that certain architectural traits, such as professionalism, are universally beneficial and have a positive main effect on all forms of stakeholder support. The evidence reviewed challenges this model.
- **Contingency Model of Organisational Capacity:** This model, which `Stakeholder-Architectural Fit` formalizes, posits that the effects of architectural traits are contingent on the stakeholder context.

## Operationalisations

This concept can be operationalized in two primary ways:
1.  **Interaction Term:** In a statistical model, it could be represented by the interaction term between a measure of an architectural component (e.g., Professionalism scale score) and a categorical variable for stakeholder type.
2.  **Perceptual Scale:** A new scale could be developed to measure the *perceived fit* from the stakeholder's perspective. Items might include: "[NPO Name]'s way of working matches what I expect from an organization like this," or "The level of formality at [NPO Name] is appropriate for its mission."

## Existing Scales

No direct scales for `Stakeholder-Architectural Fit` have been identified. Measurement would require new scale development or adaptation of existing Person-Organization (P-O) Fit scales.

## Recommended ConstructLedger Updates

Add `Stakeholder-Architectural Fit` to the Construct Ledger.
- **Type:** Relational / Moderating Construct.
- **Definition:** The degree to which an NPO's internal architecture aligns with the expectations of a specific stakeholder segment.
- **Role in BEBA:** To explain the contingent effects of the Organisational Layer on the Behavioural Layer, as mediated by the Market Layer. It is a core mechanism for the "Contingency Model" of BEBA.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Stakeholder-Architectural Fit:** A BEBA concept describing the alignment between an NPO's internal systems and processes (its architecture) and the expectations and evaluation criteria of a particular stakeholder group. High fit is theorized to enhance brand equity and supportive behaviours for that group.

## Confidence

Medium.

The theoretical need for this concept is high, as it resolves a key tension in the evidence (the opposing effects of professionalism). However, it is a newly proposed construct within the BEBA framework, lacking direct empirical validation and established measurement scales.

## Review Recommendation

Accept as a candidate concept for formal integration into the BEBA theoretical model. Prioritize the development of a formal proposition for empirical testing, specifically related to the competing "Universalist vs. Contingent" models of professionalism.
