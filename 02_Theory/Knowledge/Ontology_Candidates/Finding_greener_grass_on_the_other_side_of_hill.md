# Concept

Finding greener grass on the other side of the hill

## Canonical Definition

The "Finding greener grass on the other side of the hill" mechanism, formally termed the **Credibility Gating Effect**, describes a process in nonprofit brand equity formation where the positive influence of certain evaluative brand assets (e.g., Perceived Quality, Brand Loyalty) on overall Brand Equity is conditional upon, and significantly strengthened by, high levels of Brand Credibility.

In this mechanism, Brand Credibility is not merely an additive component of equity but functions as a higher-order, system-level enabler. It "gates" the conversion of latent stakeholder value perceptions into realised brand equity, acting as the lens through which the value of other brand signals is authenticated and accepted, particularly in high-uncertainty contexts.

## Alternative Definitions

The primary alternative is the **Additive Model of Brand Equity**, where Brand Credibility is defined simply as another first-order dimension of brand equity that contributes directly and independently to the overall construct, alongside Awareness, Associations, and Quality, without any specific moderating function.

## BEBA Layer

**Individual Layer.** The mechanism operates at the level of individual stakeholder cognition, influencing how brand perceptions are integrated into an overall evaluation of the nonprofit brand.

-   **Perceived Quality**: The brand asset whose effect is most clearly gated by credibility.
-   **Brand Loyalty**: A brand asset whose effect is strengthened by credibility.
-   **Nonprofit Brand Equity**: The outcome variable.
-   **Uncertainty**: The contextual condition under which this gating mechanism is theorised to be most potent.
-   **Giving Motivation**: A contextual factor (e.g., obligatory vs. voluntary) that may act as a boundary condition for the mechanism.

## Core Claims

The mechanism is supported by a set of integrated, higher-order claims derived from `REVIEW-000066`:

1.  **Claim (Mechanism):** Brand Credibility functions as an enabling mechanism, converting latent evaluative perceptions (e.g., Perceived Quality) into realized Brand Equity. In its absence, the link between certain organizational attributes and stakeholder value perception is broken. (Based on `BC-000034`)
2.  **Claim (Specificity):** The enabling effect of Brand Credibility is stronger for evaluative brand components (e.g., Perceived Quality, Brand Loyalty) than for perceptual salience components (e.g., Brand Awareness, Brand Associations). (Based on `BC-000034`, `BC-000035` vs. `BC-000036`, `BC-000037`)
3.  **Claim (Interaction):** The effect of Perceived Quality on Nonprofit Brand Equity is primarily expressed through an interaction with Brand Credibility, not as a direct main effect. (Based on `PQ-000009` and `BC-000034`)

## Evidence Base

The primary evidence is from `REVIEW-000066`, which synthesizes a study on the brand equity of Zakat (obligatory religious giving) institutions. The key findings supporting this mechanism are the statistically significant and positive interaction effects between Brand Credibility and both Perceived Quality (`BC-000034`) and Brand Loyalty (`BC-000035`), and the non-significant direct effect of Perceived Quality (`PQ-000009`).

## Boundary Conditions

A critical boundary condition is the **Giving Context**. The evidence originates from a context of obligatory religious giving, where the donor's primary concern may be the proper fulfillment of a duty (an issue of credibility) rather than the operational quality of service to an end-beneficiary. The mechanism's strength may vary in contexts of voluntary, secular, or egoistic giving.

## Competing Explanations

1.  **Additive Model:** Brand Credibility is simply a direct antecedent of Brand Equity, and its effect is additive rather than multiplicative. This is the most direct competing explanation.
2.  **Mediating Model:** Brand Credibility acts as a mediator, where assets like Perceived Quality first build Credibility, which in turn builds overall Brand Equity. This is different from the proposed moderating (gating) function.
3.  **Misspecification:** The observed effect is an artifact of the specific "Perceived Quality" measure used. A different measure, such as "Perceived Mission Effectiveness," might have a direct effect on Brand Equity, rendering the interaction less important.

## Operationalisations

The mechanism is operationalised as a statistical **interaction term** in a regression or structural equation model (e.g., `Perceived_Quality × Brand_Credibility`) used to predict Overall Brand Equity.

## Existing Scales

This is a mechanism, not a first-order construct, so it does not have a dedicated scale. Its measurement relies on the scales used for its constituent concepts:
-   A validated scale for **Brand Credibility**.
-   A validated scale for **Perceived Quality**.
-   A validated scale for **Brand Loyalty**.

## Recommended ConstructLedger Updates

-   **Brand Credibility:** Review the entry to ensure its definition and theoretical role explicitly include its potential function as a higher-order moderator that enables the value of other brand assets, in addition to any role as a direct antecedent.
-   **Perceived Quality:** Review the entry to consider a potential distinction between `Perceived Operational Quality` and `Perceived Mission Effectiveness`. The notes should reflect that its relationship with Brand Equity may not be direct, but rather conditional on Brand Credibility.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Credibility Gating Effect:** A mechanism in the BEBA model where Brand Credibility acts as a critical moderator, enabling or strengthening the positive relationship between evaluative brand assets (especially Perceived Quality) and overall Nonprofit Brand Equity. In this view, high credibility unlocks the value of other brand signals, particularly under conditions of uncertainty. Also known informally as the "Finding greener grass on the other side of the hill" effect.

## Confidence

**Moderate.** The theoretical insight is strong and provides a compelling explanation for the role of credibility in uncertain contexts. However, the empirical evidence is currently limited to a single study context (`REVIEW-000066`, obligatory religious giving). Replication across different nonprofit sub-sectors and giving motivations is required to increase confidence in its generalisability.

## Review Recommendation

This candidate should be reviewed for inclusion in the BEBA ontology as a core behavioural mechanism. The review should focus on:
1.  Formalizing the "Additive vs. Interactive (Credibility-Gated)" models as competing explanations to be tested in future empirical work.
2.  Initiating a concept workspace to refine the `Perceived Quality` construct based on the distinctions suggested by this mechanism.
3.  Accepting the proposed higher-order claims as official candidate claims within the BEBA knowledge base.
