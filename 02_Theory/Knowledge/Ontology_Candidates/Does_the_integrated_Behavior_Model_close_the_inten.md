# Concept

Coping Planning

## Canonical Definition

A cognitive self-regulation process in which an individual forms concrete plans that specify how they will deal with anticipated barriers and obstacles that might hinder the performance of an intended behaviour. It is an "if-then" plan focused on overcoming setbacks (e.g., "If barrier X occurs, then I will perform coping response Y").

## Alternative Definitions

-   Barrier Planning
-   Relapse Prevention Strategy (in addiction contexts)
-   Maintenance Self-Efficacy (a related but distinct concept focused on belief, not planning)

## BEBA Layer

Individual Layer. This is a cognitive process residing within the individual agent.

## Parent Concepts

-   `Self-Regulation`
-   `Planning`
-   `Implementation Intentions`

## Child Concepts

None identified. Coping planning is a specific type of planning.

## Related Concepts

-   `Action Planning`: A sibling concept. Action planning specifies the "when, where, and how" of *initiating* a behaviour, while coping planning specifies how to *maintain* it against obstacles.
-   `Actual Control`: The environmental and skill-based factors that determine the feasibility of a behaviour. The need for coping planning increases as actual control decreases.
-   `Self-Efficacy`: The belief in one's ability to perform a behaviour. Coping planning can increase self-efficacy by giving individuals a concrete strategy for success.
-   `Behavioral Persistence`: The outcome that coping planning is hypothesised to directly influence.
-   `Intention-Behavior Gap`: The problem that coping planning, along with action planning and actual control, is meant to solve.

## Core Claims

1.  Coping planning is a significant mediator of the relationship between behavioural intention and persistent or long-term behaviour, particularly when obstacles are likely (Source: CP-000001).
2.  Coping planning is distinct from, and often more critical than, action planning for behaviours that require maintenance over time or resistance to temptation (e.g., sustained volunteering, regular donations).
3.  The primary function of a BEBA in the post-intentional phase is to facilitate and scaffold the user's coping planning process.

## Evidence Base

The concept is well-established in health psychology, primarily through the Health Action Process Approach (HAPA) model (Schwarzer, 2008). The evidence base consists of numerous studies in domains like physical activity, dietary change, and smoking cessation, showing that coping planning interventions significantly contribute to behavioural maintenance. The provided synthesis document grounds this in claim `CP-000001`.

## Boundary Conditions

The utility and effect size of coping planning are contingent on the nature of the behaviour. Its importance is highest for:
-   Behaviours requiring persistence against known or anticipated obstacles.
-   Behaviours performed in unstable or unpredictable environments.
-   Complex, long-term behaviours (e.g., lifestyle changes, long-term advocacy) rather than simple, one-off actions.

## Competing Explanations

-   **General Self-Efficacy/PBC**: Simpler models (like the Theory of Planned Behavior) would argue that a general belief in one's ability to control a behaviour (Perceived Behavioral Control) is sufficient, and that a separate planning construct is redundant.
-   **Habit Formation**: Theories of habit suggest that for repeated behaviours, conscious planning becomes less relevant as automatic, context-cued responses take over. Coping planning is most relevant *before* a behaviour becomes habitual.

## Operationalisations

Coping planning is typically operationalised via self-report questionnaires that ask participants to rate their agreement with statements about whether they have made plans to overcome specific barriers.

Example item stem: "I have made a detailed plan regarding what to do if something interferes with my plan to [BEHAVIOUR]."

Interventions often involve prompting users to write down their own if-then plans for anticipated barriers.

## Existing Scales

Scales for coping planning are widely available in the health psychology literature, often associated with the HAPA model. These can be adapted for the nonprofit context. See, for example, scales developed by Ralf Schwarzer and colleagues.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Coping Planning**: A self-regulation strategy involving the creation of "if-then" plans to anticipate and manage barriers that could prevent an individual from acting on their intentions. It focuses on overcoming obstacles rather than initiating the action itself.
