# Concept

Brand Equity as Behavioural Architecture

## Canonical Definition

In BEBA, **brand equity** refers to an architecture-level umbrella concept describing how organisational brand activity shapes stakeholder brand meanings, how these meanings become behaviourally relevant as brand strength, and how resulting stakeholder behaviours generate nonprofit value outcomes.

Brand equity should not be treated as a single homogeneous latent construct. It should be decomposed into at least:

```text
Brand Description
→ Brand Strength
→ Behavioural Outcomes
→ Nonprofit Brand Value
```

## Alternative Definitions

1. **Commercial financial definition:** Brand equity as the financial asset value of a brand.  
2. **Consumer-based definition:** Brand equity as stakeholder or consumer associations, awareness, beliefs, and responses linked to a brand.  
3. **Behavioural strength definition:** Brand equity as the degree to which a brand increases choice probability, loyalty, competitive insulation, or willingness to support.  
4. **BEBA-compatible definition:** Brand equity as a layered behavioural architecture linking organisational brand activity, stakeholder cognition, behavioural disposition, behaviour, and nonprofit value outcomes.

## Core Claims

1. Brand equity is conceptually overloaded and should be disaggregated into distinct theoretical objects.  
2. Brand description refers to stakeholder-held associations, beliefs, meanings, and descriptive knowledge structures attached to a brand.  
3. Brand description is an antecedent of brand strength.  
4. Brand strength refers to the degree to which brand meanings become behaviourally relevant dispositions such as choice readiness, trust, attachment, preference, or loyalty.  
5. Brand strength can increase the probability of supportive behaviour under uncertainty.  
6. Nonprofit brand value should not be reduced to financial asset value. It should be translated into nonprofit-relevant outcomes such as behavioural reliability, mobilisation capacity, legitimacy, trust retention, donation stability, and resilience against competing appeals.  
7. Brand management can be modelled as a feedback loop in which observed stakeholder states and behavioural outcomes inform future organisational brand activity.  
8. Holistic branding is more compatible with BEBA than product-plus branding because it treats the brand as a coordinating architecture rather than as a communication add-on.

## Boundary Conditions

1. **For-profit transfer boundary**  
   Commercial brand equity outcomes such as profit margins, distribution access, and financial asset value cannot be directly imported into nonprofit theory without translation.

2. **Stakeholder boundary**  
   Effects may differ across donors, volunteers, beneficiaries, employees, institutional funders, advocacy audiences, community partners, regulators, and media actors.

3. **Behaviour boundary**  
   Brand strength may influence donation, volunteering, advocacy, retention, trust allocation, and partnership behaviour differently.

4. **Uncertainty boundary**  
   Brand strength is theoretically relevant under uncertainty because it may reduce perceived evaluation costs, but the reviewed claims do not directly demonstrate this mechanism.

5. **Measurement boundary**  
   Measurement instruments should not conflate associations, awareness, trust, attachment, loyalty, behavioural intention, actual behaviour, and organisational value outcomes.

## Competing Explanations

1. **Direct communication model**

```text
Marketing activity → Behaviour
```

Competes with the mediated BEBA model:

```text
Brand activity → Brand description → Brand strength → Behaviour
```

2. **Purely financial brand value model**

Defines brand equity primarily as financial asset value. This is insufficient for nonprofit BEBA unless translated into mission-enabling value.

3. **Bypass-route explanations**

Stakeholder behaviour may occur without brand strength through:

- urgent need,
- personal connection,
- moral obligation,
- peer influence,
- institutional requirement,
- religious or community duty,
- ideological alignment,
- direct service experience.

4. **Product-plus branding model**

Treats branding as an add-on to communication or product/service presentation. This competes with BEBA’s holistic architecture interpretation.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Brand Equity**  
In BEBA, brand equity is an architecture-level umbrella concept describing the layered process through which organisational brand activity shapes stakeholder brand meanings, converts these meanings into behavioural readiness, and generates nonprofit value through reliable stakeholder behaviour. The term should not be used as a single undifferentiated construct.

**Usage note:**  
Specify whether “brand equity” refers to brand description, brand strength, behavioural outcomes, nonprofit brand value, or the full architecture linking these levels.

**Terminology warning:**  
Avoid using “added value,” “brand value,” or “brand strength” without specifying the relevant level of analysis and outcome domain.
