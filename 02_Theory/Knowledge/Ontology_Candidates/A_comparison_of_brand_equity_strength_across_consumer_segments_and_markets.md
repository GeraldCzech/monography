# Concept

Segment-Conditioned Brand Enablement

## Canonical Definition

Segment-conditioned brand enablement is a proposed BEBA concept describing how brand knowledge becomes behaviourally effective only when its cognitive associations are sufficiently rich, action-relevant, and activated within a specific actor segment, prior-behaviour context, and uncertainty condition.

## Alternative Definitions

- The process by which brand knowledge affects behavioural propensity differently across non-supporters, occasional supporters, recurring supporters, volunteers, advocates, and high-commitment supporters.
- A segment-sensitive mechanism through which awareness, image associations, and prior behaviour combine to enable or fail to enable nonprofit-support behaviour.
- A behavioural effectiveness condition in which brand knowledge matters more for actors with prior experience, higher commitment, or greater perceived uncertainty.

## Core Claims

1. Brand knowledge does not have a uniform behavioural effect across all actors.
2. Brand knowledge appears more behaviourally consequential among actors with prior usage, support, or stronger behavioural involvement.
3. Awareness enables consideration but is insufficient for behavioural effectiveness.
4. Richer brand image associations are more likely than simple awareness to support behavioural readiness.
5. Brand image may increase behavioural propensity by reducing uncertainty in high-risk, intangible, or commitment-heavy contexts.
6. Prior behaviour may strengthen brand perceptions and association richness, creating a recursive behaviour–knowledge feedback loop.
7. Aggregate brand-equity measures may conceal meaningful differences across supporter or user segments.

## Boundary Conditions

- Non-user / non-supporter condition: brand knowledge may have weak or no behavioural effect among actors without prior behavioural relationship.
- Segment condition: effects likely differ between non-supporters, occasional supporters, recurring supporters, volunteers, advocates, and high-commitment supporters.
- Commitment condition: brand knowledge may matter more for recurring, subscription-like, identity-relevant, or high-risk behaviours.
- Uncertainty condition: brand image is likely more behaviourally relevant when impact, quality, or ethical performance cannot be directly verified.
- Measurement condition: aggregate brand-equity indicators may be theoretically weak unless decomposed by supporter segment and behavioural history.

## Competing Explanations

- Prior behaviour may predict future behaviour directly through habit, familiarity, or commitment rather than through brand equity.
- Social influence, moral motivation, perceived efficacy, trust, or situational triggers may explain supporter activation independently of brand knowledge.
- Segment membership may function as a stronger predictor than general brand-equity measures.
- In low-risk or low-commitment contexts, brand awareness or convenience may be sufficient without rich brand image associations.
- Aggregate brand equity may remain descriptively useful but may not explain behavioural differences adequately.

## Operationalisations

Possible operational indicators:

- Supporter segment: non-supporter, first-time donor, occasional donor, recurring donor, volunteer, advocate, legacy donor, high-commitment supporter.
- Prior behavioural relationship: frequency, recency, monetary value, volunteering history, advocacy history.
- Brand knowledge: awareness, recall, recognition, perceived familiarity, knowledge of mission or activities.
- Association richness: number, diversity, accessibility, favourability, uniqueness, trust relevance, and action relevance of associations.
- Uncertainty condition: perceived risk, perceived information asymmetry, perceived impact ambiguity, perceived ethical uncertainty.
- Behavioural propensity: donation intention, recurring donation intention, volunteering intention, advocacy intention, retention intention.
