# Concept

Intention-Behavior Gap

## Canonical Definition

The Intention-Behavior Gap is the observed discrepancy between an individual's stated intention to perform a specific behavior and their subsequent failure to enact that behavior. It represents the failure to translate positive behavioral intentions into corresponding actions, a phenomenon that cannot be fully explained by factors that determine the intention itself.

## Alternative Definitions

-   The "volitional problem" in action control.
-   The challenge of the "post-intentional" or "volitional" phase of behavior.
-   The discrepancy between motivation and action.

## BEBA Layer

-   **Primary:** Individual Layer (deals with psychological constructs like intention, planning, habit, and self-regulation).
-   **Secondary:** Societal Layer (contextual factors like resource availability, time, and social opportunity moderate the gap).

## Parent Concepts

-   Behavioral Failure
-   Self-Regulation Failure

## Child Concepts

Not applicable. The gap is a phenomenon, not a concept with sub-types. Its drivers are distinct concepts (see Related Concepts).

## Related Concepts

-   `Intention` (The necessary but insufficient precursor)
-   `Behavior` (The unrealized outcome)
-   `Post-Intentional Phase` (The temporal and psychological space where the gap occurs)
-   `Volitional Control` (The capacity that, when failing, leads to the gap)
-   `Planning` (A key mechanism for bridging the gap, includes `Action Planning` and `Coping Planning`)
-   `Habit` (An automatic mechanism that can bypass the gap)
-   `Automaticity` (The cognitive process that reduces the need for volitional control)
-   `Identity` (The alignment of behavior with self-concept, which can strengthen the link)
-   `Inclined Abstainer` (The persona type defined by experiencing the gap)
-   `Context` (Environmental and social factors that moderate the gap)

## Core Claims

-   `IB-000033`: A substantial discrepancy exists between behavioral intentions and actual behavior across numerous domains.
-   `I-000004`: Intention is a necessary but often insufficient predictor of behavior, explaining only a moderate portion of the variance in action.
-   `TO-000010`: Foundational intention-based models like the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) and Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA) are effective at predicting intention but are weak at explaining the conversion of that intention into behavior.
-   `PI-000005`: Explaining the intention-behavior gap requires incorporating post-intentional psychological constructs that govern the volitional phase of action control.
-   `IB-000035`: No single, universally accepted model adequately explains the intention-behavior gap; it is a multi-causal phenomenon.

## Evidence Base

The concept is supported by an extensive body of meta-analytic evidence across multiple domains (e.g., health, environmental, consumer behavior). The primary source for this candidate is the synthesis of 14 claims from literature review `REVIEW-000107`. Key evidence points to the moderate correlation between intention and behavior, leaving a significant "gap" in variance unexplained.

## Boundary Conditions

-   **Intention Strength:** The gap is most relevant for individuals with medium-to-strong intentions. The concept is less applicable where intention is weak or non-existent (`I-000006`).
-   **Contextual Constraints:** The translation of intention to behavior is constrained by real-world factors such as resource availability, time, and social/physical opportunity. A purely psychological explanation is insufficient (`C-000003`).

## Competing Explanations

The BEBA framework posits the Intention-Behavior Gap as its core problem. It does not compete with theories of intention formation (like TPB), but rather complements them. Competing explanations for bridging the gap itself are often single-mechanism theories focusing exclusively on one of the following, whereas BEBA aims to be an integrative framework:
-   Implementation Intentions (Planning)
-   Habit Formation models
-   Self-Identity theories
-   Dual-Process models (focusing on automaticity)

## Operationalisations

The gap is typically operationalised as the statistical discrepancy between a measure of intention at Time 1 and a measure of behavior at Time 2. Methodologically, this can be studied through:
-   Predictive models (regression) showing the variance in behavior not explained by intention.
-   Categorization of participants into groups (e.g., "inclined actors", "inclined abstainers", "disinclined actors", "disinclined abstainers").
-   Dynamic, within-person designs (e.g., Ecological Momentary Assessment) to capture volitional struggles over time (`RM-000002`).

## Existing Scales

The Intention-Behavior Gap is an outcome variable, not a latent construct measured by a scale. Its components are measured using established scales for:
-   Behavioral Intention (e.g., semantic differential or Likert scales).
-   Planning (e.g., Action and Coping Planning scales by Sniehotta et al.).
-   Behavior (e.g., self-report frequency, objective observation, device tracking).

## Recommended ConstructLedger Updates

-   Refine the `Planning` construct to formally distinguish between two sub-constructs:
    1.  `Action Planning` (Specifying the when, where, and how of action initiation).
    2.  `Coping Planning` (Anticipating barriers and pre-planning solutions).

## Recommended Glossary Entry

-   **Intention-Behavior Gap:** The common failure to translate positive behavioral intentions into corresponding actions. It is the central problem that post-intentional theories of behavior, including BEBA, aim to solve.
-   **Inclined Abstainer:** An individual who holds a positive intention to perform a behavior but fails to enact it, thus experiencing the intention-behavior gap. This is the primary target persona for BEBA interventions.

## Confidence

High. The existence of the Intention-Behavior Gap is a robust, well-documented, and foundational phenomenon in modern behavioral science. Its relevance as the core problem for BEBA is strongly supported by the reviewed claims.
