# Concept
Habit

## Canonical Definition
Within the BEBA framework, Habit is defined as a non-deliberative psychological process through which a stable contextual cue (e.g., a nonprofit brand) automatically triggers a specific, previously performed behavior (e.g., donating) with minimal conscious intention or cognitive effort. It represents the automatic pathway in BEBA's dual-process model of recurring supportive behavior.

## Alternative Definitions
- **Lay Definition**: Habit as merely the high frequency of a past behavior. This is distinguished from the BEBA definition, which requires automaticity and cue-dependence, not just repetition.
- **Behaviorist Definition**: A simple stimulus-response association learned through reinforcement, without reference to internal cognitive states. The BEBA definition includes the cognitive mechanism of `Cognitive Automaticity`.

## BEBA Layer
Individual Layer. Habit is a psychological property of an individual donor or supporter.

## Related Concepts
- **Past Behavior**: The primary antecedent for habit formation.
- **Cognitive Automaticity**: The core cognitive mechanism and operationalization of Habit within BEBA.
- **Relational Commitment**: The parallel, deliberative pathway for recurring behavior that complements the habit pathway.
- **Context Stability**: A critical moderator required for habits to form and exert influence.
- **Brand as Cue**: The specific contextual trigger that activates the habitual response in the BEBA model.
- **Intention-Behavior Gap**: The research problem that the habit pathway helps to explain and resolve.

## Core Claims
- `PB-000029`: Past donation behavior is a significant predictor of future donation behavior. (Habit is a key explanation for this observed link).
- `DP-000002`: The effect of past donation on future donation is mediated by cognitive automaticity and relational commitment. (This places Habit as a core mediator).
- `HF-000001`: Context stability moderates the relationship between past behavior and cognitive automaticity. (This defines a key boundary condition for Habit).

## Evidence Base
The primary evidence base is the synthesis from `REVIEW-000103`, which integrates claims from literature on the intention-behavior gap, dual-process theories of behavior, and nonprofit marketing. The concept draws heavily on social psychology research into habit formation and automaticity.

## Boundary Conditions
- **Context Stability**: The influence of habit is significantly strengthened in stable contexts and weakened in volatile or novel ones. If the cues change, the habit loop is disrupted (`HF-000001`).
- **Goal Conflict**: The influence of habit can be overridden by strong, conflicting conscious goals or intentions, particularly for high-stakes decisions. The BEBA model predicts the deliberative (`Relational Commitment`) pathway becomes dominant in such cases.

## Competing Explanations
- **Purely Deliberative Models (e.g., Theory of Planned Behavior)**: These models explain recurring behavior through stable, conscious constructs like attitudes, norms, and intentions. They attribute the effect of past behavior to its influence on these deliberative constructs, rather than to a separate, automatic process.
- **Simple Frequency Heuristic**: The idea that people simply repeat what they've done before as a cognitive shortcut, without the specific cue-driven automaticity central to the BEBA definition of habit.

## Operationalisations
The concept of Habit is operationalized within the BEBA framework as the measurable construct of **Cognitive Automaticity**. This focuses on the psychological experience of automaticity (e.g., "I donate without thinking," "Donating is something I do automatically").

## Existing Scales
The **Self-Report Habit Index (SRHI)** (Verplanken & Orbell, 2003) is a widely used and validated scale for measuring the automaticity component of habit. It is the recommended starting point for operationalizing the construct.
