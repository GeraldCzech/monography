# Concept

Charity_BE_TPB

## Canonical Definition

A candidate BEBA integration concept describing how nonprofit brand equity constructs become behaviourally effective by shaping donation intention through Theory of Planned Behavior-related mechanisms, especially trust, attitude toward donating, perceived risk reduction, decision simplification, and contextual moderation.

## Alternative Definitions

- Brand equity–TPB integration in charitable giving.
- Nonprofit brand-enabled donation intention architecture.
- Behavioural mechanism model of nonprofit brand equity.
- Charity brand equity as an antecedent to TPB-based donation intention.

## Core Claims

1. Nonprofit brand equity can positively influence donation intention.
2. Nonprofit brand trust is a central uncertainty-reduction mechanism linking brand perceptions to donation intention.
3. Nonprofit brand image can function as an antecedent of attitude toward donating.
4. Brand-related perceptions may influence TPB antecedents such as attitude, subjective norm, and perceived behavioural control.
5. Known and trusted nonprofit brands can function as heuristics that reduce perceived risk and decision complexity.
6. Brand salience may be especially influential for new donors with limited prior experience.
7. Social media marketing activity can shape brand image, trust, and donation intention.
8. Donation context, social media context, CEO reputation, donor disposition, and donor experience can moderate brand-related effects.
9. The reviewed literature does not support one standardized brand equity–TPB structural model.
10. Evidence for donation intention should not be overextended to sustained giving without longitudinal behavioural evidence.

## Boundary Conditions

- Donor experience: new donors may rely more strongly on salience, trust, and heuristic cues.
- Donation context: effects may vary between digital/offline and monetary/time-based donation settings.
- Communication environment: social media may function as both antecedent environment and moderator.
- Leadership cues: CEO reputation may strengthen, weaken, or bypass brand-based perceptions.
- Donor dispositions: materialistic attitude may moderate response to corporate or brand cues.
- Brand perception valence: negative and ambivalent perceptions are insufficiently covered.
- Time horizon: longitudinal sustained-giving effects are insufficiently covered.

## Competing Explanations

BEBA should not assume a single pathway such as:

```text
brand equity → trust → attitude → intention → donation
```

Competing model families should remain active:

1. Direct brand equity model  
   ```text
   nonprofit brand equity → donation intention
   ```

2. Trust-mediated model  
   ```text
   brand perceptions → brand trust → donation intention
   ```

3. Brand image–attitude–intention model  
   ```text
   brand image → attitude toward donating → donation intention
   ```

4. Brand equity–TPB integration model  
   ```text
   brand constructs → TPB antecedents → donation intention
   ```

5. Heuristic / risk-reduction model  
   ```text
   brand familiarity + trust → reduced risk / reduced complexity → donation intention
   ```

6. Organisational communication pathway  
   ```text
   social media marketing → brand image → trust → donation intention
   ```

7. Context-moderated model  
   ```text
   brand equity dimension → donation intention
   moderated by context / donor disposition / donor experience / leadership cue
   ```

Possible bypass routes should also be retained:

```text
CEO reputation → trust / intention
```

```text
social media context → subjective norm / attitude / intention
```

```text
donor disposition → intention
```

```text
prior donor experience → trust / intention
```

## Operationalisations

Candidate operationalisations may include:

- Nonprofit brand equity as multidimensional brand perception construct.
- Brand image as donor evaluation of nonprofit associations.
- Brand trust as perceived reliability, integrity, and confidence in the nonprofit.
- Brand salience as donor awareness or retrieval likelihood in choice contexts.
- Attitude toward donating as evaluative orientation toward donating to the nonprofit or cause.
- Donation intention as self-reported likelihood or willingness to donate.
- Perceived risk as uncertainty about organizational quality, impact, or trustworthiness.
- Decision complexity as perceived difficulty of choosing whether or where to donate.
- Donor experience as prior interaction or familiarity with the nonprofit.
- Context variables as donation channel, donation type, social media environment, or communication setting.

No empirical coefficients, fit indices, or behavioural validation claims should be added without verified pipeline outputs.

## Confidence

Medium.

Rationale: The reviewed claim set consistently supports brand-related effects on donation intention and highlights trust, attitude, and heuristic simplification as plausible behavioural mechanisms. Confidence is limited by fragmented model structures, insufficient evidence on negative or ambivalent brand perceptions, and insufficient longitudinal evidence on sustained giving.
