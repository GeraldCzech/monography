# Concept

Moral Capital

## Canonical Definition

Moral capital refers to the perceived stock of moral credibility, legitimacy, and value-based trust that enables a nonprofit organisation to be regarded as worthy of support under conditions of uncertainty.

## Alternative Definitions

- The perceived moral worthiness of an NGO or nonprofit organisation as inferred by donors from reputation, mission credibility, perceived integrity, and value alignment.
- A form of nonprofit-specific credibility that helps donors decide whether an organisation deserves trust and support when direct verification is limited.

## BEBA Layer

Primary layer: Societal

Secondary layer: Individual

Moral capital is primarily societal because it reflects wider moral expectations, legitimacy standards, and trust conditions placed on NGOs. It connects to the individual layer when donors translate perceived moral credibility into brand associations, identification, commitment, and supportive behavioural intentions.

## Core Claims

1. NGO brand effectiveness may depend partly on perceived moral capital because donors expect NGOs to justify trust, legitimacy, and value alignment more strongly than other organisation types.

2. Moral capital may help nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under uncertainty by reducing donor doubts about organisational worthiness, integrity, and mission credibility.

3. Moral capital should not yet be merged with brand authenticity, reputation, trust, or legitimacy because its discriminant boundaries remain unresolved.

4. Moral capital may operate as a societal-level condition that shapes individual-level donor perceptions, including brand associations, identification, and commitment.

## Evidence Base

Evidence is based on `REVIEW-000024`, especially claim `MC-000002`, with related support from:

- `BA-000011`: brand associations include perceived reputation, differentiation, and identification with NGO values.
- `DB-000004`: brand associations positively affect donor-based brand equity.
- `DB-000005`: brand commitment positively affects donor-based brand equity.
- `BC-000010`: commitment includes attitudinal and emotional dimensions.
- `BF-000005`: familiarity did not show a significant direct effect on donor-based brand equity in the reviewed model.

The reviewed empirical base relies primarily on a PLS-SEM study using a Spanish convenience sample of 131 respondents focused on childhood-related NGOs.

The evidence supports moral capital as a promising candidate concept but is not sufficient for canonical ontology status.

## Boundary Conditions

- Evidence is specific to childhood-related NGOs.
- Evidence comes from a Spanish context.
- The sample is a convenience sample of 131 respondents.
- Findings may not generalise to other nonprofit types, including religious, cultural, environmental, advocacy, humanitarian, health, or member-based organisations.
- The distinction between NGOs and broader nonprofit organisations remains important.
- Moral capital may overlap empirically with reputation, trust, legitimacy, authenticity, or perceived integrity.
- Evidence is not sufficient to determine whether moral capital is a distinct construct, a higher-order construct, or an interpretive umbrella concept.

## Competing Explanations

1. Reputation-based explanation  
   Donors may respond to general organisational reputation rather than moral capital specifically.

2. Trust-based explanation  
   The observed effects may reflect donor trust rather than a distinct moral-capital mechanism.

3. Legitimacy-based explanation  
   Donor support may depend on perceived organisational legitimacy rather than moral credibility as a separate construct.

4. Brand association explanation  
   Moral capital may be part of value-laden brand associations rather than an independent concept.

5. Authenticity explanation  
   Some aspects of moral capital may overlap with brand authenticity, although the reviewed model did not retain authenticity as a valid measurement component.

6. Familiarity explanation  
   Donor support may still depend partly on familiarity, but the reviewed evidence suggests familiarity alone is insufficient without evaluative meaning or commitment.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Moral Capital**: A candidate BEBA concept referring to the perceived moral credibility, legitimacy, and value-based trust that enables a nonprofit organisation to be regarded as worthy of support under uncertainty. Moral capital may connect societal-level expectations of nonprofit worthiness with individual-level donor trust, value identification, commitment, and supportive behaviour. Its boundaries with trust, legitimacy, reputation, and authenticity remain unresolved.

## Confidence

Low to medium.

The concept is theoretically important for BEBA and fits the research question on how nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective under uncertainty. However, the empirical base is limited by organisational category, national context, sample type, and unresolved measurement boundaries.

## Review Recommendation

Retain as an ontology candidate.

Do not promote to canonical ontology status yet.

Recommended next steps:

1. Compare moral capital against trust, legitimacy, reputation, authenticity, and brand associations.
2. Review additional literature on NGO moral legitimacy and donor trust.
3. Test whether moral capital is empirically distinct from reputation and perceived integrity.
4. Examine whether moral capital applies beyond NGOs to other nonprofit forms.
5. Use moral capital as a candidate bridge between societal uncertainty and individual donor behaviour in BEBA theory development.
