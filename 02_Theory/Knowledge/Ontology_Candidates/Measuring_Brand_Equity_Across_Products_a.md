# Concept

Brand Equity Measurement Adaptation

## Canonical Definition

Brand equity measurement adaptation is the process of translating and modifying general or commercial brand equity constructs so that they validly represent nonprofit-specific behaviour-enabling assets, supporter relationships, organisational meanings, and support behaviours.

## Alternative Definitions

- The sector-specific adjustment of brand equity dimensions to fit nonprofit organisations, supporter behaviour, cause contexts, and uncertainty conditions.
- The methodological translation of commercial brand equity indicators, such as price premium, market share, perceived quality, and loyalty, into nonprofit-relevant constructs such as support premium, perceived impact, organisational credibility, repeated support, advocacy, and persistence under uncertainty.

## Core Claims

1. Nonprofit brand equity should be measured as a behaviour-enabling asset system rather than as a single financial or fundraising outcome.

2. Commercial brand equity constructs require nonprofit-specific translation before they can validly represent supporter behaviour or nonprofit brand strength.

3. Price premium can be translated into support premium only when the relevant supporter cost is explicitly specified.

4. Perceived quality in nonprofit contexts should be translated into perceived competence, effectiveness, professionalism, or impact quality.

5. Awareness measures should be adapted to brand maturity: recognition may be more appropriate for new or niche nonprofits, whereas recall and top-of-mind awareness may be more appropriate for established organisations.

6. Loyalty should be interpreted through repeated support, recommendation, advocacy, willingness to donate more, willingness to volunteer, and persistence under uncertainty.

7. A single aggregate nonprofit brand equity index is theoretically risky unless construct inclusion, weighting, and aggregation are explicitly justified.

8. Organisational associations may be especially important in nonprofit brand equity because support decisions involve trust, credibility, legitimacy, and perceived mission integrity.

## Boundary Conditions

- Sector adaptation boundary: Commercial brand equity measures cannot be directly imported into nonprofit settings without conceptual adaptation.
- Brand maturity boundary: Appropriate awareness indicators depend on whether the nonprofit brand is new, niche, established, or highly visible.
- Supporter segment boundary: Brand equity indicators may differ across loyal supporters, occasional supporters, switchers, lapsed supporters, and non-supporters.
- Market behaviour boundary: Donation volume or market share of giving may reflect short-term tactics rather than durable brand equity.
- Construct overlap boundary: Perceived quality, perceived impact, perceived value, and donation efficacy may overlap and require empirical separation.
- Symbolic visibility boundary: Brand personality may matter more when supporter behaviour is socially visible or symbolic.
- Evidence-confidence boundary: Brand differentiation, brand leadership, and cause-area leadership should remain lower-confidence candidates until supported by stronger nonprofit-specific evidence.

## Competing Explanations

- Financial-outcome model: Treats fundraising volume, donation share, or income as sufficient indicators of brand equity.
- Aggregate-index model: Treats nonprofit brand equity as a single composite score.
- Awareness-only model: Treats public awareness as sufficient for behavioural effectiveness.
- Loyalty-only model: Treats loyalty as the full explanation of nonprofit brand equity.
- Service-satisfaction model: Explains supporter loyalty through satisfaction rather than brand equity.
- Cause-driven behaviour model: Explains support through cause salience, moral identity, emergency need, or social pressure rather than the nonprofit brand.

## Operationalisations

Candidate operationalisations include:

- Awareness: recognition, recall, top-of-mind awareness, adjusted by brand maturity.
- Associations: perceived organisational meanings, values, mission fit, symbolic meanings.
- Organisational associations: trustworthiness, credibility, competence, transparency, mission integrity, legitimacy.
- Perceived nonprofit quality: perceived competence, professionalism, effectiveness, impact quality.
- Perceived donation efficacy: belief that one’s contribution is meaningful, well-used, and impactful.
- Loyalty: repeat donation, repeat volunteering, recommendation, advocacy, persistence despite uncertainty.
- Support premium: willingness to donate more, volunteer more, tolerate inconvenience, advocate publicly, or remain loyal despite alternatives.
- Differentiation: perceived distinctiveness from comparable nonprofit organisations.
- Leadership: perceived cause-area prominence, innovativeness, influence, or momentum.
- Segmentation: loyal supporters, occasional supporters, switchers, lapsed supporters, non-supporters.
