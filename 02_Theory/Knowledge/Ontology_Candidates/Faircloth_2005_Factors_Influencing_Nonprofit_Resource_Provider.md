# Concept

Resource-Provider-Based Nonprofit Brand Equity

## Canonical Definition

Resource-provider-based nonprofit brand equity is the behavioural advantage a nonprofit brand creates by biasing resource-provider support decisions toward the organisation under conditions of uncertainty.

## Alternative Definitions

- Resource-provider-based brand equity is the incremental effect of nonprofit brand perceptions on support intentions beyond general altruistic disposition.
- Nonprofit brand equity is the configuration of evaluative, relational, cognitive, and heuristic brand cues that makes support more likely.
- Resource-provider-based brand equity is a biased decision to support a nonprofit brand rather than an otherwise comparable alternative.

## BEBA Layer

Primary layer: Individual  
Secondary layer: Organisational

The concept is primarily individual-level because it concerns resource providers’ perceptions, evaluations, associations, and support intentions. It has organisational relevance because it concerns how nonprofit organisations acquire behavioural advantage through brand meaning.

## Evidence Base

Evidence comes from `REVIEW-000022`, based on the reviewed claim set concerning Faircloth 2005.

The evidence base is primarily:

- exploratory,
- single-organisation,
- based on telephone survey data,
- focused on resource-provider support intentions,
- not based on observed donation, volunteering, advocacy, or long-term behaviour.

The reviewed claims support the concept as a candidate for BEBA theory development, but not as a canonical construct with settled dimensionality.

Direct evidence for mediation through uncertainty reduction is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

## Boundary Conditions

1. The evidence concerns support intentions, not observed resource-provider behaviour.

2. Findings may be organisation-specific because the empirical context is a single nonprofit organisation.

3. Awareness and familiarity are not necessarily positive. Their behavioural relevance depends on the valence and content of activated associations.

4. Brand image and awareness should not be treated as unitary constructs. Relevant distinctions include recall, familiarity, scale, character, respectability, differentiation, and evaluative valence.

5. The uncertainty-reduction mechanism is theoretically relevant but remains candidate-level because direct mediation evidence is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

6. Positive associations are not automatically behaviourally effective. Some favourable brand meanings may be symbolically positive but behaviourally inert.

## Competing Explanations

### General Prosocial Motivation Explanation

Resource-provider support may be explained by altruistic disposition alone.

Status: Incomplete.  
`NB-000026` suggests brand-related antecedents explain additional variance beyond altruistic disposition.

### Awareness-First Brand Equity Explanation

Support follows from brand awareness or top-of-mind recall.

Status: Weakened.  
Top-of-mind awareness was not a significant predictor in the reviewed study, and familiarity may be negatively associated with support intentions.

### Evaluative Association Explanation

Support is enabled by favourable brand meanings such as personality, image, differentiation, respect, and perceived scale.

Status: Strengthened as candidate.  
Supported by multiple reviewed claims.

### Relational Trust / Legitimacy Explanation

Support is enabled when brand meanings make the nonprofit appear respectable, trustworthy, or suitable as a resource-receiving partner.

Status: Strengthened conceptually.  
Direct mediation evidence is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

### Uncertainty-Reduction / Heuristic Explanation

Resource providers use nonprofit brand image to simplify support decisions under uncertainty.

Status: Strengthened as theoretical candidate.  
Direct empirical mediation evidence is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

### Self-Congruence Explanation

Support is more likely when nonprofit brand personality fits the resource provider’s self-concept.

Status: Candidate, low confidence.  
The reviewed material presents this as proposed rather than directly tested.

## Operationalisations

Possible operational dimensions include:

- support intention toward the nonprofit,
- brand personality perceptions,
- perceived brand respectability,
- perceived differentiation,
- perceived brand image,
- perceived organisational scale,
- top-of-mind awareness,
- brand familiarity,
- valence of familiarity,
- perceived congruence between brand personality and self-concept,
- perceived trustworthiness or relational suitability.

Observed support behaviour should not be inferred unless separately measured.
