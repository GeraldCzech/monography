# Concept

Volunteer Brand Equity (VBE)

## Canonical Definition

Volunteer Brand Equity (VBE) is a stakeholder-specific form of internal brand equity representing the value created by volunteers through their brand-aligned attitudes and behaviours. It is the outcome of an effective internal Brand-Enabled Behaviour Architecture (BEBA) that translates organisational brand management practices into volunteer brand commitment, role clarity, and subsequent supportive behaviours (e.g., brand advocacy, citizenship).

## Alternative Definitions

VBE is conceptualised as a direct adaptation of Employee Brand Equity (EBE) to the nonprofit context, focusing on the unique relational and motivational aspects of volunteering. It stands in contrast to externally-focused Customer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE), as its antecedents are internal management practices and its primary manifestation is through the behaviour of internal stakeholders rather than the perceptions of external ones.

## BEBA Layer

-   **Organisational Layer:** VBE is an outcome of architectural components like `Internal Brand Management`, `Perceived Management Support`, and `Relational Brand Architecture`.
-   **Individual Layer:** The mechanisms that build VBE operate at the individual level, involving psychological constructs such as `Volunteer Role Clarity` (cognitive) and `Brand Commitment` (affective).

## Core Claims

1.  **Claim (Mechanism):** The BEBA for internal stakeholders (volunteers) operates through a causal chain where organisational Internal Brand Management practices influence individual psychological states (Brand Commitment, Role Clarity), which in turn mediate the effect on brand-aligned behaviours. (Derived from `REVIEW-000091`)
2.  **Claim (Primacy of Affect):** For generating volunteer advocacy behaviours (e.g., positive word-of-mouth), the affective pathway via Brand Commitment is a more potent driver than the cognitive pathway via Role Clarity. (Derived from `REVIEW-000091`, based on claim `BC-000041`)
3.  **Claim (Architecture Composition):** The social and relational components of internal brand management (e.g., trust, support, respect) are necessary conditions for an effective BEBA, not merely supportive factors for its informational components. (Derived from `REVIEW-000091`)

## Evidence Base

The concept is supported by claims `VB-000003`, `VB-000004`, `BC-000041`, and `IB-000023`, which are synthesized in `REVIEW-000091`. The primary evidence comes from a single exploratory study of formal volunteers in the Portuguese nonprofit sector.

## Boundary Conditions

-   **Context:** Evidence is limited to the Portuguese cultural context.
-   **Stakeholder Type:** The model applies specifically to formal, registered volunteers. It may not generalize to informal, episodic, or micro-volunteers.
-   **Methodology:** Findings are based on correlational and exploratory factor analysis data. The proposed mediation model requires confirmatory testing (e.g., via SEM).

## Competing Explanations

-   **Generic CBBE Models (Aaker, Keller):** These models are insufficient as they lack the internal constructs (e.g., internal management, role clarity) necessary to explain the behaviour of internal stakeholders like volunteers.
-   **General Organisational Behaviour Models:** VBE provides a brand-centric explanation, linking behaviour specifically to the brand as the focal object, whereas other models might attribute it more generically to organisational commitment or job satisfaction without this brand-specific link.

## Operationalisations

VBE is operationalised as a multidimensional, latent construct measured via self-report surveys completed by volunteers. The dimensions are adapted from established Employee Brand Equity (EBE) scales.

## Existing Scales

Scales are adapted from EBE literature. The specific items used in the foundational study must be consulted for a precise measurement model. No canonical, widely-validated VBE scale currently exists.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Volunteer Brand Equity (VBE):** A form of internal brand equity representing the value volunteers contribute to a nonprofit's brand through their brand-aligned attitudes and behaviours. It is the result of organisational efforts to build brand understanding and commitment among its volunteer workforce.

## Confidence

**Candidate - Exploratory.** The concept is theoretically sound and has initial empirical support from a single study. However, it requires significant further validation across different contexts and with confirmatory methods before it can be considered canonical.
