# Concept

CBBE_TPB Integration

## Canonical Definition

A candidate theoretical integration in which Customer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) is modelled as an upstream antecedent of Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) mechanisms, especially stakeholder attitude and behavioural intention.

In BEBA terms, the concept proposes that brand equity becomes behaviourally effective when it shapes evaluative, motivational, or decision-relevant structures that influence supportive behaviour.

## Alternative Definitions

- Brand-equity-enabled behavioural intention model.
- CBBE-as-antecedent TPB model.
- Integrated brand-behaviour model.
- Brand-enabled attitude formation pathway.

## Core Claims

1. Brand equity may be behaviourally consequential when integrated with behavioural theory.

2. CBBE may operate upstream of TPB-related constructs, especially attitude and behavioural intention.

3. Attitude is a central candidate mechanism translating brand equity into behavioural intention.

4. Brand equity may also influence behavioural intention directly, so full mediation through attitude should not be assumed.

5. Integrated brand-behaviour models may explain behavioural intention better than brand-equity-only or TPB-only models.

6. Satisfaction may mediate brand equity effects in repeated or post-experience supportive behaviours.

7. Brand equity effects are likely contingent on behaviour type, involvement, perceived sacrifice, stakeholder group, and mission field.

8. Nonprofit-specific claims remain candidate claims because the reviewed evidence base is mainly cross-sectoral and for-profit.

## Boundary Conditions

1. Sectoral boundary condition  
   Most evidence is for-profit or cross-sectoral. Nonprofit generalisation remains uncertain.

2. Mission-field contingency  
   Brand equity dimensions may operate differently across humanitarian, environmental, health, cultural, educational, religious, advocacy, or other nonprofit fields.

3. Behaviour type  
   Initial support, repeat support, donation, volunteering, advocacy, membership, and recommendation may involve different behavioural mechanisms.

4. Involvement level  
   Brand awareness may be less influential in high-involvement decisions.

5. Perceived sacrifice  
   Nonprofit support may involve financial, temporal, emotional, opportunity, social, or reputational costs.

6. Stakeholder heterogeneity  
   Demographic and stakeholder-group differences may moderate brand-behaviour pathways, but the reviewed material does not specify stable moderator patterns.

## Competing Explanations

1. TPB-only explanation  
   Behavioural intention is explained by standard TPB constructs without brand equity.

2. Brand-equity-only explanation  
   Behavioural intention is explained directly by brand equity dimensions.

3. Full mediation model  
   Brand equity influences intention only through attitude.

4. Partial mediation model  
   Brand equity influences intention both indirectly through attitude and directly.

5. Satisfaction-based repeat-behaviour model  
   For repeat behaviour, satisfaction may become a stronger mediator than initial attitude.

6. Contingent model  
   Brand equity effects vary by mission field, involvement, perceived sacrifice, stakeholder group, and behaviour type.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**CBBE_TPB Integration**  
A candidate BEBA concept describing the integration of Customer-Based Brand Equity with the Theory of Planned Behavior, where brand equity is modelled as an upstream structure that may shape stakeholder attitude, behavioural intention, and related behavioural mechanisms.
