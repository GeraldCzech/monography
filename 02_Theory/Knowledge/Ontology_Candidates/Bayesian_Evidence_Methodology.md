# Concept

Bayesian Evidence Methodology

## Canonical Definition

A meta-theoretical framework for cumulative theory building that uses Bayesian epistemology to formalize the process of research. It treats theory development as the systematic, transparent, and replicable aggregation of uncertain evidence into a coherent knowledge structure. The framework shifts the goal from discrete hypothesis testing (e.g., NHST) to the continuous updating of degrees of belief in a set of competing theoretical explanations.

## Alternative Definitions

- **Null-Hypothesis Significance Testing (NHST) Paradigm:** A frequentist approach focused on rejecting null hypotheses based on p-values, often treating studies as discrete, decisive tests rather than sources of evidence to be aggregated.
- **Narrative Literature Review:** A qualitative, often informal method for synthesizing research findings, which can be susceptible to subjective interpretation and lacks a formal mechanism for weighing evidence.

## BEBA Layer

**Organisational Layer.** This concept defines the epistemological and procedural "operating system" (BEBA-ROS) for the entire research project, governing how claims at all other layers are generated, evaluated, and integrated.

## Core Claims

The methodology is defined by a set of foundational claims:
- **BM-000008:** The synthesis of Bayesian epistemology, the likelihood paradigm, and knowledge graph technology constitutes a novel and coherent methodological framework for theory building in nonprofit brand research.
- **BE-000065:** Evidence from multiple, heterogeneous sources can be formally aggregated using Bayesian methods to update belief in a claim.
- **LP-000001:** The likelihood ratio is the proper and sufficient measure for quantifying the strength of statistical evidence for one hypothesis versus a competitor.
- **JC-000001:** Jeffrey Conditionalization provides a formal rule for updating beliefs in light of new evidence that is itself uncertain.
- **ET-000001:** A rigorous workflow proceeding from raw evidence to structured theory via a formal ontology is necessary for transparent and cumulative science.
- **KG-000001:** A knowledge graph is the optimal data structure for representing the complex relationships between evidence, claims, constructs, and theories in the BEBA project.

## Evidence Base

The evidence for this methodology is not empirical but is rooted in foundational literature from the philosophy of science, epistemology, and statistics. Key conceptual pillars include the work on Bayesian reasoning (E.T. Jaynes), the Likelihood Paradigm (A.W.F. Edwards, Richard Royall), and Jeffrey Conditionalization (Richard C. Jeffrey). Its application in the form of knowledge graphs draws from computer science and information theory.

## Boundary Conditions

The applicability of this methodology sets a boundary for the BEBA theory itself. For a claim to be considered a well-supported component of the BEBA theory, it must be justifiable and processable through the defined `Evidence-Theory-Ontology Workflow`. Claims or findings that cannot be represented and evaluated within this framework are considered outside the formal scope of the theory.

## Competing Explanations

The primary competing methodological frameworks in the broader social sciences are:
1.  **Null-Hypothesis Significance Testing (NHST):** The dominant paradigm focused on p-values and dichotomous accept/reject decisions for individual studies.
2.  **Informal/Narrative Synthesis:** The traditional method of literature review that relies on authorial judgment without a formal, replicable calculus for evidence aggregation.

## Operationalisations

This methodology is operationalised within the BEBA Research Operating System (BEBA-ROS) through:
1.  The **Evidence-Theory-Ontology Workflow** as the defined research process.
2.  The use of a **Knowledge Graph** (implemented via markdown files and supporting tools) to represent all research objects and their relationships.
3.  The explicit use of **Likelihoods** and **Bayesian updates** (conceptually, and where possible, computationally) to assess the status of claims and models.
