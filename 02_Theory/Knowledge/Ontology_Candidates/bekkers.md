# Concept

Multi-mechanism Giving Architecture

## Canonical Definition

A **multi-mechanism giving architecture** is the configuration of interacting cognitive, motivational, social, economic, and contextual mechanisms through which charitable giving becomes more or less likely under conditions of uncertainty.

In BEBA, the concept specifies that nonprofit giving should not be explained by a single motive or brand perception, but by the way nonprofit brand architecture activates, frames, coordinates, and stabilises multiple giving mechanisms.

## Alternative Definitions

- Charitable giving as the outcome of multiple interacting donor mechanisms.
- Integrated giving mechanism architecture.
- Behavioural architecture of charitable giving.
- Multi-route explanation of donation behaviour.

## Core Claims

1. Charitable giving is better explained as the outcome of multiple interacting mechanisms than as the result of one dominant motive.

2. Nonprofit brands become behaviourally effective when they make need salient, credible, and actionable.

3. Branded solicitation can function as a behavioural trigger by structuring the ask and reducing action ambiguity.

4. Nonprofit brands can reduce uncertainty about whether a contribution will make a meaningful difference.

5. Giving may be supported by psychological benefits such as warm glow, moral satisfaction, or self-image reinforcement.

6. Giving may be supported by reputational benefits when donations are socially visible and meaningful to relevant observers.

7. Value congruence between donor and nonprofit mission is a distinct route through which brands support giving.

8. Cost effects depend not only on objective financial incentives but also on their communicative framing.

9. Public funding signals do not have a universal effect on private giving and should be treated as contingent field-level conditions.

10. BEBA should model not only individual mechanisms but also their interaction, sequencing, and relative salience.

## Boundary Conditions

- **Beneficiary representation:** Identifiable beneficiaries may produce stronger responses than statistical presentations of large beneficiary groups.
- **Social observability:** Reputational benefits depend on whether giving is visible to socially relevant observers.
- **Strength of social ties:** Social signalling effects are likely stronger when observers are meaningful to the donor.
- **Incentive framing:** Economically equivalent incentives may have different effects when framed differently, for example as matching grants or rebates.
- **External credibility signals:** Leadership gifts or seed money may strengthen perceived efficacy by signalling quality or viability.
- **Public funding context:** Government funding may crowd out, crowd in, or have no effect on private donations.
- **Mechanism salience:** Different giving mechanisms may dominate in different campaign, donor, organisational, or field conditions.

## Competing Explanations

1. **Altruism-dominant model**  
   Giving is primarily caused by concern for beneficiaries.

2. **Warm-glow model**  
   Giving is primarily caused by internal psychological reward.

3. **Reputation/signalling model**  
   Giving is primarily caused by social visibility and reputational benefit.

4. **Efficacy model**  
   Giving is primarily caused by confidence that the gift will make a difference.

5. **Solicitation-trigger model**  
   Giving is primarily caused by direct asks and behavioural prompts.

6. **Value-congruence model**  
   Giving is primarily caused by alignment between donor values and nonprofit mission.

7. **Cost-framing model**  
   Giving is primarily caused by perceived cost, incentives, and incentive framing.

8. **Linear brand-equity model**  
   Giving is primarily caused by awareness, attitude, trust, or preference.

9. **BEBA integrated model**  
   Giving is caused by the configuration, interaction, and salience of multiple mechanisms enabled by nonprofit brand architecture.
