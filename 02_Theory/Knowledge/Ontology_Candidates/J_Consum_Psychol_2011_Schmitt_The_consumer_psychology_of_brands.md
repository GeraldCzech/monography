# Concept

Layered Brand Engagement

## Canonical Definition

Layered Brand Engagement refers to the degree and form through which a stakeholder relates to a brand at object-centered, self-centered, and social levels, with each level potentially enabling different forms of behavioural readiness under uncertainty.

## Alternative Definitions

- Brand engagement as stakeholder involvement with a brand as an object of attention, evaluation, identity relevance, or social connection.
- Brand engagement as a multi-level psychological relation linking brand cognition, meaning formation, attachment, community, and behaviour.
- In Schmitt’s framework, brand engagement can be understood across object-centered, self-centered, and social engagement levels.

## Core Claims

1. Brand engagement can occur at object-centered, self-centered, and social levels.
2. Object-centered engagement is associated with brand attitude and evaluative response.
3. Self-centered engagement is associated with attachment, identity relevance, and personal meaning.
4. Social engagement is associated with brand community, interpersonal meaning, and socio-cultural embedding.
5. Engagement levels should not be treated as a mandatory linear sequence.
6. Behavioural effectiveness may depend on which engagement level becomes salient for a stakeholder in a given context.
7. Nonprofit brands may become behaviourally effective when engagement supports uncertainty reduction, identity signalling, or collective meaning.
8. Stakeholder characteristics may moderate which engagement level becomes behaviourally relevant.

## Boundary Conditions

- Engagement levels may not unfold in a fixed sequence.
- Cultural context may affect brand meaning, symbolism, and personality structure.
- Stakeholder self-construal may influence whether self-centered or social engagement becomes salient.
- General brand engagement tendency may affect whether stakeholders treat brands as psychologically meaningful objects.
- Object-centered engagement may be sufficient for evaluation or preference but insufficient for stronger nonprofit behaviours such as volunteering, advocacy, or long-term commitment.
- Social engagement may be especially relevant where behaviour depends on community, collective identity, or peer coordination.
- Evidence from consumer brand psychology must be adapted cautiously to nonprofit contexts.

## Competing Explanations

1. **Linear Funnel Explanation**  
   Brand effects progress from awareness to knowledge to attitude to behaviour.  
   Status: Weakened by the non-linearity claim.

2. **Attitude-Only Explanation**  
   Brand attitude is the main mediator of brand-enabled behaviour.  
   Status: Insufficient because attachment and community represent deeper engagement levels.

3. **Knowledge-Structure Explanation**  
   Behavioural readiness depends on associative brand knowledge and cognitive availability.  
   Status: Supported, but incomplete without meaning and social engagement.

4. **Signification Explanation**  
   Brands enable behaviour by functioning as informational cues, identity signals, and cultural symbols.  
   Status: Supported as a candidate BEBA mechanism.

5. **Social-Community Explanation**  
   Nonprofit brands enable behaviour through social embedding, community formation, and collective interpretation.  
   Status: Supported, especially for advocacy, volunteering, and movement-like behaviours.

6. **Stakeholder-Fit Explanation**  
   Brand effects depend on person-brand-context fit rather than brand properties alone.  
   Status: Supported as a necessary moderation perspective.

## Operationalisations

Possible operational distinction:

- Object-centered engagement: brand attitude, evaluation, preference, perceived relevance.
- Self-centered engagement: attachment, identification, self-brand connection, personal meaning.
- Social engagement: brand community, shared identity, peer-based meaning, collective participation.
- Behavioural readiness: intention or readiness to donate, volunteer, advocate, recommend, participate, or support.
- Uncertainty reduction: perceived diagnosticity, trust cue function, symbolic clarity, identity reassurance.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Layered Brand Engagement**  
A multi-level stakeholder–brand relation in which engagement may remain object-centered, become self-centered, or become socially embedded. In BEBA, layered brand engagement helps explain how nonprofit brands move from cognitive availability and meaning formation toward behavioural readiness under uncertainty. The levels should not be assumed to occur in a fixed sequence.
