# Concept

Warm_Glow_Giving

## Canonical Definition

The private, non-pecuniary utility, often experienced as emotional satisfaction, that an individual derives from the act of giving itself, independent of the actual impact of the donation on the recipient (Andreoni, 1989, 1990). It is the core component of impure altruism models.

## Alternative Definitions

-   Egoistic giving
-   The "joy of giving"
-   A form of private consumption purchased through donation

## Core Claims

-   A significant portion of charitable giving is motivated by the warm glow utility an individual derives from the act of giving (WG-000001).
-   Charitable giving is better explained by an impure altruism model (combining warm glow and pure altruism) than by a pure altruism model (IA-000001).
-   Warm glow explains why individuals donate even when their contribution has no marginal effect on the total funds raised (e.g., in the presence of government grants that crowd out private donations) (WG-000002).
-   The psychological mechanisms generating warm glow utility include self-image enhancement (affecting participation likelihood) and social-image management (affecting donation amount) (SI-000001, SI-000002).
-   Fundraising appeals that emphasize the donor's emotional benefit can be more effective than appeals focused solely on recipient outcomes (FA-000001).

## Evidence Base

The concept is supported by a large body of experimental evidence, particularly from economics. Key evidence types include:
-   **Crowd-out experiments:** These studies show that individuals continue to donate even when their donation is perfectly substituted by a third-party grant, a behaviour inconsistent with pure altruism but predicted by warm glow (EVID-000032, EVID-000033).
-   **Dictator games and variations:** Experiments show that giving behaviour is influenced by factors related to the self, such as whether the funds were earned or a windfall (EVID-000040).
-   **Fundraising appeal studies:** Field experiments demonstrate that messaging targeting donor benefits can increase participation and/or donation amounts (EVID-000041, EVID-000042).

## Boundary Conditions

The warm glow effect is not universal and can be moderated or suppressed under certain conditions:
-   **Cause-Related Marketing Context:** The effect can be negated or reversed by consumer skepticism, especially if the donation amount is perceived as too large or overly publicized, triggering suspicion about the firm's motives (CR-000001, WG-000004).
-   **Religious/Moral Context:** Warm glow may not fully explain giving in contexts where behaviour is primarily driven by a sense of duty, moral obligation, or religious doctrine (RG-000001).
-   **Cultural Context:** The nature and prevalence of warm glow motivations may vary across cultures, a factor noted as underexplored (EVID-000049).

## Competing Explanations

-   **Pure Altruism:** The theory that individuals are solely motivated by a concern for the well-being of the recipient. Warm glow is proposed as a necessary addition to this model, not a complete replacement.
-   **Duty-Based Morality:** Frameworks (e.g., Kantian ethics, certain religious doctrines) where prosocial acts are performed out of a sense of moral obligation, irrespective of emotional payoff.
-   **Signaling Theory:** While related to the social-image component of warm glow, signaling theory can be a broader explanation focused on demonstrating wealth, status, or prosocial traits to an audience, without necessarily requiring an internal 'glow'.

## Operationalisations

Warm glow is a latent theoretical construct and is typically operationalised or measured indirectly through:
-   **Experimental Design:** Isolating the warm glow motive by creating conditions where a donation has no marginal impact on the public good (e.g., crowd-out designs). The residual giving is attributed to warm glow.
-   **Behavioural Observation:** Measuring donation likelihood versus donation amount, which are theorized to be differentially affected by self-image (participation) and social-image (amount) motives.
-   **Manipulated Stimuli:** Using fundraising appeals that vary in their emphasis on donor-centric emotional benefits versus recipient-centric outcomes.
