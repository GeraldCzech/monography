# Concept

Brand Equity Network

## Canonical Definition

A **Brand Equity Network** is a stakeholder-specific configuration of brand-related associations, evaluations, and relational meanings whose strength, structure, and activation patterns shape how a nonprofit brand becomes behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty.

## Alternative Definitions

- A networked representation of brand equity in which brand knowledge, trust, identification, perceived competence, and behavioural readiness are connected rather than treated as independent dimensions.
- A cognitive-relational architecture through which nonprofit brand encounters activate meanings that influence support behaviour.
- A process-oriented alternative to static brand equity indices, emphasizing association structure and behavioural activation rather than aggregate brand equity scores.

## BEBA Layer

**Primary layer:** Individual

The concept primarily concerns stakeholder-level brand knowledge, association structures, trust, identification, understanding, perceived competence, and behavioural response.

**Secondary layers:**

- **Organisational:** supports diagnosis of where brand-enabled behaviour breaks down.
- **Market:** varies across cause domains, stakeholder markets, platforms, and competitive environments.
- **Societal:** moderated by cultural, institutional, and legitimacy conditions.

## Core Claims

1. Nonprofit brand equity is better understood as a processual and networked behavioural architecture than as a static aggregate score.

2. Static brand equity indices may be useful for benchmarking, monitoring, and descriptive comparison, but they are insufficient for explaining how brand equity produces behaviourally relevant effects.

3. Brand equity operates through mediating cognitive and relational constructs, including brand knowledge, understanding, trust, identification, perceived competence, and relationship strength.

4. Brand knowledge is not merely a list of associations but a structured network whose configuration and activation influence stakeholder interpretation and behavioural readiness.

5. Nonprofit brand encounters become behaviourally relevant when they activate association patterns that reduce uncertainty, support relational evaluation, or make support behaviour more intelligible.

6. The behavioural effectiveness of brand equity mechanisms is contingent on cultural, sectoral, platform, cause-domain, and institutional conditions.

7. Brand-enabled behaviour should be distinguished from financial valuation. Financial outcomes are possible organisational consequences of behaviour, but they should not define nonprofit brand equity itself.

## Boundary Conditions

1. **Commercial-to-nonprofit transfer boundary**  
   Commercial brand equity mechanisms should not be imported directly into nonprofit contexts. Nonprofit support behaviours differ from purchase behaviour because they often involve moral motivation, uncertainty about impact, trust in organisational integrity, cause legitimacy, social desirability, and lower direct personal benefit.

2. **Behaviour type boundary**  
   Different nonprofit behaviours may require different network configurations and activation pathways. One-time donation, recurring donation, volunteering, advocacy, and public endorsement should not be assumed to follow the same brand equity process.

3. **Context boundary**  
   Cultural, national, platform, cause-domain, crisis, and institutional conditions may moderate which brand associations matter and how trust, legitimacy, or identification are formed.

4. **Measurement boundary**  
   The concept requires careful construct mapping. Constructs such as brand knowledge, trust, identification, understanding, and perceived competence must not be treated as interchangeable.

5. **Static benchmarking boundary**  
   Static brand equity measures may remain useful for dashboards and descriptive comparison but should not be used as full explanations of brand-enabled behaviour.

## Competing Explanations

The Brand Equity Network concept should be evaluated against alternative explanations of nonprofit support behaviour, including:

- emergency salience,
- moral obligation,
- social pressure,
- peer request,
- religious or ideological duty,
- tax incentives,
- habit or subscription default,
- direct beneficiary identification,
- general institutional trust,
- political or cause-based mobilisation.

These mechanisms may bypass nonprofit brand equity or interact with it. BEBA should therefore avoid assuming that all support behaviour is brand-enabled.

## Operationalisations

Possible operationalisations include:

- strength of brand associations,
- valence of brand associations,
- centrality of trust-related associations,
- perceived clarity of organisational purpose,
- perceived competence or effectiveness,
- identification with the organisation or cause,
- perceived legitimacy,
- relationship strength,
- behavioural intention,
- actual support behaviour where verified pipeline data are available,
- network structure of brand associations,
- activation patterns following brand exposure or brand encounter.

Operationalisation should distinguish between:

```text
brand awareness
brand knowledge
brand understanding
trust
identification
intention
behaviour
```

These should not be collapsed into a single brand equity score unless the purpose is explicitly descriptive benchmarking.

## Confidence

**Conceptual confidence:** High  
The reviewed claim set strongly supports the move from static brand equity indices toward processual and network-oriented brand equity architecture.

**Nonprofit-specific empirical confidence:** Medium to low  
The nonprofit transferability of the reviewed evidence remains a boundary condition because some claims appear grounded in commercial consumer brand equity research.

**Measurement confidence:** Medium  
The concept is theoretically useful but requires careful construct mapping and scale documentation before empirical use.

## Review Recommendation

**Recommended status:** Candidate integration, high priority.

The concept should be retained as an ontology candidate and used to support BEBA’s process-oriented architecture. It should not yet be canonized without manual review of nonprofit-specific evidence, construct definitions, and measurement implications.

Recommended next steps:
