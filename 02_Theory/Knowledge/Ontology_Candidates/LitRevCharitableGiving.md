## Canonical Definition

Charitable giving, in the Austrian BEBA context, is a resource-constrained prosocial behaviour in which individuals donate money or equivalent support to nonprofit causes or organisations, shaped by solicitation, socio-economic capacity, trust, institutional welfare-regime framing, and cause-allocation dynamics.

---

## Alternative Definitions

1. **Behavioural outcome definition:**  
   Charitable giving is the observable act of donating to nonprofit causes or organisations.

2. **BEBA mechanism-sensitive definition:**  
   Charitable giving is a behaviour activated by asks, legitimised by trust and institutional context, and allocated across competing causes under resource constraints.

3. **Austrian context definition:**  
   Charitable giving is a widespread but comparatively modest private contribution practice that operates as a supplement to state responsibility in a corporatist welfare regime.

## Boundary Conditions

1. **Austrian welfare-regime boundary**  
   Austria’s corporatist welfare context frames charitable giving as supplementary to state responsibility.

2. **Resource constraint boundary**  
   Income and education affect giving likelihood and amount.

3. **Solicitation exposure boundary**  
   Brand effects may depend on whether the donor is asked.

4. **Brand familiarity boundary**  
   External trust signals may matter more for lesser-known organisations than for familiar ones.

5. **Crisis-specific boundary**  
   Evidence for cause substitution is linked to Covid-19-related giving; generalisation to other crises remains uncertain.

6. **Tax-incentive evidence boundary**  
   Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

7. **Digital fundraising evidence boundary**  
   Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature for the Austrian charitable giving context.

8. **Experimental cause-choice boundary**  
   Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

## Competing Explanations

1. **Direct Brand Equity Model**  
   Nonprofit brand equity directly increases donation behaviour.

2. **Solicitation Model**  
   Being asked explains donation incidence more strongly than passive brand attitudes.

3. **Behavioural Activation Model**  
   Brand equity becomes effective when it legitimises or increases receptivity to an ask.

4. **Trust/Familiarity Model**  
   Trust and brand familiarity reduce donation risk and enable giving.

5. **Certification Model**  
   Formal labels and external trust signals reduce uncertainty, especially for lesser-known organisations.

6. **Socio-Economic Resource Model**  
   Donation behaviour is primarily constrained by income, education, and capacity.

7. **Welfare-Regime Model**  
   Institutional norms define the ceiling and meaning of private giving.

8. **Cause Allocation Model**  
   Brands affect where donations go more than whether total giving increases.

## Operationalisations

Possible operational dimensions:

1. **Donation incidence**  
   Whether a person donated within a defined period.

2. **Donation amount**  
   Monetary amount donated within a defined period.

3. **Donation frequency**  
   Number of donation acts within a defined period.

4. **Cause allocation**  
   Distribution of giving across causes.

5. **Organisation choice**  
   Selection of a specific nonprofit organisation.

6. **Solicitation exposure**  
   Whether and how the donor was asked.

7. **Trust in nonprofit organisation**  
   Perceived honesty, competence, and reliability.

8. **Brand familiarity**  
   Recognition or prior knowledge of the nonprofit.

9. **Perceived donation risk**  
   Concern that donated resources may be misused, wasted, or ineffective.

10. **Trust-signal exposure**  
   Awareness of certification, labels, seals, transparency cues, or third-party endorsements.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Charitable Giving**  
Charitable giving refers to the act of donating resources, usually money, to nonprofit causes or organisations. In the Austrian BEBA context, charitable giving should be treated as a multidimensional behaviour involving donation incidence, donation amount, donation frequency, cause allocation, and organisation choice. It is shaped by solicitation, socio-economic capacity, trust, institutional framing, and uncertainty reduction rather than by brand attitudes alone.
