# Concept

Brand Attachment

## Canonical Definition

Brand Attachment is the strength of the cognitive-emotional bond connecting a brand with a person’s self-concept.

## Alternative Definitions

- A self-connected and emotionally meaningful brand bond.
- A brand relationship state indicated by the extent to which brand-related thoughts and feelings are personally connected and cognitively accessible.
- In the reviewed claim set, Brand Attachment is distinguished from Brand Attitude Strength: attachment refers to the brand–self bond, whereas attitude strength refers to the strength, stability, or certainty of evaluation.

## Core Claims

1. Brand Attachment and Brand Attitude Strength are conceptually and empirically distinct.
2. Brand Attachment is constituted by the strength of the brand–self bond.
3. Brand Attachment is indicated by Brand–Self Connection and Brand Prominence.
4. Brand Attachment is especially relevant for difficult, costly, identity-relevant, enacted, and resource-intensive support behaviours.
5. Brand Attitude Strength may be sufficient for easy, low-cost, or weakly involving behaviours.
6. Behavioural Difficulty moderates the relative explanatory importance of attachment-based versus attitude-based pathways.
7. Brand Prominence should not be reduced to basic brand awareness; it refers to accessibility of brand-related thoughts and feelings.
8. Positive Brand Attachment should not be used directly to explain aversive brand relations such as rejection, distrust, antagonism, or moral opposition.

## Boundary Conditions

- **Behavioural Difficulty:** Attachment is expected to matter more when support requires money, time, effort, social exposure, emotional commitment, or sustained involvement.
- **Easy versus difficult behaviour:** Attitude strength may explain easy support behaviours sufficiently; attachment becomes more relevant for difficult or resource-intensive behaviours.
- **Positive brand bonds only:** The reviewed attachment model applies to positive relationships and should not be extended to negative nonprofit brand relations without additional theory.
- **Identity-based versus instrumental self-connection:** Attachment may differ depending on whether the nonprofit brand expresses supporter identity or helps the supporter achieve valued goals.
- **Relationship length:** Attachment may develop over time through repeated experience, accumulated trust, or identity integration; confidence in this implication remains low based on the reviewed material.

## Competing Explanations

1. **Attitude Strength Model**  
   Support behaviour is explained by favourable, stable, or confident brand evaluations.

2. **Attachment Model**  
   Support behaviour is explained by self-connected, emotionally meaningful, and prominent brand bonds.

3. **Awareness / Salience Model**  
   Support behaviour is explained by brand awareness, recall, or top-of-mind accessibility.

4. **Intention Model**  
   Stated support intentions are treated as sufficient indicators of likely support behaviour.

5. **Relational-Development Model**  
   High-commitment support emerges through cumulative relationship experience, familiarity, trust, and identity integration.

Recommended BEBA framing: compare attitude-only, attachment-only, and difficulty-contingent dual-pathway models.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Brand Attachment**  
Brand Attachment is the strength of the cognitive-emotional bond connecting a brand with a person’s self-concept. In BEBA, it is treated as an individual-level mechanism that may explain high-commitment, costly, identity-relevant, or enacted nonprofit support behaviour, especially under behavioural difficulty. It is distinct from Brand Attitude Strength, which refers to the strength or stability of evaluative judgment toward a brand.

## Confidence

**Moderate.**

The conceptual distinction between Brand Attachment and Brand Attitude Strength appears well supported in the reviewed claim set. Confidence is stronger for the general distinction and the relevance of Brand Attachment to difficult behaviours. Confidence is lower for claims about relationship length and the precise action-readiness role of Brand Prominence.
