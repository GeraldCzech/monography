# Concept

Moral Norm

## Canonical Definition

A self-based standard of conduct, rooted in an individual's personal values, that creates a perceived moral obligation to perform or refrain from a specific behavior. It is an internalised sense of what is right and wrong, independent of direct social pressure.

## Alternative Definitions

-   **Personal Norm:** Often used interchangeably, referring to an individual's own expectations for their behavior in a given situation, which can be accompanied by self-administered sanctions like guilt.
-   Sometimes conflated with `Attitude`, but distinguished by its focus on moral obligation rather than a general positive or negative evaluation of the behavior.

## Related Concepts

-   **`Social Norm`:** Moral Norm is distinct. `Social Norm` reflects perceived social pressure from others (what others do or approve of), whereas `Moral Norm` reflects internalised, personal moral standards.
-   **`Intention`:** Moral Norm is a significant direct antecedent of behavioral intention.
-   **`Attitude`:** While related, `Moral Norm` has been shown to have predictive power over and above attitude, suggesting it captures a unique dimension of an individual's motivation.
-   **`Nonprofit Brand Equity`:** A core BEBA hypothesis is that effective nonprofit brands resonate with and activate these pre-existing moral norms.

## Core Claims

-   **MN-000008:** Moral norms are a significant and independent predictor of charitable giving intentions, over and above the standard TPB components.
-   **C-BEBA-012 (Candidate):** A key component of nonprofit brand equity is its ability to resonate with and activate an individual's pre-existing moral norms, making support feel like an obligation rather than a choice.

## Evidence Base

The evidence originates from extensive research on extensions to the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) and Integrated Behavioral Model (IBM), particularly in pro-social domains like charitable giving and environmental behavior. Meta-analyses and empirical studies consistently show that adding a `Moral Norm` construct significantly increases the explained variance in behavioral intentions (as per reviewed claim MN-000008).

## Boundary Conditions

-   The influence of moral norms is likely stronger for behaviors with clear ethical or value-laden implications (e.g., donating, volunteering, recycling) than for purely instrumental or self-interested behaviors.
-   Their predictive power may be heightened when an individual's moral identity is salient.

## Competing Explanations

The standard Theory of Planned Behavior is a competing model, which argues that the motivational force of moral considerations is sufficiently captured within the `Attitude` construct (i.e., "donating is a good thing to do"). The evidence base (MN-000008) directly challenges this, supporting the view that moral obligation is a distinct and separate motivator.

## Operationalisations

`Moral Norm` is typically operationalised via self-report survey items that measure an individual's perceived moral obligation or responsibility to perform the target behavior.

## Existing Scales

While specific scales vary by study, they commonly use Likert-type items anchored on statements such as:

-   "I feel a moral obligation to [donate to this NPO]."
-   "I believe I have a moral responsibility to [support this cause]."
-   "It would go against my principles if I did not [perform the behavior]."

A formal literature review is required to select a canonical scale.
