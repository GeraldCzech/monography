# Concept

Warm_Glow_Giving

## Canonical Definition

**Candidate definition:**  
Warm-glow giving is an individual-level motivational mechanism in which stakeholders derive intrinsic positive affect, moral satisfaction, or self-reward from the act of giving itself, partly independent of the objective impact of the contribution.

## Alternative Definitions

- Giving motivated by the emotional reward of helping.
- Donor utility derived from the act of contributing rather than only from social outcomes.
- Affective self-reward associated with prosocial behaviour.
- “Impure altruism” mechanism in which giving reflects both concern for beneficiaries and private emotional benefit.

## BEBA Layer

**Primary layer:** Individual

Warm-glow giving operates primarily at the level of stakeholder motivation, affective evaluation, behavioural intention, and repeated giving.

**Secondary relevance:** Societal / contextual

The strength and acceptability of warm-glow motivations may vary by cultural norms, moral identity, religious background, public visibility of giving, and cause type.

## Core Claims

1. **Warm-glow giving may function as an affective antecedent of nonprofit behavioural intention.**  
   It can strengthen willingness to donate or support an organisation by making the act of giving emotionally rewarding.

2. **Warm-glow giving may complement, but should not be conflated with, trust-based uncertainty reduction.**  
   Trust concerns confidence in the organisation or action pathway, whereas warm glow concerns the affective reward of acting.

3. **Warm-glow giving may partially explain why stakeholders act even when impact information is incomplete.**  
   Under uncertainty, stakeholders may still give because the act itself produces moral or emotional satisfaction.

4. **Warm-glow giving may compete with impact-driven explanations of nonprofit behaviour.**  
   Some giving may be motivated less by expected social effectiveness and more by the donor’s anticipated emotional benefit.

5. **Warm-glow giving may support repeat behaviour when giving produces positive post-action affect.**  
   Experienced warm glow may reinforce future intention, especially when combined with satisfaction and trust.

## Evidence Base

Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

The reviewed claim set `REVIEW-000001` primarily concerns integration of customer-based brand equity with IBM/TPB-style behavioural models. It supports BEBA-relevant mechanisms such as trust, perceived quality, satisfaction, awareness, attitude, perceived behavioural control, and behavioural intention, but it does **not** directly review warm-glow giving literature.

Therefore, warm-glow giving should remain a candidate concept requiring separate literature verification.

## Boundary Conditions

- **Behaviour type:** Most directly relevant to donating, but potentially also relevant to volunteering, advocacy, sharing, and membership.
- **Stakeholder role:** Likely strongest for donors and volunteers; relevance for beneficiaries, institutional funders, or policy actors is not sufficiently covered.
- **Cause type:** May vary by humanitarian, religious, environmental, cultural, health, or political causes.
- **Visibility of action:** Public giving may introduce reputational motives that are analytically distinct from warm glow.
- **Impact uncertainty:** Warm glow may become more important when stakeholders cannot easily verify outcomes.
- **Cultural context:** Moral satisfaction from giving may vary across cultures and normative environments.
- **Repeat behaviour:** Warm glow may influence retention only if the giving experience confirms expected emotional or moral reward.

## Competing Explanations

- Pure altruism: giving motivated by beneficiary welfare rather than donor affect.
- Impact efficacy: giving motivated by belief that the contribution produces meaningful outcomes.
- Trust-based action: giving motivated by confidence in the organisation.
- Social norms: giving motivated by perceived expectations of others.
- Reputation seeking: giving motivated by public image or social approval.
- Identity congruence: giving motivated by alignment with personal or group identity.
- Guilt reduction: giving motivated by avoidance or relief of negative affect.
- Habit: giving driven by routinised behaviour rather than active emotional reward.
- Incentives: giving influenced by tax benefits, matching campaigns, or material rewards.

## Operationalisations

Possible operationalisation should distinguish between:

- **Anticipated warm glow:** expected emotional satisfaction before giving.
- **Experienced warm glow:** emotional satisfaction after giving.
- **Moral satisfaction:** sense of having done the right thing.
- **Affective reward:** happiness, pride, fulfilment, or inner satisfaction from giving.
- **Independence from impact beliefs:** whether the emotional reward persists even when objective impact is uncertain.

Candidate item domains:

- “Giving to this organisation would make me feel good.”
- “I would feel personally satisfied after supporting this organisation.”
- “Supporting this cause would give me a sense of moral fulfilment.”
- “Even a small contribution would feel worthwhile to me.”
- “I would feel that I had done something good by supporting this organisation.”

These are candidate item domains, not validated scale items.
