# Concept

Warm Glow Giving

## Canonical Definition

Warm glow giving refers to the private utility or positive affective reward that individuals derive from the act of donating, partly independent of the objective public-good impact of their contribution.

## Alternative Definitions

- Private emotional reward from giving.
- Donor-centred utility generated by the act of donation.
- Affective reinforcement associated with acting generously or prosocially.
- The “good feeling” donors experience from giving, even when marginal impact is uncertain or unverifiable.

## BEBA Layer

**Primary layer:** Individual

**Secondary layers:**

- Organisational: fundraising appeals, brand touchpoints, recognition practices, and choice architecture can activate or inhibit warm glow.
- Market: cause-related marketing and commercial–prosocial hybrids may alter warm glow through skepticism or perceived inauthenticity.
- Societal: religious, moral, or institutional obligation systems may modify or compete with warm glow mechanisms.

## Core Claims

1. Warm glow functions as an individual-level behavioural reinforcement mechanism through which nonprofit brands can enable giving under uncertainty.

2. Warm glow helps explain why donors may give even when they cannot verify beneficiary outcomes or assess the marginal impact of their contribution.

3. Warm glow should not be collapsed into brand attitude, trust, perceived impact, or objective organisational effectiveness.

4. Nonprofit brands may activate warm glow by making giving emotionally rewarding, morally meaningful, identity-consistent, socially recognised, or easy to enact.

5. Warm glow is compatible with impure altruism, where giving combines beneficiary-oriented concern with donor-centred psychological reward.

6. Observability can amplify giving through social-image motivation but may also inhibit warm glow if giving appears inauthentic or excessively publicised.

7. Warm glow is bounded by context, including perceived authenticity, source of funds, moral or religious obligation, choice architecture, and commercial mediation.

## Boundary Conditions

1. **Observability**  
   Warm glow may interact with whether giving is private, public, recognised, or socially visible. Public giving may activate social-image motivation, while private giving may rely more strongly on self-image and affective reward.

2. **Perceived authenticity**  
   Warm glow may decline when giving, appeals, or prosocial brand communication appear inauthentic, over-publicised, or instrumentally self-serving.

3. **Source of funds**  
   Donations from earned income may generate stronger warm glow than donations from windfall income because the resource carries different psychological meaning.

4. **Moral or religious obligation**  
   In contexts where giving is governed by duty, ritual, religious normativity, or institutionalised obligation, warm glow may be weaker, differently structured, or secondary to obligation-based mechanisms.

5. **Choice architecture**  
   Donation behaviour and warm glow may vary depending on available alternatives, defaults, framing, or the moral structure of the decision environment.

6. **Commercial mediation**  
   In cause-related marketing or corporate–nonprofit partnerships, warm glow may be reduced by skepticism, attribution ambiguity, or perceived prosocial display.

7. **Donation decision stage**  
   Self-image motivation may be more relevant for donation initiation, while social-image motivation may be more relevant for donation amount or escalation once the decision to give has been made.

## Competing Explanations

1. **Impact-based model**  
   Donors give because they believe their contribution improves beneficiary outcomes.

2. **Trust-based model**  
   Donors give because they trust the nonprofit organisation to use resources competently and appropriately.

3. **Pure altruism model**  
   Donors give solely because they care about beneficiary welfare.

4. **Impure altruism model**  
   Donors give because beneficiary concern and donor-centred psychological reward are both active.

5. **Social signalling model**  
   Donors give to be perceived as generous, moral, or prosocial by others.

6. **Moral obligation model**  
   Donors give because giving is required by duty, religion, community expectation, or institutionalised norm.

7. **Nudge or choice architecture model**  
   Donors give because the decision environment makes giving easier, more salient, or behaviourally default-like.

Current assessment:

- The reviewed claim set supports impure altruism as the stronger baseline than either pure altruism or pure warm glow alone.
- Warm glow should be treated as complementary to, not a replacement for, perceived impact, trust, obligation, and social-image mechanisms.
