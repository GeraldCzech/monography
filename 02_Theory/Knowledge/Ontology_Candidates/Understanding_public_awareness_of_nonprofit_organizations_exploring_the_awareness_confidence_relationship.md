# Concept

Confidence

## Canonical Definition

Confidence is an affective-evaluative state representing a stakeholder's belief in a nonprofit organization's capability and effectiveness in fulfilling its stated mission and managing its resources. It is the affective consequence of cognitive awareness and a direct antecedent to trust and other supportive behaviours.

## Alternative Definitions

-   **Colloquial:** Often used interchangeably with `Trust`. However, in BEBA, Confidence is defined as a belief in *competence* (`Can they do it?`), whereas Trust involves a belief in *benevolent intent* (`Will they do it for the right reasons?`).
-   **Financial:** Sometimes narrowly defined as confidence in financial stewardship or transparency, which BEBA treats as a dimension or child concept of overall Confidence.

## BEBA Layer

-   **Individual Layer:** Confidence is a psychological state held by an individual stakeholder (e.g., donor, beneficiary, employee).

## Related Concepts

-   **Awareness:** The primary cognitive antecedent. One cannot have confidence in an NPO one is unaware of.
-   **Trust:** A related but distinct affective state. Confidence (in competence) is proposed as a necessary, but not sufficient, condition for Trust (in intent).
-   **Reputation:** A broader, aggregate judgment. Widespread confidence among stakeholders contributes to a positive reputation.
-   **Credibility:** The perceived expertise and trustworthiness of the organization. Confidence is a major component of credibility.
-   **Behavioural Intention:** Confidence is a key mediator between awareness and intentions to donate, volunteer, or utilize services.

## Core Claims

-   Cognitive Awareness is a necessary precursor to the affective state of Confidence (`HO-000002`).
-   Higher Awareness is a significant positive predictor of Confidence levels in the general public (`A-000009`).
-   Confidence is a key mediating variable in a sequential hierarchy of effects (`Awareness -> Confidence -> Behaviour`).
-   Confidence levels regarding specific operational domains (e.g., financial stewardship) may be significantly lower among internal stakeholders (employees) compared to the general public (`C-000002`).

## Evidence Base

The primary evidence for this conceptualisation comes from the synthesis of claims in `REVIEW-000090`. Key supporting claims include `A-000009`, `HO-000002`, and `C-000002`.

## Boundary Conditions

-   **Stakeholder Role:** The relationship between antecedents (like employment status) and Confidence is not uniform. The mechanism appears to differ for internal vs. external stakeholders (`C-000002`).
-   **Socio-demographic Profile:** The formation of Awareness, the necessary antecedent to Confidence, is moderated by education, income, and race/ethnicity, suggesting Confidence levels will also be inequitably distributed (`A-000010`, `A-000011`).
-   **Context:** The initial evidence is from a single US county study, requiring further validation in different geographic and cultural contexts.

## Competing Explanations

-   **Unitary Trust Models:** Models that do not differentiate between confidence-as-competence and trust-as-intent represent a competing view. BEBA's position is that this distinction is theoretically and empirically valuable.
-   **Direct Effect Models:** Models proposing a direct link from Awareness to Behaviour (e.g., donation) without the mediation of affective states like Confidence are challenged by this evidence.

## Operationalisations

Based on the source review, Confidence is operationalised as a stakeholder's assessed level of agreement with statements concerning an organization's ability to achieve its mission, make a positive impact, and manage funds effectively. The precise items must be sourced from the literature cited in `REVIEW-000090`.
