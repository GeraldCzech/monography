# Concept
Consumer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE)

## Canonical Definition
Consumer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) is a second-order latent construct representing the differential effect that brand knowledge has on consumer response to the marketing of that brand. It is composed of four distinct, correlated first-order dimensions: Brand Awareness, Brand Associations, Perceived Quality, and Brand Loyalty.

## Alternative Definitions
1.  **Aaker (1991)**: Conceptualised brand equity as comprising five dimensions: brand loyalty, name awareness, perceived quality, brand associations, and other proprietary brand assets.
2.  **Keller (1993)**: Defines CBBE as the differential effect of brand knowledge on consumer response. Brand knowledge is structured as a pyramid with two primary components: brand awareness and brand image (composed of brand associations).
3.  **Yoo & Donthu (2001)**: Proposed a more parsimonious model where Brand Awareness and Brand Associations are collapsed into a single dimension, resulting in a three-factor model (Awareness/Associations, Perceived Quality, Loyalty).

## BEBA Layer
Individual Layer. CBBE resides in the cognitive and affective structures of individual stakeholders.

## Core Claims
-   **CB-000020**: Defines Consumer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) as a higher-order construct with four dimensions: Brand Awareness, Brand Associations, Perceived Quality, and Brand Loyalty.
-   **CB-000021**: States that Brand Awareness and Brand Associations are empirically distinct dimensions.
-   **BA-000067**: Specifies that Brand Associations can be measured including brand personality and organizational associations.
-   **BL-000009**: Specifies that Brand Loyalty can be measured as an attitudinal construct.

## Evidence Base
The primary evidence base for this specific four-dimensional, second-order structure is the empirical work validating a measurement model across different product categories and countries, most notably in high-involvement commercial goods. This structure was shown to have good psychometric properties and discriminant validity between its dimensions. The claims cited are derived from a review of this evidence stream.

## Boundary Conditions
The model's empirical validation is primarily situated in commercial contexts, specifically with high-involvement consumer goods (e.g., cars, electronics) in developed consumer markets (e.g., Australia). Its direct applicability to low-involvement goods, services, or the nonprofit sector is a subject for further testing and adaptation.

## Competing Explanations
1.  **Three-Factor Model**: A primary competing explanation is a model where Brand Awareness and Brand Associations are collapsed into a single factor, arguing they are not empirically separable (e.g., Yoo & Donthu, 2001). Claim `CB-000021` directly refutes this.
2.  **Five-Factor Model (Aaker)**: Aaker's (1991) original model includes "other proprietary assets" as a fifth dimension, which is often omitted in consumer-based measurements due to its firm-centric nature.
3.  **Nonprofit Adaptation (BEBA Hypothesis)**: Within the BEBA project, a key competing explanation will be a nonprofit-specific model where dimensions are adapted (e.g., 'Perceived Quality' becomes 'Perceived Mission Impact') or new dimensions are added (e.g., 'Stakeholder-Mission Resonance').

## Operationalisations
This concept is operationalised as a second-order factor model in a structural equation modeling (SEM) framework. Measurement is conducted via a multi-item survey instrument where each of the four first-order constructs (Awareness, Associations, Quality, Loyalty) is measured using its own validated scale. The overall CBBE construct is a latent variable explained by the covariance of these four first-order factors.

## Existing Scales
The **Multidimensional Brand Equity (MBE) scale**, developed and refined by authors such as Yoo, Donthu, and Lee, is the most direct and widely used scale for this specific four-factor operationalisation.

## Recommended Glossary Entry
**Consumer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE)**: A multidimensional construct representing the value of a brand from the consumer's perspective. It is measured as a higher-order factor reflecting four key dimensions: what consumers are aware of (Brand Awareness), what they associate with the brand (Brand Associations), their judgment of its overall excellence (Perceived Quality), and their commitment to it (Brand Loyalty).

## Confidence
**High**: The validity of this four-dimensional, second-order model is well-established within its original commercial, high-involvement product context.
**Medium**: The direct, unmodified applicability of this model to the nonprofit sector is considered a strong hypothesis but requires empirical testing.

## Review Recommendation

It provides a robust, evidence-based, and falsifiable starting point for developing and testing nonprofit-specific adaptations of brand equity.
