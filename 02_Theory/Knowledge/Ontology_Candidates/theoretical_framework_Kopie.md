# Concept

Brand-Enabled Behavioural Architecture  
Abbreviation: BEBA  
Object type: Theory / Model candidate  
Status: Ontology candidate for manual review

## Canonical Definition

Brand-Enabled Behavioural Architecture is a candidate explanatory architecture describing how nonprofit brand equity becomes behaviourally effective under conditions of uncertainty by shaping both intention formation and behavioural enactment.

In BEBA, nonprofit brands do not merely increase donation intention. They help donors form beliefs, reduce uncertainty, develop trust and commitment, and translate intention into donation behaviour through enactment conditions such as knowledge, salience, habit, and reduced perceived constraints.

## Alternative Definitions

1. BEBA as an integrated nonprofit brand equity model linking brand perceptions to donation behaviour through TPB-style belief formation and IBM-style enactment conditions.

2. BEBA as a mechanism-oriented theory of nonprofit brand effectiveness under credence-good uncertainty.

3. BEBA as a cross-layer behavioural architecture connecting market uncertainty, organisational brand equity, individual beliefs, intention, and donation behaviour.

## BEBA Layer

Cross-layer concept.

Primary layers:

- Market Layer: uncertainty, credence-good conditions, fundraising crowding, information overload.
- Organisational Layer: nonprofit brand equity architecture and relational brand assets.
- Individual Layer: trust, attitude, subjective norm, moral norm, perceived behavioural control, intention, enactment conditions, behaviour.

## Core Claims

1. Nonprofit giving often occurs under credence-good uncertainty because donors cannot directly verify impact, quality, efficiency, or organisational integrity at the point of decision.

2. Under such uncertainty, nonprofit brand equity becomes behaviourally relevant because it can reduce perceived risk and provide orientation.

3. Trust is a central mechanism through which brand equity becomes behaviourally effective.

4. BEBA should distinguish intention formation from behavioural enactment.

5. TPB constructs explain part of the intention-formation layer but do not fully explain donation behaviour.

6. IBM-style enactment conditions help explain why donation intentions do or do not become donation behaviour.

7. Brand equity may affect behaviour through both mediated intention pathways and direct or indirect enactment pathways.

8. Relational brand equity, especially trust and commitment, may be more proximal to sustained giving than awareness or image alone.

9. Moral norm and donor identity may compete with or bypass brand-attitude pathways.

10. Brand effectiveness is context-sensitive and may be amplified under uncertainty, crowding, and information overload.

## Boundary Conditions

1. Credence-good condition  
BEBA is most relevant when donors cannot directly observe or verify nonprofit impact or organisational quality.

2. Uncertainty and information asymmetry  
The architecture is especially relevant where donors face uncertainty about whether the organisation is competent, honest, efficient, or impactful.

3. Market crowding  
Brand equity may become more behaviourally useful when many nonprofit options compete for donor attention.

4. Information overload  
The lighthouse mechanism is most plausible when donors need simplified orientation.

5. Social influence ambiguity  
Subjective norm and social information should not be assumed to have uniformly positive effects.

6. Moral and identity salience  
Where moral norm or donor identity is highly salient, brand attitude may become less central.

7. Repeated versus one-off giving  
Habit and commitment pathways are likely more relevant for repeated giving than for isolated donation acts, but this requires further evidence.

8. Contextual portability  
Possible national or cultural context dependence is suggested but not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

## Competing Explanations

1. Brand-equity-only explanation  
Donation behaviour is explained primarily by brand awareness, image, and brand attitude.

2. TPB-only explanation  
Donation intention and behaviour are explained through attitude, subjective norm, perceived behavioural control, and moral norm, without requiring brand equity as an architectural input.

3. IBM-only explanation  
Behaviour is explained through intention plus enactment conditions such as knowledge, salience, habit, and environmental constraints.

4. Moral-identity explanation  
Moral norm and donor identity explain donation behaviour more strongly than brand attitudes or brand perceptions.

5. Relational-brand explanation  
Trust and commitment are more behaviourally important than cognitive-evaluative brand constructs such as awareness and image.

6. Contextual explanation  
Observed brand effects may depend primarily on market crowding, uncertainty, fundraising context, or national setting.

## Confidence

Overall confidence: Medium-high as a theoretical integration candidate.

Higher-confidence components:

- Donation as uncertainty-laden credence-good exchange.
- Trust as an important nonprofit giving mechanism.
- Need to distinguish intention from behaviour.
- Need to avoid intention-only brand equity models.

Medium-confidence components:

- Dual-pathway BEBA architecture.
- Relational brand equity priority.
- Integration of TPB and IBM-style enactment conditions.

Lower-confidence components:

- Specific causal ordering among trust, commitment, identity, habit, and salience.
- Brand equity effects on environmental constraints.
- Superiority of processual or network brand equity models.
- Cross-national non-portability claims.
- Empirical sufficiency of the integrated model.
