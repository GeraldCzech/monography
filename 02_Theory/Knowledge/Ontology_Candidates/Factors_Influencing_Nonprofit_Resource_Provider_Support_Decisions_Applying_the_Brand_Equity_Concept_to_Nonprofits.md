# Concept

Resource Provider-Based Brand Equity

## Canonical Definition

Resource provider-based brand equity is the differential effect of nonprofit brand knowledge on resource providers’ support intentions or behaviours, beyond their general altruistic disposition.

## Alternative Definitions

- A nonprofit adaptation of customer-based brand equity focused on donors, volunteers, advocates, funders, partners, or other actors who provide resources.
- The behaviourally relevant value of a nonprofit brand as perceived by stakeholders who may contribute money, time, legitimacy, advocacy, or other support.
- A behaviour-enabling configuration of nonprofit brand perceptions that increases the likelihood of resource-provider action under uncertainty.

## Core Claims

1. Nonprofit brand-related perceptions can explain variation in resource-provider support intentions beyond general altruistic disposition.

2. Resource provider-based brand equity should be defined behaviourally, by its effect on support intentions or behaviour, not merely by awareness, favourability, or symbolic value.

3. Brand knowledge is multidimensional; different dimensions may have different behavioural consequences.

4. Perceived brand respect may function as a behaviour-enabling cue by signalling credibility, worthiness, or social esteem.

5. Perceived brand differentiation may help resource providers distinguish one nonprofit from alternatives under uncertainty.

6. Perceived brand scale or organizational substance may operate as a heuristic for capacity, reliability, or impact.

7. Brand awareness may support consideration-set inclusion but is not equivalent to behavioural activation.

8. Brand familiarity is not necessarily positive; its behavioural effect may depend on the valence and substance of pre-existing perceptions.

9. Brand personality may become behaviourally relevant when stakeholders interpret the nonprofit brand as a relationship partner.

10. Brand image and brand personality require construct separation to protect construct validity.

## Boundary Conditions

- Evidence is based on a single nonprofit organization and should be treated as exploratory.
- The reviewed evidence concerns resource-provider support intentions, not necessarily observed support behaviour.
- Findings should not be generalized automatically to beneficiaries, employees, policymakers, partners, or other stakeholder groups.
- Familiarity effects may depend on whether prior associations are positive, neutral, negative, controversial, or fatigue-inducing.
- Awareness may increase cognitive availability without producing support.
- Perceived organizational scale may operate differently across contexts; it may signal capacity in some settings but could signal reduced need in others. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- Brand image and brand personality remain conceptually ambiguous in parts of the reviewed literature and should not be collapsed without measurement justification.

## Competing Explanations

1. Pure altruism model  
   Resource providers may support nonprofits mainly because of general altruistic disposition, independently of brand perceptions.

2. Awareness-only model  
   Support may be assumed to follow from recall, recognition, or salience. The reviewed claims challenge this as insufficient.

3. Familiarity-as-trust model  
   Familiarity may be assumed to increase trust and support. The reviewed claims challenge this because familiarity can be negatively associated with support intentions.

4. General favourability model  
   Positive perceptions may be assumed to increase support uniformly. The reviewed evidence qualifies this because brand character was not significantly associated with support intentions.

5. Category-need model  
   Resource providers may respond to perceived cause need or category urgency rather than organization-specific brand equity. Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Resource Provider-Based Brand Equity**  
The differential effect of nonprofit brand knowledge on the support intentions or behaviours of actors who may provide money, time, advocacy, legitimacy, partnership, or other resources. In BEBA, this concept is treated as behaviourally defined and distinct from mere awareness, familiarity, or general favourability.

## Confidence

Medium.

The concept is strongly aligned with BEBA’s behavioural definition of nonprofit brand equity and is supported by the reviewed claim set. However, confidence is limited because the evidence is exploratory, single-organization-based, and focused on intentions rather than observed behaviour.
