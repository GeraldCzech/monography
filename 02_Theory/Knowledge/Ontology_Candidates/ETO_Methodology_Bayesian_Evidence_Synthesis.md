# Concept

Warm-Glow Giving

## Canonical Definition

The private, non-pecuniary utility, often experienced as a positive emotional state or satisfaction, that an individual derives from the act of giving itself, independent of the outcome for the recipient.

## Alternative Definitions

-   **Impure Altruism Component:** The self-interested or egoistic component of a donor's utility function in an impure altruism model, complementing the purely altruistic component derived from the recipient's welfare.
-   **Intrinsic Satisfaction of Giving:** The internal reward or satisfaction experienced by an individual for adhering to personal or social norms of prosocial behaviour.

## BEBA Layer

**Individual:** Warm-glow giving is a psychological motivation that operates at the level of the individual stakeholder, influencing their decision to engage in prosocial behaviour like donating.

## Core Claims

-   `WG-000001`: A significant portion of charitable giving is motivated by the private, non-pecuniary utility an individual derives from the act of giving itself.
-   `IA-000001`: Charitable giving is better explained by an impure altruism model that combines concern for recipients with the private utility of giving (warm glow) than by a pure altruism model alone.
-   `WG-000002`: Individuals donate even when their contribution has no marginal effect on the charity’s total funds, which is inconsistent with pure altruism.
-   `SI-000001`: Self-image motives (feeling good about oneself) primarily increase the likelihood that an individual participates in charitable giving.
-   `SI-000002`: Social-image motives (appearing generous to others) primarily increase the amount donated among individuals who have already decided to give.

## Evidence Base

The concept is supported by a large body of experimental evidence, particularly from laboratory and field experiments in economics using methodologies like dictator games and crowd-out designs (`EVID-000033`). Key evidence includes the observation that individuals give even when their donation is fully crowded out by a third party, demonstrating a utility from the act of giving itself (`EVID-000032`). Further evidence comes from studies showing that donating earned income generates a stronger warm glow than donating windfall income (`EVID-000040`) and that fundraising appeals emphasizing donor benefits can be highly effective (`EVID-000041`).

## Boundary Conditions

-   **Religious/Moral Context:** The warm glow model may be less explanatory in contexts where giving is primarily driven by a strong sense of religious duty, moral obligation, or pursuit of salvation (`RG-000001`).
-   **Cause-Related Marketing (CRM):** The effect is negatively moderated by consumer skepticism. Perceptions that donation amounts are cynically large or overly publicized can trigger skepticism and reduce warm glow (`CR-000001`, `WG-000004`).
-   **Source of Funds:** The effect is stronger for personally earned funds compared to windfall gains (`WG-000003`).
-   **Cultural Context:** Cross-cultural variation in warm glow motivations is an acknowledged underexplored area, suggesting the universality of the effect is a boundary condition (`EVID-000049`).

## Competing Explanations

-   **Pure Altruism:** The theory that individuals are motivated solely by a selfless concern for the welfare of the recipient. Warm glow posits that this is an incomplete explanation.
-   **Social Pressure / Signalling:** While social-image motives are considered a *mechanism* of warm glow, a purely social-pressure model (where giving is only to avoid sanction or gain status) can be a competing explanation if it excludes any internal utility.
-   **Duty-Based Morality:** A deontological view where giving is motivated by a perceived moral or religious duty, regardless of personal emotional benefit or recipient outcome.

## Operationalisations

The primary operationalisation is through experimental designs that aim to isolate the utility of giving from the utility of the outcome. The most common method is the "crowd-out" experiment, where a subject's donation may be partially or fully replaced by an experimenter's contribution. Continued giving in the face of 100% crowd-out is considered evidence of warm glow.

## Existing Scales

Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature. The concept is typically measured behaviourally in experiments rather than through psychometric scales.

## Recommended ConstructLedger Updates

-   **Construct Name:** Warm-Glow Motivation
-   **Canonical Definition:** The motivation to engage in a prosocial act based on the private, non-pecuniary utility an individual expects to derive from the act itself.
-   **Proposed Role in BEBA:** A core individual-level driver of donation behaviour that can be triggered or amplified by nonprofit brand communications. It represents a self-interested pathway to prosocial behaviour that co-exists with trust- or efficacy-based pathways. It is a critical concept for explaining behaviour under uncertainty about impact, as the donor's utility is not contingent on that impact.
-   **Measurement Note:** Recommend noting that measurement is primarily experimental (e.g., crowd-out designs) and that validated psychometric scales are not common in the core literature.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Warm-Glow Giving:** A term from behavioural economics describing the motivation to donate based on the positive feeling or private utility an individual gets from the act of giving, separate from any concern about the actual impact of the donation. It is a key component of "impure altruism" models.

## Confidence

**High:** The core phenomenon is well-established and supported by decades of experimental research in economics.
**Medium:** Confidence is medium regarding the precise psychological mechanisms, the relative importance of self- vs. social-image, and its generalizability across all cultural and behavioural contexts.

## Review Recommendation

Accept as a core candidate concept for the BEBA ontology. It provides a necessary and powerful competing explanation for nonprofit stakeholder behaviour, especially donation. Integrating warm glow is crucial for BEBA to avoid an overly rationalistic model of behaviour that relies solely on brand trust and perceived impact. The brand's role is not just to signal efficacy (for pure altruists) but also to frame the act of giving in a way that generates warm glow for impure altruists.
