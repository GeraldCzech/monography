# Concept

Behavioural Brand Architecture

## Canonical Definition

A structured configuration of brand-based memories, meanings, trust cues, normative signals, and perceived action affordances through which nonprofit brand equity enables or constrains stakeholder behaviour under uncertainty.

## Alternative Definitions

- A brand-based meaning system that shapes behavioural salience, confidence, moral interpretation, and perceived capacity to act.
- An organised nonprofit brand equity structure linking awareness, associations, trust, reputation, moral norms, social norms, and perceived behavioural control to stakeholder action.

## Core Claims

1. Nonprofit brand equity should be understood as an organised behavioural meaning architecture rather than merely as favourable brand attitude or donor preference.

2. Brand equity becomes behaviourally effective through multiple routes, including cognitive accessibility, attitude formation, uncertainty reduction, perceived control, moral-norm activation, social-norm salience, and feedback from behavioural experience.

3. In nonprofit contexts, the attitudinal route is not necessarily dominant; moral norms, uncertainty reduction, and perceived control may be stronger or more contextually relevant mechanisms.

4. The behavioural effectiveness of nonprofit brand equity depends on stakeholder group, behaviour type, involvement, social information format, and reputation valence.

5. Negative organisational or leadership reputation can constrain or narrow the behavioural pathways through which brand equity affects intention and behaviour.

## Boundary Conditions

- Stakeholder group: donor, volunteer, employee, beneficiary, advocate, partner, institutional funder.
- Behavioural context: donation, volunteering, advocacy, employment, beneficiary trust, institutional partnership.
- Reputation valence: positive reputation may enable behaviour; negative reputation may constrain pathways.
- Involvement: low- and high-involvement stakeholders may respond differently to brand-enabled norms.
- Norm type: subjective norms, descriptive norms, social proof, peer behaviour, institutional endorsement, and public recognition may have different effects.
- Uncertainty level: effects should be strongest where stakeholders face uncertainty about legitimacy, effectiveness, moral appropriateness, impact credibility, personal ability to help, transaction security, or reputational risk.

## Competing Explanations

1. Brand-attitude dominant explanation  
   Brand awareness / associations / quality influence attitude, which influences intention and behaviour.

2. Moral-norm dominant explanation  
   Moral norms directly explain charitable intention and behaviour, with brand equity playing a limited or indirect role.

3. Brand-enabled moral norm explanation  
   Nonprofit brand meaning activates, clarifies, or reinforces moral obligations.

4. Uncertainty-control explanation  
   Trust, reputation, and perceived quality reduce uncertainty and increase perceived behavioural control or self-efficacy.

5. Social-norm explanation  
   Brand image and reputation shape perceived social expectations, but effects are unstable and moderated.

6. Reputation-conditioned explanation  
   Reputation valence moderates which behavioural pathways remain effective.

7. Dynamic feedback explanation  
   Behavioural experience updates brand equity over time.
