# Concept

Warm Glow Giving

## Canonical Definition

Warm glow giving refers to donor-side private emotional or self-related utility derived from the act of giving itself, partly independently of the donation’s consequential impact on recipients.

## Alternative Definitions

- Private emotional utility from charitable giving.
- Donor-side affective reward generated by the act of giving.
- Impure-altruistic motivation in which donors value both recipient benefit and the act of giving itself.
- Self-image or social-image reward associated with behaving charitably.

## Core Claims

1. Warm glow represents a non-brand motivational pathway through which charitable behaviour can become rewarding independently of perceived nonprofit brand equity.

2. Warm glow should be treated as a competing or complementary mechanism to brand equity, not as a subdimension of brand equity.

3. Charitable giving may persist even when marginal impact is low, absent, or uncertain because donors derive private emotional, self-image, or social-image utility from giving.

4. Nonprofit brand equity may influence giving partly by shaping whether warm-glow opportunities are trusted, credible, identity-congruent, or socially meaningful, but this relationship is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

5. Warm-glow-driven giving may make nonprofit brands behaviourally effective while diverging from welfare-efficient or impact-maximising allocation.

6. Warm glow should not be used as a residual explanation for unexplained giving behaviour; it requires independent conceptual and measurement specification.

## Evidence Base

Candidate-level evidence only.

The reviewed claim set supports warm glow as a mechanism associated with:

- impure altruism and incomplete crowd-out;
- private emotional utility from giving;
- self-image, social image, prestige, and observability;
- donor-benefit framing and fundraising communication;
- cause-related marketing and skepticism;
- inefficient allocation and effective-altruism tensions;
- measurement risks and SEM architecture gaps.

Direct evidence that nonprofit brand equity causes warm glow or that warm glow mediates the brand-equity-to-donation-intention pathway is **not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature**.

## Boundary Conditions

- Warm glow is especially salient when giving persists despite low or uncertain marginal impact.
- Giving from earned versus windfall income may condition warm-glow utility.
- Observability may increase the relevance of social image and prestige.
- Donor-benefit framing may activate or suppress warm-glow-related motivation.
- Skepticism may suppress warm glow when donation claims appear self-serving, exaggerated, or inauthentic.
- Religious or obligation-based giving may depend more on duty, community, religious identity, or obligation than on private emotional utility.
- Warm glow in repeated giving is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- Warm glow in the Austrian charitable context is not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.
- Warm glow must not be inferred from behaviour alone.

## Competing Explanations

- **Pure altruism:** Giving is motivated by concern for total recipient welfare rather than donor-side utility.
- **Impact model:** Giving is driven by perceived beneficiary impact or marginal effectiveness.
- **Brand equity model:** Giving is explained by brand trust, awareness, associations, authenticity, identification, or brand attitude.
- **Moral obligation model:** Giving is driven by duty, obligation, religious norm, or community expectation.
- **Social-image model:** Giving is driven by public recognition, prestige, or reputational benefit rather than private affect.
- **Skepticism-suppression model:** Giving is inhibited when donor-benefit appeals or cause-related claims appear inauthentic.
- **Effective-altruism model:** Giving is guided by welfare-maximising allocation rather than emotionally satisfying giving.
