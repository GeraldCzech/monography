# Concept

Brand Hypocrisy

## Canonical Definition

A stakeholder's perception of a significant discrepancy between an organization's espoused values, identity, or claims (its "saying") and its subsequent observable behavior (its "doing").

## Alternative Definitions

- **Corporate Hypocrisy**: The belief that a firm claims to be something it is not (Wagner, Krumm, & Wagner, 2009).
- **Brand Betrayal**: A brand's violation of the norms and expectations governing its relationship with a consumer.
- **Mission Drift**: An organization's gradual and often unintentional shift away from its core social mission towards more commercial objectives.

## BEBA Layer

Individual Layer. Hypocrisy is a subjective perception held by an individual stakeholder, which influences their personal behavioural decisions.

## Parent Concepts

- Stakeholder Perception
- Brand Risk
- Cognitive Dissonance (Stakeholder)

## Child Concepts

- **Mission Hypocrisy**: A perceived say-do gap specifically related to the organization's core social mission.
- **Values Hypocrisy**: A perceived say-do gap related to the organization's stated ethical or operational values.
- **Stakeholder Hypocrisy**: A perceived say-do gap in how the organization claims to treat a specific stakeholder group versus how it actually does.

## Related Concepts

- **Trust**: Hypocrisy is a primary driver of distrust.
- **Authenticity**: Hypocrisy is the antithesis of perceived authenticity.
- **Brand Legitimacy**: Perceived hypocrisy can delegitimize a nonprofit in the eyes of its stakeholders.
- **Reputation Damage**: Hypocrisy is a significant cause of reputational harm.

## Core Claims

- **C-HYP-001**: Perceived brand hypocrisy negatively moderates the relationship between positive brand equity dimensions and supportive stakeholder behaviours (e.g., donating, volunteering).
- **C-HYP-002**: Brand hypocrisy erodes stakeholder trust and perceptions of brand authenticity more severely than simple service or performance failures.
- **C-HYP-003**: The perception of hypocrisy functions as a critical failure condition within the BEBA, deactivating the positive behavioural effects of the brand architecture.

## Evidence Base

The conceptualisation is primarily derived from `claim NB-000104` from `review REVIEW-000100`, which states: "The brand equity-behaviour relationship is likely negatively moderated by brand hypocrisy." A full literature review is required to source primary evidence, particularly from the corporate social responsibility and marketing ethics literature.

## Boundary Conditions

- **Perceptual Threshold**: The effect is only triggered when the say-do gap is large enough to be perceived and considered significant by the stakeholder.
- **Domain Centrality**: Hypocrisy related to the core mission or defining values of the nonprofit is likely to have a much stronger negative effect than hypocrisy in peripheral administrative domains.
- **Stakeholder Salience**: The effect is strongest among stakeholders for whom the brand and its mission are highly salient.

## Competing Explanations

- **Service Failure**: A negative outcome may be attributed to a simple failure to deliver a promised service, without the stakeholder inferring a deeper, hypocritical motive.
- **Negative Word-of-Mouth (NWOM)**: Stakeholders may be reacting to negative information from others, which may or may not be framed as hypocrisy.
- **Expectation Disconfirmation**: The negative reaction may be a standard response to unmet expectations rather than a specific moral judgment about a say-do gap.

## Operationalisations

The construct would be operationalised as a multi-item reflective scale measured at the individual stakeholder level. Items would assess the perceived gap between the nonprofit's public statements (e.g., in campaigns, on its website) and its perceived actions.

Example items (Likert scale):
- "This organization does not practice what it preaches."
- "This organization's actions are inconsistent with its stated values."
- "There is a large gap between what this organization says and what it actually does."

## Existing Scales

Scales for corporate hypocrisy exist in the commercial marketing literature (e.g., Wagner, Krumm, & Wagner, 2009). These would need to be reviewed and adapted for the specific context of nonprofit organizations and their diverse stakeholder groups.
