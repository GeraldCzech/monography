# Concept

Donor Self-Concept

## Canonical Definition

Donor self-concept refers to an individual’s perception of themselves in relation to giving, helping, moral responsibility, charitable action, or prosocial identity.

## Alternative Definitions

- The donor’s self-perceived identity as someone who gives, helps, supports, or acts prosocially.
- The self-related meaning through which individuals interpret charitable or nonprofit-related behaviour.
- A donor’s identity-relevant understanding of themselves in relation to nonprofit support.

## Core Claims

1. Donor self-concept may directly influence giving intention.  
   Supported by `SC-000002`.

2. Nonprofit brand personality may influence giving intention partly through donor self-concept.  
   Supported by `NB-000042` and `NB-000045`.

3. Nonprofit brand awareness may influence giving intention partly through donor self-concept.  
   Supported by `NB-000043` and `NB-000046`.

4. Nonprofit brand image may influence giving intention directly but does not appear, in the reviewed evidence, to operate through donor self-concept.  
   Supported by `NB-000040`, `NB-000044`, and `NB-000047`.

5. Different nonprofit brand equity dimensions may connect to donor self-concept in different ways.  
   Supported by the contrast between brand personality, brand awareness, and brand image effects.

## Evidence Base

Evidence comes from `REVIEW-000032`, based on a cross-sectional survey study from China concerning nonprofit brand personality, nonprofit brand image, nonprofit brand awareness, donor self-concept, and giving intention.

Relevant claim IDs:

- `NB-000039`: Nonprofit brand personality → giving intention
- `NB-000040`: Nonprofit brand image → giving intention
- `NB-000041`: Nonprofit brand awareness → giving intention
- `SC-000002`: Self-concept → giving intention
- `NB-000042`: Nonprofit brand personality → self-concept
- `NB-000043`: Nonprofit brand awareness → self-concept
- `NB-000044`: Nonprofit brand image does not significantly affect self-concept
- `NB-000045`: Self-concept partially mediates brand personality → giving intention
- `NB-000046`: Self-concept partially mediates brand awareness → giving intention
- `NB-000047`: Self-concept does not mediate brand image → giving intention

Evidence status: candidate, medium confidence, context-bound.

## Boundary Conditions

- The outcome is giving intention, not observed giving behaviour.
- Evidence is cross-sectional; causal claims should remain cautious.
- Evidence is based on China; generalisability to other nonprofit, cultural, religious, welfare-state, or philanthropic contexts is not established in the supplied material.
- The role of donor self-concept may depend on how self-concept, brand personality, brand awareness, and brand image are operationalised.
- The evidence does not support treating nonprofit brand equity as a homogeneous construct.
- The evidence does not justify generalising from intention to actual donation behaviour without additional verified empirical outputs.

## Competing Explanations

1. Direct brand effect model  
   Brand personality, brand image, and brand awareness directly influence giving intention without requiring donor self-concept mediation.

2. Identity mediation model  
   Brand personality and brand awareness influence giving intention partly through donor self-concept.

3. Differentiated pathway model  
   Different brand dimensions operate through different mechanisms:
   - Brand personality → donor self-concept → giving intention
   - Brand awareness → donor self-concept → giving intention
   - Brand image → giving intention, but not through donor self-concept

4. Non-brand identity model  
   Donor self-concept may influence giving intention independently of nonprofit brand dimensions.

5. Measurement artefact explanation  
   The non-significant brand image → self-concept pathway may reflect operationalisation choices rather than a true absence of identity relevance.

## Operationalisations

Candidate operationalisations should distinguish donor self-concept from general attitudes toward the nonprofit.

Possible indicators may concern whether respondents see themselves as:

- charitable,
- helping-oriented,
- socially responsible,
- morally committed to giving,
- aligned with the nonprofit’s mission,
- the kind of person who supports nonprofit causes.

Operationalisation should avoid collapsing donor self-concept into:

- brand attitude,
- brand image,
- donor satisfaction,
- general willingness to donate,
- observed giving behaviour.

## Existing Scales

Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

The reviewed claim set indicates that self-concept was operationalised empirically, but the supplied material does not provide enough verified scale information to identify or validate a canonical BEBA scale.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Donor Self-Concept**  
Donor self-concept refers to an individual’s perception of themselves in relation to giving, helping, moral responsibility, charitable action, or prosocial identity. In BEBA, it is a candidate individual-level mechanism through which some nonprofit brand dimensions may become behaviourally effective by aligning with or activating the donor’s self-understanding.

## Confidence

Medium.

Rationale: The reviewed evidence supports the relevance of donor self-concept for giving intention and suggests differentiated mediation pathways. However, confidence is limited by the cross-sectional design, China-based context, intention-only outcome, and lack of verified scale details in the supplied material.
