# Concept

Volunteer Brand Equity

## Canonical Definition

Volunteer Brand Equity is a candidate internal stakeholder construct describing the value a nonprofit brand has in shaping formal volunteers’ brand-related understanding, commitment, and behaviour, after adapting employee brand equity concepts to the specific conditions of volunteer engagement.

## Alternative Definitions

- An adapted form of Employee Brand Equity for nonprofit volunteers.
- The degree to which volunteers understand, commit to, and enact the nonprofit brand.
- A volunteer-specific internal brand equity construct linking internal brand management to role clarity, brand commitment, brand citizenship behaviour, retention intention, satisfaction, and word-of-mouth.

## Core Claims

1. Volunteer Brand Equity can draw on Employee Brand Equity models but requires linguistic and conceptual adaptation for nonprofit volunteer contexts.

2. Internal brand management is associated with volunteer role clarity.

3. Internal brand management is associated with volunteer brand commitment.

4. Relational internal brand management may be more strongly associated with volunteer brand commitment than informational or involvement-based dimensions.

5. Volunteer role clarity is associated with brand citizenship behaviour, satisfaction, and intention to stay.

6. Volunteer brand commitment is associated with brand citizenship behaviour, satisfaction, intention to stay, and positive word-of-mouth.

7. Cognitive role clarity and affective brand commitment may have different behavioural relevance depending on the outcome.

8. Brand commitment appears especially relevant for positive word-of-mouth compared with role clarity.

## Evidence Base

Evidence status: candidate.

The reviewed claim set is based on exploratory, cross-sectional evidence concerning Portuguese formal volunteers. Confidence is medium.

Evidence supports the relevance of adapting Employee Brand Equity concepts to volunteers, but it does not justify strong causal claims. Recommended phrasing should use “is associated with,” “predicts in cross-sectional models,” or “is consistent with.”

Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature: longitudinal evidence, experimental evidence, cross-cultural replication, and evidence for informal or episodic volunteers.

## Boundary Conditions

- Applies primarily to formal volunteers in nonprofit organisations.
- Should not automatically be generalised to donors, beneficiaries, employees, board members, activists, informal volunteers, or episodic volunteers.
- Evidence appears contextually bounded to Portuguese formal volunteers.
- Causal interpretation is limited because the evidence is exploratory and cross-sectional.
- Construct validity depends on whether Employee Brand Equity measures are appropriately adapted for volunteer contexts.
- Role clarity must be distinguished from general task clarity unless explicitly tied to brand enactment.

## Competing Explanations

1. **Cognitive Clarity Explanation**  
   Volunteer outcomes may be explained by role clarity rather than by broader brand equity.

2. **Affective Commitment Explanation**  
   Volunteer outcomes, especially positive word-of-mouth and discretionary behaviour, may be driven mainly by brand commitment.

3. **General Volunteer Management Explanation**  
   Effects attributed to brand equity may instead reflect general volunteer management, role design, organisational support, socialisation, or organisational climate.

4. **Relational Management Explanation**  
   Volunteer brand commitment may depend less on brand communication and more on relational support, management behaviour, and socialisation.

5. **Employee Brand Equity Transfer Explanation**  
   Volunteer Brand Equity may be an adaptation of Employee Brand Equity, but not a direct equivalent.

Important operational distinction:

- **Brand-specific role clarity:** clarity about how to enact the nonprofit brand.
- **General role clarity:** clarity about tasks and responsibilities independent of brand.

BEBA should prefer the former when testing brand-enabled behaviour.

## Existing Scales

Existing measures appear to be adapted from Employee Brand Equity and internal brand management instruments.

Corporate employee brand equity measures should not be transferred directly to volunteers without adaptation. Terms related to employment, job training, formal goals, and intention-to-stay horizons may require modification for nonprofit volunteer contexts.

Specific validated scale details are not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.

## Recommended Glossary Entry

**Volunteer Brand Equity**  
A candidate internal stakeholder construct describing the value of a nonprofit brand in shaping formal volunteers’ brand-related understanding, commitment, and behaviour. It draws on Employee Brand Equity but requires adaptation because volunteers are not employees and operate under different motivational, contractual, and organisational conditions.

## Confidence

Medium.

The concept is theoretically relevant for BEBA and supported by the reviewed claim set, but the evidence is exploratory, cross-sectional, and context-specific. It should remain a candidate rather than a canonical ontology entry.
