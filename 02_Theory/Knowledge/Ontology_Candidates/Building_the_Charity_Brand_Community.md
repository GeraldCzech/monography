# Concept

Building the Charity Brand Community

## Canonical Definition

A theoretical model, derived from Hassay & Peloza (2009), that describes the process of cultivating durable supporter commitment within the nonprofit sector. It posits that commitment is developed through two distinct, cyclical, and potentially complementary pathways:
1.  **Cognitive-Learning Pathway:** Initiated by a supporter's psychological `Identification` with the nonprofit, which then drives `Behavioral Involvement` and fosters a `Perceived Sense of Community`, ultimately leading to `Commitment`.
2.  **Experiential-Learning Pathway:** Initiated by a supporter's `Behavioral Involvement` in the nonprofit's activities, which fosters a `Perceived Sense of Community` and subsequent `Identification`, ultimately leading to `Commitment`.

This model frames supporter engagement not as a linear outcome of branding, but as a dynamic, self-reinforcing system where brand perceptions and behaviours mutually influence each other.

## Alternative Definitions

This model stands in contrast to simpler, unidirectional models of brand equity formation where supporter behaviour (e.g., donating, volunteering) is treated exclusively as a terminal outcome of a linear progression of cognitive states (e.g., Awareness -> Image -> Loyalty). The "Building the Charity Brand Community" model explicitly incorporates behaviour as an *input* to and shaper of brand perceptions and identification.

## BEBA Layer

-   **Primary: `Individual` Layer.** The model's core constructs (`Identification`, `Behavioral Involvement`, `Perceived Sense of Community`, `Commitment`) are psychological and behavioural phenomena experienced by the individual supporter.
-   **Secondary: `Social` Layer.** The `Perceived Sense of Community` construct acts as a critical bridge, linking the individual's psychological state to the collective identity and social fabric of the supporter base.

## Core Claims

The model is built upon a set of foundational claims (BC-000025 to BC-000032). These can be summarised into two meta-claims for the BEBA architecture:

1.  **Duality of Commitment Pathways:** Supporter commitment can be generated through two distinct causal sequences: one initiated by cognition (`Identification`) and one initiated by behaviour (`Involvement`).
2.  **Centrality of Community:** `Perceived Sense of Community` is a key mediating variable that translates both cognitive identification and behavioural action into durable attitudinal `Commitment`.

## Evidence Base

The model is currently conceptual, based on the theoretical framework proposed by Hassay & Peloza (2009). The claims represent a logical system derived from brand community literature. Empirical validation of the dual-pathway structure, particularly in a competitive model setting, is a key research gap and a primary objective for the BEBA empirical pipeline.

## Boundary Conditions

The relative dominance of one pathway over the other may be moderated by several factors:
-   **NPO Maturity/Awareness:** The cognitive pathway may be more accessible for well-known NPOs, while the experiential pathway may be essential for newer or less-known NPOs.
-   **Type of Involvement:** High-effort, high-cost, or socially visible behaviours may be more effective at triggering the experiential pathway than low-effort, private actions.
-   **Supporter Motivation:** The supporter's initial motivation (e.g., value-expressive, social, utilitarian) may determine the entry point into the cyclical system.

## Competing Explanations

-   **Internal Competition:** The cognitive-led pathway (`Identification` first) and the experiential-led pathway (`Involvement` first) are competing explanations for the primary mechanism of commitment formation.
-   **External Competition:** The entire cyclical model competes with simpler linear, unidirectional models that do not account for the feedback loop from behaviour to brand cognitions.

## Operationalisations

The model is highly suitable for operationalisation using Structural Equation Modeling (SEM). This allows for a direct empirical test of the competing pathways:
-   **Model A (Cognitive-Led):** A model specifying the path `Identification -> Involvement -> Sense of Community -> Commitment`.
-   **Model B (Experiential-Led):** A model specifying the path `Involvement -> Sense of Community -> Identification -> Commitment`.
-   **Model C (Non-Recursive):** A more complex model allowing for reciprocal relationships and simultaneous operation of both pathways.

## Existing Scales

`Not sufficiently covered in the reviewed literature.` A systematic search is required to identify and vet scales for the four core constructs:
1.  `Supporter Identification` (e.g., drawing from Mael & Ashforth's Organizational Identification Scale).
2.  `Behavioral Involvement` (likely a formative scale capturing donating, volunteering, advocating, etc.).
3.  `Perceived Sense of Community` (e.g., drawing from McMillan & Chavis's Sense of Community Index).
4.  `Supporter Commitment` (e.g., drawing from Meyer & Allen's three-component model of organizational commitment).
