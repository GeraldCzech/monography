The concept of brand equity (BE) has been the subject of much recent discussion in the field of
nonprofit branding and also has been the concept of brand co-creation in the field of branding. This
study combines these aspects and examines the BE co-creation of a nonprofit entity, the Finnish
Defence Forces military driving school. Empirical data were collected from industrial logistics
service providers and military recruits. A theoretical BE model was further developed using structural
equation modeling in an exploratory manner to reveal whether the BE model of the military driving
school is similar from both viewpoints. Similar workable models were found, indicating strong BE
co-creation and thereby contributing to the field. To support the future co-creation of BE of the military
driving school, it is important to ensure that the recruits take positive experiences from the school and
industrial logistics service providers carefully consider how they relay their experiences about the
school to other truck drivers. The attitudes of recruits towards the military driving school are likely
eventually to conform to those of civilian truck drivers. Finding workable models required omitting
some BE dimensions and measures of earlier models. Moreover, using data from one country alone
may be a limitation of the study. Therefore, nonprofit BE measures should be further developed and
tested with data from further afield.

and a literature is review presented. Thereafter,
methodological issues are described, followed by
results. Finally, the paper offers conclusions and
suggests further topics for research.

The Finnish Defence Forces military
driving school
The transport industry is an important player in services markets. Globally, 85% of domestic and 81%
of international transport have been outsourced
(Capgemini, 2008). According to Solakivi et al.
(2009), in Finland, 90% of transport services have
been outsourced. Further, Finland has a large
number of mainly small transport companies, and
hence, competition in the industry is intense,
resulting in low profitability and a high rate of
bankruptcies (Juntunen et al., 2011b). In addition,
a large number of truck drivers are approaching
pension age and therefore inducing a high turnover
among drivers within companies. Hence, companies

need a constant supply of trained new employees
(Juntunen et al., 2011b).
Basic transport driver training approved by the
Finnish Vehicle Administration (Autorekisterikeskus,
AKE) is offered at professional schools. However, as
transport companies co-operate closely with the
FDF to support both peacetime and possible wartime
activities, the FDF military transport division, specifically the military driving school, has for decades

Those who successfully complete their training

civilian employers. Almost all those who graduate
from the military driving school continue to work
as drivers after their military service. Hence, the
military driving school plays an important part in
the commercial environment in Finland (Juntunen
et al., 2011a, 2011b).

Literature review
Brand co-creation

Brand co-creation (see e.g., Brodie et al., 2006;
Boyle, 2007; Gregory, 2007; Fyrberg and Jüriado,
2009; Payne et al., 2009; Juntunen, 2012; Juntunen
et al., 2012) often arises out of value co-creation
(Prahalad and Ramaswamy, 2004) or co-production
(e.g., Bendapudi and Leone, 2003; Etgar, 2008),
and service-dominant logic, where value is seen as
starting with the supplier understanding customers’
value-creating processes and learning how to support their co-creation activities (Vargo and Lusch,
2004; Vargo and Lusch, 2008). Unlike product and
corporate branding, the brands in brand co-creation
are not built solely by organizations, but instead the
customers (Boyle, 2007; Pitt et al., 2006) and all
other internal and external stakeholders (Brodie
et al., 2006; Gregory, 2007; Fyrberg and Jüriado,
2009; Payne et al., 2009) who actively engage
in the brand co-creation process. According to

Brodie et al. (2006), the service brand plays an

commonly known practical example of this are the
educational services—which is also our empirical

The quality of interaction between network
players is fundamental to co-creation (Fyrberg and
Jüriado, 2009). The value-creating process can be
looked at from the viewpoint of the supplier, the
customer or the encounter, in which both the
supplier and the customer simultaneously interact
and co-create experiences (Payne et al., 2008; Payne
et al., 2009). However, encounter experiences may
vary in different phases of the relationship (Payne
et al., 2009).

Brand equity models

Two widely used BE models in the domain of marketing are those of Aaker (1996) and Keller (1993).
Aaker’s BE model consists of brand loyalty, brand
awareness (BA), perceived quality, brand associations and other proprietary brand assets, whereas
Keller’s (1993) model focuses on brand knowledge
and its two components, BA (recall and recognition)
and brand image (BI) (favorability, uniqueness and
strengths of brand associations, attributes, benefits
and attitudes). In the context of logistics, Keller’s
(1993) model is frequently utilized and further
developed (e.g., Davis et al., 2008, Juntunen et al.,
2011c). Davis et al. (2008), examining BE in logistics
service markets, proposed that BE accrues to a firm,
rather than to a product. They found a positive relationship between BA and BE, as well as between BI
and BE. Juntunen et al. (2011c) found that corporate
BA and corporate BI affect corporate BE among
logistics service providers. They also identified a
one-way relationship between corporate BA and
corporate BI.
In the nonprofit sector, Faircloth (2005) highlighted the lack of research devoted to exploring
BE. Since then, however, the number of studies
has increased enormously (see e.g., Stegemann and
Thompson, 2005; Lee and Back, 2008; Hou et al.,
Copyright © 2012 John Wiley & Sons, Ltd.

2009; Laidler-Kylander and Simonsin, 2009; Camarero
et al., 2010; Lee and Back, 2010). According to
Faircloth (2005), nonprofit BE consists of brand personality (brand respect and brand differentiation),
BA (brand familiarity) and BI (brand character and
brand scale). Camarero et al. (2010) examined the
differences between internal and external customers of an exhibition by considering the impact of
loyalty (from previous exhibitions visited), perceived quality (various intrinsic features of the destination), BI (brand personality) and brand values
(religious and cultural values of the exhibition) on
BE (intention to visit future exhibitions and willingness to pay for the visit). They concluded that consumer loyalty and perceived quality are key factors
in BE, regardless of visitor origin. Furthermore, BI
was not a determinant of BE for internal visitors,
although it was for external visitors, and the impact
on BE of the brand values conveyed by the exhibition
was greater on internal visitors than on external
visitors.
The existing research on nonprofit BE does not
explicitly use the term “brand co-creation,” even
though several studies seem to have elements of it.
For example, the comparative study of Camarero
et al. (2010) examines the experiences of two
stakeholder groups in an encounter suggested by
Payne et al. (2008, 2009). The study by Lee and
Back (2008), which investigated conference attendee
behavior from the perspective of attendee-based BE in
tourism marketing, can be considered in terms of
customers engaging in brand co-creation (Boyle,
2007; Pitt et al., 2006). Hankinson (2000) observed
that in the nonprofit sector, different stakeholders
perceive brands in different ways. The importance
of the various stakeholders suggested by several
brand co-creation researchers (e.g., Brodie et al.,
2006; Gregory, 2007; Fyrberg and Jüriado, 2009)
is, in a sense, a reference to brand co-creation.
Thus, it is legitimate to examine nonprofit BE from
the point of view of brand co-creation.

Our aim was to find corresponding models for

internal and external stakeholders. Camarero et al.

As military driving school is also a nonprofit organization and we consider recruits as internal stakeholders and LSPs as external stakeholders, we
assume that the impact of BI is higher among LSPs
than recruits. Therefore, we hypothesize:
H1: The impact of BI will be greater for the LSP’s
than for the recruits.
Additionally, because recruits are internal stakeholders who actively engage in operating in the

impact of BA is higher among recruits than external

military driving school. Thus, we hypothesize:
H2: The impact of BA is higher among recruits
than LSPs.

and 2.3% women. The questionnaire consisted of
various sections intended to shed light on recruits’

Results
We first tested whether the data sets were statistically compatible with the model by Davis et al.
(2008). The fit indices for the LSP model were as
follows: chi-square (C-S) 366.76, degrees of freedom
(DF) 41, p-value 0.000, root mean square error of
approximation (RMSEA) 0.13, normed fit index
(NFI) 0.96, non-normed fit index (NNFI) 0.95, comparative fit index (CFI) 0.97 and goodness of fit
index (GFI) 0.87. The fit indices for the recruit
model were as follows: C-S 146.16, DF 41, p-value
0.000, RMSEA 0.11, NFI 0.96, NNFI 0.96, CFI 0.97
and GFI 0.90. The chi-square tests show an unacceptable fit of the data to the models, the minimum
acceptable p-value normally being 0.05. An RMSEA
value over 0.10 would indicate a poor fit of the
model (Browne and Cudeck, 1993), which was the
case here. NNFI, the most important fit index for
large samples, should be over 0.90 (Hu and Bentler,
1999). NFI, CFI and GFI values above 0.90 indicate
acceptance (Jaccard and Wan, 1996). Because most
of the fit indices suggest that the models should
not be accepted, we rejected both of them.
Because we rejected both models, we did not
conduct multiple group structural equation modeling
(SEM) for the analysis. However, we continued
by using SEM in an exploratory manner (see e.g.,
Kline, 2005) and reduced one operational measure
both from BA (brand) and BI (predict). Modified
LSPs model’s fit indices were as follows: C-S 135.14,
DF 24, p-value 0.000, RMSEA 0.11, NFI 0.98, NNFI
0.97, CFI 0.98 and GFI 0.93. The new fit indices for
the recruit model were as follows: C-S 67.49, DF 24,
p-value 0.000, RMSEA 0.085, NFI 0.97, NNFI 0.97,
CFI 0.98 and GFI 0.94. With a large sample size
(N > 200), the chi-square tests usually show an
unacceptable fit of the data to the model, and
hence, the NNFI is the most important fit index
for large samples (Hu and Bentler, 1999). Because
most of the fit indices suggest that the modified
models should be accepted, we thereby managed
to create structural models with adequate statistical
fits for both groups (Table 2). We also checked the
modification indices of Lisrel software and modified
the recruits’ model accordingly. By freeing the error
correlation between two measures concerning
respect (comresp and resp), the “recruits” model
was statistically significantly improved. The fit indices

Discussion
This study concentrated on developing a model of
co-created BE in the nonprofit context. More specifically, the study examined whether the BE models of
internal (recruits) and external (logistics service providers) stakeholders of a nonprofit entity (military
driving school) are similar. Finding workable measurement models indicates that the model of Davis
et al. (2008) is to some extent suitable for measuring
nonprofit BE. Because two almost similar models
were found, we suggest that nonprofit BE can be
co-created with different stakeholders.
As in previous studies (e.g., Juntunen et al.,
2011c), in both models, BA and BI affected BE.
However, one measure from both BA and BI needed
to be omitted, which is understandable in this kind
of exploratory analysis. Existing models of a similar
kind would not be possible by accident—there must
be strong interaction between groups so identical
models can exist. In fact, the majority of the LSP
respondents have served their military service in
the military driving school and several recruits
worked as truck drivers both before and after their
military service, sharing their experiences about
the military driving school with others. Thus, the
brand is not built solely by military forces; instead,
both the recruits and the LSPs actively engage in

and external stakeholders in brand co-creation
(see e.g., Boyle, 2007; Brodie et al., 2006; Gregory,
2007; Fyrberg and Jüriado, 2009; Payne et al., 2009).
Following Brodie et al. (2006), this is an example of

mediating relationships for co-creating meaning and
experience in the interaction of internal and external
stakeholders. Thus, an interesting question is how
well the military forces understand both internal
and external stakeholders’ value-creating processes
and support their co-creation activities.
To examine differences between the models we
hypothesized:
H1: The impact of BI will be greater for the LSPs
than for the recruits.
H2: The impact of BA is higher among recruits
than LSPs.
As we assumed, the impact of BI was greater for
the LSPs than for the recruits. Thus, H1 is supported. This might be explained by remembering
that the LSP group gained their experiences earlier
and now have only faded recollections of their training.
This is also supported by the fact that LSPs
recount their experiences about the military driving
school to both younger and older drivers, which
increases the importance of the image. This finding
differs slightly from that of Camarero et al. (2010),
namely, that BI was not a determinant of BE for
internal visitors at all, although it was for external
visitors. The BA of the recruits had almost twice as
much influence on BE than that of the LSP respondents.
In other words, also H2 was supported. This may
indicate that the recruits are more aware of the
military driving school than LSPs because they
are currently actively engaged with it. From the
brand co-creation viewpoint, these findings may
relate to the varying encounters experienced in
different phases of the relationship as suggested by
Payne et al. (2009).
Additionally, a strong error correlation between
respect-related measures was found. This can be
explained by noting that, although there is a large
amount of transportation-related training available

driving schools is difficult because of the unique

Conclusions
The study yields three theoretical implications. First,
although the BE model of Davis et al. (2008) lacks
some dimensions put forward especially for nonprofit
BE (e.g., Faircloth, 2005; Camarero et al., 2010;
Laidler-Kylander and Simonsin, 2009; Stegemann
and Thompson, 2005), it can be used for measuring
nonprofit BE to some extent. Second, although BE
has generated considerable research in the field of
nonprofit branding (e.g., Laidler-Kylander and
Simonsin, 2009; Stegemann and Thompson, 2005;
Faircloth, 2005; Lee and Back, 2008; Hou et al.,
2009; Camarero et al., 2010; Lee and Back, 2010)
and although some studies (e.g., Lee and Back,
2008; Camarero et al., 2010; Hankinson, 2000) have
incorporated elements of brand co-creation, little
attention has been paid to nonprofit branding in
general or nonprofit BE particularly from the viewpoint of brand co-creation. Thus, this study offers
a contribution to the field. Third, despite a wide
range of brand co-creation studies (e.g., Brodie
et al., 2006; Boyle, 2007; Gregory, 2007; Fyrberg
and Jüriado, 2009; Payne et al., 2009; Juntunen,
2012; Juntunen et al., 2012), examinations of BE
co-creation remain scarce.
From a managerial perspective, the military forces
need to understand that because most recruits will
become LSPs or work for them after completing
their training, their attitudes towards the military
driving school will eventually conform to those of
civilian truck drivers. In other words, recruits are
key contributors to the co-creation of BE among
internal and external stakeholders. Therefore, it is
essential that the recruits take positive experiences
from the military driving school so as to ensure that
a positive direction in co-creation develops. To
increase the number of potential truck drivers to
fulfill the LSPs’ future needs, LSPs will be well
advised to recommend that as many young lorry
drivers as possible serve their military service in the
transport troops. As older truck drivers’ experiences
of the military driving school may influence young
drivers’ attitudes, LSPs should carefully think about

the kind of image they want the young drivers to form
about the military driving school and communicate

The present study has three limitations that merit
further discussion. First, the models provided lack
the dimensions of brand personality (Faircloth,
2005), loyalty, perceived quality and brand values
(Camarero et al., 2010), among several other dimensions (e.g., Laidler-Kylander and Simonsin, 2009;
Stegemann and Thompson, 2005) found to relate
particularly to nonprofit BE. Second, the models
work well if one measure of both BA and BI is
reduced. In other words, although the remaining
measures in our study appear to be valid measures
for the theoretical constructs, the other measures
should be further developed. Third, the data were
gathered in Finland and in a particular context,
namely logistics. This might limit the generalizability
of the results, although the study may offer new
insights for nonprofit organizations in other contexts
and in other countries of the world.
The study raises some topics for further research.
Although the aim of producing a workable measurement model was achieved, we recommend further
research to develop more sophisticated measures
of nonprofit BA and image, especially from a nonprofit brand co-creation perspective, and to validate
them with systematic methods such as confirmatory
factor analysis. Additionally, it would be interesting
to examine if nonprofit BI and/or nonprofit BA can
also be co-created. In addition, the relationship
between awareness and image identified by Juntunen
et al. (2011c) could be further examined. Finally,
the research could be extended to examine brand
equity co-creation in other contexts as well. We
hope that this study contributes new insights into
nonprofit branding and offers ideas for developing
the research.
