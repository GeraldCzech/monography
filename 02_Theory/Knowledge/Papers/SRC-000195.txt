Attitude Differences Between
Paid Workers and Volunteers
Matthew A. Liao-Troth

This study addresses the extension of research on paid employ-
ees to volunteer workers. Pearce (1978, 1993b) has postulated
that job attitudes are substantially different between volunteers
and paid employees, based on her work studying fourteen orga-
nizations employing either entirely volunteer workgroups or
entirely paid workgroups. Another interpretation of her data
would be that the organizational culture of such workgroups is
different, not the individual job attitudes.

A survey of paid and volunteer workers in similar jobs in a sin-
gle hospital setting was used to assess their job attitudes along
the dimensions of the psychological contract, organizational
commitment, and organizational justice. The psychological con-
tract was substantially similar between paid employees and vol-
unteers, with the exception of psychological contracts regarding
benefits. In addition, organizational commitment and organi-
zational justice were substantially similar between paid employ-
ees  and  volunteers,  with  the  exception  of  the  continuance
dimension of organizational commitment. This similarity of job
attitudes among volunteers and paid employees was found after
controlling for age, gender, ethnicity, income, holding a volun-
teer job in addition to this position, and holding a paid job in
addition to this position. These ﬁndings may not be generaliz-
able beyond a highly structured volunteer setting such as a
hospital.

Volunteer workers hold jobs in the public, nonproﬁt, and private
sectors in a variety of positions. Some of these jobs have no similar
parallel with paid positions (for instance, Girl Scout leader). Other
volunteer jobs are very similar to paid positions in organizations
(member of board of directors, tourist guide). Pearce (1978, 1983,
1993b) studied paid and volunteer workers in seven organizations
(local newspaper, day care, poverty relief agency, symphonic orches-
tra, family planning clinic, hospital gift shops, and ﬁre departments)
and found a variety of behavioral and attitudinal differences among
individuals in different organizations. Her main ﬁnding was that job
attitudes were more similar across type of employee (paid versus vol-
unteer) than across organization (for example, day care versus news-
paper). These differences essentially were related to the purpose of
doing the job—the worker’s motivations. Paid employees are pre-
dominantly rewarded for their work by their compensation; volun-
teers are rewarded in some other way. This distinction of expectations
and experience, Pearce argues, leads to a variety of differences within
the organization.

Although a strength of Pearce’s study (1978, 1983) is the use of
fourteen organizations of various sorts, a limitation of her study
design is the selection of only all-volunteer and all-paid-employee
organizations. Essentially, she analyzed seven wholly volunteer-
staffed organizations and compared these with seven wholly paid-
employee  organizations.  She  does  not  address  this  potential
limitation in her discussion, but she may have been picking up the
difference in the work between an exclusively volunteer workforce
and an exclusively paid workforce. In reality, many organizations that
use volunteers (such as hospitals, museums, and human service
agencies) also have professional paid staff. There are several advan-
tages to using a combination of paid and volunteer workers, espe-
cially  in  terms  of  diversity  of  skills  that  lend  themselves  to
organization knowledge with a workforce of differing employment
status (Matusik and Hill, 1998). There can also be economic savings
using a partially volunteer staff, which has incurred the ire of unions
in some settings (Liao-Troth, 1999).

HYPOTHESIS 1. Volunteer and paid employees do not differ in general job
attitudes when working in the same organization, doing substantially
similar work, and interacting with one another.

Some research on employment status in the job environment
supports this. Pearce (1993a) herself found that there were no
differences between employees and contract workers in terms
of organizational citizenship or commitment in the same work
environment.

On the other hand, some research has found that employment
status within an organization does have an effect on job attitudes.
Van Dyne and Ang (1998) found among workers in Singapore that
full-time employees had greater organizational citizenship behaviors,
psychological contract expectations, and affective commitment than
did contingent workers. There are also some job attitudes that,
although speciﬁc to paid employees, would not generalize to volun-
teers. Laczo and Hanisch (1999) recently looked at paid employees
and volunteers performing similar work in a museum setting and
found that these two groups varied in terms of job withdrawal (more
prevalent among paid employees) and organizational citizenship
behaviors (more prevalent among volunteers).

The constructs of job withdrawal and organizational citizenship
behaviors relate to why the volunteer or paid employee is in the orga-
nization. The paid employee is obviously getting paid, as well as
receiving other tangible or intangible beneﬁts. The volunteer is not
getting paid but is deﬁnitely receiving tangible and intangible bene-
ﬁts. A volunteer may or may not respond attitudinally to a reduction
in pay (or a substitute beneﬁt), but a paid employee should be much
more likely to respond. To this end, a corollary of the ﬁrst hypothe-
sis is presented:

HYPOTHESIS 2. Volunteers and paid employees differ in job attitudes—
while working in the same organization, doing substantially similar
work, and interacting with one another—when the job attitude is
speciﬁcally related to the economic relationship between the employee
and the organization.

ronment or employment status (paid versus volunteer) would be to
evaluate the job attitudes of volunteers and paid employees in the
same organizations doing essentially similar work. From the afore-
mentioned studies on employment status, the job attitudes of the psy-
chological contract, organizational commitment, and organizational
justice will be addressed. Laczo and Hanisch (1999) have already
addressed organizational citizenship behaviors, but the psychologi-
cal contract and commitment have not yet been researched in com-

The Psychological Contract
The psychological contract is created in the mind of an employee,
based on her or his perceptions of what employment issues have
been agreed upon, beyond those of any formal contract (Rousseau,
1995). This is not a contract in the legal sense, but rather the indi-
vidual’s interpretation of what agreements have been made with the
organization. Rousseau (1990) talks of the psychological contract as
falling into two broad categories: transactional, where high pay and
advancement are given for hard work, and relational, where job secu-
rity is given by the organization for an employee’s loyalty and a min-
imum  stay.  These  can  be  seen  as  directed  by  the  firm’s  human
resource management practices and how it rewards employees.

Extending and applying psychological contract literature and
research to volunteer workers would appear to be quite important.
Farmer and Fedor (1999) point out the importance of using the psy-
chological contract in understanding volunteer behavior. Measuring
volunteers’ psychological contracts would be one way of evaluating
their work expectations. Understanding these contracts would assist
people who manage volunteers in understanding what the volunteer
expects (Liao-Troth and Dunn, 1999). Unfortunately, Farmer and
Fedor (1999) incorrectly specify the psychological contract as an
expectation in their instrument (p. 363), in stark contrast to the sep-
aration of the constructs of psychological contract and expectation
(Rousseau, 1995; Robinson, 1996). Farmer and Fedor limit their
interpretation of the construct to expectations overall, and to the spe-
ciﬁc issue of perceived organizational support.

Transactional and relational contracts were originally used to
describe two broad categories of psychological contract. However,
subsequent work has focused those two categories only on what the
individual owes the organization (hard work for high recognition and
pay  as  transactional,  loyalty for  long  job  tenure  as  relational).
Rousseau and Tijoriwala (1996a, 1996b) categorized four types of
promise an organization might in turn specifically give to an indi-
vidual. These relate to the beneﬁts provided, good faith and fair deal-
ings, intrinsic job characteristics, and the working conditions. These
obligations of the organization are not tied to speciﬁc kinds of psy-
chological contract (transactional and relational) but are speciﬁc to
each psychological contract.

salary and other (sometimes intangible) compensation. The beneﬁts
offered would definitely be of great concern for someone who is a
paid individual. This should not be of such great concern for volun-

fulﬁlled, violated, or in some ambiguous state where the psycholog-

Rousseau and Anton’s work (1988, 1991) on violation of the psy-

tional justice.

Organizational Commitment
Organizational commitment is defined as the degree to which
an employee feels obligated to an employer. This is different
from the psychological contract in that there is no exchange
assumption in organizational commitment. Most models present
organizational commitment as an outcome of the psychological
contract of an employee, among other effects (see, for example,
Morrison and Robinson, 1997). Organizational commitment is most
frequently addressed in terms of how much an employee might
“like” the employing organization, but the construct has additional
meanings.

Allen  and  Meyer’s  literature  review  of  studies  in  organiza-
tional commitment (1990) led to identifying three forms of organi-
zational  commitment.  Affective  commitment is  described  as  an
employee’s emotional attachment and involvement with the organi-
zation. Continuance commitment is described as the intention of an
employee to remain with an employer in light of the cost of leaving
the employer. Normative commitment is described as an employee’s

feeling of obligation to remain with the employer. Dunham, Grube,
and Castañeda (1994) found that a decrease in any of the three forms
of commitment led to increased intention to leave the organization.
The employment status of a worker should affect some aspects of
organizational commitment. Affective commitment is the degree
of liking the organization. When people select organizations, this
is important for both volunteers and paid workers. In addition, in
the case of high employment (that is, an unemployment rate of less
than 5 percent) and multiple potential employers (in other words,
more opportunities for employment), this should continue to be
important to both volunteers and paid employees. The reason for this
is that people have several options of where they would be “happi-
est.” As continuance commitment considers the cost of changing
employers, it should be most strongly related to paid employees: vol-
unteers should have no (or low) switching costs when changing
organizations.

For normative commitment, I am making no speciﬁc predictions.
Normative is considered to be affected primarily by values, attitudes,
and beliefs. It may be found equally among paid and volunteer work-
ers, as this is a measure of a general sense of staying with an organi-
zation, regardless of liking it (affective commitment) or having a high
cost of leaving (continuance commitment). Indeed, an individual
who has a high measure of normative commitment will express it in
either an employer or a volunteer outlet.

Organizational Justice
Organizational justice addresses perceptions of fairness in the work-
place and has been delineated into two major components: distribu-
tive justice and procedural justice. The former addresses the justice
or appropriateness of outcomes, whereas the latter concerns the jus-
tice of the process as applied to the individual. This process aspect
of justice is further differentiated by some researchers as formal char-
acteristics of the process, information offered to the employee, and
interpersonal treatment (Greenberg, 1990; Gilliland, 1993). Others
define them as separate justice concepts: formal procedural justice
and interactional justice (Bies and Moag, 1986; Moorman, 1991). In
this study, I use the distinction among distributive, procedural, and
interactional justice.

The employment status of a worker, in terms of being a paid
employee or a volunteer, should not affect perceived organizational
justice. Since paid employees and volunteers are working in the same
organization, they would fall under the same work rules and feel that
the outcomes are fair or unfair, but there should be no difference in
terms  of  employment  status  for  distributive  justice.  Regarding
procedural justice, both would feel that inconsistent procedures
are unfair and consistent procedures are fair, so attitudes about pro-
cedural  justice  should  not  differ  between  paid  employees  and

Method
This study consisted of a survey of employees at a large medical cen-
ter in a metropolitan area of the southwestern United States. The sur-
vey queried several attitudes the employee held about his or her work
and employer. To control for the organizational effects that may have
created Pearce’s ﬁndings (1978, 1993b), only one site was used. How-
ever, recognition must be given to the limits of generalizability of the
ﬁndings of this study because of this methodological approach.

Sample
Subjects were paid and volunteer workers doing similar job tasks at
the medical center. One hundred eight of 300 potential participants
returned surveys (a 36 percent overall response rate), of which 67
were volunteers and 41 were paid employees. Respondents were on
average 58.68 years old (SD 17.81 years); 76.2 percent female and
23.8 percent male; 90 percent white, 4 percent Hispanic, 3 percent
other, 2 percent Asian American, and 1 percent African American.
The modal income was in the $20,000–30,000 range, with the
interquartile range covered by three categories that collectively
addressed income from $20,000 to $60,000. The mode and median
of education level was some college (n (cid:2) 42), with education rang-
ing in categories from high school graduate (n (cid:2) 21) to terminal
graduate degree (defined as Ph.D. or M.D. in the survey; n (cid:2) 2).
Finally, 7.8 percent of the respondents had a paid job in addition
to the job (paid or volunteer) for which they were answering the sur-
vey, and 33.7 percent of the respondents had a volunteer job in addi-
tion to the job (paid or volunteer) for which they were answering the
survey.

Procedures
Surveys were distributed to volunteers at their semimonthly staff
meetings, with instructions to complete one and give another to a
comparison-other paid employee. This comparison-other had to be
in the same work group and perform substantially similar work as
the volunteer. All volunteer staff at this medical center were orga-
nized into teams by job tasks. The teams were also used for the paid
staff. The positions in general were nurse assistants, but some teams
were specialized by ward or task area. The volunteers and paid
employees worked together on the same teams. Both volunteers and
paid employees were rewarded with a coupon for a cookie in the
cafeteria as a reward for their participation when they returned their
anonymous survey to a centrally placed drop box.

Measures
A survey was used to capture job attitudes of the respondents. Unless
otherwise noted, all measures are ﬁve-item Likert scales.

Employment Status. The subjects self-reported their status as a

volunteer or paid employee.

The Psychological Contract. For this study, a modiﬁed version of

Rousseau’s instrument (1998) was used (see the appendix).

Organizational Commitment. Allen and Meyer’s twenty-four item
measure (1990) of all three forms (affective, continuance, normative)
of commitment was used. Dunham, Grube, and Castañeda (1994)
provided psychometric support for these three forms of commitment.
Each form is an eight-item measure. To compare with the original

Control Variables
The control variables included were age in years, gender (female
and male), ethnic identity (collapsed into two categories, white
versus nonwhite, owing to the small number of individuals in
most ethnic groups), income (ordinal groupings of income ranges),
a paid job in addition to the job being studied, and a volunteer
job in addition to the job being studied. The latter two are con-
trolled out of concern that individuals in multiple organizational
settings may build their job impressions from more than the site of
study.

Analysis
A hierarchical regression was used for each dependent variable. Con-
trol variables were entered on the ﬁrst step (as a simultaneous entry
of a block of variables) to ensure that significant results were the
result of employment status rather than an artifact of a demographic
variable, and the employment status (volunteer or paid employee)
was entered on the second step.

Discussion
Although many generalizations are made in the management
literature about worker attitudes, actual testing to support the
generalization to voluntary workers is spotty at best. I have
attempted to address this issue with speciﬁc tests in regard to psy-
chological contracts, organizational commitment, and organizational
justice.

Findings and Implications for Research
Some have found effects for work status with contingency or con-
tract workers when compared with regular employees, but very lit-
tle has been done in comparing volunteers to paid employees. Laczo
and Hanisch (1999) have already addressed job withdrawal (there is
a difference) and organizational citizenship behaviors (again, a
difference). This latter finding is not consistent with Pearce’s find-
ings (1993a) on the differences between regular and contract employ-
ees who did not differ in organizational citizenship behaviors. To
Laczo and Hanisch’s findings, I add the observed similarities and
differences between volunteers and paid employees in terms of
psychological contracts, organizational commitment, and organiza-
tional justice.

For the psychological contract, Van Dyne and Ang (1998) found
a difference in that regular workers had stronger psychological con-
tracts than contingent workers, when the psychological contract is
perceived organizational support. In this present study, I did not look
speciﬁcally at the psychological contract of perceived organizational
support, but rather at the broader conceptual categorical content of
psychological contracts. In looking at these broader categories, the
only difference I found between paid employees and volunteers was
the organization’s promises regarding beneﬁts. This issue is measured
with the four statements “pay tied to your performance,” “a com-
petitive salary,” “good opportunities for promotion,” and “bonuses
based on your performance.” Three of these statements address
salary specifically, which is an inherent difference between paid
workers and volunteers. This lends itself to the observation that not
only is the psychological contract important in the context of vol-
unteer management (Farmer and Fedor, 1999) but also, with the
exception of contract issues that concern economic matters, ﬁndings
about the psychological contract with paid employees may be gen-
eralized to apply to volunteers as well.

For affective commitment, Van Dyne and Ang (1998) found
that full-time workers had a greater sense of commitment than
contingent workers, but Pearce (1993a) found that there were no
signiﬁcant differences between regular and contract workers. In this
study, I looked at both affective commitment and continuance com-
mitment. In terms of continuance commitment, my findings are in
line with Pearce’s observations. This may be a result of the national
culture of the United States (for this and Pearce’s studies) versus
Singapore (for Van Dyne and Ang’s study). It might also be a result
of an organizational effect on their results: they were looking at two
large service firms. However, this difference of findings is highly
unlikely with only two organizations; both Pearce and I were com-
paring workers within the same organizational environment. These
differences might be more attributable to the concept of organiza-
tional investment (Tsui, Pearce, Porter, and Tripoli, 1997). Organi-
zations that overinvest or have mutual investment between the
individual and the organization have workers who express higher
levels of affective commitment (Tsui, Pearce, Porter, and Tripoli,
1997). Thus, although the national location of the studies does not
directly matter, the inclination and ability of the organization to treat
its employees in this way would have an impact on affective com-
mitment.

Finally, with organizational justice I introduced a new workplace
issue to address in employee status research. Having found no dif-
ferences indicates that the workplace environment may be more
important than the status of the employee, but it is hard to place this
in the greater context without research on other employee status
comparisons.

The ﬁndings in this work do not necessarily negate the earlier
work of Pearce (1978, 1993a, 1993b). What they do demonstrate is
that some caveats must be made about generalizations based on the
work by Pearce, who demonstrated that all-volunteer organizations
and all-paid-employee organizations have job attitudes that differ
from each other across seven types of organizations. My present
study demonstrates that volunteers and paid employees in the same
location, performing similar work, and subject to similar work rules,
procedures, contracts, expectations, discipline, and evaluations have
job attitudes similar to one another for psychological contracts
(except regarding benefits), affective commitment, and organiza-
tional justice. This is very important, as there have not been any
studies demonstrating that a job attitude observed among paid
employees is generalizable to volunteers. However, the vast major-
ity of organizations using volunteers do not have them working
side-by-side with paid employees, nor are they entirely on their
own. Many organizations using volunteers have paid managers who
supervise a volunteer workforce.

Findings and Implications for the Manager of Volunteers
Any organization using volunteers is confronted with the question
of the degree to which the work experience for volunteers should be
formalized. In the hospital setting presented in this study, volunteers
were highly trained, had speciﬁc limitations on what they could and
could not do (such as restrictions on touching patients), and had
contracts specifying their term of service as a volunteer. This is not
the norm. At the other extreme, organizations can have free-ﬂowing
volunteers with minimal instructions and wide latitude in performing
tasks (such as picking up trash in a park). This range of formaliza-
tion is much more akin to the employee-versus-contractor dichotomy
established in legal tradition.

An organization using volunteers will ﬁt the task to the people
available and recruit people for the necessary tasks to be accom-
plished. In these two approaches, some differences may be observed.
When recruiting people for a speciﬁc task that is previously deﬁned,
a more formal relationship may need to be established, especially as
the complexity of the task increases (such as aiding nurses in a hos-
pital setting). In such a setting, a more professional environment may
be necessary, and this study indicates that at least in one instance vol-
unteers take on job attitudes that are quite similar to those of paid
employees. In a grassroots organization—where one finds people
seeking a task, as it were—people deﬁne the organization’s environ-
ment, and one may end up with a situation in which their job atti-
tudes are radically different from those of paid employees.

Finally, several constructs were addressed in this study. Oth-
ers have developed models on how these constructs relate (see
Morrison and Robinson, 1997), but for the practicing manager
understanding the nuanced differences here can help in successfully
understanding and managing volunteers (Liao-Troth and Dunn,
1999). Volunteers may believe that they have made certain agree-
ments with the organization as to what they will provide the organi-
zation and what the organization will provide them. If a manager is
not aware of her or his volunteers’ psychological contracts, then he
or she may unintentionally violate the volunteers’ psychological con-
tracts, which can have negative consequences in terms of job per-
formance. Likewise, a negative attitude about the organization can
hurt the work environment, and if expressed publicly it can hurt the
external reputation of the organization. Finally, the sense of fairness
and justice in the workplace, which has been shown to have an effect
on such issues (as with people planning to leave), is important not
only to paid employees but to volunteers as well.

Limitations
There are several limitations of this study: the motivations of the vol-
unteers, the organization studied, and the sample.

Volunteer Motives. An issue not addressed in this study that may
be of paramount importance is the motive to volunteer. The differ-
ences in motive of volunteers can have a tremendous impact on their
behavior and determine the appropriate method of managing them.
The motives of the volunteers would potentially moderate all effects
that were found in this study. Determining the motives of volunteers
is a common issue in volunteer research, but understanding how
important these motives are in managing them appropriately is lack-
ing. These motives direct volunteers toward certain volunteer oppor-
tunities and away from others. Volunteers seek out organizations that
provide what they want and expect (Clary and Snyder, 1999). These
potential volunteers will form perceptions of what they commit to
an organization, and what they expect the organization to provide
them. In this way, they are more like paid employees.

Organization of Study. The ﬁndings in a hospital may not be gen-
eralizable to other organizations using volunteers; the ﬁndings in this
study may not even be generalizable to other hospitals. Two issues
in this sample limit generalizability to other organizations using
volunteers.

The ﬁrst is volunteer contracts. The hospital of study uses con-
tracts to specify and describe volunteer positions. A person volun-
teering with this hospital signs a contract that delineates job duties
and expectations of job performance. A check of other hospitals in
the area found that this is a common though not uniform practice; it
is also not a universal practice among nonproﬁts in other areas of ser-
vice. This may have had an unanticipated effect on some ﬁndings or
skewed the data in the direction of the hypotheses by making the vol-
unteer feel more like an employee.

A second issue is job prohibitions on volunteers in hospitals.
Many hospitals, especially private for-proﬁt, are restricted in terms
of which jobs volunteers can do. These are not in place by any
state statute or federal law; they are developed in negotiated work
agreements with labor unions representing hospital employees.
Some for-profit hospitals have engaged in a practice of using
volunteer workers to reduce the payroll and lay off paid employ-
ees. Indeed, in another metropolitan area of the southwestern
United States, all the major hospitals had such agreements, irre-
spective of their for-proﬁt, nonproﬁt, or public status (Liao-Troth,
1999). This restriction was not found in the hospital studied
but makes comparison, replication, and generalization to other hos-
pitals difﬁcult.

The Sample. The sample may not be representative of volunteers
in general. As mentioned before, the proportion of women volun-
teering in this hospital is quite high, and the racial diversity of the
subjects is low when compared to volunteers in general. For this
reason, the control variables were entered first in the hierarchical
regression, but these demographics indicated that the subjects them-
selves may not be representative of the population.

Measuring the Psychological Contract
For this study, an early version of Rousseau’s instrument (1998) was
used. Some psychometric aspects of the instrument are discussed in
Rousseau and Tijoriwala (1996a, 1996b). The instrument used for
this study was obtained at that time (personal communication, Aug.
14, 1996). Rousseau’s 1998 instrument is a further development of
her instrument used for Rousseau and Tijoriwala (1996a, 1996b).

The measure separates out what the organization promises
to the employee from what the employee feels that he or she
has promised to the organization. I reduced the number of
items in Rousseau’s instrument in an effort to achieve parsimony. A
total of 339 upper-division business students in a southwestern
public university were asked to retrospectively consider a job held
either as a volunteer or a paid employee. They were then asked to
evaluate that job along Rousseau’s dimensions. Results were evalu-
ated using two exploratory factor analyses, with a principle-com-
ponents method of extraction and varimax (orthogonal) rotation of
factors—one for the issues promised to the organization by the
individual, and one for the issues promised to the individual by
the organization.

The number of factors was prespecified for both obligations of
the worker and obligations of the organization. Items were eliminated
on the basis of three rules: (1) those with alpha correlation of (cid:4)0.3
were to be removed (this did not apply to any item); (2) factor loads
of (cid:6)0.3 on two or more factors were removed; and (3) primary fac-
tor loads of (cid:4)0.5 were removed. Restricting the items in this man-
ner reduces the measure from fourteen to twelve items addressing
the promises of the individual to the organization, and from thirty-
two to nineteen items addressing the promises of the organization to
the individual. Results can be found in Table 5, and the actual items
can be found in Table 6. Single measures for all six dimensions of the
psychological contract were formed by averaging the multiple mea-
sures of each item.
