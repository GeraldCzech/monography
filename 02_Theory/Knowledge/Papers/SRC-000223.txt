Abstract
Operating in a progressively more competitive and turbulent environment, nonprofit organizations are beginning to recognize the need to
become more ‘‘businesslike’’ and of the value of marketing in achieving a competitive advantage. Whilst marketing alone is not purported to
be a ‘‘quick-fix’’ elixir in this regard, it does have the potential to make a useful and ongoing contribution to organizational performance in
the nonprofit sector. Indeed, marketing scholars have for some time now been examining the role, function, relevance and transferability of
various marketing principles and practices to nonprofit organizations. Surprisingly, the concept of brand management has been largely
overlooked in this process. Using Keller’s [Harv. Bus. Rev. 78 (2000) 147] brand report card (BRC) as a point of departure, this article
describes the development of a reliable, valid and generalizable multidimensional scale to assess nonprofit brand orientation (NBO). Findings
from two empirical surveys are presented, conclusions drawn and future research directions outlined.

1. Introduction
Marketing scholars have long recognized the potential
transferability of key concepts and philosophies to the
nonprofit sector (Kotler and Levy, 1969). Advertising
effects, market orientation and relationship marketing in
nonprofit organizations have been among the issues recently examined by researchers in this field (Caruana et al.,
1998; Marchand and Lavoie, 1998; Voss and Voss, 2000).
Yet, despite the utility of marketing both conceptually and
functionally, researchers have also cautioned against the
overadoption of marketing in nontraditional settings (see,
e.g., Hutton, 2001). In general, however, marketing has
gained acceptance among the nonprofit practitioner community (Sargeant, 2001). It is therefore a little surprising
that the efficacy of brand management in nonprofit organizations has received only scant attention—particularly
since the conceptual development of brand management

in the for-profit sector has progressed rapidly since the

In an attempt to address this gap in the literature, the
present study focuses on the development of a scale to
capture the nuances of branding practices and philosophies
within this sector. The psychometric properties of the
resulting ‘‘nonprofit brand orientation scale’’ (NBOS) are
then assessed. The paper commences with a review of the
extant literature on branding in the nonprofit sector. It then
describes the scale development process adopted and
details the procedure for assessing the reliability, validity
and generalizability of NBOS. Managerial implications and
applications of the scale are addressed before concluding
with limitations of the approach and directions for ongoing
research.

2. Branding in the nonprofit sector
In any competitive marketplace, an organization’s longterm survival will depend on its ability to sustain an adequate
level of profitability. This is equally true for both profit and
nonprofit organizations, albeit not always the primary motivator for the latter. Nonprofit organizations form an integral

842

M.T. Ewing, J. Napoli / Journal of Business Research 58 (2005) 841–853

and relatively large sector of many economies. By way of
example, there are approximately 1.3 million tax-exempt
organizations in the United States, of which nearly 700,000
are public charities and the remainder comprising such
institutions as private schools, foundations, hospitals and
religious organizations (Lowell et al., 2001). With the
nonprofit ‘‘marketplace’’ becoming more competitive and
resources becoming increasingly scarce (Lindenberg, 1999),
the principle focus for many nonprofit managers is toward
attracting and raising funds and establishing viable partnerships with others (Lowell et al., 2001). It is not surprising
that many nonprofits are well aware of the role marketing
can play in achieving such objectives (Rees, 1998).
Although brands and their management have been well
entrenched in the commercial sector for over a century (Low
and Fullerton, 1994), it is only in recent years that researchers
have begun to investigate the salience of brand management
within a nonprofit context. Scholarly research efforts to date,
however, have been either conceptual or exploratory. Saxton
(1994), for instance, uses case examples, personal experience
and anecdotal evidence to describe the importance of, and
processes involved in, the creation of strong charity brands.
Such contributions are important as they delineate the differences between managing for-profit versus nonprofit brands.
Likewise, Tapp (1996) examines the extent to which branding
theories and models developed for the commercial sector are
adaptable to the charity sector, drawing the conclusion that
nonprofit organizations have much to gain by adopting some
of these practices. Some empirical research has also been
undertaken. Several researchers have reported a relationship
between stakeholder attitudes toward a charity and their
donating behavior, with higher levels of contribution attributed to those individuals with a more positive attitude
(Bendapudi et al., 1996; Harvey, 1990; Webb et al., 2000).
Implicitly, these findings suggest that brand image plays an
important role in shaping stakeholder attitudes and actions.
As such, creating a suitable brand identity is becoming as
important for nonprofit organizations as it is for their commercial counterparts and involves, amongst other activities,
giving consideration to a brand’s functional elements as well
as its symbolic components (Tapp, 1996). Efforts should be
directed toward building brand trust, aligning a brand’s
identity with the inherent psychological needs and desires
of potential donors and communicating these points of
difference to stakeholders (Saxton, 1994; Tapp, 1996). In
so doing, an organization is better able to use its brand to
communicate its organizational values to stakeholders (Tapp,
1996), change public opinion (Lindsay and Murphy, 1996),
build donor trust (Tonkiss and Passey, 1999), achieve its
objectives (Graham et al., 1994; Hankinson, 2002; Simoes
and Dibb, 2001) and also attract a higher proportion of
voluntary income (Hankinson, 2001). In spite of the potential
benefits, however, brands still remain a largely underutilized
strategic asset within the nonprofit sector (Tapp, 1996).
Cognizant of the importance of branding to the nonprofit sector, Hankinson (2001, 2002) provides interpretive

insights into brand orientation within the charity sector.
Her contribution marks one of the first attempts to assess
brand orientation for charity organizations and to gauge its
impact on performance. Hankinson (2001) defines brandoriented organizations as those who regard the organization as a brand and whose actions and attitudes are
consistent with the brand construct. In this capacity,
organizational actions are directed toward the development,
acquisition and leveraging of branded products and services in the pursuit of competitive advantage (Noble et al.,
2002; Urde, 1994, 1999). Adopting such a philosophy
enables organizations to become more adept at achieving
positive performance outcomes (Hankinson, 2001; Noble
et al., 2002).
Whilst pursuing a brand orientation is intuitively logical,
it also has strong theoretical support. Clark and Marshall’s
(1981) theory of mutual knowledge explains how individuals
come to believe with great certainty that both they and
another share the same knowledge, beliefs or information
regarding an object. From a brand management perspective,
brands are likely to be more successful when an audience’s
perception of a brand mirrors the firm’s view of the brand
concept and both are identical to the consumers’ original
specifications (Haynes et al., 1999). One of the primary
functions of brand management, then, is to create, coordinate, monitor and adjust interactions between an organization and its stakeholders (Schultz and Barnes, 1999), such
that there is consistency between an organization’s brand
vision and stakeholders’ brand beliefs. On this basis, a
brand-oriented organization is better able to establish and
maintain mutual brand knowledge and thereby enhance
organizational performance.
While marketing scholars continue to expand the domain
of nonprofit marketing, most brand-related studies have
focused on a single nonprofit sector (Hankinson, 2002;
Saxton, 1994; Tapp, 1996). Hankinson (2001, 2002) has,
however, provided a useful foundation on which to conceptualize brand orientation in the nonprofit sector as a whole.
The present study expands on her work by seeking to develop
a psychometrically robust and generalizable measure of
nonprofit brand orientation (NBO). We define NBO as the
organizational wide process of generating and sustaining a
shared sense of brand meaning that provides superior value to
stakeholders and superior performance to the organization.
Accordingly, NBO reflects an organization’s focus on the
internal and external activities necessary to build and sustain
strong brands in the marketplace. Such a philosophy can
assist an organization in establishing mutual brand knowledge with stakeholders and realizing the benefits that a strong
brand can deliver.

3. The brand orientation construct
Researchers have viewed brand management from several
different perspectives. Some have taken a broad overview of

M.T. Ewing, J. Napoli / Journal of Business Research 58 (2005) 841–853

the brand management process (Kapferer, 2001; Keller, 1998;
Park et al., 1986), while others have focused on specific
elements. These can be grouped into four distinct themes:
creating a unique brand identity, structuring brand portfolios,
managing brand communications and monitoring brand value (Aaker, 1991; Berthon et al., 1999; de Chernatony and
Riley, 1998; Keller, 2000). By focusing on each of these
aspects, an organization is able to effectively monitor consumers’ brand perceptions, identify whether such brand
attitudes confer with their own brand vision and instigate
strategies that reinforce positive brand beliefs or change
negative perceptions. Future growth opportunities can also
be identified and estimations made of the relative value of a
brand in the minds of consumers. In so doing, organizations
can create brands that truly resonate with stakeholders and
deliver to them the tangible and intangible benefits they
desire. Ultimately, this will lead to the development of
stronger brands and an improvement in organizational performance. Although developed in the context of for-profit
organizations, we argue that such principles are equally
applicable to the nonprofit sector. We also contend that these
antecedents are broadly captured in Keller’s (2000) brand
report card (BRC).

4. Toward the development of NBOS
Scale items were generated for this study by following a
deductive approach (Schwab, 1980). Keller’s (2000) BRC,
which consolidates many of the varying perspectives on
brand management and encapsulates the key dimensions of
the construct, was used to generate a total of 37 scale
items. These were then assessed for face validity by two
marketing academics, which resulted in only minor modifications being made to item wording. The second stage
of the purification process involved undertaking three
focus group interviews. Senior managers representing a
broad cross section of Australian nonprofit organizations,
including charities, education, health and social service
organizations, participated in the sessions. Each session
consisted of between 10 and 12 participants and lasted for
approximately 1.5 hours. Participants were provided with a
list of the refined scale items and were requested to
comment on the relevance of each statement to their
organization. Furthermore, they were questioned in relation
to whether they understood the specific terms used within,
and the intended meaning of each statement. Information
was also obtained regarding key performance measures
used within their respective organizations. Through this
process, seven brand orientation items were identified as
irrelevant to nonprofit organizations and were subsequently
discarded. Minor adjustment to the wording of some statements was also required to remove unfamiliar marketing
jargon (e.g., ‘‘brand equity,’’ ‘‘push and pull strategies,’’
‘‘points of parity’’) and replace the term ‘‘customer’’ with
‘‘stakeholder’’ (to avoid potential confusion in attempting

843

to differentiate between ‘‘recipients’’ and ‘‘donors’’ as
‘‘customers’’). The remaining 30 statements formed the
pool of items from which NBOS would be constructed.
Each item was placed on a 7-point Likert-type scale and
respondents were asked to rate the extent to which their
organization currently engaged in the activity described. A
response of 1 reflected to a very little extent, and 7, to a
very great extent. NBOS also included two subjective
measures of performance, 14 items measuring market
orientation (Narver and Slater, 1990) and several classificatory variables.

5. Sample
Nonprofit sectors were selected based on 12 categories
defined in the International Classification of Non Profit
Organizations (ICNPO) (Sargeant, 1999). Using the Standard Industry Classification (SIC) codes, the ‘‘industries’’
that most closely resembled each of the ICNPO categories
were selected. The sample frames used in this study were
composed of Dun and Bradstreet’s Business Who’s Who of
Australia and a listing of Australian charities provided by
Action Mailing Lists. Australian nonprofit organizations
within each sector were then identified through a simple
random sampling technique. In line with Churchill’s (1979)
recommendation, NBO was administered to two separate
samples. The data collected from Sample 1 were used to
purify the measure and data from Sample 2 to confirm the
factor structure and assess validity.

6. Results
Data was collected from the Chief Executive Officer (or
equivalent) of each organization via a mail survey. In total,
1300 questionnaires were distributed to Australian nonprofit organizations in Sample 1. One hundred ten questionnaires were returned unopened and a further 29 surveys
could not be used in the analysis as they were either
incomplete or were returned from organizations classified
as private hospitals. As a result, 233 usable questionnaires
were obtained from Sample 1, which represents an effective response rate of 19.2%. Following purification of scale
items, the questionnaire was then administered to a second
sample, consisting of 1000 organizations. With the second
sample, 168 questionnaires were returned unopened and 15
responses were received from commercial organizations
(i.e., private hospitals). A further 6 surveys could not be
used due to incomplete responses. This resulted in 170
usable questionnaires being obtained from Sample 2,
representing an effective response rate of 20.9%. These
figures are consistent with response rates reported in other
marketing studies (Cannon and Perreault, 1999; Joshi and
Campbell, 2003) and those that have used commercial
databases (Maignan and Ferrell, 2001). A profile of re-

7. Nonresponse bias

Q19

Armstrong and Overton’s (1977) procedure was used to
assess for nonresponse bias. This technique is based on the
assumption that late respondents are similar to the ‘‘theoretical’’ nonrespondent. If no significant differences are
observed between early and late respondents, it can be
assumed that nonrespondents are similar to survey participants and the effects of nonresponse bias minimal (Armstrong and Overton, 1977). Using data from Sample 1, an
independent samples t test was used to compare responses
along the 30 brand orientation items between early and late
respondents, which were represented by the 1st and 4th
quartiles, respectively. This process was repeated for Sample 2. Results for both samples indicated that there were no
significant differences between early and late respondents.
As such, it can be assumed that the probability of nonresponse bias is minimal.

Data from Sample 1 (16 items; n = 224) was subjected to
principal component analysis with varimax rotation. Factor
extraction according to the MINEIGEN criterion (i.e., all
factors with eigenvalues >1) was employed. The analysis
produced a three-factor solution. However, several items (9,
21, 24 and 26) loaded on multiple factors. These were
subsequently removed from the analysis. The final threefactor solution, which accounts for 66% of the variation in
the data, is shown in Table 3. From the rotated factor matrix
it can be seen that Items 2 –6 load on Factor 1, items 12, 16,

18, 19 and 20 load on Factor 2 and Items 22 and 23 load on
Factor 3.
Factor 1, labeled ‘‘interaction,’’ assesses the extent to
which an organization establishes a dialogue with key stakeholders and responds to changes in the environment. It
captures the degree to which an organization uses market
feedback to create and deliver superior value to stakeholders.
This provides an indication of their level of responsiveness to
changes in market conditions and stakeholder needs. Factor
2, labeled ‘‘orchestration,’’ assesses the degree to which the
brand portfolio and related marketing activities are suitably
structured and effectively communicated to both internal and
external stakeholders. It measures an organization’s ability to
implement integrated marketing communications activities
that deliver consistent messages to stakeholders. Finally,
Factor 3, labeled ‘‘affect,’’ assesses the degree to which an
organization understands the extent to which they are liked/
disliked by key stakeholders and more specifically, exactly
what about the organization is most liked/disliked and why.
In the advertising response literature, for example, there is an
impressive body of evidence underpinning the salience of
likability (see Biel and Bridgwater, 1990; Du Plessis, 1994,
for a detailed discussion) and its ability to predict attitudes
towards the ad/brand/product, and more importantly, actual
past behavior and intended future behavior. Thus, ‘‘stakeholder likability’’ is a salient antecedent to nonprofit brand
performance.

Using Sample 2, structural equation modeling (SEM)
was used to perform a confirmatory factor analysis on the
proposed model, which consisted of a first-order, threefactor structure. Specifically, the model comprised three
latent variables (interaction, orchestration and affect), with
the observed variables loading in accordance with the
pattern revealed in the exploratory factor analysis on Sample
1 (see Fig. 1).
There are several tests to ascertain whether an SEM
model fits the observed data. The chi-square statistic (v2)
provides a measure of how well the model fits the data,
with a nonsignificant v2 supporting the null hypothesis that
the model is a good fit to the data. Overall goodness of fit
for a model can also be assessed by three additional
indices: the root mean residual (RMR), adjusted goodness-of-fit index (AGFI) and comparative fit index (CFI).
A model is regarded as having an acceptable fit if the RMR
is less than .10, AGFI exceeds .8 and CFI is greater than .9
(Bentler, 1990). The results for the three factor NBO model
indicate a v2 = 58.69 (df = 51, P=.214), RMR=.093,
AGFI=.917 and CFI=.992. Based on the criteria proposed
by Bentler (1990), the three-dimensional model produces
an acceptable fit to the data and confirms the underlying
structure of NBOS.

11.2. Validity of NBOS
Whilst it is important to assess the reliability of a
measure, validity should also be established to ensure that
the scale in fact measures what it purports to measure (Hair
et al., 1998). Several aspects are considered, namely convergent, discriminant, nomological, criterion and content
validity.
11.2.1. Convergent and discriminant validity
The average variance extracted (AVE) was calculated for
each of the factors and used to evaluate the convergent and
discriminant validity of the scale. If the shared variance
accounts for 0.50 or more of the total variance, then
convergent validity can be assumed. Similarly, discriminant
validity is evident when the AVE for each construct is
greater than the squared correlation between that construct
and any other construct in the model (Fornell and Larcker,
1981). The results are presented in Table 4, which attest to
both the convergent and discriminant validity of NBO.
Factor analytic techniques can also be used to assess the
discriminant validity of a scale. The underlying premise of
this approach is that when items purported to measure
distinct constructs are factor analyzed, they should not load
together onto the same dimensions (Hinkin, 1995). In this

11.2.2. Nomological validity
Nomological validity assesses the relationship between
theoretical constructs (Malhotra et al., 1996). This involves
identifying theoretically supported relationships from prior
research and then assessing whether the scale has
corresponding relationships (Hair et al., 1998). Drawing
on Clark and Marshall’s (1981) theory of mutual knowledge, we can surmise that effective brand management
enables a firm to generate and sustain a shared sense of
brand meaning with its stakeholders. Such organizations
endeavor to identify stakeholder needs and satisfy expectations by providing unique and relevant brands. Furthermore, they have systems in place to monitor prevailing
brand perceptions and are able to adjust a brand’s marketing
programs to reinforce or change these beliefs. Organizations
that effectively manage their brands are more likely to build
stronger brands, which will subsequently enhance organizational performance (Aaker and Jacobson, 1994; Keller,
2000). From this perspective, brand-oriented organizations
should be in a better position to deliver satisfaction to
stakeholders and achieve superior performance.
We used two subjective measures of organizational performance to assess the nomological validity of NBOS. The
first related to respondents’ perceptions of an organization’s
ability to serve stakeholders better compared to competitors
and the second, to their ability to achieve both short- and
long-term objectives. The three brand orientation dimensions
were formed by computing the mean scores for items that
constituted each factor. These were then regressed onto the
two performance measures. As can be seen in Table 6a and b,
both regression models are significant. The first analysis
(Table 6a), where the dependent measure relates to perceived
ability to serve stakeholders better, produces a significant R2
of .305 ( P < .001). Interestingly, only two of the three NBO
dimensions (interaction and orchestration) are significant
predictors of an organization’s ability to provide superior
service to stakeholders. The result of the second multiple

nomological validity of NBOS has been established.
11.2.3. Criterion validity
The criterion validity of an instrument is established if it
performs as expected in relation to other variables selected
as meaningful criteria. The criterion validity of NBOS was
assessed through a multiple regression of the three dimensions against an independent overall measure of brand
management effectiveness. As can be seen in Table 7, the
analysis produces an adjusted R2 of .567 ( P < .001). Furthermore, interaction, orchestration and affect all have a
significant impact on the criterion variable. These results
provide compelling evidence of criterion validity for NBOS.
However, it is worth noting that we have only assessed
concurrent-criterion validity.
11.2.4. Content validity
Content validity assesses the extent to which individual
scale items cover the range of meanings included in the
concept (Babbie, 1992; Hair et al., 1998). A one-way
ANOVA was used to examine the relationship between the
mean scores for each NBO dimension and respondents’
ratings of the overall measure of brand management effectiveness. As can be seen in Table 8, there appears to be a
strong positive relationship between perceptions of the
overall effectiveness of an organization’s brand management
practices and the evaluation of the individual dimensions.
That is, the higher the respondents rate the overall effectiveness of the organization’s brand management practices,
the higher the average rating of the individual NBO dimensions. Thus, it can be accepted that NBOS possesses content
validity.

12. Discussion
This study has provided new insight into the salient
brand management practices of nonprofit organizations.
Building on the work of Hankinson (2001, 2002), we have

M.T. Ewing, J. Napoli / Journal of Business Research 58 (2005) 841–853

developed a valid, reliable and generalizable measure of
brand orientation for nonprofit organizations across diverse
sectors. To this end, we operationalized Keller’s (2000)
BRC and modified the statements to reflect the idiosyncrasies of the nonprofit sector. Three underlying dimensions
were identified, which have been labeled interaction, orchestration and affect. Nonprofit managers must ensure that
all brand-related activities undertaken within the firm are
fully coordinated to maximize market awareness and performance. Furthermore, it is equally important for nonprofit
organizations to be aware of the needs of their stakeholders
and understand how these are changing as a result of
internal and external pressures. Finally, consideration needs
to be given to developing an understanding of what aspects
of the organization’s brand(s) are most liked and disliked by
stakeholders. Combined, these activities can contribute
positively to the overall performance of nonprofit organizations, particularly in terms of enabling them to serve their
stakeholders better and achieve both long and short-term
objectives. Thus, it seems that certain commercial branding
principles are transferable to nonprofit organizations. However, it may well be that certain practices are immediately
transferable, while others may only be transferable in the
medium term, if at all. Specifically, we are referring to the
fact that through the rigorous process of scale development,
refinement, testing and retesting, we have effectively adapted and reduced Keller’s (2000) original 37 items to 12
items in the final NBO scale. Our rationale for doing this is
that brand management is a relatively new concept in the
nonprofit sector. While it has been practiced for well over 50
years at a sophisticated level in the commercial sector, the
practice is still very much in a state of infancy in the
nonprofit sector. In fact, we would argue that it is presently
evolving and that over time, more of Keller’s original items
may yet become applicable. Accordingly, we anticipate
future researchers to, in due course, perhaps expand on
the present scale.
Overall, the results provide evidence of the validity,
reliability and generalizability of the NBO scale. As a
managerial tool, NBOS can be used to guide the internal
and external brand management activities of nonprofit
organizations, as well as to benchmark current practices.
That is, managers of nonprofit organizations can use NBOS
to compare their brand management practices against other
organizations operating in a range of sectors or to identify
deficiencies in their current brand management practices.
This will enable an organization to improve the overall
effectiveness of their branding activities and enhance its
relative performance. From an academic perspective, NBOS
provides the mechanism by which to measure brand orientation in nonprofit organizations and explore relationships
and associations with other constructs. For instance, NBOS
can be used to examine the potential antecedents to and
consequences of pursuing a brand orientation. Furthermore,
this study has contributed to the existing body of knowledge
on brands and their management. As an extension of

851

Hankinson’s (2000) work, it provides a step forward in
developing deeper empirical insights into the nature and
scope of NBO. In so doing, Keller’s (2000) BRC has
successfully been operationalized and adapted to the nonprofit sector. This lends some support to the view that
nonprofit organizations can potentially learn much about
branding from their commercial counterparts (RobertsWray, 1994; Tapp, 1996).

13. Limitations and directions for future research
Several limitations of this study are noted. First, given
that commercial databases have been used to identify and
select nonprofit organizations in Australia, it is possible that
some organizations may have been omitted from the sample
frame. Second, NBOS was based on Keller’s (2000) BRC,
which raises the question of whether this checklist fully
captures the nuances of branding in nonprofit organizations.
Third, the use of mail surveys brings with it several known
limitations, such as nonresponse and survey bias.
Following is an agenda for ongoing research in the area.
First, research could be directed toward expanding NBOS
to include additional statements from other brand orientation inventories, as well as concepts that may be perhaps
unique to the nonprofit sector. Second, future researchers
may wish to identify the key antecedents to and consequences of brand orientation within nonprofit organizations. Third, it may be useful to identify the challenges and
barriers that prohibit nonprofit organizations from pursuing
a brand orientation as their guiding business philosophy.
Fourth, researchers could undertake a comparison of brand
management practices of nonprofit organizations across
cultures or, alternatively, a comparison between for-profit
and nonprofit organizations. Fifth, the relationship between
brand management effectiveness and resource acquisition
could be examined; that is, the question of whether
organizations that are more effective at managing their
brands are able to acquire a greater proportion of the
‘‘funding pie’’ can be addressed. Finally, qualitative research could be used to elicit deeper insights into how
strong nonprofit brands are in fact created and managed.
Moreover, such research may be useful in developing an
understanding of whether nonprofit practitioners view
brand management in the same way as their commercial
counterparts. That is, to identify how comfortable nonprofit
practitioners feel in describing their organization’s activities in commercial marketing/branding terms and the
reasons behind any potential discomfort in this regard. In
conclusion, this study has provided some useful insights
into the brand management practices of nonprofit organizations. Specifically, it has resulted in the development,
application and verification of a reliable, valid and generalizable measure of NBO. We hope our results will draw
further attention to this important yet underresearched
domain.
