ABSTRACT
Public concerns about economic inequality are large and growing, and they have
been shown to affect people’s charitable giving and volunteering. While empirical
literature addresses this issue, it provides little evidence on the underlying mechanisms linking inequality and civic engagement. Scholars from different disciplines
have proposed numerous hypotheses about these mechanisms, with the social disintegration, conflict, relative power, and unmet needs hypotheses among the most
frequently suggested. In this study, we apply mediation analysis to survey data from
Austria and Germany (N = 1,691) to assess which of these mechanisms help explain the relationship. Our results support the social disintegration and unmet needs
hypotheses: individuals who completely agree that income inequality is too high
are slightly less likely to give to charity (volunteer) due to the indirect relationship
through generalised social trust and substantially more likely to give to charity (volunteer) due to the indirect relationship through impure altruism compared to those
who completely disagree that income inequality is too high. These findings imply
that multiple mechanisms work in opposing directions. Our study provides an important step in theory development in this field and serves as a basis for future
experimental studies.
KEYWORDS Perceived income inequality; charitable giving; volunteering; social disintegration hypothesis; unmet needs hypothesis; conflict hypothesis; relative power hypothesis

1. Introduction
The question ‘Does inequality matter?’ (OECD, 2021, p. 1) can be unambiguously answered with ‘yes’ when it comes to public concerns: across 32 OECD
countries, an average of 80% of the population agree that income differences in
their country are too large (OECD, 2021). In public perception and historical
sources, donating money and volunteering for charity are often seen as a potential remedy to the problem of growing inequality (Duquette, 2018; Mohan and
Breeze, 2016). The data, however, rarely support this narrative. Two recent reviews of the evidence on that issue show, on average, a small negative relationship
between economic inequality and civic engagement in the forms of giving and
volunteering (Schröder and Neumayr, 2023; Yang and Konrath, 2023). While
there is a lot of heterogeneity across studies, people seem to generally decrease
their level of civic engagement when inequality increases. A clear limitation of
our current understanding pointed out by these two reviews, however, is the lack
of knowledge about the potential underlying mechanisms linking inequality to
individuals’ civic engagement. Across different disciplines, numerous hypotheses have been put forward to explain the relationship (Alesina and La Ferrara,
2000; Duquette, 2018; Lancee and Van de Werfhorst, 2012; Payne and Smith,
2015; Rotolo and Wilson, 2014). To improve our understanding of why inequality affects civic engagement, we set out to test these explanations.
The purpose of this study is to empirically examine competing but also complementary hypotheses explaining the relationship between economic inequality and civic engagement and therefore identify how (through which channels)
economic inequality is linked to charitable giving and volunteering. Many of
the explanations proposed in the literature suggest that civic engagement is affected by inequality because individuals’ perceptions of inequality affect their
attitudes and values, such as their trust in others, which in turn affect their civic
engagement. However, recent literature has shown that there is sometimes little overlap between actual and perceived economic inequality (Gimpelson and
Treisman, 2018; Hauser and Norton, 2017; Kenworthy and McCall, 2008; Mijs,
2018). Peoples’ beliefs about inequality are not simply (potentially inaccurate)
reflections of actual societal inequalities; they are individually and socially constructed, based on experiences and information obtained throughout socialisation, including within the family, schools, and the labour market (Mijs, 2018).
For many of the proposed mechanisms, it is individuals’ perceptions of inequality
that are thought to be crucial for behavioural consequences. We therefore formulate the corresponding research question: What underlying mechanisms explain the relationship between perceived income inequality and individual giving
and volunteering? Our empirical analysis is based on survey data on monetary

donations to nonprofit organisations and formal volunteering by individuals in
Austria and Germany (n = 1,691). The data include information on individuals’
perceptions of income inequality in the respective countries and individual values that are assumed to be affected by perceived income inequality (generalised
social trust, perceived injustice, perceived powerlessness, and impure altruism).
On average, empirical studies show a similarly sized negative association with
economic inequality for both charitable giving and volunteering (Yang and Konrath, 2023), and underlying mechanisms have been proposed to work similarly
for both forms of civic engagement (Schröder and Neumayr, 2023). However, individual studies have found diverging results (e.g., Okten and Osili, 2004; Payne
and Smith, 2015; Uslaner and Brown, 2005), and conceptually, charitable giving
and volunteering are clearly distinct behaviours ( Jones, 2006; Lee et al., 1999).
While our hypotheses about the mechanisms are therefore almost always the
same, we analyse the link between inequality and giving and volunteering separately. In addition, we examine how perceptions of income inequality are related
to giving and volunteering for different fields of nonprofit activity, as engagement
in fields that pursue more redistributive goals, such as social services, may have a
different relationship with inequality than, for example, engagement in arts and
culture.
The main contribution of this study is the joint and systematic test of several competing and complementary hypotheses about the effect of economic
inequality on civic engagement. By bringing together hypotheses from different schools of thought, our interdisciplinary approach can provide an important
piece of the puzzle in understanding how the often observed (positive or negative) relationship between economic inequality and civic engagement might
come about. In addition, by eliciting participants’ perceived income inequality,
we go beyond the crucial but not necessarily realistic assumption implicit in most
research of a tight link between actual and perceived economic inequality.
2. Theory and hypotheses
Explanations of how economic inequality affects people’s civic engagement are
manifold and come from a variety of disciplines, including economics, political science, psychology, and sociology. In two recent studies, these explanations have been reviewed and distilled down to their main theoretical arguments
(Schröder and Neumayr, 2023; Yang and Konrath, 2023).
In principle, economic inequality may affect civic engagement through both
its material consequences and its effects on perceptions of inequality. On the
one hand, inequality may leave some individuals with less monetary resources
or social capital (e.g., associational membership, social network density) that are

necessary for civic engagement (Lancee and Van de Werfhorst, 2012; Putnam,
2000; Schröder and Neumayr, 2023). On the other hand, economic inequality may shape perceptions and subjective evaluations of the level of inequality,
which in turn determine the attitudes and values that influence civic engagement (e.g., generalised social trust). But how are these perceptions formed, and
how do they relate to actual levels of inequality? Actual levels of inequality may
translate into perceptions of income inequality through the media and personal
observations of the social environment (Gugushvili et al., 2020; Hauser and
Norton, 2017). However, personal observations are often biased by homophilous interactions and heterogeneous reference groups (Knell and Stix,
2021). In addition, individuals’ perceptions and evaluation of income inequality depend on the extent to which they perceive it as legitimate or unfair (Du
and King, 2022; Mijs, 2019). Because of these ways in which economic inequality is perceived and rationalised, the level of inequality and concerns about it do
not necessarily coincide (Gimpelson and Treisman, 2018; Hauser and Norton,
2017; Kenworthy and McCall, 2008; Mijs, 2018). For example, in both Austria and Germany, the countries under study in this paper, most people agree
or strongly agree that income differences are too large, while actual inequality
is relatively average in international comparison (OECD, 2021), although it has
increased in recent decades in Germany (Hoffmann et al., 2020).
In this paper, we aim to test four of the theoretical explanations for why perceptions of economic inequality might affect civic engagement. Next, we describe the specific mechanisms underlying each of the four hypotheses in more
detail.
2.1. Social disintegration hypothesis

The social disintegration hypothesis is the most often cited explanation in the
literature and suggests that economic inequality leads to a decline in civic engagement in a society. Based on sociological theories of social integration and
social structure (Blau, 1960; Tomeh, 1969), it argues that the unequal distribution of resources leads to status differences among people and increases social distance, social disorganisation, and segregation among them (Buttrick and
Oishi, 2017; Collins and Guidry, 2018; McVeigh, 2006). Such inequalities in
turn undermine the social integration of a community and reduce social cohesion among its members. Social disorganisation on the societal level translates
to reduced generalised social trust (GST, hereafter also referred to as “trust”) on
the individual level, as trust is more likely to develop between people who can
identify with each other and therefore likely lower in more heterogeneous groups
(e.g., Alesina and La Ferrara, 2000; Uslaner and Brown, 2005). Lower GST on

Therefore, we derive the following two hypotheses for our study:
• H1-C (charitable giving): There is a negative indirect relationship between perceived inequality and the incidence of charitable giving that is mediated by the
lack of generalised social trust of individuals.
• H1-V (volunteering): There is a negative indirect relationship between perceived inequality and the incidence of volunteering that is mediated by the lack
of generalised social trust of individuals.
2.2. Relative power hypothesis

According to the relative power hypothesis, the link between economic inequality and civic engagement is because an unequal distribution of resources leads
to an unequal distribution of power between people. It postulates that inequality in resources also gives wealthier people more power, which they use to influence political and civic affairs in their favour. In this way, they can also prevent
issues relevant to less affluent people from becoming part of the public debate
(Collins and Guidry, 2018; Filetti and Janmaat, 2018; Solt, 2008; Uslaner and
Brown, 2005). This makes it very difficult for less affluent people to make their
voices heard. Their confidence in the law and political institutions diminishes,
and they feel that they are at the mercy of others and no longer have control over
many things in their lives, increasing their sense of powerlessness (Uslaner and
Brown, 2005). Finally, they realise that the political system is incapable of defending their interest and decide to abandon their civic engagement (Filetti and
Janmaat, 2018), so ‘as not to waste their time and energy on an unfair system’
(Godfrey and Cherng, 2016, p. 2221). The relative power hypothesis thus postulates a negative effect of inequality on civic engagement, mediated by individuals’
level of perceived powerlessness. Our hypotheses therefore are
• H2-C (charitable giving): There is a negative indirect relationship between perceived inequality and the incidence of charitable giving that is mediated by the
perceived powerlessness of individuals.
• H2-V (volunteering): There is a negative indirect relationship between perceived inequality and the incidence of volunteering that is mediated by the perceived powerlessness of individuals.
2.3. Conflict hypothesis

The conflict hypothesis assumes that economic inequality leads to an increase
in civic engagement because inequality gives rise to class conflicts and a sense of

injustice among the population (Fieldhouse and Cutts, 2010; Gurr, 1970;
Karakoc, 2013; McVeigh, 2006; Meltzer and Richard, 1981; Oliver, 1999). As
economic inequality increases, affluent and less affluent citizens develop different policy preferences in many respects, such as toward redistributive measures.
While these become more expensive for the wealthy, they become more attractive to the have-nots, placing these groups in distributional conflict (McVeigh,
2006; Solt, 2008). Such an increase in divergent policy preferences creates a
sense of injustice among the population and, in particular, encourages the havenots to organise (e.g., in a nonprofit) in opposition of policies that favour the
wealthy with the aim of bringing about social change (Duncan, 2010; Karakoc,
2013; Solt, 2008). At the same time, it encourages the wealthy to express their
opposition, such as to redistributive measures. Thus, the conflict hypothesis
proposes that a rise in economic inequality specifically promotes activist forms
of engagement that ‘draw together individuals with common interests and
place them in confrontation with the state or with other opponents in society’
(McVeigh, 2006, p. 515). We therefore propose two effects: first, that higher levels of inequality have a positive indirect effect on civic engagement through individuals’ perceived injustice, and second, that people get particularly committed
to causes that aim to bring about social change, which we label “activist causes.”
• H3-C (charitable giving): There is a positive indirect relationship between perceived inequality and the incidence of charitable giving that is mediated by the
perceived injustice of individuals.
• H3-V (volunteering): There is a positive indirect relationship between perceived
inequality and the incidence of volunteering that is mediated by the perceived
injustice of individuals.
• H3a-C (charitable giving): The higher the perceived income inequality, the
higher the incidence of charitable giving for activist causes.
• H3a-V (volunteering): The higher the perceived income inequality, the higher
the incidence of volunteering for activist causes.
2.4. Unmet needs hypothesis

The unmet needs hypothesis1 is rooted in economic thinking and postulates a
positive correlation between inequality and civic engagement that is explained
by the impure altruism of individuals (Andreoni, 1990; Batson and Shaw, 1991;
Duquette and Hargaden, 2021; Mastromatteo and Russo, 2017; Schröder and
Neumayr, 2023). It argues that an increase in inequality tends to make unmet

needs in society more pronounced and visible, as resources are concentrated
among a privileged few to the detriment of the broader population (Yang and
Konrath, 2023). As rising perceived inequality increases the perceived number
of people in need and the demand for public goods, it may activate individuals’
impurely altruistic motivation (Batson and Shaw, 1991). The concept of impure
altruism consists of two components. First, it is defined as the selfless concern for
the well-being of others, which makes people engage because they derive benefits
from contributing to the well-being of the recipient or, more generally, the public
good. Second, the ‘warm glow’ makes people engage because of their own wellbeing, as they earn a ‘joy of giving’ utility from the act of helping itself (Andreoni,
1990; Mastromatteo and Russo, 2017). Since impure altruism is among the main
mechanisms driving civic engagement (Bekkers and Wiepking, 2011a), higher
perceptions of inequality that activate impure altruism are suggested to increase
levels of engagement. While this mechanism has been primarily used to explain
an effect of economic inequality on charitable giving, it has also been applied to
volunteering (Okten and Osili, 2004). We therefore hypothesise
• H4-C (charitable giving): There is a positive indirect relationship between perceived inequality and the incidence of charitable giving that is mediated by the
impure altruism of individuals.
• H4-V (volunteering): There is a positive indirect relationship between perceived
inequality and the incidence of volunteering that is mediated by the impure altruism of individuals.
For both charitable giving and volunteering, the unmet needs hypothesis would
predict that higher levels of inequality lead people to most strongly support
nonprofit organisations that serve redistributive purposes, such as social services (Duquette, 2018; Nair, 2018; Yang, 2022). Therefore, we formulate further,
more specific hypotheses:
• H4a-C (charitable giving): The higher the perceived income inequality, the
higher the incidence of charitable giving for redistributive causes.
• H4a-V (volunteering): The higher the perceived income inequality, the higher
the incidence of volunteering for redistributive causes.
• H4b-C (charitable giving): There is a positive indirect relationship between perceived inequality and the incidence of charitable giving for redistributive causes
that is mediated by the impure altruism of individuals.
• H4b-V (volunteering): There is a positive indirect relationship between perceived inequality and the incidence of volunteering for redistributive causes that
is mediated by the impure altruism of individuals.

Of the four mechanisms hypothesised to translate inequality into civic engagement, it is unlikely that only one will operate. Rather, they are likely to operate simultaneously, which can cause their effects to cancel out, mitigate, or
amplify each other. To our knowledge, these mechanisms have never been studied together, which means that no theoretical basis exists for saying whether
the overall relationship between perceptions of income inequality and civic engagement is positive or negative. Our final hypothesis about the total effect of
perceived income inequality on civic engagement is therefore based on empirical results. As noted, study results have a large heterogeneity, observing positive, negative, and no associations. In aggregate, the relationship between income
inequality and the incidence and amount of charitable giving and volunteering
is weakly negative (Schröder and Neumayr, 2023; Yang and Konrath, 2023).
Therefore, our last set of hypotheses reads
• H5 (charitable giving): The higher the perceived income inequality, the lower
the incidence of charitable giving.
• H5 (volunteering): The higher the perceived income inequality, the lower the
incidence of volunteering.

3.2. Independent variables

Perception of income inequality: Perceived income inequality was measured using
participants’ responses to the statement ‘I think that the income differences in
Austria/Germany are too large’ on a four-point scale ranging from ‘completely
agree’ (1) to ‘completely disagree’ (4), adapted from the International Social
Survey Programme (ISSP) question ‘Differences in income in [country] are too
large’ (ISSP Research Group, 2021). This question is commonly used to measure perceptions of income inequality (Kenworthy and McCall, 2008; OECD,
2021) and combines a positive and normative measure of perceived inequality:
it captures participants’ perception of the tension between the actual extent of
inequality and the assessment of how high it should be. We reverse coded the
variable such that higher values reflect higher perceived income inequality. The
mean value was 3.3.
3.3. Mediator variables

opinion, to what extent is it generally possible to trust people?’ on a seven-point
scale ranging from ‘People cannot generally be trusted’ (1) to ‘Most people can

3.5. Statistical analysis

We first use confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) to assess the reliability of multiitem measures and obtain factor scores of latent variables using the regression
method (Rosseel, 2012; Skrondal and Laake, 2001). We use these predicted
factor scores in the mediation analyses, since causal mediation analysis with latent variables is not possible using free and open-source software (Valente et al.,
2020). The CFA includes the multi-item scales for the latent variables impure
altruism and perceived powerlessness, which are measured by the items given in
Table 2. All CFA models are estimated using full information maximum likelihood, and missing values are therefore treated as missing at random.
For the mediated relationships, we use causal mediation analysis following
the approach developed by Imai et al. (2010), which is implemented in the R
package ‘mediation’ (Tingley et al., 2014). The main advantage of causal mediation analysis for our setting is that it provides unbiased estimates of indirect effects for nonlinear models. In addition, it clarifies the assumptions necessary to
interpret the findings on indirect relationships as causal effects. For our study,
these assumptions are no unmeasured confounding of the relationship between

5. Discussion
While the effect of economic inequality on individuals’ civic engagement in
terms of giving and volunteering has frequently been studied empirically, the underlying mechanisms that cause and may thus explain this influence have hardly
been examined. We tested various competing and complementing hypotheses
about how perceived income inequality is related to civic engagement.

Our findings provide evidence for two mechanisms proposed in previous
studies. First, our results indicate that the link between perceived income inequality and both charitable giving and volunteering is mediated by impure altruism, as proposed by the unmet needs hypothesis. We find a clear positive relationship between perceived income inequality and individuals’ impure altruism,
which in turn is positively related to charitable giving and volunteering. This indirect relationship is substantial in size, with an eight (seven) percentage point
higher probability of giving to charity (volunteering) due to the indirect relationship through impure altruism. This mechanism is further supported by our
results on civic engagement for redistributive causes (i.e., for nonprofit organisations active in the field of social services and international aid). For these causes,
we also observe a positive indirect relationship through people’s impure altruism.
In addition, the relative increase in the probability of giving and volunteering due
to the indirect relationship through impure altruism is larger for redistributive
causes than the indirect relationship for all causes.
A second explanation for the relationship between inequality and civic engagement supported by our data is the social disintegration hypothesis. Higher
levels of perceived income inequality are related to lower GST among individuals, which in turn is related to lower engagement. The indirect negative relationship through lower GST, however, was rather weak, accounting for only about a
one percentage point difference in the probability of charitable giving and volunteering between those who completely agree and those who completely disagree
that income inequality is too high.
In contrast, the results for the other two mechanisms tested are not in line
with our hypotheses. We did not find support for the relative power hypothesis,
which states that higher perceived income inequality decreases civic engagement
due to its positive effect on perceived powerlessness. The estimated indirect relationship is very small and not statistically significant.
As for the conflict hypothesis, our results challenge its applicability for
explaining the relationship between inequality and charitable giving and volunteering. It argues that inequality fuels a sense of injustice among the population,
which increases civic engagement, particularly for activist causes. For volunteering, we do not find a substantial indirect relationship through perceived
injustice. For charitable giving, we instead find a substantial negative indirect
relationship indicating a seven percentage point lower probability of charitable
giving due to this mechanism, which is the opposite of our expectations. While
perceived income inequality is positively related to perceived injustice, people
with greater perceived injustice are less rather than more likely to donate, even
when accounting for individual income. Perceived injustice therefore associated
with resignation rather than a desire to make a difference, a result that we

expected to find when examining perceived powerlessness rather than perceived
injustice. Interestingly, we did find support for the empirical prediction derived
from the conflict hypothesis that perceived inequality increases charitable giving
to activist causes (i.e., human rights and advocacy). This is consistent with the
literature, which mentions the conflict hypothesis mainly in the context of
activism. However, it is mainly used to explain volunteering or even political
engagement (rather than charitable giving) for activist causes (Karakoc, 2013),
while we find it for charitable giving but not volunteering. Yet, this finding
could not be attributed to an indirect relationship through perceived injustice
in exploratory analysis. Our study therefore supports an empirical prediction
derived from the conflict hypothesis for charitable giving but not the explanation proposed by the conflict hypothesis that this association is mediated by
individuals perceived injustice.
Taken together, our results imply that several of the previously hypothesised
pathways linking (perceived) inequality to civic engagement seem to hold explanatory value and partly work in opposing directions. Estimating the total association between perceived income inequality and civic engagement, we did not
find a significant association with charitable giving or volunteering. In the context of results on the underlying mechanisms, it seems that these thus cancel each
other out. For example, the observed positive indirect relationship through impure altruism postulated by the unmet needs hypothesis seems to be partially
offset by the negative indirect relationship though GST postulated by the social disintegration hypothesis. One explanation for this observation may be that
the positive relationship between impure altruism and charitable giving and volunteering depends on the extent to which potential beneficiaries are perceived
as in-group members (Balliet et al., 2014; Batson and Shaw, 1991; Nair, 2018).
Since people prefer to give to members of their in-group, the mechanisms proposed by the unmet needs hypothesis may only come into play if inequality is
not accompanied by social segregation. For charitable giving, the positive indirect relationship seems to be additionally offset by the unexpected indirect
negative relationship through perceived injustice. For volunteering, it is likely
that additional, unknown mechanisms determine the total association. Yang and
Konrath (2023) suggest two potentially distinct additional mechanisms through
which higher perceived income inequality may exert a negative effect on civic engagement, namely, status anxiety and perceived increased government spending
(crowding-out hypothesis), which should be taken into account in future studies. Another promising direction of research for future studies with larger sample sizes is the analysis of moderated mediation to detect to what extent these
mechanisms operate for different groups of people, such as differentiated by their
income.

Adding to a body of empirical literature showing mixed results on the association between economic inequality and civic engagement, our results reveal an
overall null-effect of perceived income inequality on giving and volunteering in
Austria and Germany. Given this finding and that almost 80% of the population
in Austria and Germany—and similar proportions in other OECD countries—
agree or completely agree that income inequality in their country is too large,
there seems limited potential for public policy to use increased awareness of
this issue to increase private civic engagement. Though many people perceive
inequality in their countries as too high, these perceptions are not associated
with a higher likelihood of donating money or volunteering for a nonprofit
organisation.
However, some limitations need to be considered when interpreting the
results of this study. The first limitation is its observational design, which means
that the estimated relationships should not be considered causal effects. Our
sensitivity analysis shows that the indirect relationship through GST is relatively sensitive to potential omitted confounders between the mediator and
giving/volunteering, while the influence of omitted confounders would have
to be fairly sizeable for the indirect relationship through impure altruism to no
longer hold. The results of this study should therefore be interpreted as first
evidence on the plausibility of different mechanisms being at work. Future
studies using a causal design should be conducted to validate our findings, such
as by conducting a survey experiment where one group is exposed to an ‘informational shock’ about the level of income inequality in their country (versus no
information in the control group). Such a study could also provide insights into
the malleability of the different mediators in response to changing perceptions of
inequality when they are measured with a similar degree of specificity in relation
to perceptions of income inequality. Another limitation, as mentioned, is that
the variable measuring perceived powerlessness, one out of our four mediator
variables, does not have satisfactory internal reliability, resulting in reduced
statistical power for a test of the mediated relationship. In this respect, another
scale to measure perceived powerlessness should be used in future studies. In addition, our results are based on individuals’ perceived income inequality, which
is measured with a single item. Although this measure is established in the field,
a differentiated analysis of the effects of a positive and normative assessment of
inequality would be desirable. A final limitation of our study is generalisability,
since results are based on data from Austria and Germany, two countries with
similar and moderate levels of perceived income inequality by international
comparison (OECD, 2021). Absolute levels of income inequality are rather low
in both countries, and we did not find significant differences in the total association between perceived income inequality and giving or volunteering between

these countries (see supplementary material sections 1.1 and 1.2). While the
literature does not provide strong arguments that mechanisms should depend
on the absolute level of inequality, it might be fruitful to test to what extent the
findings are similar when using a larger and more diverse set of countries.
In conclusion, our interdisciplinary study advances the literature by providing a comprehensive and systematic examination of mechanisms that explain the
relationship between perceived income inequality and civic engagement. It provides evidence for the various ways in which perceived income inequality relates
to people’s likelihood of charitable giving and volunteering, demonstrating that
this relationship is mediated by individuals’ impure altruism and GST. While our
study covers a broad range of explanatory mechanisms, there are likely additional
consequences of the actual (as opposed to perceived) distribution of resources.
For example, the resource hypothesis argues that the unequal distribution of resources leaves a larger proportion of the population with little opportunity to
engage (Lancee and Van de Werfhorst, 2012; Schröder and Neumayr, 2023).
Since actual and perceived inequality do not necessarily go hand in hand, our
findings do not necessarily generalise to the consequences of actual inequality.
Studies should therefore look at both the actual and perceived levels of inequality to move toward a holistic understanding of the relationship between it and
civic engagement, which is highly relevant when (perceived) inequality in many
countries is increasing.
