Abstract
Purpose – This paper aims to examine consumers’ non-profit brand image, brand typicality and past behaviour as determinants of intention to
donate to two children charity brands.
Design/methodology/approach – Data for this study were obtained from two separate studies via a questionnaire, both in the context of two
children charities, one for Barnardo’s and the other for BBC Children in Need charity. A theoretical model is developed, tested and compared across
the two charity brands.
Findings – Findings highlight that different factors influence intentions to donate time and money according to the charity brand. Brand typicality
is a key determinant of time donations, while the impact of non-profit brand image dimensions on time and money donations differs across the two
charities. Past behaviour affects intentions to donate money in both charities but impacts time donations in only one of the two charities
investigated.
Research limitations/implications – The study examines specific dimensions of non-profit brand image across two different charity brands and
offers theoretical insights about the value of brand image in a non-for profit context in shaping consumer outcomes (i.e. consumer intentions to
donate).
Originality/value – The study sheds further light into the notion of typicality put forward by Michel and Rieunier (2012) for two children’s charity
brands that differ in terms of their strength and income levels and examined past behaviour as a determinant to donate to charity brands.
Keywords Brand image, Intention to donate, Past behaviour, Typicality

differentiate themselves from other charitable organisations.

To address the role of branding in the non-profit sector, this
paper focusses on non-profit brand image, brand typicality
and past behaviour as predictors of the intention to donate to
two children’s charity brands; Barnardo’s, (hereafter referred
to as “B’s”), and BBC Children in Need, (hereafter referred to
as “CN”). A theoretical model is derived and tested
empirically for the two different charity brands, using two
different data sets, via structural equation modelling. The
findings highlight the role of non-profit branding, brand
typicality and past behaviour in charitable donations and
contribute to an understanding of these key determinants for
non-profit brands, in their attempt to increase charitable
donations. More importantly, this research highlights the
effects of four dimensions of non-profit brand image, (i.e.
affect, efficiency, dynamism and usefulness), on charitable
donations, examines and establishes the mediating role of
typicality, as well as the importance of past behaviour in
donating money and time to children’s charity brands. By
comparing two charity brands that belong to the same sector,
albeit highly differentiated in terms of their financial situation,
we show how key, non-profit, brand-related dimensions, such
as affect and usefulness, translate into behavioural outcomes,
such as charitable donations. Similarly, the study sheds further
light onto the concept of “typicality” as put forward by Michel
and Rieunier (2012), in directly and indirectly impacting upon
intentions to donate to charity brands. Typicality emphasises
that certain characteristics which relate to a given brand affect
an individual’s perceptions of the representativeness of that
specific brand, indicating that typicality interacts with the
non-profit brand image to influence key consumer outcomes,
in this case, intentions. Findings thus highlight the variable
weight of the key determinants of the intention to donate
money and time between the two charity brands, resulting in
interesting conclusions based upon the usefulness of the
non-profit brand image and typicality in shaping donations,
with significant practical implications for the two charity
brands. Lastly, an additional contribution of the study derives
from the study’s research design and the use of two separate
data sets, each capturing a separate charity brand, (i.e. B’s and
CN), which enables an empirical comparison of the
theoretical model and the factors which determine intentions
to donate across the two charity brands. The paper starts with
a literature review of the key and relevant concepts, leading to
the development of a theoretical model and associated
hypotheses. The research design and methods are then
presented and a discussion of the analysis and findings follows.
The paper ends with some concluding remarks, highlighting
the key contributions of this study for theory and practice.

Non-profit brand image
A positive brand image for a charity is essential, considering
that a unique, strong and favourable brand image permits the
brand and its “meaning” to achieve positive differentiation in
the minds of consumers which, in turn, contributes to the
potential for enhanced brand equity and to the acquirement of
new and returning donors (Pitta and Katsanis, 1995).
Effective management of a brand image may reveal a positive
impact upon charities’ income generation, as was the case in
several of the UK’s high profile charities in the 1990s, which
experienced an income increase of 10 per cent per annum
soon after implementing changes (Denney, 1999; Kennedy,
1998). Scarce empirical results corroborate the positive
impact that a favourable non-profit brand image has on the
number of donations (Bennett and Gabriel, 2003; Michel and
Rieunier, 2012). Despite the argued importance of the
non-profit brand image and the uniqueness of the non-profit
sector (Bennett and Sargeant, 2005; Sargeant, 1999; Saxton,
1995), little research has been dedicated to the concept of a
non-profit brand image (Bennett and Gabriel, 2003; Michel
and Rieunier, 2012). The conceptualisation of a non-profit
brand image has been largely informed by literature on
commercial branding and, in particular, from brand
personality measures. For example, Aaker’s five brand

Past donations
It is argued that past behaviour acts as a risk reducer, leading
to a situation where a positive experience in the past will most
likely lead to repeated behaviour, as the perceived risk
associated with such an action is far less in such instances
(Sargeant et al., 2008). In the context of charity donation, past
donation behaviour is shown to be an essential determinant of
future donation (Cheung and Chan, 2000; Diamond and
Kashyap, 1997; Ouellette and Wood, 1998; Sutton, 1994).
Thus, more favourable intentions are likely to be developed
towards the acts performed frequently in the past (Ouellette
and Wood, 1998). Similarly, Lee et al. (1999) also argue that
past behaviour is a significant predictor of the intention to
donate, suggesting that repeated or habitual actions will most
likely result in an increase in donations (Camic, 1986). Hence:

Brand typicality
The notion indicating how representative an object is of a
certain category is known as typicality (Nedungadi and
Hutchinson, 1985). “Typical” products or brands are
perceived as having higher value attributes, benefiting from
first recall. Typical products are also classified in a more
accurate and speedy manner (Ward and Loken, 1988) and can
be used as reference points, in comparison with less typical
ones. Previous research highlights the positive relationship
between typicality and brand preference (Ward and Loken,
1988); however, the authors assert a word of caution, implying
that typicality effects on preference and comparison may not
be able to be generalised across all product categories and
should be carefully evaluated before the connection is applied
to a specific product category (Ward and Loken, 1988).
Within the charity sector, typicality is proven to have a
significant impact on the intention to donate money and time
(Michel and Rieunier, 2012). Determinants of a charity’s
typicality, (i.e. being a representative brand in its product
category, e.g. B’s as a children’s charity), involve how its
mission category compares to others in the same group, the
extent to which it meets the public’s “ideal” charity traits and
the extent to which the public is exposed to the particular
charity (Loken and Ward, 1990). In line with previous
research, which adopts Rosch’s (1973) theory of

Context and sample
The model was tested on two charity brands within the same
charitable sector which allows for a comparison of the results.
We focussed on the child and youth charity sector. This sector
had been selected following a pre-test, which sought for a
strong “spontaneous recognition” of charities among
respondents. Respondents were asked to name the charitable
sector that first came into their mind. The results of the
pre-test showed that the child and youth charity sector was the
most recognisable charity sector among respondents.
Consequently, we decided to include two charities from the
child and youth sector, including B’s and the CN Appeal. The
reason for choosing these two children’s charity brands was
guided by their contrasting income levels; B’s with an excess of
£244 million and CN, with an excess of £40.20 million

Theoretical model

Measures
A questionnaire was prepared, containing four sections.
Existing measures were used to capture all the predictors of
the intention to donate. In particular, the first section involved
a measurement for a non-profit brand image, derived from
Michel and Rieunier (2012) using a scale of 14 items,
capturing four dimensions, namely, usefulness, efficiency,
affect and dynamism. We used this measure of a non-profit
brand image as compared to others (Sargeant et al., 2008;
Venable et al., 2005), as it is the latest, grounded on scholarly
research and has a good predictive validity. The following
section of the questionnaire addressed brand typicality of the
charity, which was measured in line with Rosch and Mervis
(1975). In particular, respondents were asked to rate the
following: “[Non-profit organisation X] is a representative
brand of the Children and Youth Charity sector”, and
“[Non-profit organisation X] is a good brand example of the
Children and Youth Charity sector”. The third section
included measures of previous donations as well as their
intentions to donate time and money. Previous donation was
measured by a single item, whilst the intention to donate time
and money was based on the Michel and Rieunier’s (2012)
study and involved questions such as: “If I were to contribute
some money to a non-profit organisation, I would contribute
to X‘ and ’If I were to contribute some personal time to a
non-profit organisation, I would contribute to X”. Particular
attention was paid at this point, so that the intentions to
donate time and money were recorded separately, as
volunteering in time is believed to require more involvement
and, therefore, may generate different results from donating

Discussion and implications
This study builds on previous research by exploring the role of
non-profit brand image dimensions, typicality and past donor
behaviour on consumers’ intentions to donate time and
money in two different contexts: a “weaker charity” brand
(based on the Charity Brand Index) that generates high
income (i.e. B’s) and a stronger brand that generates lower
income compared to the former brand (i.e. CN). The study
provides some insights into the factors that affect donations
and illustrate the key aspects that can increase donations to
charity. The theoretical model developed is strong in terms of
explanatory power and explains a high proportion of variance
for both time and money donations in both contexts; 87 per
cent for time donations and 70 per cent for money donations
for B’s brand and almost 98 per cent for time and 74 per cent
for money donations for CN. This research also allows for
comparisons to be made between a stronger and a weaker
charity brand. Our results show that B’s is perceived as a more
typical charity brand (mean ⫽ 5.29, SD ⫽ 1.017) than CN
(mean ⫽ 3.93, SD ⫽ 0.806). Brand typicality is found to play
a key role in the intention to donate time in both contexts.
Interestingly, although B’s is perceived as a more typical
children’s charity brand, the impact of brand typicality on
time donations is greater for CN. Our findings corroborate
previous research, which highlights that brand typicality is a
significant determinant of time donations (Michel and
Rieunier, 2012). However, our study differs from that of
Michel and Rieunier (2012) in that we examine the mediating
role of brand typicality in charitable donations in money and
time between the two charity brands. Contrary to time
donations, the direct effect of brand typicality on money
donations seems to depend on the brand investigated.
Specifically, our results illustrate that for a weaker brand, such
as B’s (compared to CN) which nevertheless generates a
higher income, typicality does not play an important role in a
consumer’s intention to donate money. The results of this
study suggest that brand typicality has a direct impact on time
donations in both contexts and money donations (in CN).
The mediating role of typicality is only partially supported in
one of the two contexts investigated. Interestingly, our results
indicate that none of the non-profit brand image factors
explain money donations for B’s. In this context, only past
donations seem to influence money donations. Other factors,
such as subjective norms (Ajzen, 1991) may, however, play a
key role in the case of a more established charity brand that
benefits from greater exposure to the public.
Brand typicality seems to be influenced by all the non-profit
brand image dimensions in the case of B’s but only by
usefulness and dynamism in the case of CN. The additional
affective factor that influences brand typicality for B’s can be

Managerial implications
This research has a number of managerial implications. First,
the results highlight the important role of brand typicality
in the intention to donate both time and money. Brand
managers need to work on establishing typicality for their
brands and this can be achieved by first understanding the
public’s ideal charity brand traits and trying to match these.
Charities can also increase the exposure of their brand to the
public and invest more in campaigns and events, in order for
them to enjoy first recall. Second, brand managers are advised
to work closely on their brand image and, in particular, the
affective and dynamic elements to increase time donations.
Time donations, being a more emotional process, are more
difficult to stimulate and, therefore, this is a strategy that could

Limitations and future research
This study focusses on one charity sector, that of children.
Future research should address other charity sectors and
charity brands to understand whether factors affecting the
intention to donate differ, based upon the sector in which a
charity brand operates. In addition, future research could also
investigate the effect of certain rewards or motivations on
intentions to donate time and money. Previous research
suggests that attitudes and behaviour towards charitable
giving are shaped by different motivations (Kottasz, 2004). In
line with the theory of planned behaviour (Ajzen, 1991), the
role of subjective norms could improve the explanatory power
of the model tested in this study and, therefore, could be
included in future research to gain more insights into the role
of significant others in the intention to donate. Extant
evidence suggests that factors such as conformity with family
wishes (Cermak et al., 1994) or family utility (Sargeant et al.,
2006) play a key role in charity giving. This research
investigates the role of brand typicality as a mediator between
the non-profit brand image and the intention to donate. Other
empirical research (Pillai and Goldsmith, 2008) suggests that
brand typicality plays a moderating role in effects on brand
attitudes. Following on from such evidence, future research
could examine the role of brand typicality as a moderator. On
the methodological front, this study uses a shorter version of
an established scale, measuring non-profit brand image
(Michel and Rieunier, 2012). Future research should focus on
the scale and provide evidence that supports its validity in
different non-profit contexts. Finally, previous research
highlights that there could be a gap between the intention and
actual behaviour in that intention is not always a good predictor
of actual behaviour (Carrington et al., 2010; Fukukawa, 2003).
Thus, future research could investigate actual giving behaviour to
enhance knowledge and practice in this area.

About the authors

Milena Micevski is a Doctoral Candidate and a Research
Group Officer at the School of Business and Economics,
Loughborough University. Her key research areas involve
consumer behaviour within the context of social media, issues
in the area of marketing and sales strategy, as well as
applications of sociological and psychological theories to the
understanding of intergroup and interdepartmental
relationships within organisations.

Nina Michaelidou is a Reader in Marketing at the School of
Business and Economics, Loughborough University. Her
research interests encompass consumer behaviour including
decision-making, personality traits, attitudes and emotions,
consumer involvement and engagement as well as brand
image and social media marketing. She is the leader of the
Academy of Marketing Special Interest Group on Consumer
Research and has published papers in various journals
including Journal of Marketing Management, European Journal
of Marketing, Industrial Marketing Management, Journal of
Strategic Marketing, Journal of Business Research, Journal of
Travel Research and Journal of Consumer Affairs and Food
Policy. Nina Michaelidou is the corresponding author and can
be contacted at: n.michaelidou@lboro.ac.uk
