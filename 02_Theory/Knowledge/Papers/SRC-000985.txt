Charitable Giving: What Influences Donors’ Choice Among
Different Causes?
Michaela Neumayr1 • Femida Handy2

Published online: 1 March 2017
Ó International Society for Third-Sector Research and The Johns Hopkins University 2019

Abstract While the literature is replete with studies that
identify factors explaining why people are likely to make
monetary contributions, less is known about which particular charitable causes they are likely to choose and how
much they donate to them. This article examines donor
choice among eight different causes using survey data
collected in 2011 for a nationally representative sample in
Austria. In particular, the study investigates the role of
individual-level factors: subjective dispositions such as
empathic concern, trust, and religiosity, and resources such
as education and income. We find that subjective dispositions rather predict a donor’s incidence of giving among
causes but not the amount donated. Human resources, in
contrast, are associated with both the incidence and the
amount donated to particular causes, and they also mediate
the impact of subjective dispositions. What is more, the
study reveals that being asked to donate has the highest
explanatory power regarding the incidence of giving
among all causes investigated.

Introduction
Charitable giving is a ubiquitous global phenomenon
(Wiepking and Handy 2015), and this naturally raises
questions like: Why do people give away their money, and
when they do, what influences their choice of what causes
to support? While the extant literature is replete with
studies that examine why people make contributions of
money, less is known about the factors that predispose an
individual’s choice to the particular causes. The literature
survey undertaken by Bekkers and Wiepking (2011),
across various disciplines and methodologies, reveals eight
mechanisms that explain donors’ decisions to give. They
include the awareness of need by the donor, being solicited
for donations, and donors’ reputational concerns and values
(Bekkers and Wiepking 2011). While these mechanisms
may explain why individuals donate, they do not explain
donors’ choices among the various causes. For example, an
individual might be motivated by his/her values to make a
charitable donation; however, what influences the individual’s choice among a plethora of causes seeking funds?
In this paper, we examine this underexplored question
using a nationally representative sample from Austria and
investigate factors that are likely to predict which causes
people donate to and the amount donated.
While this study primarily draws on literature that has
identified mechanisms that predict charitable giving, it also
includes studies that have established human resources and
subjective dispositions–such as personality traits, attitudes
and values– that may impact donations (Bekkers 2006;
Einolf 2011; Wilson 2012). The principal focus of this

Moreover, we examine the influence of both individual

whether the effects of subjective dispositions are mediated

Review of the Literature on Donors’ Choice
We first examine the research addressing donors’ giving to
various causes, followed by the literature on the determinants of donor choice. From this, we derive hypotheses
related to (1) subjective dispositions and (2) resources.
Overview of Studies on Donor Choice
A large number of studies investigate the factors that affect
giving among one or two types of charities in a single
study. Most frequently, these studies analyze giving to
secular and religious causes (e.g., Helms and Thornton
2012) or explore giving to international and national causes
(e.g., Rajan et al. 2008). Other studies concentrate on a
single cause (e.g., arts and culture, see Barnes 2011) or
focus on a particular factors’ impact on donor choice, such
as income (e.g., Breeze and Lloyd 2013; Ostrower 1997) or
gender (e.g., Andreoni et al. 2003; Brown and Ferris 2007;
Piper and Schnepf 2008; Rooney et al. 2007).
Few empirical studies investigate factors influencing
donor choice between more than two causes in a single
study. Among them is an article of Casale and Baumann
(2015) employing data from the USA, the studies of
Micklewright and Schnepf (2009), Sargeant et al. (2008),
Kottasz (2004), and Bennett (2003), all of them referring to
data from the UK, and the work of Wiepking (2010) and
Srnka et al. (2003), both from continental Europe (see
Table 1).
While all of them investigate determinants of donor
choice across at least three causes, donor choice is often
reduced to the incidence, while the amount donated is not
considered, a serious limitation in the study of donor
choice. Some of them solely examine donations for

investigated English-speaking countries and non-representative samples.
Determinants of Donor Choice and Hypotheses
The large disciplinary and interdisciplinary literature on the
determinants of prosocial behavior in general and giving in
particular is surveyed in noteworthy exhaustive literature
reviews by Bekkers and Wiepking (2011), Wiepking and
Bekkers (2012b) and Wilson (2012). It finds that psychological theories tend to focus on subjective dispositions
such as personality traits, attitudes, values, norms, selfconcepts, and motivations in order to explain prosocial
behavior, while sociological theories emphasize individual
socio-demographic characteristics and human resources
(Wilson 2012, p. 178). Scholars unanimously suggest to
combine insights from various disciplines when studying
the determinants of charitable behavior in order to generate
new knowledge and not replicate findings from single
disciplines (Wiepking and Bekkers 2012b).
Drawing on previous research that explored whether
individual resources compensate or moderate subjective
dispositions’ impact on charitable giving (Bekkers 2006;
Einolf 2011), we include both resources and subjective
dispositions to explain donor choice. We next present,
based on the literature, how (1) subjective dispositions and
(2) human resources affect donor choice. The term subjective disposition comprises a wide range of concepts,
including personality traits, motives, attitudes, norms, and
values which all refer ‘to the way people interpret themselves and the world around them’ (Wilson 2012, p. 179).
In our study, the subjective dispositions used are generalized trust, empathic concern and religiosity. Human
resources generally include the individual assets that may
impact giving such as education and income.

Empathic Concern
It is intuitively plausible that individuals who score higher
on the empathic concern scale (i.e., responding to another
person’s situation with feelings that resemble the experience of the other) understand the needs of others better than
those who do not score that high and thus are more likely to
show prosocial behavior (Wiepking and Maas 2009; Wilson 2012). Indeed, empirical studies reveal that empathic
concern is positively related to charitable giving (Bekkers
and Wiepking 2011; Bennett 2003). Bekkers (2006), for
instance, finds a positive relationship between empathic
concern and generosity, with higher empathy related to
higher total donations. The ability to empathize with others
matters particularly when the beneficiaries are vulnerable
and need some sort of protection (Webb and Khoo 2010,
p. 2) or are suffering (Verhaert and Van den Poel 2011,
p. 1293). This suggests that empathic donors are more
likely to be attracted by causes that activate empathy such
as international relief. Cheung and Chan (2000) found
empathy to be related to the incidence of giving to international causes. Bennett (2003), in his study of donor

concern was related to the incidence of giving for cancer

donor choice, we hypothesize:

Domestic Relief, International Relief, Social Services,
Health and Animal Welfare.
Religiosity
Individual level of religiosity, regardless of whether indicated by religious affiliation or religious service attendance, is a significant predictor of the likelihood and the
level of giving (Bekkers and Wiepking 2011). The mechanisms involved in this relationship differ for the two
indicators of religiosity. Religious affiliated people give
because they are taught it is their duty to help and because
they may have stronger prosocial values compared to not
religious affiliated individuals. People who regularly attend
religious services are—in addition of having stronger
prosocial values—more strongly involved in the religious
community; they give because they face a stronger social
norm on giving and higher levels of social pressure to
conform to group standards (Bekkers and Wiepking 2011).
Moreover, religious service attendance brings people into
opportunities to engage in promoting the collective good as
they receive more solicitations (at religious service) to
contribute to religious and secular causes the congregation
supports (Brooks 2003; Brown and Ferris 2007). Empirical
studies find religious affiliation and attendance at religious
services positively related to religious giving and also
secular giving (Bekkers and Wiepking 2011). For example,
Wiepking (2010) finds that religious organizations have an
increased likelihood of receiving donations from religiously affiliated people, while Casale and Baumann
(2015) find that religious attendance is a significant predictor of religious giving as well as giving to international
causes. The latter is due to ‘religious institutions taking up
special collections for international programs’ (p. 115).
Taking account for the different mechanisms at work for
religious affiliation and religious attendance, we formulate
two hypotheses:
Hypothesis 3a Religious Affiliation is positively related
to the incidence of giving and the amount donated for
Religious Causes.
Hypothesis 3b Religious Attendance is positively related
to the incidence of giving and the amount donated for
Religious Causes and International Relief.

Human Resources: Education and Income
Education
The level of formal education is one of the main predictors
of charitable giving, and it is positively related to the
inclination to give and the amount donated (Bekkers and
Wiepking 2011). This is because of values, as education
fosters prosocial motivation, and because education brings
people into social networks, entailing higher levels of
solicitations (Bekkers and Wiepking 2011; Brown and
Ferris 2007). Also, it is argued that people with higher
levels of education have higher cognitive abilities, which
make them ‘better able to understand’ complex situations
and therefore the needs of distant others (Wiepking and
Maas 2009, p. 1977). In addition, higher levels of education are assumed to increase the degree of abstract thinking, which is necessary for making charitable donations to
less concrete causes without quantifiable goals (Wiepking
and Maas 2009, p. 1978), such as international relief or
environmental issues. In other words, the awareness of
need (Bekkers and Wiepking 2011) for rather distant and
abstract causes is assumed to be more developed among
people who have attained higher levels of formal education. Prior empirical research underpins these assumptions;
it identifies a positive relation between higher educated
donors and giving to international causes (Micklewright
and Schnepf 2009; Rajan et al. 2008; Ribar and Wilhelm
1995). Srnka et al. (2003) in their bivariate analyses find
education positively related to development aid, human
rights, environment, and animal protection. We note that
animal welfare includes both animal shelters as well as the
protection of endangered wildlife, which is less proximate
(elephants, for example) and likely to be abstract as compared to animal shelters. Based on these considerations, we
thus hypothesize:
Hypothesis 4 Higher Education is positively related to
the incidence of giving and the amount donated for International Relief, Animal Welfare, and Environmental
Issues.
Income
While income is consistently identified as an important
predictor of the amount donated, the results are mixed for
the association between income and the incidence of giving; studies either find a positive or no relationship. As
people with all levels of income are capable of making
donations, those with higher income can afford to give
more (Wiepking and Bekkers 2012a). With regard to
donors’ choice, individuals with higher income may also

Voluntas (2019) 30:783–799

prefer to donate to different causes than those with lower
incomes.
Due to their life-experience, wealthy people and poor
people ‘might have different perspectives of the issues that
are most deserving assistance’ (Bennett 2012, p. 873). As
individuals with lower incomes are closer to social problems such as bad housing, vulnerability to crime, or lowquality medical care, they are said to be particularly more
aware of social needs of others (Bennett 2012). Others
argue that giving provides an opportunity to express personal taste, passion, personal interest and is an act of selfactualization (Bennett 2003; Ostrower 1997; Radley and
Kennedy 1995). Donors thus give to causes which relate to
their personal taste and also care about the cause’s reputation (Breeze and Lloyd 2013; Kottasz 2004). Breeze and
Lloyd (2013), Banks and Tanner (1999), and Sargeant and
Woodliffe (2007) show that affluent people predominately
give to arts and culture and higher education in the UK.
According to Kottasz (2004), high-earning professional
males are attracted to giving to arts and cultural charities,
particularly well-established organizations from which they
can then receive invitations to special events and are likely
to be solicited. Similar results were found by Ostrower
(1997), who investigated elite giving. Furthermore, qualitative studies provide evidence that wealthy people are
more likely to donate to arts and culture, education, health,
and medical research, but not that frequently to environmental causes, religious organizations, and overseas
development (Lloyd 2004, p. 2). Following the reasoning
above, we thus hypothesize:
Hypothesis 5 Income is positively related to the incidence of giving and the amount donated for Culture and

Our control variables are socio-demographics that are
found to influence charitable giving in the literature. We
include a dummy variable for gender (female = 1) and
measure age by three categories with a dummy variable for
each of them. Dummy variables for age are used as the
relationship between age (in years) and giving may be
nonlinear. In other words, age might be related to giving
only when it is above a certain threshold (cf. Bekkers
2003b, p. 608). The three categories used are: greater than
64 years old, between 30 and 64 years, and less than
30 years old, which is the reference category.2

opposed to single, divorced and not-cohabiting, and widowed people. This controls for the belief that individuals
with more extended social networks are more often solicited for donations (Wiepking and Maas 2009, p. 1976).

The stepwise approach allows us to examine the impact
of subjective dispositions and human resources sequentially and to see whether the effects of the predictor variables stay robust when adding the controls for solicitation
and volunteering. By introducing subjective dispositions
before human resources in our regressions, we are able to
test whether effects of subjective dispositions are mediated
by resources, following Bekkers (2005). We employ the
same regressors in the first (selection) and in the second
(outcome) equation of the Heckman model, except for the
variable on an individuals’ community type, which is
excluded in the second-stage regression as this variable has
a stronger influence on the incidence and not intensity to
donate (AFP 2010).
In order to compare the effect sizes of the various predictors, variables were Z-standardized before running
regression analyses. Our analysis on total giving refers to
the full sample; all subsequent analyses on donor choice
are based on donors only, i.e., individuals who have
donated to at least one cause.

Method

In this section, we first describe the findings on the factors
that influence total giving, both on the decision to give and
the amount donated. Subsequently, we report the results on
the factors that influence donor choice for the different
causes and make reference to the relevant hypotheses with
respect to our findings.

For our analytical strategy, we utilize Heckman’s two-stage
regression models (Heckman 1979) to investigate factors
that influence donor behavior. This is done as the decision
to donate precedes the amount donated, and this prior
decision may be influenced by different factors than the
secondary decision on the amount given (Forbes and
Zampelli 2011, p. 1149). The Heckman two-stage regression model estimates the first selection (to donate or not)
with a probit and incorporates the results of the first-stage
selection in the second-stage decision (amount donated)
(Forbes and Zampelli 2011, p. 1149). We conduct our
analysis with Stata/SE 14.1.
In order to test the hypotheses of this study, for each of
the eight charitable causes we present three Heckman
regression models.3 The first model (Model I for each
cause) includes our controls for age, gender, children,
marital status, and the four predictor variables on subjective dispositions: level of Generalized Trust, Empathic
Concern, Religious Affiliation, and Religious Attendance.
In the second model (Model II), we add our predictors on
human resources, i.e., Education and Income. In the final
model (Model III for each cause), we add the variables on
solicitation and volunteering.

Findings

Results on Total Giving
Table 4 exhibits the coefficients of the Heckman two-stage
regression on individuals’ amount donated. We find that all
subjective dispositions predictors: Generalized Trust,
Empathic Concern, Religious Affiliation, and Religious
Attendance are positively and significantly related to the
incidence to donate. Empathic Concern and Religious
Attendance are also positively related to the amount
donated, while Religious Affiliation and the amount
donated are negatively associated. Human resources,
Education and Income, are not associated with the decision
to donate in our data; however, Education is related positively to the amount donated: People who have attained
twelve or more years of education are more generous
compared to those with fewer years of education.

Discussion
What factors determine which causes donors choose to
support: donors’ subjective dispositions or their human
resources? In particular, we examined whether donors’
inclination to give to charitable causes and the amount
donated to these causes can be predicted by their subjective
dispositions or their resources. While prior studies had
investigated donors’ choice among organizations, few have
examined more than two causes, and often focusing on the
likelihood of giving and not the amount donated. These
limitations were addressed in this study by investigating
eight subsectors. Based on the literature review, we
developed five hypotheses on donor choice for particular
causes that we tested on our nationally random sample

Voluntas (2019) 30:783–799

795

using Heckman two-stage regressions. We find partial
support for our hypotheses (see Table 6) as related to
Generalized Trust, Empathic Concern, Religious Attendance, and Education and no support for Religious Affiliation and Income.
Donor’s subjective dispositions generally influence the
choice for a particular cause, but not the amount donated to
a cause. For instance, Empathic Concern is associated with
giving to International Relief, Social Services, and Health,
but not with the amount donated to these causes. We relate
this to the literature that individual attitudes and values
tend to predict behaviors that incur low material costs, but
not when substantial costs are involved, referred to as the
low-cost hypothesis (Neuberg et al. 1997). This low-cost
hypothesis may well explain our results: While subjective
dispositions are useful to predict who is more likely to take
out their wallets to donate, they cannot predict how much
they donate. Giving small amounts of money is a low-cost
measure that easily alleviates the social pressure of the
‘ask’ while giving higher amounts is costly to the donor,
therefore not undertaken. Further research on the alignment
of the cause of the solicitation and the donor’s values need
to be made to understand who is willing to undertake a
high-cost measure of donating generously.
In contrast to subjective dispositions, human resources
are associated with both, donors’ decisions to give and the
amount donated to particular causes in our study. This
general pattern is also supported by non-hypothesized
relationships that turned out to be significant, showing
Education positively associated with the amount donated to
Domestic Relief and Religious Causes, and Income positively related to giving to Domestic Relief (see Table 7).
We also found that effects of subjective dispositions are
mediated by human resources, as slightly significant effects

that asking for donations provides the donor an opportunity
to donate as well as exerts some social pressure to donate

donors not solicited. When comparing the coefficients in
the regression models (which our analyses allow for

has the strongest influence on donor decision to give for all
of the eight causes investigated. This contributes to the
literature on charitable giving, which often looks to individual-level characteristics to explain donations separately

Conclusion
This research is among the first few studies—following
Wiepking’s (2010) research based in the Netherlands that
investigates the factors that impact donors’ choice in continental Europe. Our study builds on previous findings, and
examines how individual-level subjective dispositions and
human resources affect donor choice and the amount
donated across eight different causes.
We find that while human resources are significant
predictors for both donors’ likelihood to give and the
amount donated among causes, subjective dispositions
such as Empathic Concern may only predict donors’ incidence to give, but not the amount donated to any of the
causes analyzed. This refers to the low-cost hypothesis,
stating that individual attitudes and values do not predict
behaviors when substantial costs are involved. Moreover,
our research corroborates a findings of prior studies,
namely that human resources mediate the impact of subjective dispositions on donor choice.
This study also explains why certain findings differ from
studies undertaken with data from North America or UK.
We find that in a representative, random sample, the
mechanisms at work are those typical for a welfare-mix in
continental Europe. For example, findings such as income
not being related to donor choice for Art and Education
might be due to the political context. Public funding for
arts and culture and education in Austria is very different
than in the USA or UK, where there are long-standing
traditions of support for higher education that is private and
alumni-supported. Thus, the generalizability of the findings
to other countries with a different welfare-mix is limited.
Our study has limitations. We did not consider tax
effects for charitable giving on donors’ choice. From prior
literature, we know that tax deductions affect the level of
contribution by way of price and income effects (Peloza
and Steel 2005). Since the incentive was introduced in
Austria only in 2009 for certain causes (Schober et al.
2015), and the data looked at donations made in 2010, it is
likely that donor decisions were not made on the basis of
this law. Future studies on donor choice need to examine

797

the impact of tax incentives, as they are only granted for
donations to particular causes.
Furthermore, our finding that being asked to donate has
the highest explanatory power regarding the incidence of
giving among all causes investigated has to be interpreted
with caution. An obvious difficulty here is that solicitation
may also be a consequence of prior donations to this
organization. While most studies do not take this endogeneity effect into account (e.g., Forbes and Zampelli
2011), Yörük’s (2007) study controlled for it; he found that
solicitations still explain an increase in the likelihood and
the amount of giving.
Further research on charitable giving and particularly on
donor choice should particularly address the interaction of
different predictors, for example resources, attitudes and
values. Also, revisiting the low-cost hypothesis in research
on charitable behavior is much needed. The alignment of
the cause of the solicitation and the donor’s values need to
be made to understand who is willing to undertake a highcost measure of donating generously. Finally, with regard
to solicitation, it is necessary to untangle whether it was the
opportunity (being made aware of the cause) or the social
pressure to which donors are responding, as both factors
may interact to produce generous donors.
