ABSTRACT

KEYWORDS

We developed and tested a conceptual model’s influence on
a sample of active volunteers’ retention intentions and on their
intentions to support their organizations in other ways (e.g.
donations). Approximately 200 nonprofit organizations were
contacted to participate in this study by asking their volunteers
to complete our online survey. This resulted in over 600 com­
pleted questionnaires. Data were analyzed using PLS-SEM tech­
niques. We examined the influence of brand strength on six
outcome variables (1-year retention intentions, 5-year retention
intentions, donation intentions, bequest intentions, volunteer
recruitment intentions, and word-of-mouth intentions). Brand
strength’s effects on all the outcome variables were significant.
The influence of brand strength on bequest and donation inten­
tions was partially mediated through its influence on organiza­
tional transparency. Additionally, the influence of seven
proposed moderators (organizational transparency, volunteer
morale, organizational socialization, training program quality,
organizational trust, confidence in leadership, organizational
pride, and value congruence) was also tested, with mixed
results.

Introduction

Because of the potential benefits, there is a growing interest in marketing and
branding among nonprofit managers (Apaydın, 2011). For example, Boenigk
and Becker (2016) argue that effective brand strategies can have a favorable
influence on donation intentions and volunteer involvement. Napoli (2006)
found that most successful nonprofit organizations tended to be brand
oriented. Unfortunately, there is comparatively little research on the influence
of brand-related constructs on nonprofit outcome variables (Liu et al., 2015).
To address this lack on research, Liu et al. (2015) investigated the benefits of
a nonprofit brand management orientation and found a positive influence of
having a brand orientation on staff attachment to the nonprofit brand. On
another nonprofit brand topic, Curran et al. (2016) investigated the influence
of brand heritage on volunteers’ satisfaction with management. They found

that volunteer engagement mediates the influence of brand heritage on volun­
teers’ satisfaction with management. Another study found that charity brand
personality and donors’ satisfaction mediated the influence of charity brand
experience on donation intentions (Ali et al., 2022).
Laidler-Kylander et al. (2004) argued that there is a strong need for more
research on the role of nonprofit brand constructs in retaining volunteers.
Volunteer retention is an important issue for many nonprofit organizations
(Garner & Garner, 2011; Netting et al., 2005). A high volunteer turnover rate
can place additional pressure on volunteer recruitment demands and also
negatively influence volunteer morale and service delivery (Bidee et al., 2013;
Cho et al., 2020; Wong et al., 2011). Hager and Brudney (2004) reported that
about 40% of volunteers discontinue their service due to poor volunteer
management practices. While prior studies on volunteers’ motivations have
added to our understanding their behavior (Bang et al., 2012; Hallmann &
Harms, 2012; Li & Wu, 2018), examining the influence of other factors on
volunteer retention would be valuable (Cho et al., 2020). In the investigation
reported in this paper, we are interested in the influences of constructs in our
conceptual model on active volunteers. In this investigation, we are interested
in exploring influences on volunteers’ retention intentions and on volunteers’
intentions to support their organizations in other ways.
The investigation reported in this paper adds to our knowledge in the
literature gap areas of nonprofit branding and volunteer retention. In helping
to fill these gaps, we examine the influence of brand strength on volunteer
retention intentions. However, since volunteers may support their organiza­
tions in additional ways, we include other support intentions (donations,
bequests, recruiting volunteers, and word-of-mouth referrals) as outcome
variables. Our investigation includes the examination of a potential mediator
(organizational transparency) and moderators that may interact with brand
strength’s influence on our proposed volunteer behavioral intentions. The
moderators included in our study are volunteer morale, organizational socia­
lization, training program quality, organizational trust, confidence in leader­
ship, organizational pride, and value congruence.
The scientific and practical relevance of this study is that it adds to our
knowledge on construct relationships that influence volunteers’ retention inten­
tions and intentions to support their organizations in additional ways.
Researchers can use our discoveries to further our understanding of these
phenomena. Managers can use our discoveries to inform their managerial prac­
tices to more effectively retain their volunteers and deepen their commitment.

Outcomes – supporter intentions

Outcomes, supporter intentions, are the desired effects or consequents
presented in our conceptual model. In this study, our supporters are
active volunteers. In practice, nonprofit organizations use marketing tac­
tics to attain desired benefits or outcomes such as support from indivi­
duals. They seek support from individuals in a variety of ways like
donating, volunteering, word-of-mouth referrals, and so forth (Peloza &
Hassay, 2007). In Figure 1, we present a conceptual model having six
outcome intentions. As our model is relatively complex and as the prior
literature is limited, we shall consider our outcomes collectively when
developing our hypotheses, but we shall test the model’s influence on our
outcomes individually.
The outcomes in our conceptual model include volunteer retention inten­
tions, referring to volunteers’ plans to continue their volunteer service (Harp
et al., 2017). It is useful to differentiate retention intentions in the near term
and longer term. Hence, we include retention intentions for the next year
(VR1) and for the next five years (VR5).
Wymer and Rundle-Thiele (2016) recommend that researchers include
multiple outcomes in their conceptual models. This enables a richer under­
standing of the dynamic influences at play. Furthermore, volunteers can
manifest their support in a variety of ways. Some volunteers may choose to
donate their time as volunteers instead of donating their money as donors.
Some volunteers may choose to donate both their time and money. Falvo
(2018) reported that nearly two-thirds of volunteers also donated money to
their organizations. Accordingly, we thought it would be useful to assess
volunteers’ intentions to provide monetary support to their organizations.
Relevant monetary support intentions include donation intentions (DI) and
bequest intentions (BI).

Volunteers may also support their organizations in other ways as

Brand strength

Marketing’s purpose is to help the nonprofit organization acquire and retain
support, such as volunteers, donations, and so forth. A brand orientation is
a managerial mind-set in which managing the organization as a brand facil­
itates resource/support acquisition and retention. The brand object or entity
that is branded is the nonprofit organization. The brand is a latent psycholo­
gical construct that refers to the perceptions, associations, and meaning of the
organization in the minds of priority audiences (individuals whose engage­
ment and support the organization seeks to acquire and retain) (Wymer, 2013;
Wymer & Casidy, 2019). A contribution of this inquiry is that we chose active
volunteers as a priory audience and examine brand strength from their
perspectives.
The overarching goal of brand management is to make the brand (i.e.,
a nonprofit organization) a strong brand, ideally the strongest brand among
its peer set (Wymer, 2013). A strong brand enjoys benefits not attainable to
a weak brand. For example, the Red Cross, a strong brand, gets the prepon­
derance of donations compared to other relief organizations in the aftermath
of a natural disaster (Wymer et al., 2016).
Wymer et al. (2016) conceptualized a strong brand as having high levels of
its three dimensions: brand familiarity, brand remarkability, and brand atti­
tudes. Brand familiarity refers to the level of knowledge a priority audience has
about a brand object, in this context, a nonprofit organization or charity.
Brand remarkability refers to the degree to which a brand object (i.e., charity)
is perceived by a priority audience (i.e., volunteers) to be extraordinary. Brand
attitudes refer to the degree to which a brand (i.e., a charity) is perceived
favorably by a priority audience.
Related prior research leads us to believe that brand strength may influence
volunteers’ retention intentions and other support intentions. Casais and
Santos (2018) found that supporters only donated to charities with which
they were familiar. The more knowledgeable people are about a charity, the
more aware they are of the charity’s mission and its good works (TorresMoraga et al., 2010). The degree to which a charity is well-known can
influence individuals’ trust in a charity as individuals are more likely to trust
a familiar than an unfamiliar charity (Paço et al., 2015; Rim et al., 2016).
Bennett and Gabriel (2003) reported that individuals’ familiarity with a charity
influences their perceptions of the charity’s reputation, which, in turn, influ­
ences their donation behaviors. Although we make inferences on prior

research on donors to guide our hypothesis development, a contribution of
this inquiry is that we extend prior research by examining influences on active
volunteers.
Wymer and Casidy (2019) argued that brand familiarity is a predictor of
brand attitudes. Bennett (2003) found that individuals’ attitudes toward cha­
rities influenced their choice of charities for donations. Other research has
found a relationship between charity brand attitudes and donation intentions
(Smith & McSweeney, 2007; Teah et al., 2014).
Bennett and Gabriel (2003) reported that an excellent charity image was
a determinant of donation income. For a priority audience to perceive
a charity as having an excellent image, the priority audience must be somewhat
familiar with the charity and believe that the charity is exceptional (Werke &
Bogale, 2023). When donors are deciding where to donate, the charity that is
perceived to be mediocre or no better than average may not be chosen; instead,
the charity that is perceived to be outstanding is more likely to be the donor’s
recipient. This reasoning is consistent with prior research which reports an
influence between a charity’s reputation and donor giving (Bennett, 2009;
Bennett & Ali-Choudhury, 2009; Sargeant & Woodliffe, 2007) and donor
perceptions of charity service delivery excellence and giving (Michel &
Rieunier, 2012). Although the prior research has focused on reputational
influences on donation intentions, we predict that a related dynamic exists
between brand strength and all our volunteer support intentions.
H1: Brand strength has a positive influence on volunteer support intentions.
Organizational transparency

Organizational transparency refers to the degree to which individuals perceive
a nonprofit organization to share information with its priority audiences to
enhance their understanding of the organization’s operations and practices, to
build trust, and to create accountability (Alhidari et al., 2018). Priority audi­
ences’ perceptions of a charity’s transparency are influenced by how accessible
a charity makes its information, the completeness of the information, and the
perceived accuracy of the information (Dethier et al., 2023). Sanzo-Pérez et al.
(2017) report that there is little research on nonprofit organizational transpar­
ency and its effects. Helmig and Thaler (2010) identify exploratory research on
transparency in the context of nonprofit marketing as a critical need.
Furneaux and Wymer (2015) and Wymer et al. (2021) reported that
transparency is an important influencer of public trust in a charity. Farwell
et al. (2019) argue that accountability and transparency influence the
public’s trust in a charity. In crowdfunding campaigns, operational trans­
parency or regular updates of progress positively affect donations due to
donors’ perceptions of effort and trust (Mejia et al., 2019). In the human
resource management literature, prior research has reported that

information flow from the organization has a positive influence on employ­
ee’s trust in their supervisors (Shockley-Zalabak et al., 2000). While the
behavioral influences on paid employees may not be identical to those on
volunteers, prior human resource research may be useful in developing
hypotheses in some instances.
Charity watchdog organizations attempt to protect donors and to encou­
rage charities to adhere to transparency and good stewardship (Stötzer et al.,
2023; Szper & Prakash, 2011). It is unclear how widely donors rely on watch­
dog ratings of charities to target their donations. Charity watchdog ratings are
probably more important for lesser-known charities (that is, weak brands)
than well-known charities (Stötzer et al., 2023) and when making larger
donations which incurs greater potential donor risk.
Prior research on charity transparency is concerned with the construct’s
influence on donors (Liket & Maas, 2015). Transparency is advocated to
provide assurance and to enhance trust to donor audiences (Barber et al.,
2022; Bodem-Schrötgens & Becker, 2020; Chu & Luke, 2023). Sanzo-Pérez
et al. (2017) argue that transparency become one of the most important
dimensions for nonprofit organization, while Harris and Neely (2021)
reported that higher level of transparency contributes to higher supporter
intentions or future contributions. Organizational transparency may influence
volunteers as well. Hence, examining transparency’s influence on volunteers
represents an additional contribution of this inquiry.
Wymer and Najev Čačija (2023) argue that organizational transparency
may not be as important for well-known charities as it is for relatively
unknown charities. They argue that charities that are perceived to be strong
brands enjoy being well-known (brand familiarity) and have superior reputa­
tions (brand remarkability and brand attitudes) and are more likely to be
trusted than weak charity brands. Hence, brand strength’s influence on sup­
porter outcomes may be at least partially mediated through organizational
transparency because of brand strength’s influence on audiences’ perceptions
of organizational transparency.
It is likely that brand strength has a positive influence on volunteers’
perceptions of organizational transparency. Strong levels of brand strength
indicate that volunteers are familiar with their organizations, have positive
attitudes toward their organizations, and believe their organizations are excep­
tional. Thus, the greater familiarity combines with a halo effect from high
levels of brand attitudes and brand remarkability would likely exert a positive
influence on perceived organizational transparency. From a practical view­
point, organizations must communicate with priority audiences to increase
brand familiarity. Organizational communication should also enhance per­
ceived organizational transparency.
Although we bundle our outcome variables collectively as supporter inten­
tions, it is likely that our constructs will differentially influence our six

outcome variables. Generally, there is an insufficient body of prior research to
guide precise hypothesis predictions of the model’s constructs on each indi­

H2: The influence of brand strength on volunteer support intentions is

Moderation effects

To further add to our knowledge of the influence of brand strength and
organizational transparency on supporter outcomes, we sought to identify
and test potential moderators of these relationships. We selected potential
moderators that we believe could affect the influence of brand strength on our
outcome variables. We also selected potential moderators that are typically
present in the volunteering experience.
Volunteer morale. Volunteer morale refers to degree to which volunteers have
positive attitudes toward their organization, its leadership, and its staff/volun­
teers (Arunchand & Ramanathan, 2013; Sania et al., 2015). There is deficit of
research on volunteer morale, so we rely on prior research on employee
morale to provide guidance. Overall, there seems to be a relationship between
employee morale, employee attitudes, motivation, satisfaction, and perfor­
mance (Milligan, 2006). Extending prior research on employee behavior to
volunteer behavioral intentions represents a potential contribution of this
inquiry.
Rostina et al. (2023) found that employee enthusiasm has a positive influ­
ence on productivity. Conversely, Shaban et al. (2017) found that low
employee morale negatively influenced performance. Prior research reports
that corporate reputation has a positive influence on employee commitment
(Almeida & Coelho, 2019). Gray and Balmer (1998) argue that corporate
reputation and morale interact and influence employee performance. We
argue that brand strength has a similar effect as organizational reputation
and that volunteers’ morale is likely influenced by brand strength. Drawing
inferences from prior human resource research, volunteers’ morale will likely
interact with brand strength’s influence on their support intentions.

H3a: Volunteer morale moderates brand strength’s influence on supporter
intentions.

With respect to organizational transparency, Ingram (2009) found that an
employer’s transparency with respect to hiring and promotions had a positive
influence on employee morale. Kwon and Park (2019) argue that organiza­
tional transparency can be part of a strategy to improve public sector employee
morale. Nabors (2019) found that managerial transparency influences
employee morale. Based on prior research in the human resource field, we
predict an interaction between transparency and morale on volunteers.
H3b: Volunteer morale moderates organizational transparency’s influence on
supporter intentions.
Organizational socialization. Whereas onboarding refers to an organization’s
process of integrating a new employee into the organization, organizational
socialization is more specific and, in our context, refers to facilitating a new
volunteer into the social community of the nonprofit organization (Bauer &
Erdogan, 2012). Manifestations of successful organizational socialization
include healthy and positive social interactions between a volunteer and
other members of the organization. They include feeling known by an orga­
nization’s leadership, staff, and other volunteers. They include feelings of
belonging and being accepted (Bauer & Erdogan, 2012).
Given the lack of research on volunteers, we will use prior research on
employees from which to draw inferences, thus extending this research to
volunteer management. In the human resource management literature, suc­
cessful organizational socialization has been positively associated with orga­
nizational commitment (Bauer & Green, 1998), job satisfaction (Saks et al.,
2007), performance (Bauer et al., 2006); and negatively associated with turn­
over (Fu et al., 2005). Nguyen et al. (2021) found that organizational socializa­
tion had a positive influence on employees’ proactive behaviors and work
engagement.
It is reasonable to believe that organizational socialization would influence
volunteers’ attitudes and behaviors. We predict that organizational socializa­
tion would interact with brand strength’s effect on our volunteers’ support
intentions. We argued for a direct effect of brand strength previously. Since,
we predict direct effects on our outcomes from both organizational socializa­
tion and brand strength, it is likely that the two constructs, since they both
have a positive influence on our outcomes, interact. For example, volunteers’
negative perceptions of their organization’s socialization efforts would likely
negatively affect their attitudes toward the organization (brand attitudes),
which would reduce brands strength.

H4a: Organizational socialization moderates brand strength’s influence on
supporter intentions.

We were unable to identify prior research which examined the relationship
between organizational socialization and transparency. However, we think it is
reasonable to believe that some relationship does exist. To be effectively
socialized into an organization can be expected to enhance organizational
trust (Haski-Leventhal & Bargal, 2008). How could one feel a part of an
untrustworthy organization? Transparency is a necessary precursor of trust
within a nonprofit organization (Helmig & Thaler, 2010). Organizational
socialization enhances volunteers’ familiarity with the organization, facilitat­
ing transparency perceptions. Poor organizational transparency reduces
volunteers’ feelings of familiarity with the organization. Hence, we predict
an interaction effect between organizational socialization and organizational
transparency.
H4b: Organizational socialization moderates organizational transparency’s
influence on supporter intentions.
Training program quality. When volunteers are prepared for service through
an excellent training program, positive benefits should be expected.
Volunteers’ attitudes toward the organization are more positive. Volunteers
can be expected to be less frustrated and more successful and, thus, more
satisfied with volunteering. Hence, an excellent training program, all other
things being equal, should have a positive influence on volunteers’ support
intentions.
Some volunteers are motivated to give their service to acquire marketable
skills and training (Lammers, 1991). Certainly, these volunteers would be
dissatisfied if those motivating expectations were unmet. With respect to
volunteers with other motives, it is probable that the quality of their organiza­
tion’s training program as they begin their volunteer service would influence
their perceptions of the organization’s management quality and, hence, their
attitudes toward the organization (Gidron, 1983; Murk & Stephan, 1991).
Their attitudes toward the organization reduce their brand strength percep­
tions. Volunteers’ perceptions of the quality of their organization’s training
program influence their attitudes toward the organization and, thus, should
interact with brand strength.

H5a: Training program quality moderates brand strength’s influence on sup­
porter intentions.
One would expect that an excellent volunteer training program would famil­
iarize the volunteer trainee with the organization. If the training program
results in the volunteer becoming more familiar and knowledgeable about the
organization, then it seems reasonable to predict that the volunteer would
perceive the organization to be more transparent than if a weak or absent

training program left the new volunteer with little knowledge and familiarity
of the organization. Hence, volunteers’ perceptions of their organizations’
training programs should interact with organizational transparency.
H5b: Training program quality moderates organizational transparency’s influ­
ence on supporter intentions.
Organizational trust. Organizational trust refers to a psychological state com­
prising the intention to accept vulnerability based upon positive expectations
of the organization’s intentions or behavior (Möllering et al., 2004; Mühl,
2014). Prior research has found that organizations with higher levels of
organizational trust tend to be more efficient, effective, adaptive, and innova­
tive (Shockley-Zalabak et al., 2000). Hence, organization trust should influence
volunteers’ attitudes, which influences brand strength.
Salas (2008) found that organizational commitment is a predictor of volun­
teers’ intentions to remain which is influenced by volunteers’ trust in the
organization’s mission, leadership, and activities’ effectiveness. Bekkers
(2012) reported that people with low levels of trust are more likely to quit
volunteering. Other research has not found trust to be a predictor of volunteer
retention (Washinko, 2022). Windon et al. (2024) argued that the influence of
effective communication skills on volunteer retention is mediated through
their effect on trust. A. Becker et al. (2020) argued that a lack of trust in the
fundraising organization has a negative influence on donation intentions.
We predict that organizational trust interacts with brand strength’s influ­
ence on supporter intentions. We argue that the positive effects of a high level
of brand strength are enhanced when organizational trust is high and reduced
when organizational trust is low. Hence, we offer the following hypothesis.

H6a: Organizational trust moderates brand strength’s influence on supporter
intentions.
It was previously discussed how prior research has consistently reported
a relationship between organizational transparency and trust. We, therefore,
predict that organizational transparency interacts with organizational trust
and offers the following hypothesis.
H6b: Organizational trust moderates organizational transparency’s influence
on supporter intentions.
Confidence in leadership. Confidence in leadership refers to volunteers’ assur­
ance that the organization’s supervisory and management team leads the
organization and supervises its volunteers in an excellent manner. There is
reason to believe that volunteers’ confidence in their organization’s leadership

influences their retention and support intentions. Senses-Ozyurt and
Villicana-Reyna (2016) reported that leader integrity and inclusiveness pre­
dicted volunteer retention. Glass (2018) reported that nonprofit leaders who
were collaborative, motivating, and good communicators were more effective
in retaining volunteers. Ngah et al. (2022) reported that servant leadership
influenced volunteer retention through job satisfaction. Based on this prior
research, we offer the following hypothesis.
It is likely that volunteers’ attitudes toward their organizations’ leadership
interacts with brand strength. If volunteers have poor attitudes toward their
organizations’ management, this can be expected to be manifested in lower
attitudes toward the organization, which is reflected in lower brand attitude
levels and, hence, lower brand strength.
H7a: Confidence in leadership moderates brand strength’s influence on sup­
porter intentions.
Transparency is linked to accountability, which refers to the extent to which
the organization can be held to its promises (Lloyd, 2005). Being accountable
and transparent engenders trust (Putnam, 1993). An organization’s relation­
ships with its stakeholders depend on transparency and trust (Lamming et al.,
2001). Buell et al. (2021) recommend that in an era of declining trust in
government the delivery of government services to the public should be
designed in a more transparent manner to foster greater trust in public
institutions.
We predict that volunteers’ confidence in their organization’s leadership
interacts with organizational transparency. Organizational transparency influ­
ences leadership confidence. Volunteers may be distrustful of management
when there is little organizational transparency. A high level of transparency
may enhance volunteers’ confidence in management. Hence, we offer the
following hypothesis.
H7b: Confidence in leadership moderates organizational transparency’s influ­
ence on supporter intentions.
Organizational pride. This construct refers to an individual’s pleasure or
satisfaction from having an association with an organization (Helm, 2013).
With respect to paid employees, organizational pride has been found to
enhance commitment and reduce turnover (Pereira et al., 2021). In a study
on employee turnover, Helm (2013), predicted (relying on social identity
theory) that the influence on an employer’s reputation on employee turnover
intentions was mediated by employees’ organizational pride and job satisfac­
tion. Groza and Groza (2022) examined influences on episodic event volun­
teers’ intentions to volunteer for the event in the following year. They found

JOURNAL OF NONPROFIT & PUBLIC SECTOR MARKETING

829

that an organization’s reputation influenced episodic volunteers’ returning
intentions through organizational pride (mediator).
We could not find any prior research which examined the relationship
between brand strength and organizational pride. However, we believe
a likely interaction effect may exist. If volunteers have low (high) levels of
organizational pride, this is likely to negatively (positively) influence volun­
teers perceive brand attitudes, and thus, brand strength.
H8a: Organizational pride moderates brand strength’s influence on supporter
intentions.
Organizational transparency engenders trust (Jiang & Luo, 2018).
Organizational pride, likewise, may be inversely related to trust in the organi­
zation. Organizational pride and organizational transparency likely covary. It
is counterintuitive to view the two constructs and unrelated. The positive
affect of strong organizational pride would indicate organizational trust,
which would indicate organizational transparency. Organizational pride may
accentuate the influence of organizational transparency on volunteers’ inten­
tions. Conversely, low organizational pride may reduce the positive effects of
organizational transparency (Bennis et al., 2008).
H8b: Organizational pride moderates organizational transparency’s influence
on supporter intentions.
Value congruence. Value congruence refers to individuals’ perceptions of the
degree to which their values are like those of an organization (Wymer et al.,
2021). Since volunteering is a value expressive behavior (Okun et al., 2015),
value congruence should be an important type of fit between volunteers and
their organizations (Penner, 2000). Volunteering provides individuals with an
opportunity to act on their core values, which are often not able to be
expressed through paid employment, like redressing social injustice, returning
one’s good fortune, and reducing suffering and deprivation (Cnaan &
Goldberg-Glen, 1991).
Charities’ values are manifested in their missions, the types of causes they
support, and the people they help (in some cases). Wymer and Rundle-Thiele
(2016) reported that people who had feelings of loyalty toward charities felt
loyalty toward their charities’ missions and the people helped by the charities’
work. We predict that when volunteers perceive a close value congruence with
their organizations, the value congruence enhances volunteers’ perceptions of
their organizations’ brand strength.

H9a: Value congruence moderates brand strength’s influence on supporter
intentions.

Organizational transparency and organizational trust are linked. In prior
research, value congruence has been found to promote trust in relationships
(Lau et al., 2007). A lack of transparency reduces trust in the organization
(Farwell et al., 2019). A lack of trust in the organization may reduce the
meaningfulness of volunteering (Im & Chung, 2018). Hence, value expressive
benefits of volunteering in a low transparency, low trust context may be
reduced.
H9b: Value congruence moderates organizational transparency’s influence on
supporter intentions.
Next, we will describe an empirical study that we conducted to test our
hypothesis and conceptual model.

Methods and procedures
Measures

The scale items used to measure the conceptual model’s variables are listed in
Table 1. In the questionnaire, respondents were presented with the scale items
as Likert statements and asked to circle the response choice that corresponded
most closely with their level of agreement with the statement. The antecedent,
mediator, and moderators were measured with seven-point scales, from 1 =
strongly disagree to 7 = strongly agree). The single-item outcome variables
were measured with seven-point scales, from 1 = very unlikely to 7 = very
likely).
Participants and procedures

Since we were interested volunteers’ retention and support intentions,
we sought to recruit a sample of active volunteers. We wanted respon­
dent volunteers to come from a variety of organizations and locations.
We asked nonprofit organizations to participate in our study by asking
their volunteers to complete an online questionnaire. Over 200 nonpro­
fit organizations across Canada were contacted between August and
November 2023. Ethics review board anonymity requirements were
such that we were unable to identify all the organizations that partici­
pated as well as individual volunteers completing our online question­
naire. Some of the nonprofit organizations we contacted were umbrella
organizations and some organizations asked their peer organizations to
participate, so the exact number and profile of participating organiza­
tions is not known. This procedure resulted in 631 completed
questionnaires.

Sample

In total, 631 participants enrolled in this study. We examined potential outliers
and, as a result, 32 cases indicated as severe outliers in multiple items of
questionnaire were excluded. We proceed with the analysis with final sample
size N = 599. Descriptive analysis of the sample revealed sample age range
from 18 to 92, with mean age of 57.64 years. Related to gender, the majority
were females, (78.1%), while 1.3% were declared as others. Average years of
education were 16.1, indicating at least high school diploma of participants.
Related to volunteer behavior, participants spend in average 13.24 h (monthly),
with average number of weeks since last volunteered 5.38, and frequency of
monthly volunteering 4.41 times.
Analysis

We used variance-based partial least squares structural equation modeling
(PLS-SEM) with SmartPLS 4.0 software (Ringle et al., 2022). This
approach is appropriate for models with single and multi-item constructs
(Chin, 2010), non-normally distributed data, and examination of complex
model of factors influencing volunteer support intentions with primarily
explanatory purpose (source). The minimal sample size was estimated
with Inverse square root method (Kock & Hadaya, 2018) on a level of
a minimal expected path of 0.15, indicating sample size adequacy (599
cases obtained compared to 276 needed). No missing values were deter­
mined in items related to constructs, only in demographic characteristics
of respondents. Since no control variables were used in the model, we
treated missing demographic data as missing in the dataset.
Nonparametric percentile bootstrapping method was used with 599 cases
and 10,000 sub-samples (J. M. Becker et al., 2023).
Common method bias was checked following the procedure proposed by
Podsakoff et al. (2003) in two phases. First, we ensure temporal, proximal
and psychological separation of predictor and criterion variables in our
questionnaire. Next, we used single common method factor approach with
Harman’s single factor (Kock, 2015) as predictor and criterion variables
could not be obtained from different sources but can be measured in
different context and source of method bias cannot be identified, as recom­
mended by Podsakoff et al. (2003). The first factor accounted for 45.14% with
unrotated principal component analysis extraction method (below the
threshold of 50% (Kock, 2020)).
Variance inflation factors (VIF) for all reflective lower order constructs
were examined for inner model expressing values below the cutoff value of 3.3
(Kock, 2015) for all items representing support intentions except for confi­
dence in leadership (CL, VIF −3.67) and volunteer morale (VM, VIF − 3.491).

Since the proposed model is reflective, we did not examine outer model VIFs.
Due to the high VIF values, that can cause potential collinearity issues, CL and
VM constructs were excluded from further analysis.
In the next step, we estimated measurement model in two phases, first for
lower order, and then for higher order construct measurement model. We
followed procedure as proposed by Hair et al. (2019). For measurement model,
we used constructs’ internal consistency, reliability, and convergent validity.
For estimation of structural model, we used explanatory power, out-of-sample
predictive power (R2, Q2, PLSpredict) and significance and relevance of the
path coefficients.
Lower order measurement model validation

Validity and reliability analysis for the lower order measurement model
indicate good fit. Factor loadings and Cronbach alphas are well above the
threshold of .70 (Hair et al., 2019) (all above .80). Composite reliability scores
range from .828 to .944, which is within suggested range from .70 to .95 (Hair
et al., 2014). The average variance extracted (AVE) is also above the cutoff of
.50, confirming convergent validity (Hair et al., 2019). All indicators’ loadings
are also higher for own construct than on others.
Discriminant validity was confirmed, as presented in Table 2. All constructs
square root of AVE is higher than the correlation with other constructs
(Fornell-Larcker criterion) and HTMT ratio of correlation is below .90
(Chin, 2010; Henseler et al., 2015).

Mediation analysis

Our proposed model predicts partial mediation of Organizational trans­
parency (OR) on six Supporter intention items and direct influence of
Brand strengths (BS) on Supporter intentions, so both paths were
included in the model. Effects were examined with bootstrapping techni­
que and 10.000 subsamples. Brand strength influences Organizational
transparency (β = .533, t = 15.937, p = .000), and Organizational transpar­
ency influences two out of six supporter intentions (DI: β = .219, t = 3.402,
p = .001; BI: β = .172, t = 2.877, p = .004). Brand strength directly influences
all six supporter intentions, as presented in Table 4. Based on these
results, we conclude that there is complementary partial mediation of
organizational transparency in the relationship between Brand strength
and Donation and Bequest intentions.

Structural model validation

We validated our structural model by analyzing the coefficient of determina­
tion (R2), redundancy measure (Q2) and path coefficients significance
(Sarstedt et al., 2022). We used PLSpredict procedure (Shmueli et al., 2019)
for out-of-sample predictive power analysis to determine ability of proposed
model to predict same relationships with new observations (Hair & Sarstedt,
2021).
The R2 values for endogenous constructs for supporter intentions (BI = .146;
DI = .122; REC = .253; WOM = .478; VR1=.232; VR5=.175) indicate weak to
moderate influence. The R2 value for OR (.284) also indicate weak influence
based on Hair et al. (2014) recommendations for R2 values (0.75 substantial,
0.50 moderate, and 0.25 weak influence).
Q2 values derived from PLSpredict software feature were used for estima­
tion of predictive relevance of the proposed model for supporter intentions

Discussion

The scientific and practical relevance of this study is that it adds to our
knowledge on construct relationships that influence volunteers’ retention
intentions and intentions to support their organizations in additional ways.
Researchers can use our discoveries to further our understanding of these
phenomena. Managers can use our discoveries to inform their managerial
practices to more effectively retain their volunteers and deepen their
commitment.
This study finds that brand strength has a significant influence on all our
outcome variables. Prior research on consumer behavior for commercial
marketing has found many benefits for strong brands. Consumers have
more favorable attribute and benefit perceptions and an overall preference
for strong brands. Consumers are more willing to pay a price premium for
strong brands (Hoeffler & Keller, 2003).
Grau and Kleiser (2012) argue that a strong brand can provide benefits to
nonprofit organizations, including greater awareness for the cause, greater
loyalty among donors and volunteers, and greater differentiation among
competitors. Our findings support the relationship between brand strength
and volunteer retention and commitment. It may be worthwhile for nonprofit
managers to view increasing their organization’s brand strength as a strategic
priority.
Effective volunteer retention has long been viewed as resulting from effec­
tive volunteer supervision, leadership, and management (Hager & Brudney,
2004; Studer, 2016). It would be interesting for a future study to examine the
mediation influence of brand strength in the relationship between volunteer
supervision practices and volunteer retention and commitment.
Hoeffler and Keller (2003) report that strong brands enjoy favorable ben­
efits with respect to consumer advertising. They claim that consumers pay
greater attention, react more positively, and retain more information for
strong brands. Future research on the audience effects of brand strength in
marketing communications for nonprofit brands would enhance our knowl­
edge in this area.

It is interesting to note that the influence of brand strength on our financial
support intentions (donation and bequest intentions) was partially mediated
through organizational transparency. Supporting one’s organization through
monetary gifts seems to be influenced by additional factors. Perhaps when it
comes to donating money, supporters may be more risk adverse than when
supporting an organization in other ways. Prior research has shown that trust
in a charity is influenced by its perceived transparency (Furneaux & Wymer,
2015; Wymer et al., 2021). Our study finds that a portion of brand strength’s
influence on monetary support intentions is channeled through its influence
on transparency. Future research would contribute to our knowledge in this
area by investigating the relationship among brand strength, trust, transpar­
ency, and donation intentions.
The influence of transparency on volunteers’ support intentions extended
beyond monetary support when transparency interacted with other factors.
For example, volunteer morale interacted with transparency to influence oneyear retention intentions. Training program quality interacted with transpar­
ency to influence one-year retention intentions and volunteers’ intentions to
help their organizations’ volunteer recruitment efforts. Organizational socia­
lization interacted with transparency to influence five-year retention inten­
tions. These findings remind us that the influence of factors on our outcome
variables can be transmitted through interaction effects.
As age as control variable only has a significant impact on two of the six
supporters’ intentions which are of poor strength, it can be concluded that age
has no real influence on the outcome variables. For example, bequest inten­
tions could be considered an outcome that is more pronounced in older
people. The beliefs and attitudes of the general population (including volun­
teers) change due to changes in the environment, regardless of age. Digital
literacy and the variety of sources for information via digital channels have an
impact on everyone. In this way, the impact of age (e.g. life experience and
wisdom) is minimized, as many things can be (pseudo) experienced via virtual
channels.
We examined a comparatively complex model. Our model included six
outcome variables, one moderator, and seven moderators. While parsimo­
nious models are easier to interpret and less prone to statistical anomalies,
a more complex model may allow the stronger influences to emerge in
a context more closely approximating the dynamic environment in which
volunteering takes place. It is valuable to examine a complex model that
reflects the multiple influences at play in a real setting rather than testing
a simple model that does not allow for the multiple influences and interactions
that are present on volunteers in their actual experience.
There is usually some balance to be made between internal validity and
external validity when developing a research design. The potential increase in
Type I error, while imperfect, was addressed in two ways. First, when multiple

842

W. WYMER AND L. N. ČAČIJA

variables are examined in a single test as part of a PLS model, the probability of
a Type I error does not increase to the extent it would if each variable’s
significance was tested individually. For example, if four factors are included
in an ANOVA test, since the means are being tested at the same time the
probability of a Type I error does not increase as it would if we did four
separate t-tests. Second, the p-values for our significant results far below the
0.05 significance threshold, closer to 0.01.
It is interesting to note that a more complex model has fewer degrees of
freedom than a simple model with fewer variables. As the degrees of freedom
decrease, it is more difficult to achieve statistical significance. Hence, while in
total it appears that there are comparatively few significant relationships in our
conceptual model, those significant relationships have substantial comparative
importance and are both theoretically and managerially valuable.
Conventional wisdom assumes volunteers may support their organization
in additional ways. Our study enriches our understanding of influences on
volunteers’ retention intentions as well as other support intentions. The
importance of brand strength is amplified when its effects on an array of
support intentions are better understood.
Managerial implications

Effective volunteer management is manifested in high volunteer retention
rates and strong volunteer organizational support. However, as our findings
show, influences on volunteer retention and other support are not limited
to effective supervision. Indeed, brand strength and organizational trans­
parency are the responsibility of an organization’s leadership at the execu­
tive and board levels. Volunteer supervision does not occur in isolation but
within the context of an organization’s culture and norms. Hence, respon­
sibility for effective volunteer management is distributed across the
organization.
Our findings support the argument that an organization that is a strong
brand enjoys better outcomes from its marketing activities. All other things
being equal, a nonprofit organization that is a stronger brand should experi­
ence higher volunteer retention rates and greater volunteer commitment than
an organization that is a weaker brand. Managing an organization in such
a manner that inspires prospective supporters to perceive it as excellent,
exceptional, and superior is foundational. Marketing an organization in such
a manner that it becomes well known and the organization’s superiority is
established is required as well.
It is interesting to note that perceived organizational transparency mediates
the influence of brand strength on volunteers’ donation and bequest inten­
tions. This finding is consistent with prior research on donors that finds
transparency a significant influence (Furneaux & Wymer, 2015). When

considering a bequest or donation, individuals appear to be influenced by the
perceived transparency of the organization. Perhaps transparency is related to
trust and potential donor risk. Further research might aid our understanding
of these relationships.

Future research

Previously, in our literature review, we cited prior studies which found
a relationship between an organization’s reputation and donation intentions.
Whereas reputation is a somewhat nebulous construct, brand strength is more
clearly conceptualized and measured (Wymer et al., 2016). It may be more
strategically advantageous to emphasize increasing brand strength as
a managerial focus rather than reputation. Brand strength may prove to be
an important metric for managerial or marketing effectiveness. An increase in
brand strength may prove to be a leading indicator of positive benefits to the
organization.
As discussed in the introduction, brand-related research for nonprofit
organizations is not well developed. Conceiving of an organization as
a brand as latent psychological construct in the minds of stakeholder
groups may be quite useful in helping the organization to more effectively
attract and retain support from target audiences. Whether trying to
increase volunteer retention and commitment or trying to stimulate greater
support from other groups, increasing brand strength provides a strategic
focus. Future research is needed to compare increasing brand strength with
other management strategies. A brand management orientation is likely to
have greater relevance for organizations that have a greater dependence on
attracting and retaining support from individuals in a competitive
environment.
Future research is needed to better understand the context in which brand
strength is differentially beneficial to organizations. Additionally, managers
need to understand how they can increase the brand strength of their organi­
zations. What are antecedents to brand strength and its dimensions?
Discovering brand strength antecedents that have a strong influence would
add to our knowledge in this area as well as provide practical utility for
managers.
Our results showing some differential influences on monetary support
intention are interesting and informative. Future research is needed to better
understand supporter behavior dynamics that distinguish monetary support
decisions from volunteering support decisions.

Limitations

All scholarly inquiries have limitations. Although we attempted to obtain
a sample of volunteers from a variety of organizations across Canada, respon­
dents were not randomly selected from the global population of volunteers.
Hence, generalization of these findings will need to be supported through
future research. The scope of our study was examining the influences of
constructs in our model on volunteers. We do not generalize our findings to
populations of nonvolunteers.
Furthermore, we tested a complex model which necessitated measurement
model evaluation and structural model analysis through advanced techniques.
Our model contained factors and variables which were generally latent psy­
chological constructs, which are unable to be perfectly measured. Hence, our
findings are limited to the scope of our investigation, the construct relation­
ships we examined, and the precision of our measures.
