Abstract
This study examined both donors' personal characteristics and the multidimensional attributes of charitable
organizations to illuminate how donation intention is
formed. The proposed research model was developed
based on the theory of planned behavior, and a total of
291 responses were used for empirical analysis. It was
found that both altruistic motivation and egoistic motivation have a positive effect on donors' attitudes toward
donation. We further found that donation attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control all have
a positive effect on donation intention. Moreover, the
results showed three dimensions of trust in charitable
organizations—perceived ability, perceived integrity, and
perceived benevolence—positively influence donation
attitudes and donation intention. This study's approach
is significant because it considers both individual aspects
and attributes of charitable organizations as factors leading to donation behavior. We believe that our findings
are useful for developing effective fundraising strategies.
KEYWORDS

donation attitudes, donation intention, motivational factors,
theory of planned behavior, trust in charity organizations

USA reported that the United States-based charitable organizations (COs) pulled in 326.87 billion

Along with favorable sentiment toward donations, charities' fundraising and donation culture have emerged as a social phenomenon. Against this backdrop, the topics related to
fundraising efforts, charitable activities, or philanthropy are gaining more attention from
researchers (Bekkers & Wiepking, 2011; Chen et al., 2019; Mittelman & Rojas-Mendez, 2018).
From an individual's behavioral perspective, earlier studies on donation behavior mentioned a
critical role of intrinsic factors because donation behavior was regarded as the result of one's
decision, and that decision is influenced by one's psychological traits (Deci, 1971;
Madurapperuma & Kim, 2020). From an organizational perspective, the trustworthiness of
charities may encourage more donations (Hou et al., 2017; Kirk & Abrahams, 2017). Therefore,
we seek to focus on the organizational attributes of charities by examining three components of
trust—integrity, ability, and benevolence (Alhidari et al., 2018).
First, it is required to examine a variety of factors to understand the impact on individual
donation behavior (Harvey, 1990; Mainardes et al., 2016; Sargeant et al., 2000; Zubairi &
Siddiqui, 2019). Prior studies on donation explored different subjects through different methodologies. Since the conditional factors (Darley & Latane, 1968) were suggested as major factors of
giving behavior, past research had addressed an individual's altruistic or egoistic motivation
(Batson et al., 1981) and focused more on analyzing personal intrinsic factors because donation
behavior was believed to be closely related to a person's psychological factors. Since donation
behavior is closely related to a person's psychological factors, it is considered a fundamental
cause of donation behavior (Myers, 2010). A traditional approach suggested that altruistic
motives lead to giving or helping behaviors. However, this is not always true because donation
behavior could be caused by selfish motives (Sisco & Weber, 2019). If some people weigh up
immediate or long-term gains before engaging in donation behavior, they could be motivated
by selfish motives (Brown & Cialdini, 2015; Schroeder & Graziano, 2015).
Several phycologists claimed that people behave in their own interests, which is the basic
principle of human behavior. Skinner's (1965) early behaviorism reported that helping acts
come from one's selfishness and pursuit of pleasure, and are reinforced by various compensation such as expressing gratitude, compliments from others, feeling of pride, or enhanced reputation. After experiencing such rewards, helping behavior is more encouraged. Since the goal of
egoistic helping is to serve the interests of the helper, helping behavior is no more than a way
to gain benefits. Conversely, altruistic motives are one's desire to help others and some other
benefits the helper may gain from helping are considered secondary.
Most previous studies on donation have primarily focused on altruistic motivation
(Bekkers, 2004; Mount, 1996). However, there is a clear contrast between egoistic motivation
and altruistic motivation at an individual level. Therefore, the impact of each motivation on
donation behavior needs to be analyzed and interpreted from various viewpoints. Reddy (1980)
found that an egoistic determinant of donation to charities is tax incentives. Another example
shows that where to put a donation box is important. People tend to donate more when the box
is located in a busy street than in an empty street. In the same vein, the names of donors are
displayed on the walls or designated areas of buildings to acknowledge donors' financial support. Therefore, egoistic motivation can be considered a variable that has a significant impact
on donation behavior.

People's perceptions and attitudes are linked to each other, which influences most human
behavior (Bandura, 1982; Oosterhof & Todorov, 2009). In the same vein, the degree of perceived
trust toward charities can induce positive attitudes, which may affect donation behavior. Therefore, cultivating legitimacy and trust is of utmost importance in allowing charities to achieve
their missions and conduct various charitable activities (Becker et al., 2019; Furneaux &
Wymer, 2015; Shier & Handy, 2013). What's more, being legitimate and trustworthy can
encourage donors to be more dedicated (Peng et al., 2019).
Meanwhile, charity trust varies depending on an individual's belief propensity, so it is
required to be studied in multiple dimensions (e.g., perceived ability, perceived integrity, perceived benevolence), not in the existing one-dimensional trust (e.g., Chaudhuri &
Holbrook, 2001; Esch et al., 2006; Ha, 2004). In addition, one of the very important points for
charities in inducing individual “continuous” donations is that donation attitudes are formed
based on multidimensional trust rather than being seen as a commercial image (De Vries
et al., 2015; Hyndman & McConville, 2018; Rho et al., 2020).
Thus, it is necessary to focus on the effect of trust in COs on donors' donation attitudes. An
individual's attitudes serve as a critical antecedent that induces his/her behavioral intention
and positively affects the continuity of intention (Yin et al., 2020). The effect of trust in COs, a
factor at an organizational level, on individual donors’ donation intention was already examined in the previous research. However, few studies have focused on the effect of trust in COs
on donors’ donation attitudes.
This study comprehensively investigated two motivational dimensions of donors—altruistic
motivation and egoistic motivation—and three components of trust in COs—perceived ability,
perceived integrity, and perceived benevolence. By doing so, we aimed to invent the mechanism
by which donation attitudes and donation intentions are formed. While most prior studies on
donation used the data collected in the United States, the data for this study was gathered from
Korea, providing a better understanding of the global donation trend.
To achieve our research purpose, we borrowed a theoretical framework of our research from
the theory of planned behavior (TPB). Based on the notion that a person's intention plays a vital
role in eliciting a specific behavior (Bilic, 2005), this theory explains that the behavior is performed when a person has the intention and volition to do it. Our approach touching upon the
factors affecting an individual's donation intention can contribute to extending the domain of
research, and also provide practical guidelines that help facilitate effective donation activities
from potential donors.

2.1 | Theory of planned behavior
Behavioral intention is considered the most critical concept in theory of reasoned action (TRA)
(Ajzen & Fishbein, 1980) and theory of planned behavior (Ajzen, 1991). It represents the degree
of voluntariness and efforts to perform a certain behavior. Since one's behavior is determined
by behavioral intention, the two theories suggested that behavioral intention is a predictor of
one's future behavior (Luc, 2020).
TPB stated that behavioral intention is determined by three elements—attitudes, subjective
norms, and perceived behavioral control. Attitudes toward a behavior are defined as a positive
or negative evaluation of a given behavior and are guided by behavioral beliefs and outcome

positive outcome caused by his/her donations (including money, time, service, and so on) and
consider it significant, which is his/her attitude toward behavior. Behavioral beliefs are one's
subjective beliefs about the outcome resulting from a particular behavior, and outcome evaluation represents one's beliefs in evaluating the consequence of a behavior that can be positively
or negatively valued. Subjective norms, a social factor determining the intention (Ajzen, 1991;
Bayer & Peay, 1997), are defined as an individual's perceived social pressure about one should
perform a specific behavior (Ajzen, 1985). For instance, an individual may be likelier to donate
if most other people, who are important to him/her, decide to donate. In other words, subjective
norms are determined by normative beliefs and motivation to comply, indicating that an individual's behavior is influenced by both his/her evaluation and others’ evaluation of particular
behaviors. Perceived behavioral control, determined by control beliefs, represents how difficult
it is to perform a specific behavior. For example, the decision to donate is entirely up to him/her
if an individual is confident to perform a donation behavior, which is the biggest difference
from the TRA. TPB defines perceived behavioral control as the extent to which an individual
perceives to be able to perform a behavior in a specific situation because TPB assumes that an
individual may behave differently in different situations. Therefore, perceived behavioral control is an important addition that helps predict human behavior (Ajzen & Madden, 1986).
TPB has been applicable to extensive research relating to behavior prediction in various disciplines. According to Allport et al. (1954), an attitude is a state of readiness to engage in certain
acts or responses. In the same vein, forming a positive attitude and belief toward donation may
drive individuals to engage in donation behavior. As with the influence of subjective norms,
various information given by others may play as a catalyst or pressure for donation behavior
because meeting expectations from significant others can lead to social pressure (Smith &
McSweeney, 2007; van der Linden, 2011).
Moreover, people would engage in donation behavior without hesitation when they find giving opportunities, as long as they have the ability to control their behavior. Therefore, individuals' donation behavior can be encouraged by behavioral control (Cronan & Al-Rafee, 2008;
Shaw & Shiu, 2003; Veldhuizen et al., 2011). Variables of TPB serve as useful perspectives to discuss an individual's donation behavior. Table 1 shows the list of previous studies on donation.

2.2 | Trust in COs
Trust encompasses diverse concepts and has been defined in different ways by many
researchers. Trust is a reliable relationship in which one party believes in another party's words,
promises, or fulfillment of duties (Anderson & Weitz, 1989; Moorman et al., 1993; Schurr &
Ozanne, 1985). It also represents one party's expectation that another party will not take advantage of the situation or relationship (Gefen et al., 2003). According to prior studies’ definitions,
trust is the expectation one party has that the other party will act in the interests of trustors
(Mayer et al., 1995).
Hansmann (1980) argued that nonprofit organizations provide a better quality of services
when information asymmetry exists between consumers and producers because they neither
seek profit maximization nor distribute extra income to stakeholders, which makes them more
trusted than profit-seeking organizations. In the same vein, potential donors' trust in the CO is
the basic condition of getting donations (Tonkiss & Passey, 1999). Potential donors can
make donations when they trust that donated money is used wisely and transparently.

Researchers suggested diverse dimensions that constitute the concept of trust (Becker, 2018;
Sirdeshmukh et al., 2002). Among them, Alhidari et al. (2018) explained trust in COs based on

Ability refers to the possession of knowledge, competencies, or skills needed to do something

social welfare and fulfill their obligations effectively (Pavlou & Dimoka, 2006). Benevolence
refers to what one party wants to do for the benefit of the other party with good intentions, with

as the belief in their activities—doing good or getting the right things done for the well-being of
society (Mayer et al., 1995; McKnight et al., 2002).

3.2 | Antecedents of donation intention
The three components reported in TPB—attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral
control—have been widely used to explain an individual's behavioral intention in various disciplines. We adopted TPB to investigate donation behavior by applying three antecedents of donation intention. First, attitudes toward donation refer to being interested in or having the
tendency to engage in donation behavior over time (Erlandsson et al., 2018). Directly relating to
people's various perceptions, and attitudes influence behavioral intention (Bandura, 1982). In
other words, a positive attitude toward donation behavior can directly lead to donation intention (Pauli et al., 2017; Susanto et al., 2021).
Second, subjective norms regarding donation refer to the extent of support from relevant
others about donation activities. Prior studies reported that subjective norms closely relate to
donation intention. (e.g., Croson et al., 2010; Greenslade & White, 2005). Also, Keating et al.
(1981) confirmed that individuals' donation intention varies depending on how much support
they get from peers or those around them, and explained that subjective norms can be experienced as coercion.
Lastly, perceived behavioral control is a factor directly influencing behavioral intention
(Hsu & Chiu, 2004), thus it is considered the key element of voluntary helping behaviors
(Greenslade & White, 2005). Perceived behavioral control regarding donations represents the
perception of one's ability to donate and perform a donation behavior (Bouchard &
Loehlin, 2001). Since the beliefs in one's financial ability influence donation behavior, the
income level relates positively to donation intention, and the current financial constraints are
considered a reason for not donating. Also, it is reported that the current income level and the
financial status in the future both positively influence attitudes and efforts toward donation
(e.g., Edmundson, 1986; Halfpenny, 1990). The perception of one's ability to engage in donation
behavior is not only influenced by financial competencies but also by diverse environmental
aspects. Self-determination or self-efficacy regarding donations is within the perceived behavioral control that can influence donation intention. Applying three antecedents of behavioral
intention suggested in TPB to the context of donations, the following relationship may be
presumed;
Hypothesis H1. Attitudes toward donation relate positively to the intention to
donate to COs.
Hypothesis H2. Subjective norms relate positively to the intention to donate
to COs.
Hypothesis H3. Perceived behavioral control relates positively to the intention to
donate to COs.

affects volitional element (behavioral intention). Consequently, an individual's behavior is the
result of all these elements working together (Fishbein & Ajzen, 1975, 1980). Behavioral beliefs

Motivation, the underlying reason to establish particular goals that give rise to actions
(Deci & Ryan, 1985), refers to the psychological tendency or intrinsic force that causes a person
to pursue goals by initiating, eliciting, and maintaining particular behaviors (Petri &
Govern, 2005). Motivation for donations has been explored from various perspectives, and it
contains both altruistic and egoistic aspects (Bendapudi et al., 1996). Altruistic motivation
comes from pure goodwill of helping or benefiting recipients without expecting rewards,
whereas egoistic motivation concerns the benefits given back to donors such as social recognition, honor, or respect that donors gain from their actions, tax incentives, etc.
However, motivations for donation are not dichotomized but are located somewhere
between altruism and egoism. If there are two closely located on a continuum from altruistic to
egoistic, the difference between them would not be noticeable. Although the extremes of altruistic and egoistic motivations are very different, an individual's behavior can fall anywhere on a
continuum from altruistic to egoistic. These discussions indicate that donation decisions are
made by the intertwined motivation of egoism and altruism, which is consistent with the
assumption that all human actions are different depending on situations to serve the best interests (Clary & Snyder, 1999). Several studies also mentioned that egoism and altruism are in contrast, but they can coexist (Yin et al., 2020). Neuroeconomic research using brain imaging
techniques reported the activity of VMPFC (Ventromedial prefrontal cortex), a brain region
activated when calculating the value associated with one's choice, in assessing the value of
donation decisions (Hare et al., 2010). This result indicates that egoistic choice and altruistic
choice share the same compensation and value computation mechanisms. What compensation
motivates a person to donate to others depends on an individual's choice. A donation motivated
by tax incentives is different from an altruism-based donation in terms of motivation, but its
outcome may be identical.
For example, people can experience satisfaction, enjoyment, or fulfillment from donations or
helping activities. Since these benefits are part of the egoistic achievement, it is hard to conclude
that donation behavior is purely driven by altruism (Howard, 1982). In other words, the pursuit
of socialization, experience, or enjoyment is the egoistic motivation for volunteering, whereas the
desire to help others or return favors belongs to altruistic motivation (Sundeen & Raskoff, 1995).
Harbaugh (1998a, 1998b) demonstrated that types of charities' announcements can make a difference in the behavior of donors and the size of donations when the motivation for donation is
prestige. He empirically showed that category reporting is effective in raising donations and
changing donors' behavior when donors are egoistically motivated by prestige or social status.
This result also explains why special events can draw more donors and increase donations. In
other words, the more prestige benefits or satisfaction donors enjoy from a fundraising event, the
more donations they can make, indicating that benefits gained from interacting with others or
social exchange serve as another egoistic motivation for donation (Inoue, 2016).
Helping acts, stemming from an egoistic motive may be the result of calculating gain and
loss from helping or not helping others (Piliavin et al., 1981). Therefore, the argument that
donation is only driven by altruistic motivation may cause disagreement. Instead, donors may
be influenced by altruistic and egoistic motivations in combination (Oh, 2016), and their attitudes, feelings, or tendency about donation, may change. Consequently, we propose the following hypotheses:

3.4 | Features of COs and formation of trust
Three features of COs—ability, integrity, and benevolence—significantly influence trust in COs
(Alhidari et al., 2018). Mayer et al. (1995) also mentioned organizational abilities as the most
vital feature in building trust in COs. In social work, the abilities of COs including their administrative skills and expertise in investing or leveraging the donated funds are considered an
essential element in attracting donations (Morgan & Hunt, 1994; Pavlou & Dimoka, 2006). Likewise, donors' trust in COs comes from their perception of CO's abilities to allocate and distribute donations correctly and appropriately to those in need. Since COs, like an agency, are
responsible for managing and using funds, their abilities are not limited to collecting donations
for those in need (Sargeant & Lee, 2002).
Trust in COs represents the extent to which donors are willing to donate money based on
the belief that COs' knowledge, expertise, and human resources will be appropriately managed
for intended purposes. Therefore, the following hypothesis is proposed:
Hypothesis H6. Perceived ability relates positively to trust in COs.
Another element of trust is integrity which refers to donors' beliefs that COs are dedicated to
their role while complying with the principles acceptable to donors (Mayer et al., 1995). In
other words, CO's integrity includes being consistent in words and actions and following high
ethical standards (Le Berre, 2010). People usually show great concern over the management of
funds by COs involved in unethical conduct (van Iwaarden et al., 2009). The amount of donations is clear evidence of the reliability of COs (Sargeant et al., 2006; Tonkiss & Passey, 1999).
When donors have trust in COs, they believe that COs are properly protecting and using charitable funds in accordance with ethical missions. Therefore, the following hypothesis is
proposed:
Hypothesis H7. Perceived integrity relates positively to trust in COs.
Benevolence refers to the belief in COs doing good for the interests of the public (Mayer
et al., 1995; McKnight et al., 2002), not for the benefit of an individual, to promote social welfare. Several studies already reported that benevolence positively influences building trust in
COs (Doney & Cannon, 1997). Bennett and Barkensjo (2005) confirmed that charitable acts for
those in need help donors trust COs and assess COs they support positively. According to Le
Berre (2010), high visibility, commitment to charitable actions, and beneficial achievements
contribute to generating donors' perceived benevolence of COs. Based on the previous research,
it may be presumed that COs' charitable acts to achieve the cause positively influence trust in
COs. Therefore, the following hypothesis is proposed:
Hypothesis H8. Perceived benevolence relates positively to trust in COs.

3.5 | Trust in COs, attitudes toward donation, and intention to donate
to COs
Trust in COs, reflecting a positive attitude toward COs, refers to the degree of donors' beliefs
that COs are working to serve the public interest as they expect. Previous studies discussed that
trust has a positive impact on donors' attitudes toward donation and donation intention
(Han, 2004; Lee, 2011).
Social cognitive theory, discussing the interaction between specific human behavior and
cognition, asserted that human perceptions and attitudes are interrelated and affect human
behavior (Bandura, 1982; Oosterhof & Todorov, 2009). As donating to COs is perceived as moral
behavior, a favorable perception about COs is critical in the decision of donations. Positive trust
in COs influences motivations and attitudes for donations (Lee & Jang, 2017) and plays a significant role in building a lasting relationship. Long-term fundraising campaigns are mostly based
on continued interaction between donors and COs, which results from trust (Kotler &
Anderson, 1987). Therefore, trust in COs determines whether it is a good charity or not
(Donovan, 2007). For example, Dwyer et al.'s (1987) research showed that those who believe
charitable funds are properly used by COs they support are less likely to stop giving even
though they face financial or personal constraints. Studies in charity marketing mentioned that
high levels of trust in COs relate closely to donors' willingness to continue to donate
(e.g., Bekkers, 2003; Saxton, 1995). Furthermore, Burt and Dunham's (2009) research reported
that trust in CO's websites correlates significantly with donors’ attention to online donations
and the amount they donate. Sargeant and Lee (2002) also confirmed that 13 percent of individual donors’ donations are subject to change depending on the levels of trust in COs. Moreover,
Sargeant and Hudson's (2008) research verified a positive relationship between trust in COs and
individuals’ donations by showing that donors with high trust in COs are more engaged in giving and volunteering than those with low trust.
Based on previous studies, we presumed how trust in COs relates to attitudes toward donation and donation intention, respectively, as follows.
Hypothesis H9. Trust in COs relates positively to attitudes toward donation.
Hypothesis H10. Trust in COs relates positively to the intention to donate to COs.

4 | METHODS
4.1 | Data collection
Donations take various forms, including money, time, service, or physical goods such as clothing, books, or equipment. Among them, monetary donations are considered the most popular
type in South Korea. As the rational choice theory contends (Freeman, 1997), between the two
alternatives of donations—giving money or time, Koreans prefer to choose money donations to
fulfill their duty as good citizens (Park et al., 2019). A survey reported that the money donation
category accounts for 61.6% of all charitable giving made by Koreans (Macromill Embrain
Trend Monitor, 2019).

online survey conducted for 2 weeks in August 2020. The agency we hired selected Korean

6 | DISCUSSION
6.1 | Summary of findings and implications
This study primarily examined what impact donors' trust in COs and attitudes toward donation
have on donation intention. Here is a summary of our findings.
First, built upon TPB, we considered attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral
control as the antecedents of individuals' donation intention. Attitudes, and emotional
responses to an object (Ajzen & Fishbein, 1980), were identified as the factor that has a critical influence on donation intention. In other words, an individual's positive or negative feeling toward donations leads to a vast difference in donation intention. Subjective norms are
defined as the aggregate of social pressures to conform with the expectations of others who
are important to a person. They guide a person to behave in a certain manner (Ajzen, 1991),
which is consistent with the finding of this study; an individual's subjective norms positively
influenced donation intention. Like Martin and Randal's (2008) experiment of a transparent
donation box, the behavior, support, or approval of important others serves as the subjective
norms that determine an individual's behavior. As well, this study found that one's perceived
behavioral control positively influences donation intention. Perceived behavioral control consists of subjective beliefs in one's ability to perform a behavior effectively and one's drive to
engage in a particular behavior. Thus, positively perceived behavioral control is more likely to
generate donation intention, which is in line with the previous research (i.e., Ajzen &
Madden, 1986).
Second, this study confirmed that altruistic motivation and egoistic motivation both positively influence attitudes toward donation, which is at odds with many previous studies that
reported a more significant impact of donors' altruistic motivation. As Howard (1982) discussed,
decision-making is based on a utility function used in a given situation; for instance, people
judge their options based on a different utility function depending on whether it is a private or
public matter. When it comes to donation, altruistic motivation refers to the desire to enhance
the public good, and egoistic motivation is described as the desire to get rewards from giving.
Both motivations play a positive role in motivating donation behavior (Steinberg, 1987). Likewise, this study found that a mix of motivations—egoism and altruism—positively encourages
charitable giving and volunteering.
Third, this study revealed that the three features of COs – ability, integrity, and
benevolence—have a significant influence on trust in COs, which is consistent with Alhidari
et al.'s (2018) research. Since COs are responsible for ensuring that money donated is properly
managed and used, their performance is one of the criteria that can sustain trust from donors.
CO's integrity refers to the extent to which COs behave consistently to serve their mission,
vision, and values. As a recent rise in charity-related fraud leads to the need for the committee
supervising COs, integrity is an essential factor in building donors’ trust in COs. CO's

Fourth, we found that trust in COs is a critical factor that has a positive influence on donation attitudes and donation intention. In other words, higher trust in COs leads to a positive
attitude toward the nonprofit sector, which provides a solid foundation for a mature philanthropic culture (Bekkers, 2003).
Here is a summary of the theoretical implications of this study: First, this study derived the
antecedents of donors' donation intention based on TPB and integrated them into three components of trust in COs. This approach is an extension of TPB by adding a comprehensive perspective to understanding the mechanisms of donation behavior.
Second, this study demonstrated that trust in COs leads to positive attitudes and intentions
toward donation. As the multi-dimensional attributes of COs are the foundation for building
trust in COs, they are the standard that shows how reliable the organizations are. Also, they are
the objective criteria to evaluate COs because COs' attributes reflect various factual information
on COs (Uslaner & Brown, 2005). Even when the expected consequences of donations are
uncertain, trustworthiness of COs plays a significant role in maintaining a good relationship
between donors and COs (Eggers et al., 2013; Gefen & Straub, 2004). Therefore, this study
implies that trust in charities can increase optimism toward the charitable sector, contributing
to strengthening the public trust in the sector and making society mature (Bekkers, 2003).
Third, this study tried to reflect on what drives individual donors to help others by considering a complementary interplay between altruistic and egoistic motivation. Prior studies before
the 1990s' reported that the nominal reasons for donating mostly come from altruism (Batson
et al., 1981; Macaulay & Leonard, 1970), but it is also true that donors decide to donate for personal benefits, such as inner satisfaction from feeling good, improved recognition or reputation
(Eisenberger et al., 1990: Rousseau & Parks, 1993). Hence, charities need to appeal to both emotion and reason to induce long-term giving from donors (Drucker, 1990).
Finally, we took a comprehensive approach by studying individual perspectives and perceptions toward COs combined and suggested four variables—trust in COs, attitudes toward donation, perceived behavioral control, and subjective norms—as the antecedents that directly affect
donation intention. We compared the path coefficients, standardized values calculated through
PLS-SEM, and the four paths between each variable and the dependent variable were statistically significant. Among them, the impact of attitudes was the greatest. Furthermore, trust in
COs had an indirect impact on donation intention by way of attitudes. These results show that
this study empirically verified the role of attitudes.
As for practical implications, this study suggested that three features of COs (multidimensional attributes of COs) are the antecedents of trust-building of donors, which provides
guidelines that help COs operate effectively and transparently. We confirmed that donors' trust
in COs' dedication to their roles honestly and fairly directly leads to generating donation intention, thereby promoting more donations from donors. COs are more trusted overall as they are
believed to better distribute public goods or services in a situation of information asymmetry,
and that's why COs’ influence on social service or medical service has been significant
(Hansmann, 1980). However, various unethical scandals have undermined public trust in COs.
Against this backdrop, this finding implies the need for informing donors of what COs are
doing continuously and transparently. For example, nonprofits are increasingly leveraging
videos to publicize their activities and communicate with their supporters. A website or SNS
platform is another tool to let donors know where their money is going and how the money is
used. By providing donors with accurate information promptly, COs can build a relationship of

6.2 | Limitations and future research
We recognize that this study has some limitations that need to be overcome in future studies.
First, we considered the independent variables of this study based on two different
dimensions—individual donors' perspectives and CO's organizational attributes. However,
donation intention may be influenced by various factors including donors’ behavioral factors.
Also, the influence of other external factors cannot be ruled out, including nonprofit brand
image (Michel & Rieunier, 2012), emotions used in charities’ advertising appeals (Kandrack &
Lundberg, 2014; Small & Verrochi, 2009), moral identity (Reed et al., 2007; Reed &
Aquino, 2003), and characteristics of beneficiaries (Smith et al., 2013). Especially, the impact of
donation motivations can vary depending on an individual's empathizing ability (Batson, 2011)
or happiness level (Batson et al., 2015; Thoits & Hewitt, 2001). Thus, future studies need to
explore donations motivated by genuine egoism or altruism and how those motivations are
formed.
Second, COs have recently offered various ways of donating to build a culture of philanthropy. For example, donating intangible assets like time or offering special skills pro bono is
considered valuable as cash donations. However, few attempts have been made to research
these kinds of donations (Chen et al., 2019). In particular, time donations are conducted differently from financial giving because time is an irreversible asset, and time donations happen regularly. Therefore, new and innovative means of giving need to be addressed theoretically in
future studies.
Lastly, this study only measured donation intention, not tracking whether it leads to donation behavior or how long it is sustained. Although donation intention was mentioned as a critical antecedent of donation behavior in several studies (Ahn et al., 2018; Kashif et al., 2015;
Smith & McSweeney, 2007), it is hard to ignore the intention-behavior gap completely because
various other factors may affect the transition from intention to behavior (Sheeran, 2002).
Therefore, future studies will be able to provide useful insights into the operation of COs if they
focus more on how the intention leads to donation behavior.
