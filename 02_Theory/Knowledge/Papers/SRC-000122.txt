   Various brand evaluation approaches assess the value and equity of for-profit brands; 
accordant approaches for nonprofit brands, however, have been limited, and there is disa-
greement on what makes up a strong brand in the nonprofit sector. In response, this article 
provides insights into the conceptualization and operationalization of stakeholder-based 
nonprofit brand equity and derives an initial measurement index. We conceptualize non-
profit brand equity as having three dimensions—nonprofit brand awareness, nonprofit 
brand trust, and nonprofit brand commitment—thereby empirically investigating trust in 
nonprofit brand equity building for the first time. The methodological procedure for build-
ing the index is based on partial least squares path modeling, and we draw on a sample of 
forty brands ( N  = 3,617 brand evaluations) identified as some of the best-known nonprofit 
brands in Germany. Applying the index yields some of the strongest German nonprofit 
brands; for example, German Red Cross has by far the highest value of brand equity, fol-
lowed by Aktion Mensch and  UNICEF . The nonprofit brand equity index provides the 
basis for nonprofit managers to compare their brands’ performance over time and develop 
accordant branding strategies; it can be also used by organizations from other countries.

   nonprofit brand awareness  ,   nonprofit brand trust  ,   nonprofit brand commitment

With regard to the nonprofit sector, however, Wymer, Gross, and Helmig (  2015  ) noted that 
there is uncertainty about what makes up a strong nonprofit brand, and despite the increasing 
awareness of brands’ potential in the nonprofit sector, research on brand equity in nonprofit 
organizations remains sparse (for example, Faircloth   2005  ; Juntunen, Juntunen, and Autere 
  2013  ; Laidler-Kylander and Simonin   2009  ). In response, we aim in this study to conceptualize 
and operationalize nonprofit brand equity, thereby developing an initial brand equity index for 
nonprofit organizations, which we test empirically with a large data set from Germany. 

 We contribute to the overall discussion on nonprofit brand equity in three ways. First, 
despite the increasing awareness of the relevance of branding in the nonprofit sector (Laidler-
Kylander and Stenzel   2013  ), insights into brand evaluation approaches on the strength of 
nonprofit brands have been limited, and most findings have come from market research and 
consulting firms. For example, the Nielsen Company (  2015  ) ranks a series of US nonprofit 
brands from different categories according to their brand equity, and Cone Communications 
(  2015  ), together with Intangible Business (  2015  ), assesses the hundred leading US nonprofit 
brands using a combination of financial and stakeholder-related data. The latter identifies 
YMCA of America, the Salvation Army, and United Way of America as the best-performing 
nonprofit organizations in terms of high nonprofit brand value (Cone Communications 
  2015  ). The underlying methodologies, however, are strongly and, most often, fully aligned 
with brand evaluations in the for-profit sector. It follows, then, that corresponding rankings 
fail to reflect the public ’ s response to cases of ethical misconduct in the nonprofit sector, such 
as YMCA ’ s commercialization issues or massive losses of trust and relevance of United Way 
over the past years (Rhode and Packel   2009  ; Weisbrod   2004  ). That is, these brand evalua-
tions mainly focus on brand equity from a financial perspective; yet financial performance 
is not the main objective in nonprofit organizations, and nonprofit scholars largely do not 
account for financial brand value, suggesting a stakeholder-based perspective instead. Accord-
ingly, this study conceptualizes and operationalizes nonprofit brand equity from a stake-
holder perspective, thereby contributing to the academic literature with initial insights into 
nonprofit brand evaluation approaches and accordant index construction. 

 Second, of the few existing academic studies on stakeholder-based nonprofit brand equity, 
almost all are based exclusively on for-profit models without any adaptation to the non-
profit context. Following Aaker ’ s (  1991  ) and Keller ’ s (  1993  ) common conceptualizations of 
customer-based brand equity, most nonprofit scholars have claimed that brand awareness, 
brand image, and brand personality are substantial dimensions of nonprofit brand equity 
(Becker-Olsen and Hill   2006  ; Faircloth   2005  ; Hou, Du, and Tian   2009  ; Juntunen et al. 
  2013  ). Laidler-Kylander and Simonin (  2009  ), however, recommend distinguishing between 
nonprofit and for-profit brand building. In accordance, we question the full transferability 
of (for-profit) dimensions of the brand equity construct to the nonprofit sector. In contrast 
to most prior studies, then, this study approaches stakeholder-based nonprofit brand equity 
from the perspective of specific organizations’ and brands’ characteristics in the nonprofit 
sector. We conceptualize the construct to include communication and relationship-oriented 
dimensions that are relevant in exchange processes with nonprofit stakeholders. 

 Third, most academic studies on stakeholder-based brand equity in the nonprofit sector 
have provided a measurement based on one or a few selected nonprofit brands. For exam-
ple, Becker-Olsen and Hill (  2006  ) investigated the brand equity of two selected nonprofit 
brands, while other scholars examined single nonprofit brands with a case study approach

 In the next section, we provide an introduction to nonprofit brands and the dominant brand 
equity perspectives in for-profit and nonprofit sectors that lead to our proposed conceptual 
framework. The methodological procedure for building the index is based on partial least 
squares (PLS) path modeling and a reflective–formative measurement approach, respec-

many. After presenting the empirical results of our study, we discuss the findings and derive 
implications for researchers and provide nonprofit managers with practical suggestions to 
help strengthen their nonprofit brands.  

  Conceptualization of Nonproﬁ t Brand Equity 
 To conceptualize stakeholder-based nonprofit brand equity, we first introduce a definition of 
nonprofit brands, and show the dominant brand equity perspectives in for-profit and non-
profit sectors. This provides the basis for our nonprofit brand equity framework, with its three 
dimensions: (1) nonprofit brand awareness, (2) nonprofit brand trust, and (3) nonprofit brand 
commitment. 

  Deﬁ nition of Nonproﬁ t Brand 
 The role of brand in the nonprofit sector has significantly changed over the past years, and 
nonprofit scholars and practitioners both recognize a paradigm shift in nonprofit branding 
(Kent   2015  ; Laidler-Kylander and Stenzel   2013  ). The “old” paradigm mainly considers the 
nonprofit brand a fundraising or communication tool, with a focus on the tangible elements of 
the brand such as its name or logo; more recently, the brand has risen to the level of a strategic 
asset (Kylander and Stone   2012  ). In this function, the nonprofit brand assumes a broader role 
in the organization, with the potential to internally provide guidance and further cohesion 
among staff and volunteers while externally offering orientation to various stakeholders (Han-
kinson   2004  ; Laidler-Kylander and Simonin   2009  ). Thereby, the brand functions as a source 
of efficiency that signals the organizational core values and principles to external stakeholders, 
allowing them to draw conclusions about the organization (such as regarding its trustworthi-
ness and reputation) and, ultimately, its ability to further the mission. The nonprofit brand 
accordingly helps simplify stakeholder decision making and reduces risk in an increasingly 
complex environment (Laidler-Kylander and Stenzel   2013  ).

 In line with the strategic understanding of brands, Kapferer (  2012  , 12) defines a brand as 
“a name that symbolizes a long term engagement, crusade, or commitment to a unique set 
of values, embedded into products, services, and behaviors, which make the organization, 
person or product stand apart or stand out.” In a similar vein, and given the brand ’ s specific 
characteristics in the nonprofit sector, we define a nonprofit brand as  one or more tangible 
elements that symbolize the nonprofit organization ’ s dedication to a unique set of values and its 
ability to achieve an overall mission that enables it to stand out from other organizations in the 
nonprofit sector.   

  Perspectives of Nonproﬁ t Brand Equity 
 The brand equity construct has been approached mainly from two perspectives. The first per-
spective refers to the financial (monetary) value of the brand, or what is known as an intangible 
asset in financial terms (for example, Salinas   2011  ; Simon and Sullivan   1993  ). This perspec-
tive considers the brand ’ s equity as the amount the brand is worth as ownership or as ongoing 
future income flows (Schultz   2003  ). Many popular brand valuation models, such as those 
from Brand Finance (  2015  ) or Millward Brown (  2015  ), adopt such a financial-based brand 
equity perspective and accordingly refer to the term “brand value.” In nonprofit organiza-
tions, however, financial performance is not the main objective, and scholars have claimed that 
financial-based measures are insufficient by themselves for evaluating their performance and 
success (Leipnitz   2014  ; Garven, Hofmann, and McSwain   2016  ). Rather, nonprofit organiza-
tions primarily rely on legitimacy and resources from stakeholders, which makes a stakeholder-
based evaluation approach more appropriate (Shea and Hamilton   2015  ). 

 The second perspective focuses on the psychological value of the nonprofit brand, thereby 
centering the customer ’ s or, in our context, the stakeholder ’ s subjective perceptions of and 
attitudes toward the brand. This stakeholder-based brand equity perspective acknowledges 
the stakeholder ’ s important role in the branding process; Schultz (  2000  , 9) has noted that 
“the true value or equity of the brand resides in the customer ’ s valuation.” Related brand eval-
uation approaches attempt to measure how much value the stakeholder puts on the brand 
using attitudinal and behavioral measurement quantities (Schultz   2000  ). Aaker ’ s (  1991  ) and 
Keller ’ s (  1993  ) conceptualizations have had a decisive influence on this perspective. Aaker 
(  1991  , 15) described brand equity as “a set of brand assets and liabilities linked to a brand, its 
name and symbol that add to or subtract from the value provided by a product or service to 
a firm and/or to that firm ’ s consumers”; conversely, Keller (  1993  ) highlighted the differential 
effect of brand knowledge on stakeholder perceptions, preferences, and behavior arising from 
branding efforts. 

 Moreover, a few other brand evaluation approaches combine both perspectives, including 
stakeholders’ perceptions of the brands and financial measures (for example,  Interbrand  
2015; Nielsen Company   2015  ). In general, Schultz (  2003  ) has observed brand evaluation 
approaches from a broad spectrum, with a stakeholder-based perspective at one end (stake-
holder-based brand equity) and a financial-based perspective at the other end (brand value); 
brand equity in for-profit sectors has been equally approached from both perspectives. In 
contrast, considering the stakeholder ’ s important role in the nonprofit sector, nonprofit 
scholars have almost exclusively examined nonprofit brand equity from a stakeholder-based 
perspective (for example, Faircloth   2005  ; Hou et al.   2009  ; Juntunen et al.   2013  ) or accord-
ing to internal structures (Laidler-Kylander and Simonin   2009  ). Stakeholder-based nonprofit 
brand equity is also closely related to the concept of nonprofit brand strength (Wymer et al.

  Nonproﬁ t Brand Equity Framework 
 Figure   1   illustrates our nonprofit brand equity framework. Considering that a stakeholder-
based perspective is more appropriate in nonprofit brand building, the outlined framework 
focuses on stakeholder-based brand equity only and refrains from assessing nonprofit brand 
value from a financial approach. Not only do stakeholders play a central role in the non-
profit sector, but organizations are also confronted with many stakeholder groups, including 
resource providers, the general public, government, and other subgroups. In this study, we 
particularly refer to the stakeholder in terms of the general public and accordingly conceptual-
ize stakeholder-based nonprofit brand equity as having three dimensions: (1) nonprofit brand 
awareness, (2) nonprofit brand trust, and (3) nonprofit brand commitment. When evaluating 
brands, the first relevant dimension is its awareness among the public—that is, brand aware-
ness is essential for establishing and strengthening brand-specific associations and building 
familiarity with the brand (Aaker   1991  ). As such, brand awareness is a substantial dimension 
of nonprofit brand equity (Faircloth   2005  ; Juntunen et al.   2013  ).

(  2005  ), who first proposed this conceptualization, hypothesized that people are more likely

brand traits such as trustworthiness and a brand ’ s background in allowing stakeholders to feel 
more comfortable with the brand. He accordingly operationalizes brand personality by con-

and other nonprofit scholars who build on this conceptualization, we believe that a brand ’ s 
image and personality are of particular relevance for attracting potential resource providers in 
the nonprofit sector; yet evaluating brand equity in the nonprofit sector in terms of general 
positive associations with the brand might not be sufficient. Rather, we argue that the organi-
zation ’ s and brand ’ s specific characteristics in the nonprofit sector should be integrated more 
strongly, and thus we view public trust in brands, as a dimension of nonprofit brand equity, 
as a more thorough conceptualization. These conceptual considerations are consistent with 
findings from nonprofit governance literature that show that nonprofit organizations are par-
ticularly dependent on public trust because of their social-oriented and value-based missions 
( Kearns    2014  ; Prakash and Gugerty   2010  ). Besides, a series of recent media scandals in the 
nonprofit sector have reinforced the awareness of the high importance of public trust. 

 Although we consider brand awareness and brand trust substantial dimensions of nonprofit 
brand equity, these dimensions alone cannot sufficiently account for nonprofit brand equity, 
because they do not reflect the brand ’ s ability to develop a stronger relationship (for example, 
in terms of high engagement) with stakeholders, which has been found to be of particular 
importance in the nonprofit sector. Therefore, we include nonprofit brand commitment in 
our conceptual framework. Several scholars, mostly in for-profit literature, have highlighted 
the relevance of relationship-related dimensions such as loyalty and commitment in the 
brand equity context, acknowledging that to build strong brands, stakeholders must feel close 
and attached to the brand (Aaker   1991  ; Camarero et al.   2010  ; Lassar, Mittal, and Sharma 
  1995  ; Martin and Brown   1990  ). In the nonprofit sector, a relationship orientation has been 
a priority because nonprofit organizations are highly dependent on strong relationships with 
stakeholders, which translate into positive outcomes, including donations of time and money 
(Arnett, German, and Hunt   2003  ; Boenigk and Scherhag   2014  ). Because this article consid-
ers stakeholders in terms of the general public, we integrate nonprofit brand commitment 
in our framework, instead of the more frequently used concept of brand loyalty, because we 
focus on the perceptual dimensions of nonprofit brand equity. These conceptual consider-
ations are consistent with Martin and Brown ’ s (  1990  ) and Lassar et al.’s (  1995  ) conceptual-
ization of commitment as a perceptual rather than a behavioral dimension of brand equity. 
Each nonprofit brand equity dimension ultimately builds on the previous dimension, similar 
to the relationship cycle between the stakeholder and the brand. Following, we clarify the 
underlying rationale of each nonprofit brand equity dimension. 

  Nonprofit Brand Awareness 

 Nonprofit brand awareness constitutes an elementary dimension of nonprofit brand equity 
(Faircloth   2005  ; Juntunen et al.   2013  ) and is considered a prerequisite for further engagement 
with the brand, such as the development of brand trust (Keller   2003  ).  Brand awareness  refers 
to the stakeholder ’ s ability to recall and recognize the brand (Rossiter and Percy   1987  ) and thus 
indicates the strength of the anchor point of the brand in memory (Aaker   1991  ). Accordingly, 
widespread public awareness is essential for nonprofit organizations, and a well-known non-
profit brand can serve stakeholders through its signaling function as an orientation aid (with 
respect to the organization ’ s values and for decisions on whether to support the organization 
or not) (Ritchie, Swami, and Weinberg   1999  ). Yet a strong nonprofit brand might be well

for stakeholders (for example, in the case of a nonprofit scandal) and therefore be rather weak

that a strong nonprofit brand must also be perceived favorably, which leads into our second

  Nonprofit Brand Trust 

 Considering the prominent role of trust in discussions about governance and accountability in 
the nonprofit sector, high levels of trust are necessary for nonprofit organizations because their 
behavior and performance are often difficult to observe (Kearns   2014  ; Prakash and Gugerty 
  2010  ). It follows that stakeholders rely heavily on public trust when interacting with nonprofit 
organizations, such that they must be able to rely on the organization to remain true to its 
promised values and to carry out its mission successfully. Regarding the nondistribution con-
straint (Hansmann   1980  ), according to which nonprofits are not allowed to distribute profit 
to private parties, the existence of nonprofit organizations is assumed to be based on trust 
inherent in nonprofits, and people are more likely to trust nonprofit than for-profit organiza-
tions because of both their mission orientation and the nondistribution constraint. Within 
this context, we argue that brand trust is related to accountability, with the brand ’ s function to 
reduce stakeholders’ risk of engaging in disagreeable exchange relationships with the organiza-
tion (Keller   2003  ). 

 Despite its crucial role in the nonprofit sector, trust has hitherto played a minor role in the 
nonprofit brand equity discussion. Consistent with the aforementioned considerations, this 
article views brand trust as a central dimension of nonprofit brand equity. In an explorative 
study, Laidler-Kylander and Simonin (  2009  ) provided first insights into the importance of 
trust. They proposed an initial model of nonprofit brand equity and identified trust as a 
key dimension that drives brand equity in international nonprofit organizations and helps 
differentiate nonprofits from competitors. Regarding the development of strong stake-
holder–brand relationships, scholars have claimed that in addition to trust, emotional close-
ness is relevant, and studies have found that nonprofit brand trust affects the emotional and 
relationship-related nonprofit brand commitment (Sargeant and Lee   2004  ), which leads into 
our third dimension.  

  Nonprofit Brand Commitment 

 When stakeholders become emotionally attached to a nonprofit brand, they form a relation-
ship with it (Sargeant and Lee   2004  ) and develop a sense of commitment. Nonprofit brand 
commitment reflects a person ’ s attitude and behavior toward the organization based on a 
strong belief in and acceptance of an organization ’ s goals and is characterized by “an enduring 
desire to maintain a valued relationship” (Moorman, Zaltman, and Deshpandé   1992  , 316). 
Therefore, brand commitment and other related relationship dimensions, such as brand loy-
alty, are of particular importance in the context nonprofit brand equity (Camarero et al.   2010  ). 

 Nonprofit managers and scholars have focused extensively on the management of relation-
ships, primarily with donors, in the past and treated the development of strong organiza-
tion–stakeholder relationships with priority (Boenigk and Scherhag   2014  ). That is, strong 
exchange relationships enhance the strategic options for nonprofit managers, encouraging 
them to cooperate with stakeholders and maintain a long-term orientation that subsequently 
promotes the organization ’ s performance (Kemp, Jillapalli, and Becerra   2014  ). Arnett et al.’s

 Taken together, our conceptual framework ultimately builds on the assumption that non-
profit brand awareness, nonprofit brand trust, and nonprofit brand commitment contribute 
to stakeholder-based brand equity in the nonprofit sector. Accordingly, high nonprofit brand 
equity, which equals strong nonprofit brands, occurs when all three components are high by 
themselves, and organizations with corresponding traits have been found to enjoy higher rep-
utations (Mitchell   2015  ). It follows that a strong brand indicates that the organization is well 
known by a multitude of stakeholders, and that people believe in the organization ’ s trustwor-
thiness and credibility and are ultimately dedicated to the organization. Considering these 
components, we argue that high brand equity in nonprofit organizations acts as an additional 
safeguard and reinforcement to the public of what the nondistribution constraint is supposed 
to do. Precisely, recent cases of fraud and mismanagement in the nonprofit sector have chal-
lenged the notion of inherent trust and credibility in nonprofit organizations, so that schol-
ars doubt the power of the nondistribution constraint to successfully mitigate the effects of 
these scandals (Prakash and Gugerty   2010  ). It follows that the nondistribution constraint by 
itself may not fully prevent opportunistic behavior, and nonprofit organizations might use 
additional mechanisms to signal credibility. By providing stakeholders a “shortcut” of what 
the organization is about (Laidler-Kylander and Stenzel   2013  ), the brand provides valuable 
information and thereby reduces stakeholders’ risk of engaging in disagreeable exchange rela-
tionships with the organization (Keller   2003  ). Accordingly, the nonprofit brand functions as 
a kind of trust and quality seal. Given the increasing number of cases of ethical misconduct 
in the nonprofit sector, strong brands can protect against negative spillover effects caused by 
these cases, and the organizations are less susceptible to risk.    

  Operationalization of Nonproﬁ t Brand Equity 
 To operationalize stakeholder-based nonprofit brand equity, we conducted a two-stage meth-
odological approach, measuring the nonprofit brand equity dimensions in the first step and 
evaluating the overall construct of nonprofit brand equity in the second step. This procedure 
led to our proposed brand equity index for nonprofit organizations. 

  Measurement of Nonproﬁ t Brand Equity Dimensions 
 Building on established scales, we measured the three nonprofit brand equity dimensions (see 
Table   1  ). We operationalized nonprofit brand awareness as the recall and recognition dimen-
sion of the brand that can be assessed through unaided and aided measures. Therefore, we 
measured unaided brand awareness by means of an open-ended question; for aided brand 
awareness, we showed respondents the brand names and logos of all nonprofit organizations, 
using a dichotomous question. We further operationalized brand trust and brand commitment 
using a reflective measurement approach and seven-point rating scales anchored by “Strongly 
disagree” (1) and “Strongly agree” (7).

 Accordingly, we first included brand awareness in the index by using the percentages of the 
awareness scale that is shown in Table   3  . This approach is consistent with nonprofit and for-
profit branding literature stating that brand awareness is a precondition for further interac-
tion with the brand and acts as a basis for a stakeholder–brand relationship. Considering the 
overall low recall rates of brands and organizations in the nonprofit sector, we primarily focus 
on the aided brand awareness dimension because it is regarded a more robust measure in this 
case. For the relationship-related nonprofit brand equity dimensions, that is, brand trust and 
brand commitment, we then calculated weights using a reflective-formative measurement 
approach based on prior studies on index building (for example, Gardó et al. 2014; Guinot, 
Latreille, and Tenenhaus   2001  ; see also Becker, Klein, and Wetzels   2012  ; Diamantopoulos, 
Riefler, and Roth   2008  ; Diamantopoulos and Winklhofer   2001  ; Interbrand 2015). The use 
of formative indicators to operationalize marketing constructs has gained increasing impor-
tance over the past years (Diamantopoulos   2008  ), and is particularly appropriate when 
the index does not exist as an independent entity, and is rather a composite measure that 
includes several dimensions. Precisely, the approach involves estimating a hierarchical (sec-
ond-order) formative measurement model (with reflective first-order dimensions in our case), 
to capture distinct factors that jointly describe the formatively measured construct. That 
is, “relationship-related nonprofit brand equity” equals an overall index variable (Diaman-
topoulos and Winklhofer   2001  ) and is created from the concatenation of the relationship-
related brand equity dimensions nonprofit brand trust and nonprofit brand commitment. 
We particularly used a repeated indicator approach because of its ability to estimate all con-
structs simultaneously (Becker et al.   2012  ; Lohmöller   1989  ). We estimated the model with 
PLS path modeling (with SmartPLS3; Ringle, Wende, and Becker   2014  ), which provided 
the weights for trust and commitment in the construction of the index. In addition to the 
respective weights obtained through the analysis, we used the trust and commitment mean 
values of each nonprofit brand to calculate the brands’ individual index scores. To increase 
the readability of the index and set the highest index score to 100, the value was multiplied 
by 100 in a final step.  

  Application of the Nonproﬁ t Brand Equity Index 
 To apply the nonprofit brand equity index, we conducted two successive, quantitative online 
studies. These enabled us to ultimately provide a ranking of forty nonprofit brands according 
to their brand equity index scores. 

  Study Design 
 In May 2014, we conducted the first stage of data collection (pre-study) to generate a com-
prehensive list of the best-known German nonprofit brand names. We collaborated with the 
research laboratory of the university, surveying a random student sample ( N  = 114) to inves-
tigate the brand awareness of 216 preselected German and international (with headquarters 
in Germany) nonprofit brands. The preselection of brand names was based on several sources 
(Deutscher Spendenrat 2014; Deutsches Zentralinstitut für Soziale Fragen [DZI] 2009; Gesell-
schaft für Konsumforschung [GfK] 2009). 

 For the second stage (main study), we split our list into well-known ( N  = 108) and less well-
known nonprofit organizations, according to their degree of brand awareness (top-of-mind, 
unaided, and aided brand awareness) from the pre-study. All subsequent assessments were

then based on the well-known brands (that is, 108 brand names). In July 2014, we collabo-

survey was representative with respect to age, gender, and region/federal state for the German 
population according to the Census Bureau ( N  = 2,052; US Census Bureau   2014  ; to collect 
representative samples, the market research company usually refers to the international data-

measurement quality, we excluded 366 respondents because they gave the same answer to all 
assessed nonprofit brand dimensions. For index application, we included only brands with 
more than twenty evaluations, as otherwise no valid results would be obtained; this resulted 
in a total number of forty assessed nonprofit brands; yielding 3,617 brand evaluations.

RTL Children in Need Foundation [22.04], German Children ’ s Fund [18.86]), and nonprofit 
brands of foundations that fight diseases (for example, German AIDS Foundation [18.76], 
German Heart Foundation [17.85]) receive comparatively high index scores as well. 

        Discussion 
 This study contributes to the ongoing debate by conceptualizing and operationalizing non-
profit brand equity and by introducing an accordant measurement index. As such, it offers 
novel contributions to research in nonprofit branding, and valuable recommendations for 
nonprofit managers and leaders; still, it has some limitations that provide the basis for further 
research.

operationalizes the construct by developing an initial brand equity index for nonprofit organi-
zations. Against this background, this study contributes to recent branding discussions in the 
nonprofit sector in three main ways. 

 First, we addressed the limited research on nonprofit brand building, supplementing prior 
studies that focused on the composition of nonprofit brands in terms of brand strength and 
brand equity (Faircloth   2005  ; Hou et al.   2009  ; Laidler-Kylander and Simonin   2009  ; Wymer 
et al.   2015  ). From a review of for-profit and nonprofit branding and relationship literature, 
and building on discussions on nonprofit governance and accountability, we conceptualized 
nonprofit brand equity as having three dimensions: nonprofit brand awareness, nonprofit 
brand trust, and nonprofit brand commitment. Although dimensions of brand awareness 
and relationship-related constructs such as brand commitment have played a prominent role 
in nonprofit brand equity research, this study contributes to the nonprofit brand equity dis-
cussion by acknowledging the high importance of trust (Kearns   2014  ). High levels of public 
trust are indispensable in the nonprofit sector, but trust has received less attention in the 
nonprofit brand equity discussion. In accordance with findings from explorative nonprofit 
brand equity research (Laidler-Kylander and Simonin   2009  ), we found that public trust in 
brands plays a major role in nonprofit brand building, thereby advancing conceptual and 
empirical research in this area. In view of the limited research on conceptualizations of non-
profit brand equity that considers brands’ and organizations’ special characteristics in the 
nonprofit sector (Laidler-Kylander and Simonin   2009  ), this study provides initial insights 
into the nonprofit brand equity concept and therefore is not without restrictions. Other 
nonprofit scholars should verify the proposed framework by conducting further empirical 
research in the field. 

 Second, prior research has operationalized measures of nonprofit brand equity on the basis of 
different brand dimensions, using point-rating scales (for example, Faircloth   2005  ; Hou et al. 

Nonproﬁ t Management & Leadership      DOI: 10.1002/nml

  2009  ) that aid in summing up and calculating the averages of each dimension. Prior studies 
and practical investigations (for example, Gardó et al.   2014  ; Interbrand 2015) largely regard 
weighting methods of the respective dimensions as an adequate approach for operational-
izing overall constructs such as nonprofit brand equity. In this sense, and with the advent of 
the use of formative indicators (Diamantopoulos   2008  ), we used a second-order formative 
measurement model, thereby capturing the distinct brand dimensions. Because brand aware-
ness is considered a precondition in nonprofit brand building, we measured brand aware-
ness as an aided measure, so we cannot account for its weight and, thus, its importance for 
overall brand equity and relative to the other dimensions. Although such measures are widely 
accepted in academic literature, they exhibit limited applicability; further research could 
include point-rating scales to account for the importance of this dimension (see Wymer et al. 
  2015  ). 

 Third, we address the lack of studies on nonprofit brand evaluation approaches by introduc-
ing an initial index that supports a stakeholder-based perspective and provides important 
insights into the strength of brands. We built the nonprofit brand equity index using external 
stakeholders’ assessment of the strength of their familiarity and relationship with the brand. 
The higher the overall index score, the higher are these stakeholders’ assessments of brand 
equity. Based on the fact that we applied the index to a sample of forty nonprofit brands, 
there is potential for applying the index to larger organizational samples and for longitudinal 
measurements.  

  Implications 
 This study provides the basis for several useful recommendations for nonprofit managers and 
leaders. As a first managerial implication, we encourage nonprofit managers to apply a more 
strategic approach to the practice of nonprofit branding. Engaging in nonprofit branding 
efforts is a worthwhile endeavor for nonprofit organizations because strong nonprofit brands 
are generally related to important organizational outcomes. Accordingly, we advise managers 
to gain insights into nonprofit brand equity measurement related to brand awareness, brand 
trust, and brand commitment. Strong exchange relationships with trusting and committed 
stakeholders can enhance the strategic options for nonprofit managers and encourage them 
to cooperate with stakeholders and maintain a long-term orientation (Kemp et al.   2014  ). The 
considered dimensions build on each other and, by focusing on all dimensions together, man-
agers can enhance the public ’ s attitude toward the nonprofit brand and improve its strategic 
position in the market. 

 Moreover, achieving high brand equity can protect organizations against negative spillover 
effects occurred from scandals in the nonprofit sector. As such, strong brands (with high 
equity) can function partly as a trust and quality seal. 

 Our proposed nonprofit brand equity index provides an integrated performance indicator 
for both nonprofit managers and stakeholders; it is designed to be manageable but still yield 
meaningful results. As such, the index offers significant insights into the three important 
strategic brand dimensions. It thereby shows to what degree the nonprofit brand is able to 
function as an additional safeguard to the public in terms of a positive public attitude toward 
the organization, particularly relating to credibility and trustworthiness. Managers could use 
the overall index score as well as the respective single results for strategic decision making 
because it provides the basis for nonprofit branding strategies and measures. For example, if

a nonprofit brand scores high on brand awareness but low on brand trust and brand com-

 Furthermore, nonprofit managers can conduct comparisons with other nonprofit brands 
and for comparisons over time. Brand evaluations may serve as a compass in nonprofit orga-
nizations that have an ultimate goal of obtaining an optimal positioning while maintaining 
strong exchange relationships with stakeholders.
