Abstract
Purpose – The purpose of this paper is to: distinguish between ﬁnancial brand equity and perceptual
brand equity; distinguish among different stakeholder perspectives on customer-based brand equity,
(consumer, customer, employee and resident); contrast the
consumer-based brand equity (CBBE)
original deﬁnitions and measures of CBBE by Aaker and Keller;
identify the deviations from
the original conceptualizations and measures of CBBE by Aaker and Keller; discuss the evolution of the
most commonly used CBBE components; analyze the structural relations of the most common CBBE
components and generate conceptual, structural, relational and methodological suggestions for future
research.
Design/methodology/approach – This critical review conducted a narrative analysis of the
perceptual brand equity literature (CBBE) by inventorying about 200 empirical and conceptual CBBE
studies in several different contexts. Studies that included CBBE, brand equity, customer-based brand
equity and consumer-based brand equity were included in this review. Only 87 representative studies
that either conceptually deﬁned or empirically measured CBBE and its components were included in the
deep analysis.
Findings – The review revealed that the literature is divergent in terms of CBBE components or structural
relations among components in any context including tourism and hospitality. Even though about 40
different CBBE components exist in different contexts, the totality of the CBBE literature reﬂects a consensus
on ﬁve components of CBBE, brand familiarity/brand awareness, brand image/brand associations, perceived
quality, consumer value and brand loyalty, all of which collectively deﬁne the total meanings of a brand from
consumer/customer perspective.
Research limitations/implications – Keeping these ﬁve components intact, this study suggests a
concise CBBE deﬁnition, conceptual clariﬁcations for these components, a model reﬂecting their structural
relations and a framework of parsimonious measures. The study makes future research recommendations in
terms of using uniform CBBE components, measures and relational structure among components, identifying
the relationship between CBBE and ﬁnancial-based brand equity and comparing different stakeholder
perspectives on CBBE in future research.
Originality/value – With these observations and suggestions, this critical review provides a
guideline for more robust theory development of the CBBE construct. Additionally,
it offers a
parsimonious and practical CBBE blueprint for the practitioners who include CBBE as a strategic
market metric in their marketing and research plans. Furthermore, it suggests standardization in CBBE
research to enable systematic reviews with meta-analysis of the CBBE literature in the future. Using
standard components, measures and relational models in CBBE research, as was suggested in this
study, would allow meta-analysis for a meaningful comparison of results between different brands,
products and even industries.

Keywords Brand equity, CBBE, Brand loyalty, Brand image, Consumer value, Perceived quality

Introduction
Brand equity is considered a critical indicator of a brand’s ﬁnancial success, hence, has
received academic and practitioner attention for about three decades, which is reﬂected in
the sheer volume of research. It has been deﬁned and measured in many different ways
depending on the perspective taken on the brand. These different perspectives can be
grouped into two general categories, namely, ﬁnancial-based brand equity (FBBE) and
perception-based brand equity (CBBE). Earlier studies on brand equity were more focused
on the ﬁnancial-based indicators or market behavior of the ﬁrm such as cash ﬂows, cost
savings, sales and prices (Blattberg and Wisniewski, 1989; Farquhar, 1989; Kamakura and
Russell, 1993; Swait et al., 1993; Wentz, 1989; Yovovich, 1988). Some of these early studies
used brand equity and brand value interchangeably, as they equated brand equity to the
total worth of a ﬁrm (Kamakura and Russell, 1993; Wentz, 1989; Yovovich, 1988). Overall,
the literature indicates that successfully branded products have better consumer perceptions
and a long term competitive advantage over unbranded products through price premiums
(actual prices), higher market shares, shareholder values, cash ﬂows, revenues, proﬁts and
lower costs and price elasticities, (Blattberg and Wisniewski, 1989; Doyle, 2001; Keller and
Lehmann, 2003; Murphy, 1989; Swait et al., 1993; Vazquez et al., 2002; Yovovich, 1988).
Thus, in the 90 s, perception-based indicators, from perspectives of consumers, customers
and employees, started receiving attention.

The foundation of the CBBE framework was set by the general marketing researchers
Aaker (1991, 1996) and Keller (1993, 2003), who delineated CBBE as a conglomerate
construct inclusive of several cognitive, affective and conative responses of consumers as a
result of the branding activities of a ﬁrm. The potential of CBBE’s inﬂuence on FBBE fueled
ample attention into perception-based measures of brand equity (Christodoulides and de
Chernatony, 2010; Kandampully et al., 2018; Keller, 1993; Keller and Brexendorf, 2019;
Lassar et al., 1995). Aaker’s (1991, 1996) and Keller’s (1993, 2003) deﬁnitions and measures
have been applied in several product contexts including hospitality and tourism products
(destinations, hotels, events and restaurants). However, the literature shows different
practices in terms of the components and measurements of CBBE, as well as the relational
structure of its components.

Some researchers conducted literature reviews on CBBE or its components individually
and made recommendations. For example, Christodoulides and de Chernatony (2010)
focused on direct and indirect measures of CBBE, based on studies until 2009. On the other
hand, Tasci (2016a) delved into the consumer value as a component of CBBE rather than
analyzing CBBE in its entirety. The CBBE research gained momentum in the past decade
especially in tourism and hospitality, showing persisting divergences in conceptualization
and measurement of CBBE, calling for critical analysis and reconstruction of the CBBE
framework by clarifying conceptual and methodological
issues and suggesting more
standard measures for future research. The current study aims to achieve this goal by
providing critical analysis and synthesis of the CBBE literature. Thus, many different
studies of CBBE in different contexts are included in this critical review to capture the
original and evolved nature of CBBE. The speciﬁc objectives of this critical review are:

Method
The purpose of this review is not to ﬁnd new facts or ﬁndings but digest, sift, classify and
synthesize the facts and ﬁndings of past research (Manten, 1973, p. 75). Thus, this critical
review is conducted with a narrative review where logical reasoning is used to analyze and
synthesize past research. Using Cooper’s (1988), Taxonomy of Literature Reviews, the
characteristics of this review can be captured in six distinctions:

(1) The Focus of this review is the CBBE theory as reﬂected in the methods and

ﬁndings of past research.

(2) The Goal of this review is criticism or to “critically analyze the existing literature”
(Cooper et al., 2019, p. 5) by comparing the treatment of the CBBE construct in
different studies to those of the originating works of Aaker and Keller.

(3) The Perspective is pivoted on the original CBBE theory as suggested by Aaker and

Keller to identify the development with consistency or divergence.

(4) The Coverage is representative, as a representative body of literature was
identiﬁed and included in the review to show both consistency and divergence in
the development of CBBE. A critical review requires a comprehensive analysis of
literature including the originating ﬁeld and others that adopted it. CBBE is a
concept designed and popularized by the foundational studies of Aaker and Keller
in marketing and evolved by its applications in diverse industries including the
tourism and hospitality industry, with its hotel, destination, restaurant and event
contexts. The included “exemplars [. . .] are descriptive of many other works”
(Cooper et al., 2019, p. 6) in different ﬁelds to identify the contextual differences
reﬂected in the CBBE construct.

(5) The Organization of

the review is historical, as well as conceptual and
methodological; where the CBBE concept is introduced chronologically, with the
original works of Aaker and Keller followed by the studies listed chronologically in
a table reﬂecting both the conceptual and methodological comparison of CBBE to
those original works.

(6) The Audience is both the scholars who investigate CBBE or its components, as well as

those practitioners who use CBBE as a metric in their marketing research and plans.

databases including ProQuest, EBSCOhost, JSTOR, Web of Science, Science Direct and
journals. For effective search results, the search was

crystalized in the ﬁrst half of the 90 s, initiated by the inﬂuential studies of Aaker (1991, 1992,

equity with ﬁnancial metrics such as cash ﬂows, cost savings, sales and prices (Farquhar, 1989;
Kamakura and Russell, 1993; Swait et al., 1993; Wentz, 1989). Afterward, the focus shifted to
the perception-based brand equity with perceptual metrics such as awareness, image, perceived
quality, perceived value and loyalty (Aaker, 1991, 1992, 1996; Keller, 1993, 2003; King and
Grace, 2009; Lassar et al., 1995; Netemeyer et al., 2004). The perception-based brand equity was
also identiﬁed by different measures, including indirect measures focusing on cognitive metrics
(Aaker, 1991, 1992, 1996; Keller, 1993, 2003) or direct measures focusing on economic metrics
such as the willingness to pay higher prices (Ailawadi et al., 2003; Barwise et al., 1990).
Although there is an ample amount of studies on FBBE and CBBE separately, there is a lack of
studies combining the two. A recent study by Tasci (2020) attempted to compare several city-
level tourist destinations on ﬁnancial and perceptual equity indicators; however, the lack of
comparable data limited the level of statistics for robust ﬁndings, hence the need for relating
ﬁnancial equity to perceptual equity awaits future research.

Objective 2: diﬀerent stakeholder perspectives on perceptual brand equity (consumer,
customer, employee, resident)
Perception-based brand equity is also measured from different perspectives, including general
consumers (Lassar et al., 1995; Netemeyer et al., 2004), actual customers (Kim et al., 2008; Lin
et al., 2015) and employees of the ﬁrm (Erkmen, 2018; King and Grace, 2009). Even though
some researchers use consumer-based brand equity and customer-based brand equity
interchangeably (Keller, 1993), customer-based brand equity (CBBE) focuses on actual
customers of a brand while consumer-based brand equity (CBBE) is measured with general
consumers. Hence, some customer-based brand equity studies include constructs such as
satisfaction (Lee and Back, 2008, 2010) while the general consumer-based brand equity studies
include constructs that consumers may assess with or without ﬁrst-hand experience. Even
though CBBE has been a popular concept of destination research, the perspectives of residents
as a critical stakeholder is amiss, thus far. Thus, there is a need for studies comparing
perceptual equity from the perspectives of consumers/customers to those of employees of ﬁrms,
and those of residents or tourism practitioners in the destination context.

Objective 3: original deﬁnitions and measures of perceptual brand equity by Aaker and Keller
One of the initial discussions by Farquhar (1989) dubbed brand equity as the added value of
a brand to the product and allows for ﬂexibility on different perspectives of this added value.
What guided most researchers, however, are the original conceptualizations of Aaker (1991,
1996) and Keller (1993, 2003). Keller (1993) used the customer-based brand equity term even
though his deﬁnition referred to consumers in general. He deﬁned CBBE as “the differential
effect of brand knowledge on consumer response to the marketing of the brand” (1993, p. 2). His
conceptualizations of brand equity boil it down to the overall concept of brand knowledge,
composed of awareness and image. Aaker (1991), on the other hand, deﬁned brand equity as a
brand’s assets and liabilities without specifying it either consumer or customer-based.
Afterward, Aaker (1996) proposed The Brand Equity Ten Measures including brand
awareness, associations, perceived quality, loyalty and other proprietary assets as a practical
departure point for developing a scale with cross-product and cross-market validity. He
proposed that a change in one of the measures would cause a change in the ﬁnancial-based
brand equity measures such as prices, sales and proﬁts. However, he acknowledged the
potential use of a single summary metric for convenience in reporting and tracking purposes of
companies. Although Aaker suggested that his measures have cross-product and cross-market

validity, he also acknowledged that his measures are not an optimum set, and thus some
modiﬁcations may be needed to adapt to different product and market contexts.

Objective 4: deviations from the original conceptualizations and measures of perceptual
brand equity by Aaker and Keller
The deﬁnitions and measures of CBBE provided by Aaker and Keller guided many
empirical studies that conceptualized and measured CBBE for different product brands in
different industries over the past two decades. However, the majority of studies reﬂect
the framework of Aaker. Table 1 displays the chronological development, and thus the
cumulative nature of the CBBE components and their measures. As can be seen from the
table, almost no researcher exactly followed either Aaker’s or Keller’s proposed CBBE
structure. Some researchers deleted unﬁtting components such as proprietary brand assets,
which are related to ﬁrms rather than consumer perception. Others added more pertinent
components depending on their product and brand context. These differences reﬂect the
divergence in both the conceptualization and operationalization of CBBE in the current
literature.

The root cause of some of the additional components proposed by researchers who
adopted Aaker’s framework could be the multidimensional nature of some components.
When Aaker (1996) Brand Equity Ten Measures are scrutinized, it is clear that only
awareness was clearly deﬁned while the dimensions of some components possess the
properties of multiple concepts. He included price premium and satisfaction as dimensions
of loyalty; included leadership as a dimension of the perceived quality; and included
perceived value, brand personality and organizational associations (organization and
differentiation) as dimensions of the associations/differentiation component. Thus, some of
these dimensions were later added as distinct components, value and price premium are
such additions (Lassar et al., 1995; Netemeyer et al., 2004; Tasci, 2016a, 2016b, 2016c, 2018,
2020).

Second, some researchers renamed Aaker’s original components. For example, Yoo and
Donthu (2001) used Aaker’s model; however, they also included Overall Brand Equity,
which was operationalized similar to loyalty (e.g. “if there is another brand as good as X, I
prefer to buy X”). These scales were also adopted by others (Washburn and Plank, 2002;
Washburn et al., 2000). Similar overall brand equity conceptualizations that resemble loyalty
or willingness to pay are still applied in tourism and hospitality. For example, So and King
(2010) measured customer-based hotel brand equity by using typical loyalty items: “makes
sense to use this hotel instead of other,” “prefer to use this hotel even have same features,”
“prefer to use this hotel if another is as good,” “seems smarter to use this hotel if another is
not different.” Other researchers also measured brand equity with these items (Rodríguez-
Molina et al., 2019; Rodríguez-L(cid:2)opez et al., 2020; Seric et al., 2016; Seric et al., 2017; Šerić and
Gil-Saura, 2019). Sijoria et al. (2019), on the other hand, measured brand equity with items
that are usually used to measure loyalty and willingness to pay (“the price of hotel X would
have to go up quite a bit before I would switch to another brand of the hotel; I am willing to
pay a higher price for hotel X than for another hotel; I am willing to pay ___per cent more for
hotel X over other hotels: 0% 5% 10% 15% 20% 25% 30% or more; Hotel Y would have to
be ___per cent per cent less than Hotel X before I would switch brands: 0% 5% 10% 15%
20% 25% 30% or more.”).

framework. For example, Atilgan et al.’s (2009) study included awareness, associations,
quality and loyalty; however, they revealed a new dimension of brand trust to replace

Objective 5: evolution of the ﬁve most common perceptual brand equity components
Despite deviations from the original CBBE conceptualizations, a majority of studies
included the components of brand familiarity/brand awareness, brand image/brand
associations, perceived quality, consumer value/perceived value and brand loyalty/
consumer loyalty/customer loyalty in measuring CBBE. These components are concepts
that appeared before the emergence of CBBE. The conceptual evolution of these most
commonly used components of CBBE is discussed next.

1. Brand familiarity/brand awareness
Both awareness and familiarity have been used as a component of CBBE. The familiarity
concept has been studied before the emergence of CBBE. Deﬁned as consumers’ level of
experience and knowledge with a brand, familiarity has been acknowledged to have an
important role on many consumer behavior variables (Alba and Hutchinson, 1987; Burch,
1969; Bettman and Park, 1980; Johnson and Russo, 1984; Park and Lessig, 1981; Punj and
Staelin, 1983; Tasci and Knutson, 2004; Tasci and Boylu, 2010; Zaichkowsky, 1985).
Consumers’ high level of familiarity is expected to ease consumers’ processing of
information, reduce risk and uncertainty and induce positive affect toward an object (Burch,
1969; Tasci and Knutson, 2004; Tasci and Boylu, 2010). Destination image research reports a
positive inﬂuence of familiarity on the image as well (Baloglu, 2001; Chen and Lin, 2012;
Prentice, 2004; Prentice and Andersen, 2000; Sonmez and Graefe, 1998).

Initially, Aaker and Keller considered brand awareness as a distinct CBBE component
and agreed on the role of brand awareness as the launchpad for building the other
components. Aaker (1991) deﬁned awareness as “the ability of a potential buyer to recognize
or recall that a brand is a member of a certain product category” (p. 61). Keller’s initial CBBE
proposition stated that “(c)ustomer-based brand equity occurs when the consumer is
familiar with the brand and holds some favorable, strong and unique brand associations in
memory” (1993, p. 1). Aaker and Keller operationalized awareness as consumers’ top-of-
mind awareness and their ability to recall/recognize and distinguish one brand among other
brands (Aaker, 1996; Keller, 2003). Aaker (1996) suggested four items to measure brand

IJCHM

awareness in Brand Equity Ten Measures. Despite its importance, Aaker also cautioned
about the nature of awareness when focusing on speciﬁc brand elements such as name,
symbols and imagery in measurement and also the difﬁculty of comparing awareness
across different brands and products (1996, p. 115).

Most researchers followed suit and used awareness as a distinct brand equity element
inﬂuencing one or more of other components. However, there are also different practices
regarding the awareness component. Table 1 reﬂects that some researchers measured this
component with multi-item scales while others used one-item scales. Some researchers
combined awareness with associations (Roth et al., 2008; Washburn and Plank, 2002;
Washburn et al., 2000; Yoo and Donthu, 1997, 2001; Yoo, Donthu and Lee, 2000). The
practice of combining brand awareness with associations threatens the integrity of these
components that are apparently distinct from one another when operationalized correctly.
Hence, Washburn and Plank (2002) recognized the need to separate them in future research
for reﬁnement to comply with the theory.

Although Keller (1993) used awareness in his initial design of the CBBE components, his
later work in 2003 replaced awareness with salience, which formed the base to build on the
other components, namely, performance,
judgments, feelings and resonance.
Following this practice, Pike et al. (2010) also used brand salience in destination brand
context; hence, a semantic analysis of scale items reveals much resemblance to awareness
and associations (e.g. “this destination is very famous”).

Another group of researchers preferred using familiarity instead of awareness (Denizci-
Guillet and Tasci, 2010a, 2010b; Gartner et al., 2007; Krishnan and Hartline, 2001; Tasci, 2016a,
2016b, 2016c, 2018; Tasci and Denizci, 2009; Tasci and Denizci-Guillet, 2011, 2016). For example,
Gartner et al. (2007) included familiarity in their destination CBBE measures by operationalizing
it as a categorical variable where they assessed visitation, hearing about or not knowing about
different tourist sites with different levels of popularity. Similarly, Tasci and Denizci-Guillet
(2016) used familiarity to capture varying degrees of awareness about brands and revealed that
it is a critical factor of consumers’ judgments on the ﬁt and compatibility of brands. Some
researchers also used both awareness and familiarity in their brand equity conceptualizations
(Horng et al., 2012; Tasci, 2011); however, this may lead to redundancy especially in complex
model testing due to the inherent relationship between awareness and familiarity.

Other researchers used “familiarity” in their scale items even if they named the
component as awareness. For example, Rajh’s (2002) awareness dimensions included “this
brand is very familiar to me” and “the characteristics of this brand are unfamiliar to me”.
Similarly, Wang and Finn (2013) used familiarity as dimensions in their awareness
component: “heard of it”, “heard of but never used it”, “heard of and used it”, “presently
using”. Other researchers mentioned familiarity in explaining the role of awareness. For
example, Lee and Back (2008) recognized familiarity as the incremental levels of awareness
that leads to brand satisfaction through brand familiarity in the event context. Familiarity
replacing awareness or being included in it may be justiﬁed with the inherent relevance of
awareness to familiarity.

2. Brand image/brand associations
The image concept has surfaced about four decades before CBBE was conceptualized. The
brand image concept ﬁrst appeared as an important marketing metric in the general brand
context in the 50 s (Gardner and Levy, 1955; Newman, 1957) and made its way into the
destination brand context in the 70 s (Crompton, 1979; Hunt, 1975). Destination image, in
particular, has been well-studied and modeled for its meanings, measurements, antecedents
and consequences (Echtner and Ritchie, 1991; Tasci and Gartner, 2007; Tasci et al., 2007).

Many destination image researchers included both quality and value as destination image
attributes (e.g. quality of services, value for money). Maybe due to this precedence of the
image, it was the preferred term in later CBBE studies even though Aaker’s and Keller’s
original CBBE frameworks included associations.

Aaker (1991) conceptualized brand associations as “anything linked to the memory of a
brand” (p. 109). In his Brand Equity Ten Measures, Aaker (1996) included 10 items
measuring associations/differentiation with the dimensions of perceived value, personality,
organization and differentiation. Some researchers considered consumers to value a distinct
CBBE component (Boo et al., 2009; Buil et al., 2008; Lee and Back, 2008, 2010; Netemeyer
et al., 2004; Tasci et al., 2018).

Keller’s (1993) CBBE model also included brand associations, speciﬁcally types (beneﬁts,
attributes and attitudes), favorability, strength and uniqueness. However, he titled this
component as “image.” As can be seen from Table 1, most researchers adopted the associations
component while many researchers used the image in tourism and hospitality (Bianchi et al.,
2014; Boo et al., 2009; Frias Jamilena et al., 2017; Horng et al., 2012; Hsu et al., 2012; Huerta-
Álvarez et al., 2020; Hyun and Kim, 2011; Kim and Kim, 2004, 2005; Kim et al., 2003; Liu et al.,
2015; Martínez and Nishiyama, 2017; Oh and Hsu, 2014; San Martín et al., 2018; Seric and Gil-
Saura, 2012; Seric et al., 2014; Sürücü et al., 2019; Tasci, 2016a, 2016b, 2016c; Tasci and Denizci-
Guillet, 2011, 2016; Yang et al., 2015). Furthermore, some researchers included both images and
associations in their CBBE models. Tasci (2016b), for example, recognized the similarities
between associations and image concepts and used them interchangeably. Some researchers
also separated images into cognitive and affective dimensions (Duman et al., 2018). Table 1
reﬂects the combined number of image dimensions in such studies. Table 1 reﬂects that the
number of items included in the image component is far more than the items included in the
associations component, especially when the study context is multifaceted such as destination
brands.

3. Perceived quality
Perceived quality was also deﬁned and measured before the emergence of the CBBE
framework. A majority of research on the quality concept revolved around perceived
quality, mostly in the context of service quality which was deﬁned by many different
dimensions including consistency, security, completeness, attitude, availability, condition
and training of service providers (Sasser et al., 1978), willingness and ability to serve and
physical and psychological access (Hedvall and Paltschik, 1989), technical, functional and
reputational quality (Grönroos, 1984), interactive, physical and corporate quality (Lehtinen
and Lehtinen, 1982), internal organization, corporate image, staff-customer interaction,
the service system and customer satisfaction (Leblanc and
physical support of
Nguyen,1988). Parasuraman et al. (1985, 1988) deﬁned it as ﬁve dimensions of reliability,
tangibles, responsiveness, assurance and empathy (SERVQUAL), which received the most
attention, acceptance and critiques from researchers. In another stream of research, the
perceived quality was considered as a dimension of consumer value (Gallarza and Saura,
2006; Holbrook, 1994, 1999; Lapierre, 2000; Lee et al., 2007; McDougall and Levesque, 2000;
Patterson and Spreng, 1997; Petrick, 2002; Sanchez et al., 2006; Sweeney and Soutar, 2001;
Zeithaml, 1988). Tasci (2016a) considered including quality as a dimension of value as a
threat to the integrity of these constructs and their relationship in the CBBE framework, and
thus recommended separating them as distinct concepts.

In his CBBE framework, Aaker quoted Zeithaml (1988) in deﬁning perceived quality as
“the consumer’s judgment about a product’s overall excellence or superiority” (1991, p. 85).
In his Brand Equity Ten Measures, he included six items reﬂecting dimensions of perceived

quality and leadership. The leadership dimension was later considered a distinct CBBE
component (Baalbaki, 2012). Keller (1993), on the other hand, considered perceived quality as
a brand association dimension rather than a distinct component.

As can be seen from Table 1, almost all researchers included quality as a dimension of
the CBBE framework by including different quality attributes depending on the nature of
the product under investigation. Some researchers kept Parasuraman et al.’s (1985, 1988)
SERVQUAL dimensions (Lee and Back, 2008, 2010; Kayaman and Arasli, 2007). However,
the validity of quality in the CBBE framework was not warranted in the results of some
studies. For example, Boo et al.’s (2009) destination CBBE model
initially included
awareness, consumer value,
image, perceived quality and brand loyalty as separate
components. Because of the high levels of correlation between image and quality
components, they pooled image and quality into another conglomerate component named
destination brand experience. A similar issue was revealed in Tasci’s (2018) CBBE model
applied to multiple destination brands with single-item measures. Because of the high level
of correlation between image and quality, she eliminated the quality component to stabilize
the CBBE model ﬁt in path analysis. Even though quality was included as a destination
image dimension in many destination image studies, quality and image are commonly
accepted as distinct components of CBBE (Tasci, 2016a); thus, future research needs to take
measures to maintain their distinct nature in deﬁnition, as well as measurement.

4. Consumer value/perceived value
The value concept and its derivatives such as perceived value and consumer value have also
emerged before the CBBE concept (Hirschman and Holbrook, 1982; Perry, 1926; Sewall,
1901; Zeithaml, 1988). Consumer value is the trade-off that consumers perceive between the
beneﬁts and costs of purchasing and consuming a product or brand (Zeithaml, 1988). Similar
to perceived quality, consumer value also faced conceptual invasions.

The value was not considered as a distinct component in the original CBBE works of
Aaker or Keller. Aaker (1996) considered the validity of consumer value as a distinct
component questionable due to its inherent relation to perceived quality. Thus, Aaker (1996)
formulated two measures of consumer value within brand associations in Brand Equity Ten
Measures.

Table 1 shows that some CBBE studies in tourism and hospitality did not include
consumer value in studying CBBE (Herrero et al., 2017; Kashif et al., 2015; Martínez and
Nishiyama, 2017; Pike et al., 2010; Vinh and Nga, 2015). On the other hand, following Aaker,
some tourism and hospitality researchers measured consumer value (e.g. “reasonable price,”
“value for money,” etc.) as a dimension of the image (Kim and Kim, 2004, 2005), a dimension
of perceived quality (Konecnik and Gartner, 2007; Yuwo et al., 2013; Zanfardini et al., 2011)
or brand performance (Prasad and Dev, 2000). Nonetheless, several tourism and hospitality
researchers considered consumer value to be a distinct element of CBBE (Bianchi et al., 2014;
Boo et al., 2009; Cano Guervos et al., 2020; Lee and Back, 2008, 2010; Liu et al., 2015; Pike and
Bianchi, 2016; Tasci, 2016a, 2016b, 2016c, 2018; Tasci and Denizci-Guillet, 2011, 2016; Tasci
et al., 2018). Some researchers also coined terms such as “value in use” as a separate
dimension of brand equity; for example, Chekalina et al.
(2018) coined this term in
destination context; however, they operationalized it similar to the destination image.
Similar to the image, the meanings, measurements, antecedents and consequences of
consumer value have been well-studied in many product contexts including hospitality and
tourism brands (Tasci, 2016a). Thus, it merits being a distinct CBBE element rather than
measured as a dimension of another element.

5. Brand loyalty/consumer loyalty/customer loyalty
Brand loyalty received academic attention about ﬁve decades before the emergence of the
CBBE framework. Similar to other components of CBBE, consumer loyalty has also gone
through a conceptual and operational identity crisis (Jacoby and Kyner, 1973). Hence, many
different deﬁnitional and operational approaches exist in capturing consumer/brand loyalty
(Brown, 1952; Jacoby, 1969; McConnell, 1968; Tucker, 1964). Past literature shows many
different deﬁnitions and different operationalizations of consumer loyalty reﬂecting overt
behavioral indicators related to past and future such as repeat purchase, intentions and
positive word of mouth, as well as covert attitudinal indicators such as price sensitivity,
preference and liking (Dick and Basu, 1994; Ganesh et al., 2000; Gallarza and Saura, 2006;
Garland and Gendall, 2004; Gounaris and Stathakopoulos, 2004; Oliver, 1999; Oppermann,
1998; Reichheld and Sasser, 1990; Shoemaker and Bowen, 2003; Tasci, 2016d; Yoon and
Uysal, 2005). Some of these indicators have been used by researchers in capturing loyalty as
an element of CBBE as well, even though the original CBBE frameworks by Aaker or Keller
did not include these indicators.

Brand loyalty was not a distinct component of Keller’s (1993) CBBE framework. Although
Aaker’s (1991) CBBE framework included brand loyalty as a distinct component, his original
operationalization does not capture the behavioral and attitudinal nature of loyalty. He deﬁned
brand loyalty as “the attachment that a customer has to a brand” (1991, p. 39). However, in his
Brand Equity Ten Measures, he deﬁned loyalty by juxtaposing satisfaction and willingness to
pay higher prices (price premium) with seven items reﬂecting these concepts. Some tourism and
hospitality researchers followed suit and included either price premium, satisfaction or both as
indicators of loyalty (Kayaman and Arasli, 2007; Kim and Kim, 2004), while most others
conﬁned loyalty within its dimensions only. Recently, Tasci (2016b, 2016c, 2018, 2020) included
price premium as an indicator of “brand value” by narrowing it to premium price perception
without involving the consumer’s willingness to pay for it. Other studies also included brand
value for CBBE of hotel and destination brands (Bailey and Ball, 2006; Denizci-Guillet and Tasci,
2010a, 2010b; Tasci, 2016b, 2018; Tasci et al., 2018; Tasci and Denizci-Guillet, 2011, 2016).

The critical role of loyalty for the success of tourism and hospitality brands is evident in most
studies that included loyalty in CBBE, even though a few researchers named it as brand equity
in their studies (Rodríguez-L(cid:2)opez et al., 2020; Rodríguez-Molina et al., 2019; Seric et al., 2016; Seric
et al., 2017; Šeri(cid:2)c and Gil-Saura, 2019; Sijoria et al., 2019; So and King, 2010). Another confusion
comes from switching between Aaker’s and Keller’s conceptualizations of CBBE. For example,
Duman et al. (2018) followed the brand equity model of Keller and used brand resonance as a
component of CBBE, which they measured with behavioral loyalty and attitudinal attachment.
Yet another stream of research also included loyalty and other ramiﬁcations of it as either
another component of CBBE or as an outcome of loyalty. For example, Oh and Hsu (2014)
included both brand loyalty and brand choice intention as separate components of CBBE, even
though all dimensions included in both resemble the indicators of loyalty. Similarly, Lu et al.
(2015) also included both brand loyalty and brand choice intention in their studies; however,
they kept loyalty as the CBBE component, which consequently affected brand choice intention
as an outcome variable. Splitting loyalty into different types (i.e. past vs future attitudinal vs
behavioral) and treating different loyalty types as both a component and an outcome of CBBE
may cause conceptual confusion besides potential discriminant validity issues.

Objective 6: structural relationships of the most common perceptual brand equity components
Aaker’s (1996) CBBE conceptualization and measures did not suggest any particular
relationship among the components. He implied that awareness is the main culprit affecting
all the others, as it inﬂuences perceptions and attitudes. However, Keller’s (2003) conceptual

IJCHM

model constructed a CBBE pyramid, the base being brand salience, supporting brand
performance and imagery; brand performance deﬁning the next layer of brand judgments,
imagery deﬁning consumer effect, all collectively deﬁning consumer-brand resonance on
top. However, this model is rather conceptual, without operationally clear or directional
relations among components.

Researchers following both Aaker and Keller revealed diverse structural relations among
CBBE components with almost no consistency in the type of components or their
relationships. Some tourism and hospitality researchers considered loyalty as a CBBE
outcome (Lin et al., 2015). On the other hand, others included loyalty as a CBBE component
(Boo et al., 2009; Dedeoglu et al., 2019; Hsu et al., 2012; Kashif et al., 2015; Lee and Back, 2008;
Liu et al., 2015; Lu et al., 2015; Oh and Hsu, 2014; Seric et al., 2014; Tasci, 2018; Vinh and Nga,
2015). For example, Hsu et al. (2012) included brand loyalty as a CBBE component and
brand choice intention as the outcome of CBBE, where all components inﬂuence brand
loyalty and brand loyalty inﬂuences brand choice intention.

In a literature review of the value construct and its role in the CBBE framework, Tasci
(2016a) proposed a CBBE model with awareness as the launchpad component and loyalty as
the outcome component dependent on the interrelations of image/associations, quality and
value as perception and willingness to pay higher prices. However, in testing a CBBE
model’s cross-brand validity, Tasci (2018) found that familiarity and image had the most
effects in the path model, familiarity inﬂuencing not only the image but also consumer value
and loyalty for both general consumer and customer groups and eventually having the most
explanatory power on loyalty.

A few researchers proposed measuring CBBE with its distinct components and an
overall brand equity component (Buil et al., 2013; Yoo and Donthu, 2001). In their conceptual
model of CBBE for hotels based on a review of literature, Xu and Chan (2010) included an
overall brand equity concept that is inﬂuenced by loyalty, brand awareness, brand
associations and quality of experience. Similarly, Buil et al.
included brand
awareness, perceived quality, brand associations and brand loyalty as the components of
brand equity. They also added a 3-item overall brand equity concept which was inﬂuenced
by brand loyalty and in return, inﬂuenced willingness to pay a price premium, brand
extension, brand preference and purchase intention. However, the similarity of the items in
loyalty, overall brand equity, willingness to pay a price premium, brand extension, brand
preference and purchase intention implies issues of discriminant validity.

Loyalty needs to be treated as a component of CBBE by distinguishing it from other
outcome variables that may be included in studies. The use of additional components that
resemble loyalty or the general brand equity may cause redundancy in structural or path
models. Hence, such practices need theoretical justiﬁcation, rigorous methods and robust
results, besides transparency in pertinent validity measures for the reader to make the
ultimate judgment.

Discussion and conclusions
Conclusions
Brand equity with both ﬁnancial and perceptual dimensions has been a focus of research for
the past 30 years. As perceptual equity was commonly considered a proxy to ﬁnancial
equity, many studies tested the strength and relations of some perceptual indicators in
several product contexts including hospitality and tourism. The review of literature on
CBBE, however, reveals that both components and their interrelations are deﬁned and
measured in many different ways in different product and brand context, even though most
CBBE research is guided with the positivist paradigm that requires standardization in

Perceptual
brand equity

concepts, methods, tools, and measures (Kuhn, 2012). Although using different components
may be logical in different product contexts, some level of standardization is needed for
robust theory development and practical implications, especially when measuring CBBE for
the brands of the same product category. Toward such standardization, this study suggests
using uniform CBBE components, measures and model relationships in future research.
Brand familiarity/brand awareness, brand image/brand associations, perceived quality,
consumer value and brand loyalty are the most commonly used components of CBBE;
maybe because they have been already established concepts studied before the emergence of
CBBE as a conglomerate concept in the early 90 s. These components and their relationships
can be measured to identify the presumed relationship between CBBE and FBBE, as well as
compare CBBE from different stakeholder perspectives, especially by including residents in
destination studies. These suggestions, as Objective 7 of the study, are discussed below.

Theoretical implications (Objective 7: conceptual, structural, relational and methodological
suggestions for future research)
Despite three-decade-long research on brand equity and CBBE, there is more to do to ﬁne-
tune, adjust and better understand this construct in future research. First, researchers need
to use a uniform CBBE framework with similar measurement items rather than inventing
new CBBE components and diluting the concept. The parsimonious ﬁve-component
structure and the template measurement scales suggested in this study may help establish a
more connected theory of CBBE even if it is applied in different contexts.

Using uniform perceptual brand equity components and measures
Keeping in mind the most commonly used components, this study offers a comprehensive
yet parsimonious deﬁnition of CBBE: CBBE is a brand’s intangible strength manifested in
consumers’ level of familiarity with the brand guiding their cognition, affect and behavior in
the form of image, quality, value and loyalty related to the brand. This deﬁnition integrates
the most commonly used components of CBBE and, thus, enables a standard measure of it to
allow cumulative theory development on this concept and ability to assess its relationship to
other constructs. Although many of the newly added CBBE components in Table 1 are
potentially meaningful indicators of the success of a brand in different contexts, trying to
measure the CBBE construct with as many components as displayed in Table 1 would
possess neither scientiﬁc parsimony nor practicality. Comprehensive, yet practical, the
capture of CBBE requires measurement of the commonly used components, including
familiarity/awareness,
image/associations, perceived value/consumer value, perceived
quality and brand loyalty/consumer loyalty.

Table 2 suggests a parsimonious list of measures as a guide to measuring these
components in future research, with both single and multi-item scales in destination, hotel
and restaurant contexts, which can be modiﬁed to other contexts accordingly. These
measures need to be interpreted as a framework similar to Aaker’s (1996) Brand Equity Ten
Measures. As tourism and hospitality products are a conglomerate of physical and service
components, some CBBE components (awareness, image, quality and value) are formulated
as formative scales reﬂecting different product features. However, reﬂective items referring
to the product in general can also be used. In designing the scale items of image, quality and
value, caution is needed to differentiate them substantially. Similar verbiage of scale items
may cause unstable structural or path modeling results, as was the case in Boo et al.’s (2009)
and Tasci’s (2018) studies. For loyalty, both past and future behavioral dimensions, as well
as the attitudinal dimension of loyalty are included as a guide for future research. In the

Choice of brand familiarity or brand awareness
Even though awareness was the original Launchpad component of CBBE, awareness in the
context of popular tourism destinations or well-known hotel brands may be a granted
situation; what differentiates between competing popular tourist destinations or brands may
be the level of familiarity sourcing from different information agents. Familiarity as the
launchpad component of CBBE may provide a few advantages. First, it may be easier to

IJCHM

design a single-item or multi-item familiarity scale. Second, it is possible to also capture
different sources of familiarity in scales. Consumers may have informational familiarity
from written, audio and visual sources (Baloglu, 2001; Prentice, 2004; Sharifpour et al., 2014;
Zaichkowsky, 1985) or experiential familiarity from actual experiences with an object
(Baloglu, 2001; Prentice, 2004; Zaichkowsky, 1985), which can be captured by multi-item
scales. Third, familiarity may provide a wider variance in measurement scales as
consumers’ varying degrees of knowledge and experience can be captured with more
continuous scales rather than Aaker’s (1996) original Yes/No type questions (i.e. “have you
heard of this brand?”). Fourth, familiarity may overcome the issues of awareness that Aaker
(1996) raised; it may enable measurement of consumer familiarity in general rather than
speciﬁc brand elements and allow comparison across different products and brands. Finally,
due to its well-established connections with other constructs, familiarity may be a better
ﬁtting component of CBBE than awareness in some brand contexts such as destinations.

Choice of brand image or brand associations
Associations are the nodes of information in consumers’ memory about the brand-related
attributes, beneﬁts and emotions that shape consumers’ behavior toward the brand (Aaker,
1991, 1992, 1996; Keller, 1993, 2003). Even though associations can be used interchangeably
with image, especially in conceptual discussions to allow the ﬂexibility of choice for
different measurement contexts, in empirical studies, uniform use of one or the other may
provide more standardization of methodology and robust theory development. Image may
be a better ﬁtting construct due to existing well-established theory in certain contexts such
as destinations where its meanings, measurements, antecedents and consequences have
been well-studied and modeled since the 70 s.

The distinction between perceived quality and consumer value
As relatively well-deﬁned and measured concepts, perceived quality and value need to be
integrated as distinct components of CBBE rather than as dimensions of the other
components. Distinguishing perceived quality as a separate component ﬁne-tunes the
elements of the CBBE construct with meaningfully different dimensions that are known to
inﬂuence consumer behavior. Although the majority of researchers already follow this
practice, some researchers combine quality with another component or eliminate it due to
high correlations with an image (Boo et al., 2009; Tasci, 2018). As was also suggested by
Tasci (2018), researchers need to carefully design scale items to assure the integrity of
individual constructs for better theory development. Similarly, the distinction of value is
also critical particularly in brand contexts where products and services pose risk,
uncertainty, and thus, perceived beneﬁts and costs that may hinge on the overall CBBE of a
brand. For example, the destination brand context poses several risks, especially for distant
destinations, where consumers’ perception of quality and their loyalty tendencies may
heavily depend on the potential costs due to the foreseen or unforeseen risks. Therefore,
consumer value needs to be a distinct component of CBBE in such contexts.

The distinction between brand loyalty and price premium
Aaker’s (1996) conceptualization of loyalty by combining satisfaction and price premium not
only misses the overt and covert indicators of loyalty but also invades satisfaction, another
concept that is commonly accepted as a distinct construct. Additionally, using price premium
as a dimension of loyalty may be a disservice to the integrity of the CBBE framework. When
price premium is conceptualized as the actual price of a brand compared to actual prices of
competitor brands, it comprises an FBBE component. However, when it is conceptualized from

a consumer perspective in terms of perceived price, it may be considered as part of CBBE.
Nonetheless, loyalty needs to reﬂect both attitudinal and behavioral indicators of loyalty for a
comprehensive capture of the construct when multi-item scales are used.

Using a uniform structural model
Using different relational models is an obstacle to the development of a robust CBBE theory.
The relational structure of components needs to be modeled in line with the ﬁndings of past
literature. The model displayed in Figure 2 is suggested based on the most commonly
studied components in literature. First, brand familiarity/awareness is accepted as a priori to
all other components of CBBE (Aaker, 1991, 1996; Keller, 1993). In fact, in some contexts,
familiarity may have substantial inﬂuences on multiple components as discovered by
Tasci (2018) in destination context where familiarity inﬂuenced image, consumer value and
loyalty for both general consumer and customer groups, and thus, have the most
explanatory power on loyalty. Second, image literature also suggests that, in some contexts,
the brand image may have a substantial impact on other components. In the destination
context, for example, there is a consensus on the destination image’s inﬂuence on consumer
behavior before, during and after visiting a place (Tasci and Gartner, 2007). While
validating a CBBE model for multiple destination brands, Tasci (2018) discovered that
familiarity inﬂuenced image, which then inﬂuenced all other components. Third, consumer
value literature considers value as a perception of a tradeoff between price and quality of a
brand; hence, quality inﬂuences consumer value (Gallarza and Saura, 2006; Kuo et al., 2009;
Oh, 1999; Petrick, 2004; Zeithaml, 1988). Fourth, several studies revealed a signiﬁcant
inﬂuence of consumer value and perceived quality on brand loyalty (Bianchi et al., 2014;
Chen and Chen, 2010; Hutchinson et al., 2009; Hu et al., 2009; Kuo, et al., 2009; Lee et al., 2007;
Pike and Bianchi, 2016; Sanchez et al., 2006). As brand loyalty was originally conceptualized
as a component of CBBE rather than as an outcome, future studies need to follow this
practice to allow robust theory development on the structural nature of CBBE. When
ﬁnancial metrics are involved, they can be integrated as the outcome of CBBE. The purpose
is to standardize the CBBE model across different product and brand categories to allow
assessment of the signiﬁcance of CBBE in its relations to other concepts through and robust

The components and relationships displayed in this model agree with the commonly-
accepted principles of robust theory development. First, aligned with the principles of the
cumulative nature of science and degree of formal development (Shoemaker et al., 2004), the
model builds on previous studies and includes the most commonly-used concepts and their
relational development identiﬁed in the literature. Second, following the principle of testability
(Shoemaker et al., 2004), the model clearly states the concepts and variables to be measured.
Third, in line with the principle of falsiﬁability (Doty and Glick, 1994; Popper, 2005; Shoemaker
et al., 2004; Weber, 2012), the concepts and their relationships are speciﬁc enough to be
disproved. Fourth, following the principle of scope or generalizability (Shoemaker et al., 2004;
Wacker, 1998), all the concepts and their relationships in the model apply to different situations,
contexts and disciplines. Fifth, following the principle of parsimony, also termed as Occam’s
razor, simplicity, or efﬁciency (Shoemaker et al., 2004; Popper, 2005), the model is the simplest
explanation for the CBBE construct. Sixth, in line with the principle of importance (Weber,
2012), all the focal concepts have been the focus of scientiﬁc inquiry for decades. Seventh, the
predictive power and explanatory power of the model (Shoemaker et al., 2004; Kamps, 1999)
can be tested using the measurement items in Table 2 as a guide in future studies.

Testing the presumed relationship between perceptual brand equity and ﬁnancial brand equity
Brand equity studies have, thus, far focused on either ﬁnancial or perceptual indicators. The
critical role of CBBE can be solidiﬁed if its presumed inﬂuence on FBBE is established;
therefore, future studies need to integrate components of CBBE with indicators of FBBE. A
combination of both would provide a more comprehensive picture of brand equity,
integrating perceptual and ﬁnancial indicators. However, there are challenges to overcome
in this endeavor. Although easy to calculate with real numbers, ﬁnancial metrics have been
questioned, as they are rather short-term-focused, static, don’t apply to all marketing
strategies and require a comparison with unbranded compatible products (Aaker, 1996;
De Mortanges and Van Riel, 2003). These premises are problematic when applied to service
and destination brand contexts. The lack of information on compatible unbranded products
is a magniﬁed shortcoming for some brands such as tourism destinations. First, information
on compatible unbranded products is an unrealistic metric for the destination, as
destinations are branded with certain names, symbols and ﬂags. There is also a lack of
compatible branded products; even if they offer similar products, all destinations are unique.
Nevertheless, similarly popular tourist destinations of the same category or destinations
with similar products can be used to compare both their CBBE and FBBE as relatively
compatible brands. Another limitation is the lack of ﬁnancial metrics against competitors in
the destination context such as cash ﬂow and price to benchmark. However, as suggested by
Tasci and Denizci (2009), the number of visitors and revenues, the most commonly used
economic impacts of tourism, can be used as objective ﬁnancial metrics in the destination
context. These ﬁnancial indicators can be integrated into CBBE studies as a check on the
productivity of CBBE. Testing the relationships between CBBE and FBBE empirically
would enable researchers to conﬁrm their assumption of strong CBBE leading to strong
FBBE eventually. Additionally, connecting CBBE with FBBE allows linking separate
streams of research for more holistic theory development as suggested by Tasci (2019).

Comparing diﬀerent stakeholder perspectives on perceptual brand equity
More complete brand equity can be assessed by involving all different perspectives
regarding a brand. Studies involving consumers reveal both the reality and purely imagined

Perceptual
brand equity

perspectives of actual and potential consumers of a brand, while customer-oriented studies
reveal the perception of the reality by the actual users. Employees as the internal customers
of a brand may reveal more intimate and realistic perceptions than both consumers and
customers. Thus, a comparison of these three different perspectives may reveal a
comprehensive capture of the CBBE of a brand.

For conglomerate tourism products such as destinations, brand equity needs to be assessed
from the perspectives of multiple stakeholders involved in both supply and demand side of the
product. This is particularly critical for sustainable destination branding by strategically
balancing between the host and guest perceptions, expectations and demands. Residents are
both part of the destination product, as well as consumers of the several products and services
involved in the destination. Brand equity from resident perspectives needs to be compared to
those of visitors and tourism practitioners so that the mismatch or potential issues from
different stakeholder perspectives can be identiﬁed. If, for example, resident-based CBBE is
better than visitor-based CBBE, then there is a lack of promotion in the target markets;
conversely, in the opposite case, there is a potential lack of resident support for tourism, thus
calling for resident sentiment analysis. Furthermore, such comparative studies using the same
measures and models can yield the importance of different CBBE components in explaining
loyalty and other outcome variables from different perspectives. Is the relative inﬂuence of
CBBE components on loyalty the same or different across different stakeholders? The current
body of literature does not provide an answer to this question. Capturing all perspectives may
not be feasible in one study; nonetheless, different studies focusing on different perspectives of
the same brand would still provide useful comparisons of brands, especially when uniform
components, measures and models are used.

Furthermore, the complexity of some brands such as destinations or places with diverse
stakeholders may beneﬁt from an interdisciplinary approach with collaboration, integration
and hybridization of skills, expertise, knowledge, concepts, data, philosophies and methods
from multiple disciplines (Darbellay and Stock, 2012; Leiper, 1981). Cross-fertilizations of
ideas, concepts and methods from different ﬁelds related to destination brands such as
geography, urban development, public policy and destination management may lead to
more robust theory development.

Managerial implications
CBBE components and relationships displayed in Figure 2 are parsimonious for the
industry practitioners to use in their marketing research and plans as a strategic market
metric. Additionally, Table 2 offers a parsimonious and practical CBBE blueprint for
practitioner use. The model and measures suggested in this study may encourage
practitioners to conduct continuous monitoring of their own CBBE, as well as compare with
their competitors’ CBBE. As perceptual brand equity is becoming an important aspect of
marketing for restaurants, hotels and destinations, these practical CBBE model and
measures may become a common practice in the industry.

Limitations and future research
The current study focused on perceptual brand equity literature. Even though FBBE is
brieﬂy referenced in relation to CBBE, ﬁnancial equity deﬁnitions and measures were not
deeply analyzed. Thus, future studies need to delve into ﬁnancial metrics of brand equity
measures in hospitality and tourism product contexts such as hotels, restaurants and
destinations. Nonetheless, this critical review provides a guideline for more robust theory
development of the CBBE construct. Additionally, it offers a parsimonious and practical
CBBE blueprint for the practitioners who include CBBE as a strategic market metric in

IJCHM

their marketing and research plans. Furthermore, it suggests standardization in CBBE
research to enable systematic reviews with Meta-analysis of CBBE literature in the
future. This critical review used a narrative analysis because a systematic review with
Meta-analysis of CBBE literature is not possible due to the divergence in deﬁnitions,
components, measures and models used to study CBBE. Meta-analysis applies
objective formulas to summarize the “signiﬁcance levels, effect size estimations and the
analysis of variations in effect sizes” (Cooper et al., 2019, p. 8) and requires standard
application of deﬁnitions, measures and models. Using standard components, measures
and relational models in CBBE research, as is suggested in this study, would allow
Meta-analysis for a meaningful comparison of results between different brands,
products and even industries, and thus allow assessment of the relative effect sizes of
each CBBE component on one another and the explanatory power of CBBE in empirical
research.
