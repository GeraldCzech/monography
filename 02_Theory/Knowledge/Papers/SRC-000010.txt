WARM-GLOW GIVING: EARNED MONEY AND THE OPTION TO TAKE

ANDREW LUCCASEN and PHILIP J. GROSSMAN∗

Giving in dictator games has been shown to vary with the nature of the endowment
(earned vs. house money) and the action space (give only vs. the option to give or take).
This article is the first to test if these factors similarly affect warm-glow giving alone.
There is no reason that one would expect the same outcomes given that the motivations
for warm-glow giving are different from the motivations for total (warm-glow plus purely
altruistic) giving. We find that warm-glow giving to charity or philanthropic institutions
in a real-donation experiment increases when the endowment is earned. The option
to take does reduce warm-glow giving to charity, but significant giving remains. Our
results suggest that donating earned income creates greater utility than donating an
equal amount from a windfall gain, and that warm glow comes not merely from the act
of giving, but also from the characteristics of the recipient. (JEL C90, D64, H41)

I.

INTRODUCTION

What motivates giving? A pure altruist gives
solely out of concern for the well-being of recip-
ients; giving makes the giver better off because it
has made the recipient better off. Caring only that
the recipient is cared for, how and by whom the
care is provided is irrelevant to the pure altruist.
For a pure altruist, a dollar of charitable contri-
bution is completely crowded out by giving by
any other third party. The act of giving for a pure
warm-glow giver is what provides satisfaction
(Andreoni 1989, 1990). A pure warm-glow giver
does not care about the well-being of recipients;
giving is purely egotistical.1 Even if the recipient
is not benefited, it makes the giver feel better

Crumpler and Grossman (2008) provide a
direct test of warm-glow giving in a laboratory
experiment in which own-money motivated par-
ticipants have no incentive to contribute money to
a charity. Any contribution to charity was known
to be offset by an equal reduction of the charity’s
endowment by the experimenter. A majority of
participants gave to charity, and on average gave
a significant portion of their endowment. Using
the Crumpler and Grossman (2008; henceforth
CG) design for testing for warm-glow giving, we
examine the relative effect of earned versus house
money endowments on warm-glow giving with
two decision frames: one in which participants
can only give to charity and a second with a sym-
metric decision space in which participants can
either give to or take from a charity.

Our major findings are as follows. First and
most novel is how earned endowments affect
warm-glow giving. When the recipient is a char-
ity, we observe larger warm-glow contributions
with earned money than with house money.
Indeed, significantly more subjects contribute,
and mean contributions to charity are signifi-
cantly higher, when the endowment is earned.
This result is consistent with the interpretation
provided by Cherry et al., that dictator bargaining
is “more self-interested” when endowments are
earned relative to house money, but this result

II. RELATED LITERATURE

An ongoing debate concerns the interpreta-
tion of giving in dictator experiments (Camerer
2015; Levitt and List 2007; and List 2007). Some
the standard action
authors have argued that
space, in which the dictator can only give, engen-
ders on the part of the dictator the expectation
that a contribution should be made. This expec-
tation is further enhanced by the fact that the
experimenter, the authority figure in the rooms,
has provided the dictator with the endowment
(“house money”) from which any contribution
will be made. Finally, the expectation is even fur-
ther enhanced by the experimenter asking the dic-
tator if she would like to make a contribution to
the recipient. In this context, it is questionable if
any contribution can be truly interpreted as a self-
less action on the part of the dictator.

Cherry, Frykblom, and Shogren (2002) and
List (2007) consider the house money effect on
giving. Dictators in the Cherry et al. study earned
money by successfully answering quiz questions
prior to playing the dictator game. These par-
ticipants are paired with subjects who did not
participate in the quiz activity. List has both par-
ticipants earn their endowments, but the dictator
earns a larger endowment than the second par-
ticipant. In both studies, dictators who earn their
endowments are significantly less generous and
approximately three times more likely to make
a $0 offer than dictators in the respective house
money treatments.

To address the action space question, Bards-
ley (2008) and List (2007) adapt the basic dictator
experiment. They give the passive second partic-
ipant his own endowment and permit the dictator
to either give or take from the second participant.
Both researchers find that expanding the action
space significantly reduces, and can even elimi-
nate, dictator giving.

None of these studies, however, use the real
donation design where preferences for giving to
a public good or charity could be expressed.
Matching dictators with charities increases the
parallelism between the laboratory and the field
(Eckel and Grossman 1996, henceforth EG; and
Eckel, Grossman, and Johnston 2005). In the con-
text of a real-donation experiment, preferences
that motivate charitable giving in practice are
more likely to be active compared to allocating
money to an anonymous participant.4

The dictator experiment has also been
employed to directly test the warm-glow giving
hypothesis. Such tests often use the “real dona-
tion” design of EG, in which each participant
is matched with a charity of her choosing. For
example, Eckel, Grossman, and Johnston (2005)
do so by varying the degree of fiscal illusion in
a real donation dictator experiment. The results
are consistent with warm-glow giving. CG iso-
late the warm-glow giving in a real donation
experiment by completely crowding out dona-
tions made by participants. They report
that
participants donated 20% of their endowments
on average, and 57% of the participants made
a donation. Gangadharan, Grossman, and Jones
(2014) sequence the CG game with a standard
real donation dictator game to categorize givers
by type: nongivers, pure warm-glow givers,
pure altruists, and impure altruists. They find
substantial numbers in each category, as well as
warm-glow giving results consistent with those
reported by CG.

the experimenter) is $10 − t, and the amount
received from the charity is equal to t.

Applying Charness and Rabin’s (2002) topol-
ogy of social preferences, players exhibiting nar-
row self-interest might give, take, or neither. They
would be inclined to give if they were Andreoni
egoists; they would not give if warm glow did
not enter their utility functions. If such players
were motivated by malice toward a charity, that
malice could be acted on by selecting that charity
and then taking from it.8 Players exhibiting com-
petitive preferences would be disinclined to give
but might take if the desire to do better than the
other party was sufficiently strong and the con-
cern for the other party was sufficiently weak.
Players with difference aversion or social welfare
preferences would neither give nor take.

C. Supplemental Treatments

Two additional supplemental treatments were
conducted to ensure internal and external valid-
ity. Participants were paired with other partici-
pants instead of a charity (one house money, one
earned endowment). If observed giving is due to
confusion or a demand effect in the way the deci-
sion sheet is presented, we should observe similar
rates of giving, which we did not. As in EG, we
find that context matters: giving is significantly
reduced when participants are paired with other
participants (see Appendix S2, http://ssrn.com/
abstract=2791025).

V. SUMMARY AND CONCLUDING REMARKS

Previous work employing the Dictator Game
has shown that total (warm-glow plus purely
altruistic) giving is drastically reduced when par-
ticipants earn their endowments in a pregame task
(Cherry, Frykblom, and Shogren 2002 and List
2007), and that total giving is reduced, and con-
siderable taking occurs, when the action space
is expanded to include taking as well as giving
(Bardsley 2008 and List 2007). We test if these
results are robust for warm-glow giving alone.
The fact that the motivations for warm-glow giv-
ing are different from the motivations for purely
altruistic giving suggests that one should not nec-
essarily expect the same outcomes for warm-
glow giving as for total giving.

To test our hypotheses, we adopt the CG
design. The treatments provide context by pair-
ing dictators with charities of their own choice. A
donation to charity does not increase the amount
the charity receives. Instead, it alters the per-
ceived composition of the donation to charity

between the experimenter and the participant. We
employ a real effort task that either earns $3
for the participant, separate from the $10 used
in the real donation experiment (house money
treatment), or earns $13 for the participant, of
which $10 is used in the real donation experiment
(earned endowment treatment). We examine the
effect of earned endowments with two decision
frames. Participants can only pass money to a
charity in the give only treatment; participants can
either pass money to charity or take money from
charity in the give or take treatments.

While Cherry, Frykblom, and Shogren (2002)
and List (2007) find that earning the endowment
significantly reduces dictator (total) giving, we
find that earning the endowment increases warm-
glow giving. We observe statistically insignifi-
cant increased warm-glow giving in our give-only
treatments and statistically significant increased
warm-glow giving in our give or take treatments.
In our give only treatments, approximately 50%
more participants give and they give approxi-
mately 50% more when endowments are earned
rather than house money. The difference in our
give or take treatments is even more pronounced;
100% more participants give and average giving
is about 300% higher. The qualitative dependent
variable and limited-dependent variable mod-
els indicate that the proportion of subjects who
donate to charity and the amount given to char-
ity are higher in treatments in which the endow-
ment is earned. When we expand the action
space, we find results consistent with Bards-
ley (2008) and List (2007). Allowing subjects
to take as well as give reduces, but does not
eliminate warm-glow giving, and we observe
no taking.12

Our results are consistent with the hypothe-
sis that participants receive a warm glow from
giving. Although a selected charity receives $10
regardless of the amount passed to the charity,
a participant who passes $2 to charity may feel
as if her contribution accounts for $2 of the $10
total. It must be recognized, as the Lei, Noussair,
and Plott (2001) Active Participation Hypothe-
sis would suggest, that at least some of the giv-
ing we report is due to a desire on the part of
experiment participants to do something. While
our subjects in the give or take treatments have
two action options, the taking action may be

The results in this article allow us to refine
the interpretation of earned endowments on
dictator giving. To declare that earned endow-
ments decrease giving is an overstatement. We
do observe decreased giving in response to
earned endowments in treatments when other
anonymous participants are the recipients of
the dictators’ allocations. The results from
the real donation experiments are consistent
with the hypothesis that dictators make more
self-interested decisions when endowments
are earned. Self-interest, for a subset of dicta-
tors, includes the warm glow of donating to a
charitable organization. Therefore, more self-
interested behavior is not always equivalent to
less giving.

Our results suggest that donating one’s earned
income creates greater utility than donating
an equal amount of a windfall gain.14 These
results are consistent with the Benabou and
Tirole (2006) model of self-image. Actions
allow individuals to define their self-image.
However, Benabou and Tirole state the impor-
tance of delineating between a preference for
warm glow and “ … motives that relate to what a
person’s behavior says about him or her, which
will depend on the informational and economic
context … ” The results in this article indicate
that donating earned money is seen as more of
a sacrifice than donating house money, and this
is compounded by the social norm regarding the
recipient of donated money.
