Abstract In this study,
the factors that determine the
intentions of individuals to donate to nonproﬁt organiza-
tions (NPOs) were analyzed. Based on an extensive review
of the academic literature, research hypotheses were pro-
posed about the inﬂuences of altruism, self-esteem, trust,
donation past behavior, attitudes and brand identiﬁcation.
Data were collected by means of an online survey. The
sample was composed of 300 respondents. The empirical
investigation consisted of specifying and estimating a
model using PLS-SEM. The results showed that altruism
and self-esteem did not have statistically signiﬁcant effects
on the intention to donate. The ﬁndings suggested that
donations to NPOs were more strongly inﬂuenced by trust,
past donation behavior, attitudes, and brand identiﬁcation.
The paper discusses implications of these results for theory
and managerial practices related to philanthropy.

Introduction

Nonproﬁt organizations (NPOs) must have a detailed
understanding of the donation process to obtain contribu-
tions and attract new donors, especially when their goal is
to sustain the donor relationship over a long period of time.
A better comprehension of donors’ ‘preferences will allow
NPOs to personalize the fundraising process and attract
more donations (Sieg & Zhang, 2012).

The current literature in the ﬁeld of philanthropy has
employed the theory of planned behavior (TPB), which
was introduced by Ajzen (1991). For example, Smith and
McSweeney (2007), Van der Linden (2011), Knowles et al.
(2012), and Sura et al. (2017) applied TPB as an explora-
tory model of human donation intentions and proved its
efﬁcacy. According to this model, intention is the main
determinant of donation behavior. In turn,
intention is
determined by positive or negative evaluations of the
behavior (attitude), perceptions of social pressure to per-
form the behavior (subjective norm) and perceptions of
control over performing the behavior (perceived control).
However, instead of using the classic TPB model, Kashif
et al. (2015), Knowles et al. (2012), and Smith and
research using the
framed their
McSweeney (2007)
extended version (ETPB). The ETPB model distinguishes
between subjective norms and descriptive norms, speciﬁ-
cally, imperative norms and descriptive norms, to provide a
broader justiﬁcation for charitable intention. In addition,
these studies enhanced the original model by incorporating
the role of past behavior as a predictor of intention to
donate. Although the TPB and ETPB models have been the
dominant approaches, more recent studies have suggested
the need to consider other factors.

Individuals may have many reasons for donating,
including simultaneous motivations related to both private
and public beneﬁts.

This concept of public versus private beneﬁts have been
widely explored in the academic literature and has been
used as an operational guiding framework for possible
methods to measure the motivation to donate money or
time. According to some previous studies, motives asso-
ciated with public beneﬁts include altruism, trust in char-
ities, and trust. In contrast, motives related to private
beneﬁts include egoism, ﬁscal incentives, guilt, donation
attitude, past donation behavior and self-esteem (Konrath
& Handy, 2018).

Other studies have analyzed the concept of nonproﬁt
brand image and attempted to identify its impact. However,
conventional approaches to nonproﬁt branding have been
limited, and there is disagreement about what constitutes a
strong brand in the nonproﬁt sector (Boenigk & Becker,
2016; Michel & Reieunier, 2012).

In summary, the current research literature on the factors
that determine charitable giving is large. We have con-
ducted an extensive review of studies that refer to the
intention to donate (Table 1). This review allows us to state
that there is a wide variety of approaches and models. At
present, there is no global model that integrates the factors
proposed by ETPB theory and other factors suggested by
recent studies, such as altruism, trust, self-esteem, and
brand identiﬁcation.

The present study attempts to address this gap in the
literature. With this purpose in mind, the objective of this
paper is to explore the inﬂuence of different
intrinsic
motivations, speciﬁcally altruism, trust, past behavior, self-

esteem, donor attitude and brand identiﬁcation, on the
intention to donate to NPOs. While these factors were
analyzed separately in previous studies, few attempts were
made to formulate a comprehensive explanatory model.
The exploration of new donation mechanisms with the
inclusion of new variables is relevant because current
models of individual donor behavior require better theo-
retical foundations to justify their potential for future
research and theoretical development.

Understanding individual donor behavior is useful for
professional fundraising because NPO managers can use
this information to identify donor proﬁles to target their
campaigns to those who are most likely to donate and be
most generous.

This paper ﬁrst examines the literature on donor moti-
vation and develops hypotheses on how these factors
inﬂuence donation intentions. On the basis of the proposed
hypotheses, an explanatory model is deﬁned. In the next
section, we describe our methodology and results, and then
we provide a discussion of the theoretical and managerial
implications for NPOs.

Altruism

Altruism was described by Brewer (2003) as a cognitive
activity to help others. Moreover, altruism is a personal
characteristic that represents personal social responsibility
and a sense of mission (Rodrı´guez-Ricardo et al., 2019).

According to Konrath and Handy (2018), altruism is the
motivation that describes why individuals voluntarily give
up their private resources in exchange for goods and ser-
vices that improve the well-being of others.

In the context of conventional charity, it has long been
recognized that altruistic motives drive charitable behav-
iors (Cecere et al., 2017; Echazu & Nocetti, 2015). Altru-
ism is an interdisciplinary construct, although each
discipline seems to differ in its conception and use of the
term. In this research area, social psychology researchers
examine altruism in comparison to egoism. Scholars have
advocated the investigation of altruism on a motivational
continuum from altruistic to egotistic as opposed to using a
dichotomous model (Piatak & Holt, 2020).

Taking into account the ﬁndings of the previous studies
the following research hypothesis is

discussed above,
proposed in this paper:

Hypothesis 1 (H1) Altruism is positively associated with
the intention to donate to NPOs.

Self-esteem

High self-esteem is related to enhanced initiative and
levels of volunteerism (Briggs et al., 2007).
higher
According to the literature on donations, people are moti-
vated to give money and time to achieve prestige, respect,
and reputation (Sargeant et al., 2006; Tiltay & Torlak,
2020). Other previous studies found that self-esteem is an
important factor that inﬂuences engagement in altruistic
behaviors (Fu et al., 2017; Zheng et al., 2021).

Donating money or time to others can also increase the
donor’s self-esteem and self-conﬁdence by making the
donor feel more conﬁdent and competent (Zhang, 2009).

In this line of research, Wallace et al. (2017) establish
that self-esteem is an antecedent of donation behavior.
Similarly, Winterich et al. (2013) argued that being known
and respected are often effective motivations of giving
behavior. Because of the intrinsic advantages of high self-

esteem, it is considered an important driver of voluntary
giving. The following is therefore posited:

Hypothesis 2 (H2) Self-esteem is positively associated
with the intention to donate to NPOs.

Trust

Trust
is considered to be one of the principal factors
inﬂuencing donation intentions (Burt, 2014; Liu et al.,
2018). On this issue, Bennett and Barkensjo (2005) claimed
that trust is important in the context of donation, as it
indicates the sense of assurance that the NPO will use
donations appropriately.

Studies based on the economic theory of charitable giv-
ing have empirically demonstrated that individuals often
make donations expecting some beneﬁt or incentive in
return. One of the most common examples of this beneﬁt is
the tax deduction that is gained after the donation (Khu-
rana, 2021). Most studies have suggested that the greater
the opportunity for tax deductibility of donations, the larger
the amounts of donations (Cage´ & Gillot, 2021; Kumar &
Chakrabarti, 2021). However, tax policy varies from nation
to nation (Scharf & Smith, 2015; Mu¨ller & Lindenmeier,
2022), and only a few studies have explicitly assessed the
impact of advertising deductibility (Saeri et al., 2022).

Although many studies have analyzed the relationship
between trust and giving, the results have been very mixed,
with bivariate correlations ranging from positive (e.g.,
Ranganathan & Sen, 2012) to negative (e.g., Treiblmaier &
Pollach, 2008), and sometimes with no statistically sig-
niﬁcant association detected (e.g., Lin, 2021).

To resolve this controversy, Chapman et al. (2021)
recently conducted a systematic review of research pub-
lished between 1988 and 2020. Those authors found a
signiﬁcant relationship between trust and donations, but it
is important to emphasize that the available evidence does
not address the question of causality. Therefore, it remains
unclear whether trust is a determinant or a consequence of
charitable giving. It can be concluded that despite the
strong assumption that trust is important in encouraging
donations, the evidence supports only a positive associa-
tion, not a causal relationship.

In accordance with the above ﬁndings, in our study, we
intend to test whether trust is a determinant of donation
intention behavior. This study therefore hypothesizes the
following:

Hypothesis 3 (H3) Trust in NPOs is positively associated
with the intention to donate to NPOs.

Past Behavior

Past behavior has been considered an important determi-
nant of prosocial behaviors, including charitable giving
(Knowles et al., 2012; Mittelman & Rojas-Me´ndez, 2018;
Smith & McSweeney, 2007; Van der Linden, 2011).

However, previous

studies have shown that past
behavior is not by itself a predictor of future donation
behavior because it is mediated by other factors. On this
issue, Ajzen (2002) asserted that past behavior cannot be
treated exclusively as a sole causal factor, nor should past
behavior be accepted as an effective indicator of habit.
Other factors that have not been taken into consideration
could also contribute to the inﬂuence of habit because habit
is not explicitly represented in the theoretical models (Van
der Linden, 2011).
More precisely,

in the context of charitable giving,
Rosen and Sims (2011) established that charitable behavior
is most likely a learned habit, as they found that donating
time and money in adolescence was a strong predictor of
charitable behavior in adulthood.

There is sufﬁcient theoretical support to consider past
behavior as a determining factor of charitable giving in this
study. These ﬁndings lead to the following hypothesis:

Hypothesis 4 (H4) Past donation to NPOs is positively
associated with intention to donate to NPOs.

Donor Attitude

The prospective donor attitude toward the person who
requests the donation could make a crucial contribution to
the effectiveness of donation campaigns (Peasley et al.,
2018). Researchers need measures of attitudes toward
charitable giving to further develop and empirically test
theories of charitable giving behavior (Ranganathan &
Henley, 2008). In this context, previous studies have dif-
ferentiated between attitudes toward helping others and
attitudes toward charitable organizations.

Webb et al. (2000) maintain that for individuals with a
positive attitude toward helping others, donating to chari-
ties is only one way to achieve the goal of helping others.
This attitude involves ‘‘global and relatively enduring
evaluations with respect to helping or assisting others’’
(Webb et al., 2000, p. 300).

Furthermore, those authors stated with conﬁdence that
attitudes toward donating to charity are positively corre-
lated with charitable giving, and that
individuals with
strong positive attitudes toward philanthropy donated to
charities. In summary, attitude toward helping others is
generic in nature and can be considered an antecedent of
attitude toward charitable organizations. In our study, we
directly consider attitude toward charity donations.

Previous empirical evidence demonstrated that attitudes
toward charitable donations inﬂuenced the willingness or
intention to donate (Gorczyca & Hartman, 2017; Mittelman
& Rojas-Me´ndez, 2018; Peasley et al., 2018). Accordingly,
we hypothesize the following:

Hypothesis 5 (H5) Donors’ attitudes positively inﬂuence
the intention to donate to NPOs.

Brand Identiﬁcation

When an individual
identiﬁes with a NPO, he or she
commits themself to the successes and failures of the
organization. This suggests that individuals who identify
with an NPO are more likely to patronize the organization
because the success of the NPO is also their success
(Cornwell & Coote, 2005).

In this regard, it can be stated that donors always have a
more positive attitude and a stronger intention to donate to
NPOs that have a brand that is more congruent with their
own self-concept (Hou et al., 2014).

In summary, the literature on identiﬁcation suggests that
NPOs seeking to encourage retention should think about
the different identities of their donors. Helping donors
foster a favorable self-image, not only because they are
donors but also because of the values to which they aspire
is an effective strategy for NPOs to
or already hold,
strengthen their communication campaigns and fundraising
(Sargeant, 2013). This leads to the following hypothesis:

Identiﬁcation with an NPO’s brand is

positively associated with intention to donate to that NPO.

Results

First, the measurement model for intention to donate to
NPOs was assessed. Thus, the reliability of measurement
scales was studied. The individual reliability of the items
was evaluated through the loadings of the indicators. In
general, a high level of internal consistency was shown
(Table 4). In this case, all the loadings were greater than
0.708 except ALT2. However, the item was not removed
because the loading was greater than 0.4, and all other
indicators (below) were correct. The individual reliability
of each construct was veriﬁed using two indicators, Dijk-
stra-Henseler’s rho (qA) and composite reliability (CR)
(Hair et al., 2019). To evaluate convergent validity, the
average variance extracted (AVE) was used, which was
larger than 0.5 in all cases (Fornell & Larcker, 1981).
Likewise, the Fornell-Larcker criterion indicated a satis-
factory level of discriminant validity (Table 5). In addition,
the HTMT evaluation was realized with discriminant
validity when values were less than 0.90 (Henseler et al.,
2015) (Table 5).

Using the bootstrapping resampling procedure,
the
structural model was analyzed after the validity and relia-
bility of the measurement model were tested. Then we
proceeded to analyze the structural model.

The explanatory capacity of the global structural model
was assessed using the R2-value, which reﬂected the
explained variance of the dependent construct (Hair et al.,
2019). The R2-values were 0.453 for intention to donate to
a NPO (Table 6). Table 6 also shows the structural model
evaluation. Consequently, the path coefﬁcients as well as

Discussion

The empirical ﬁndings conﬁrmed the positive and signiﬁ-
cant relationship among trust, donor attitude, past behavior,
brand identiﬁcation and the intention to donate to NPOs.
Contrary to our expectations, altruism and self-esteem
did not have statistically signiﬁcant impact on the intention
to donate. Altruism has emerged as one of the most con-
troversial concepts in the social and behavioral sciences
(Bykov, 2017). Previous work has discussed naı¨ve theories
of altruism and focused on the norm of self-interest. These
scholars have suggested that helping, when motivated by
emotion, is selﬁsh because emotional actors are motivated
by intrinsic rewards rather than a desire to help others
(Barasch et al., 2014; Bykov, 2017; Khurana, 2021). These
authors proposed that although people may want to help
others (e.g., by supporting charitable causes), they often
hesitate to do so unless they can justify to themselves and
others that their behavior serves their own self-interest.
According to this approach, the logic inherent in altruism
as a determinant of prosocial behavior must be discounted
when individuals are motivated by the outcomes (material,
social or intrapsychic) that derive from that behavior.

Previous studies suggested a possible explanation based
on the inﬂuence of the context in which the donation takes
place. Konrath and Handy (2018) stated that donors’
motives may be conditioned by the context
in which
donations are made. Such contexts may support greater
socially enacted rewards, thereby decreasing the relative
importance of pure altruism (Gleasure & Feller, 2016).

Although many previous studies indicated that charita-
ble giving may be motivated by donors’ self-esteem (i.e.,
Konrath & Handy, 2018; Middleton & Lee, 2020; Wallace
et al., 2017), the results found in this research did not
empirically support
this relationship, contradicting the
proposed research hypothesis. Following Zhong and Lin
(2018), there are complicated arguments about the rela-
tionship among self-afﬁrmation, self-esteem, and prosocial
behavior.

In the present research, a statistically signiﬁcant rela-
tionship between past donation behavior and intention to
donate was found. This relationship was demonstrated in
several previous studies (Kashif & De Run, 2015; Knowles
et al., 2012; Smith & McSweeney, 2007; Van der Linden,
2011). To explain this ﬁnding, it can be argued that past
behavior operates as a risk-reducing mechanism. That is, a
past experience that is positive will most likely lead to
repeated behavior, as the associated perceived risk is much
lower in such cases (Michaelidou et al., 2015; Sargeant
et al., 2008). Nevertheless, one donation experience with a

given charity may not be enough to encourage the intention
to donate again. This experience (and relationship) must be
positive to signiﬁcantly inﬂuence donors’ willingness to
continue donating to an organization to which they have
previously donated (Beldad et al., 2014).

The ﬁndings in the present study revealed that donors’
attitudes signiﬁcantly contribute to the intention to donate
money. Similar ﬁndings were found in previous research,
such as that of Smith and McSweeney (2007), Mittelman
and Rojas-Mendez (2018) and Peasley et al. (2018).

To enhance (or maintain) positive attitudes toward
charitable giving, it can be useful for campaigns to remind
the community of how their contributions have translated

(or will translate) into tangible beneﬁts for aid recipients at
local, national and international levels (Knowles et al.,

increasing trust and conﬁdence in the practices of the
charitable organization.

The results of this research proved that identiﬁcation
with the brand of the NPO contributes signiﬁcantly to the
intention to donate. This ﬁnding is consistent with results
of previous studies such as those of Hou et al. (2014) and
Peasley et al. (2018). One possible explanation for this
inﬂuence is based on the principles of social identity theory
and organizational identity theory. That is, Sargeant (2013)
stated that donors are attracted to (and perhaps remain loyal
to) brands that they perceive to have a personality that
encompasses values congruent with their own, whether real
or aspirational.

However, the limitations of the foregoing explanation
must be considered in future research. Although identity
theory explains the speciﬁc outcomes of identiﬁcation in
the nonproﬁt sector, very little is known regarding how
identiﬁcation with a cause or recipient group impacts the
relationship between members and the organization (Payne
et al., 2019). In short, it is critical to differentiate between
identiﬁcation with the organization (brand) and identiﬁca-
tion with the cause or recipients.

Implications

One of the main implications for managers of NPOs is that
it is necessary to secure the trust of donors by enhancing
the reputation of the organization (Torres-Moraga et al.,
2010). To achieve this trust, the key would be to provide
effective communication between the organization and the
donor, thereby strengthening the donor’s familiarity with
the NPO. These communications should focus on con-
veying the work and actions of the organization. Therefore,
real images, videos, and objective information about the
use of donations should be used to attract potential donors
who are seeking information about potential projects with
which to become involved (Chen et al., 2019). This will
help improve perceived trust and lead to a greater intention
to donate. In addition, possible recommendations include
implementing social media campaigns or direct marketing
actions
to more effectively communicate appropriate
information to potential donors, as suggested by Romero-
Domı´nguez et al. (2021).

In this study, the inﬂuence of past donation behavior
was analyzed. For those with some donation experience,
the organization should emphasize information that is more
closely related to the individual’s own donation behavior
(Minguez & Sese, 2021).

123

A recent study conducted by Saeri et al. (2022) was
noteworthy in this area. Those authors conducted a meta-
review to identify which actions promoted charitable do-
nations. They identiﬁed four main strategies described as
focusing on individual victims, increasing the publicity of
donations, discussing the impact of donation and both
ensuring and promoting the tax-deductible status of their
charity.

Individuals who are socially attached to a group tend to
adopt supportive behaviors and are more inclined to par-
ticipate in them (Payne et al., 2019). In particular, those
authors observed that so-called ‘‘attitude homophily’’
describes the feelings of similarity that individuals have
with others and is signiﬁcantly related to each dimension of
identiﬁcation. Thus, it is proposed that NPOs build brand
messages based on increasing donors’ attitudes through
identiﬁcation. By studying the characteristics of each of the
targets identiﬁed by the NPOs, more tailored communica-
tions could be developed.

Limitations and Future Research

The present study has several limitations. First, the chosen
sample is geographically limited. Future studies could
include an analysis with participants from different coun-
tries to carry out a cross-sectional study. According to Ng
et al. (2011) and Middelton and Lee (2020), donating
behavior is inﬂuenced by culture. Second, the relationship
between the variables is analyzed according to the indi-
vidual donors of the NPO, which could limit the general-
izability of the study’s ﬁndings.

Additionally, it would be interesting to consider food, a
type of donation that has current importance. Food banks
are essential organizations that deliver groceries to food-
insecure families. However, food donations could have
declined in recent decades due to strict laws proposed by
the economic and public food security regulatory authori-
ties. Belton et al. (2020) stated that food aid organizations
rely heavily on donated food and are constrained by bud-
get, equipment, and stafﬁng considerations.
