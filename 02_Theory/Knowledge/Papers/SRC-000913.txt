Consumer‑based brand equity and consumer‑based brand
performance: evidence from smartphone brands in the USA
Deli Yang2 · Mahmut Sonmez1 · Mario Gonzalez2 · Yi Liu2 · Carol Y. Yoder3

Abstract
Consumer-based brand equity (CBBE) has attracted academic endeavor and strategic emphasis in practice to examine its role
for brand success. Despite its diagnostic power for brands, prior studies fail to: reach consensus on conceptualization and
operationalization, consider prospective consumers and competing brands, and identify linkages with brand performance.
Our study therefore proposes an integrative model of consumer-based brand performance (CBBP) to address its relationship
with CBBE. Within this aim, we intend to achieve two objectives. First, we prove that the key components of CBBE, that
is, brand attributes, associations and judgments, all play significant roles in determining CBBP. Second, we demonstrate
the detailed relationship among the three components of CBBE. Accordingly, we test hypotheses based on the structural
equation modeling of 1670 valid questionnaire responses from young consumers (aged 18–35) in the USA and comparative
analysis of two competing smartphone brands (iPhone vs. Samsung). As a result, we contribute to theoretical and empirical
understanding concerning the relationship between CBBE and CBBP. We also enrich brand empirical understanding by
addressing two main competing brands in the US smartphone industry. The combined SEM and comparative analysis add
both breadth and depth to current understandings. The results provide implications for practitioners to devise brand strategies
which consider the varied impact of consumers, as a key stakeholder, for success.

The strategic success of brands depends on consumers due
to their significant role, as a key stakeholder, for products
and services. A senior executive captures the significant consumer impact on the Coca-Cola Company: ‘If Coca-Cola
were to lose all of its production-related assets in a disaster,
the company would survive. By contrast, if all consumers
were to have a sudden lapse of memory and forget everything related to Coca-Cola, the company would go out of
business.’ (Handford et al. 2011). This remark underscores
consumer-based brand equity (CBBE) and its relationship
with brand success. Integrating leading scholars’ insights
(Keller 1993; Aaker 1996; Erdem et al. 1999), we define
CBBE as the perceptual assessment based on consumer
knowledge and experience with the brand name (i.e., company, organization, product and/or service). Understanding
consumers’ perspectives is strategically significant for brand
owners to: offer value-added products and services to consumers (e.g., Farquhar 1989), recognize the challenges with

competitors (Aaker 1991), recognize future profits (e.g.,
Crimmins 2000) and devise actionable strategies for endusers (Keller 1993). For example, since the 1980s, brand
equity has become a crucial asset for corporate mergers,
buyouts and acquisitions (e.g., Salzer-Morling and Strannegard 2004).
Alongside the strategic emphasis in practice, CBBE has
attracted scholarly endeavors and generated several key
conceptual models. Aaker (1991, 1996) emphasizes brand
awareness, perceived quality, association and loyalty. Keller
(1993) conceptualizes the differential effect of brand identity, meaning, responses and loyalty between consumers
and companies. Erdem et al. (1999) stress the significance
of consumer perceptions concerning product attributes.
Blackston (1992) proposes that brand relationships create,
build, sustain, reinforce and manage corporate brand success. Moreover, scholarly endeavor has also linked brands
with financial outcomes to quantify the current and existing
strength of brands (e.g., Simon and Sullivan 1993).
Despite the contribution of these key models, research
issues remain and motivate new research to develop this
versatile field. The above studies demonstrate diagnostic
power for brand potential (Ailawadi et al. 2003). Nevertheless, one highly recognized debate is that the CBBE models
lack consensus in both conceptualization and operationalization due to the complexity of brands (e.g., Datta et al. 2017).
Despite the consensus on the multidimensionality of CBBE,
no agreement exists on its dimensionality (Christodoulides
and de Chernatony 2010). In addition, limited empirical
studies utilize Aaker’s five-dimension model (Aaker 1991).
For example, prospective consumers are value creators for
brands and should be considered (Aaker 1996). Numerical
measurements are vital to compare competing brands for
positioning in Keller’s model (e.g., consumer reactions to
competing brands; Kapferer 2000). Moreover, empirical
findings tend to recognize unstable relations and illuminate
disagreements (e.g., Szocs 2014). Examining brand equity
based on product attributes may limit the measurements
in Erdem’s model (Myers 2003). Linking brand outcomes
(e.g., brand value) with consumer-mindset would also make
CBBE ‘complete’ (e.g., Ailawadi et al. 2003) since the existing studies center on consumer preference and repurchase
rather than their views on brand performance.
Given the above research issues, we take prior studies
one step further to examine the linkage between CBBE and
brand performance. This paper therefore aims to examine
the role of CBBE on consumer-based brand performance
(CBBP, aka Consumers’ perceived view of brand success;
Yang et al. 2015). In this process, we also clarify the relationship of major constructs within CBBE. Accordingly, we
intend to achieve two objectives to provide evidence that:
First, brand attributes, associations and judgments, as part
of CBBE, all play a significant role in determining CBBP.

Second, brand attributes, associations and judgments are
all significantly related, as key components of CBBE. To
achieve the above objectives, we rely on original data to
compare and contrast the success of two smartphone brands
(iPhone and Samsung). We formulate our hypotheses following an integrative framework upon examining previous studies. Subsequently, we test relevant relationships
based on the structural equation modeling (SEM) of 1670
valid questionnaire responses from young consumers (aged
18–35) in the USA.
We differentiate our contributions from three perspectives. First, we contribute to theoretical and empirical studies
to address the CBBE effect on CBBP and the relationship
among the key CBBE dimensions. Our proposed integrative
model addresses, from the perspective of consumers, the
connections among product attributes, judgment, association
and their role on CBBP. The findings help translate CBBE
value into corporate benefit, based on consumer behavioral
outcomes. The findings also clarify key CBBE dimensions.
In this process, we also contribute to empirical studies to
CBBE because we compare the two most competitive brands
in the smartphone industry in the USA, which has industrial
relevance to both the existing and prospective consumers.
After all, nearly 70% of the US population owns iPhone
(34%) and Samsung (33%) in 2017, based on US Statistica. Our combined SEM and comparative studies respond
to the call for measurement clarity to avoid mono-method
bias as well as provide in-depth study through comparison
of multiple groups (e.g., iPhone vs. Samsung with all-data;
goodness-of-fit comparison).
The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. The
second section examines the existing literature and establishes a theoretical framework before formulating hypotheses. The third section describes the instrument, sample,
constructs and measures, analytical techniques and model
validity. In the fourth section, we reveal the adjusted model
based on findings, respond to relevant hypotheses and conduct comparative studies. The fifth section provides discussions on how our findings relate to prior studies, detail contributions, identify limitations to guide future research and
discuss implications for practice.

Literature review, theoretical framework
and hypotheses
Prior studies on CBBE and CBBP
CBBE
Several conceptual frameworks on CBBE emerged in the
past three decades. Leuthesser (1988) argues that CBBE,
as a set of associations and behaviors, allows brands to earn

Consumer-based brand equity and consumer-based brand performance: evidence from smartphone…

greater volume or margins than it would without the brand
name, and that brand meaning is the major component of
brand equity. Farquhar (1989) suggests that CBBE adds
value since a brand endows a product with brand evaluations, brand attitudes and brand image. Srivastava and
Shocker (1991) argue that CBBE affects long-term cash
flows with brand strength and brand value. Erdem et al.
(1999) developed a framework based on product attributes
and consumer taste or utility weight. In product categories
with low involvement, intangible attributes could contribute
more to brand equity than tangible attributes (Myers 2003).
Among CBBE studies, the frameworks by Keller (1993,
2006) and Aaker (1991, 1996) are highly recognized and
influential. Aaker (1991) proposed five categories of brand
assets and liabilities linked with brands, which add or subtract value from the company. These categories of brand
assets are brand loyalty, brand awareness, perceived quality, brand association and other proprietary assets, such as
patents, trademarks and channel relationships (Aaker 1991).
Later studies suggest that each dimension uniquely contributes to brand management (Farjam and Xu 2015). For example, loyal consumers often refrain from considering alternative products, thereby helping cut costs (Ovidiu 2005).
Brand awareness (i.e., consumers’ ability to recognize/recall
product category associated with a brand) generates high
levels of purchase (Aaker 1991; Baldauf et al. 2003; Farjam
and Xu 2015). It also serves as an anchor for attaching other
brand associations (Farjam and Xu 2015). Perceived quality
facilitates brand differentiation in the market (Farjam and
Xu 2015) and is the major reason that consumers purchase a
product (Aaker 1992). Brand association is anything linked
to a brand in consumers’ memory that helps consumers process information, provide reasons to buy, and create positive feelings (Aaker 1991; Farjam and Xu 2015). Moreover,
brand assets provide a strong competitive advantage for the
firm (Aaker 1991; Ovidiu 2005). For example, patents and
trademarks protect ownership from unauthorized use allowing for competitive advantages over rivals. Subsequently,
Aaker has further improved the framework and replaced
‘brand assets’ with two new elements: brand leadership
and market behaviors (Aaker 1996). The framework is most
replicated, as a theoretical frontier in brand studies, but it
has also received its fair share of criticism. For example, it
is argued that the framework fails to acknowledge market
dynamics (Ponnam and Krishnatray 2008). That is, the loyalty measure does not consider prospective consumers who
have not yet developed loyalty to the brand.
Different from Aaker (1991, 1996), Keller argues that
brand equity is all about consumers’ minds, their thoughts,
feelings, perceptions, images, experiences—not simply
facts about brands. Brand equity is ‘the differential effect
that brand knowledge has on consumer response to brand
marketing activity’ (Keller 2016, p. 3). CBBE occurs when

consumers are familiar with the brand and hold ‘favorable,
strong, and unique brand association in memory’ (Keller
1993, p. 2). Accordingly, brand equity is what consumers know about brands (brand knowledge) and what that
knowledge implies for companies. Brand knowledge, as the
core of this framework, is descriptive and evaluative brandrelated personal meaning about a brand stored in consumers’
memory (Keller 2003). It consists of brand awareness (brand
recognition and recall) and brand image (brand association).
Brand recognition occurs when consumers can discriminate
a certain brand from others, and brand recall is the ability
to retrieve the brand memory when cued. Since a necessary
condition for creating a brand image is that a brand node
has been established in memory, brand awareness affects
consumer decision making by influencing the formation
and strength of brand association in the brand image (Keller 1993).
As the second component of brand knowledge, brand
image reflects particular brand association in consumers’
memory (Keller 1993). Brand association takes different
forms, with favorability, strength and uniqueness playing
important roles in consumer responses to brand equity
and decision making. Depending on the level of abstraction, brand association is categorized into three elements:
attributes, benefits and attitudes (Keller 1993). Attributes
are the descriptive features of a product or service, including the product/service itself, or other features (e.g., price
and packaging). Benefits are consumers’ personal values that
have been attached to the product or service (Keller 1993).
The most abstract dimension of brand image is consumers’
overall evaluation of the brand itself (Keller 1993; Wilkie
1986). Keller argues that brand evaluation is a function of
the most salient attributes and benefits of a brand.
Brand image varies depending on the favorability,
strength and uniqueness of brand association. Abstract association (e.g., benefits and attitudes) tends to be more evaluative, stays longer in consumers’ memory (Chattopadhyay
and Alba 1988; Keller 1993), and can be stored separately
from the underlying brand attribute information (Lynch et al.
1988; Keller 1993). The importance of congruence among
the dimensions of brand image is likely to determine consumers’ holistic reaction and ease of decision making (Keller
1993).
To further articulate the notion of brand knowledge,
Keller (2003) outlined more specific dimensions of brand
knowledge that include awareness, attributes, benefits,
images, thoughts, feelings, attitudes and experiences. Specifically, the newly emerged concepts are image, feelings and
experiences. Images are concrete or abstract visual information; feelings are personal affective responses to any brandrelated information; experiences are the purchasing and consumption behaviors and any other brand-related episodes
(Keller 2003, 2016).

Brand resonance represents the further development of
the framework (Keller 2001) that captures the process of
how consumers develop relationships with brands. The
foundation is brand salience, which includes the breadth
and depth of brand awareness. Salience is a much more profound concept than the previously outlined brand recognition
and brand recall (Keller 2001). The development of brand
relationship takes two routes—the rational route concerning
brand performance and judgment, and the emotional route
concerning image and feelings. The two routes are then confluent to create brand resonance. It is important to acknowledge the ‘crossover effects’ where the rational and emotional
elements do affect each other (Keller 2001, 2016). These
crossover effects set the foundation for our hypotheses.
In addition to the conceptual focus discussed above, two
empirical studies of CBBE attract scholarly attention. Yoo
and Donthu (2001) studied goods brands (e.g., athletic shoes,
television sets) and concluded that applications for goods
brands are more abundant than those for service brands. On
the other hand, Nam et al. (2011) examined service brands
and introduced an alternative empirical model. Specifically,
service brands have different characteristics (i.e., intangibility, perishability, heterogeneity and inseparability), and this
model consists of seven dimensions: physical quality, staff
behavior, ideal self-congruence, brand identification and
lifestyle congruence, brand satisfaction and brand loyalty.
Although both models are valid in the retailing sector, the
Nam et al. (2011) study demonstrated slightly higher validity
than that of Yoo and Donthu (2001) across different cultural
settings (Çifi et al. 2016).
CBBE effect on CBBP
Sporadic studies recognize how CBBE affects behavioral
outcomes. Most of them address the effect from purchasing perspectives, such as purchase or repurchase intention,
brand preference and price premium (Buil et al. 2013; CobbWalgren et al. 1995; Rambocas et al. 2018). Nonetheless,
consumer perceptions of brands based on their knowledge
and experience have intangible effects on companies, such
as brand reputation, loyalty, value and success or failure
relative to competitors (Yang et al. 2015). Brand loyalty is
directly connected with brand performance in the form of
purchase loyalty leading to greater market shares and attitudinal loyalty, which results in higher profit margins (Chaudhuri and Holbrook 2001). Consumers build their own views
of brand value based on their knowledge and experiences
with a brand and a firm (Keller and Lehmann 2006; Anisimova 2007). These values, manifested by consumers’ satisfaction and dissatisfaction with the brand, influence purchase
decisions. The previous research (e.g., Keller 1993, 2001)
has noted that the ultimate goal for the development and

application of CBBE model is to create effective corporate
strategy that improves brand performance.
In fact, Keller’s conceptualization was empirically supported by a study examining Chinese netizen’s perspective
on Internet search engines (Yang et al. 2015). Specifically,
consumers’ (netizens in this case) satisfaction, loyalty and
perceived value of brands are significant measures of CBBP.
That is, CBBP is the overall perceptions of how well the
brand is doing as perceived by relevant stakeholders, such
as consumers, managers (Yang et al. 2012) and shareholders. These perceptions are based on their direct knowledge
and experience related to products and services (Yang et al.
2015).
Consumer satisfaction influences the consumer–brand
relationship based on the attitudes formed through expectations and prior experience with the brand (Bearden and
Teel 1983; Cadotte, Woodruff and Jenkins 1987). Positive
consumer satisfaction leads to consumer actions, such as
reusing a product or a service, and ultimately to how markets react to these actions (Rust et al. 2004). Two aspects
of brand loyalty—purchase loyalty and attitudinal loyalty—
influence CBBP through their effects on market share and
high profit margins, respectively (Chaudhuri and Holbrook
2001). Consumers build their own views of brand success
based on their experiences and knowledge with a brand and
a firm (Keller and Lehmann 2006; Anisimova 2007). In turn,
consumers decide which brands have more equity based on
these features; these brands are then perceived to be more
successful than others (Villas-Boas 2004).
Competitive landscapes vary by country, promoting
distinct configuration of resources and capabilities among
firms, as a result of distinct competitive forces (Filieri et al.
2018). CBBP is influenced by the country, industry and firm
context (Yang et al. 2015). In other words, the way consumers evaluate brands’ satisfaction, loyalty and perceived
success will thus differ by the context in which brands operate. It is therefore important to examine the way consumers
form brand attributes, associations and judgments in different industries.

Theoretical framework
We can conclude, on the basis of prior studies, that the existing literature on CBBE and CBBP has demonstrated three
realities. First, diverse CBBE frameworks are divergent with
little consensus on either specific dimensions or operationalization (Christodoulides and de Chernatory 2010; Datta
et al. 2017). Nonetheless, the most recognized framework,
Keller’s CBBE, remains a popular conceptualization (Ponnam and Krishnatray 2008). Using Keller’s framework, as
a foundation, we test, using new empirical data directly
from consumers, the key components of CBBE recognized
by prior studies. Second, while the existing studies fail to

consider the relationships among CBBE dimensions (Szőcs
2014) and fail to consider competing brands (Ponnam and
Krishnatray 2008), our study will clarify how key CBBE
components relate to one another. Here, we compare two
competing brands in the smartphone industry. Third, the
relationship between CBBE and behavioral outcomes, as a
result of consumers’ knowledge and experience with brands,
is rarely addressed (Buil et al. 2013). While tangible behavioral outcomes, such as brand preference, price and repurchase intentions, are of particular interest for brand practitioners (Cobb-Walgren et al. 1995), the intangible behavior
outcomes, such as consumers’ view on brand performance,
are vital for formulating brand strategies (Keller 2001).
Therefore, we take a step forward from the previous work to
address the behavioral outcomes called CBBP (Yang et al.
2015).
On the basis of our literature critique, we have established a theoretical framework to serve our research purposes. Figure 1 illustrates several key relationships among
the CBBE constructs represented by brand attributes, brand
association and brand judgments, and between CBBE and
CBBP. Here, brand attributes are the descriptive features
of a product or service (Keller 1993). Brand association is
the link between information and brand nodes in consumer
memory that create meaning for the brand (op cit 1993).
Brand judgment refers to consumers’ personal opinions and
evaluations of the brand (Keller 2001). CBBP is defined as
the overall brand effectiveness perceived by relevant stakeholders—consumers (Yang et al. 2015). Based on this theoretical framework, we will hypothesize in the subsequent

section that CBBP is the result of simultaneous effects of
the three key components of CBBE (i.e., brand attributes,
brand association and brand judgment). The answer to this
question helps address the aim of this paper. Specifically, we
propose that brand attributes, brand associations and brand
judgments significantly affect CBBP (responding our first
objective). We also propose that the three key constructs
representing CBBE are positively related (responding our

both brand associations and brand judgments; brand associations impact brand judgments. In the subsequent section, we
detail the formulation of our hypotheses.

Hypotheses
Keller (2003) recognizes three core components of CBBE,
brand attributes, brand associations and brand judgment.
Among them, brand attributes are the descriptive features of
a product or service (Keller 1993). Consumers gain knowledge through word of mouth and their own research on competing brands. In the process, they form an understanding as
to what a brand is (e.g., expensive, user-friendly, reliable and
innovative). As a result, they form perceptions about brand
performance. Therefore, we hypothesize that
H1a Brand attributes are significantly positive to affect
CBBP.

play important roles in consumers’ decision making. All
these components are derived from consumer experiences
and perception, and directly link to brand performance. For
example, favorable and unique memory nodes attached to
a brand directly affect satisfaction, perceived value and
loyalty. A strong brand association creates positive effects,
which in turn, leads to a competitive advantage in the market, and further enhances CBBP.
H1b Brand associations are significantly positive to affect
CBBP.
Given our previous discussion of brand association, the
logic should also apply to the relationship between brand
judgment and CBBP. Rational and positive evaluations
toward a brand will enhance the overall effectiveness of
a brand and further improve a brand’s competitiveness in
the market. Brand judgment involves how consumers put
together all the different performance and association for the
brand to form varied opinions. While Keller (2001) identifies numerous influences, Aaker (1991, 1996) also suggests
that quality is an important part of brand performance. Consumers’ judgments about quality are likely to influence their
decision of purchasing, and further enhance CBBP.
H1c Brand judgments are significantly positive to affect
CBBP.
In addition, we propose that these three key components
of CBBE are related to one another. Brand attributes include
both product-related attributes (i.e., physical composition
of a product or service) and non-product-related attributes
(i.e., external aspects, e.g., price, packaging, user imagery
and usage imagery; Keller 1993). Both price and packaging
provide important information in purchasing decisions. User
imagery has to do with who will be using the product or service, and when and where the product or service will be used
(Keller 1993). These attributes are linked to feelings and
emotions to the brand and influence purchasing decisions
(Aaker 1991; Farjam and Xu 2015). Specifically, productrelated attributes, such as taste of food, quality of service
and appearance, directly contribute to consumers’ consumption experiences and satisfaction. The non-product-related
attributes, such as price and packaging, directly affect purchasing decisions. All these attributes should create memory
nodes that are linked to other associations with a brand.
H2a Brand attributes are significantly positive to affect
brand association.
Consumers’ personal opinions and evaluations of the
brand involve integrating all sorts of brand attributes. As
suggested by Aaker (1991, 1996), brand attributes influence

brand judgment. Keller (2001) suggests that quality, credibility, considerations and superiority (uniqueness) contribute
to brand judgment. Aaker (1991, 1996) further suggests that
quality is an important part of brand equity. While quality
is a product-related attribute, credibility, considerations and
superiority (uniqueness) are more relevant to the non-product-related attributes. These attributes are likely to affect
consumers’ judgment about a brand, which ultimately leads
to purchasing decisions.
H2b Brand attributes are significantly positive to affect
brand judgments.
Keller (2001) distinguished between brand feelings and
brand judgment. While brand judgment belongs to the
rational component of brand equity, brand feelings reflect the
emotional response to the brand. Such emotional responses
may also relate to social currency evoked by the brand,
which overlaps with the concept of brand association. The
emotional and rational elements do create crossover effects.
For instance, one important dimension of brand association is its favorability, which is consumers’ evaluation of
how favorable a brand association is (Keller 1993). That is,
brand association is not entirely based on emotion and affect;
instead, it has a rational component inherent to it. Thus,
brand judgments and brand association should relate to each
other in influencing purchasing and consumption decisions.
For instance, a positive brand association (i.e., perceived
benefits and positive attitude) is likely to induce positive
evaluations and judgment. Such a positive judgment, in turn,
leads to more positive feelings about the brand. Accordingly,
we hypothesize that
H2c Brand associations are significantly positive to affect
brand judgments.

Method
Instrument and data
We use an online questionnaire to collect primary data
for this study. The first and second authors pretested their
designed questionnaire with 293 undergraduate and postgraduate students in five classes of different sizes (10–120)
at two universities. These students’ feedback and responses
were useful sources for the questionnaire revision. Accordingly, a new version of the questionnaire was created in
Qualtrics, a user-friendly platform for online questionnaire
survey with helpful research support and automated system
to gather survey information.
Subsequently, all authors circulated the revised questionnaire to students in their classes to participate in our study.

Consumer-based brand equity and consumer-based brand performance: evidence from smartphone…

Respondents were asked to complete the survey to fulfill
course requirements or earn credit. They were also encouraged to seek out others from different universities across the
USA to respond using a non-probability convenience sampling method. Our questions center on consumers’ knowledge and experience about iPhones and Samsung brands.
Consumers have well-established perceptions concerning
smart phones and are comfortable making assessments
(Baalbaki and Guzman 2016).
In the questionnaire, after responding to demographic
questions, participants identified whether they had experience with iPhone and/or Samsung. They were asked to
rate 10 factors that they considered important, including the
design, cost and reputation on a 6-point Likert scale. This
was followed by questions assessing perceived knowledge,
awareness, association and satisfaction concerning Samsung
and iPhone brands. Additionally, participants were asked to
list three adjectives that have captured prominent characteristics of Samsung and iPhone brands. In the end, participants
were given the opportunity to provide comparative comments about the brands and overall performance based on
their knowledge and experience.
We have overcome the weakness of snowballing (i.e., losing track of the respondents) by following three strict sampling criteria. The respondents must be young consumers
(aged 18–35) based in the USA. They should perceive themselves knowledgeable and experienced about the two smartphone brands. This was detected through their response to
brand ownership and purchasing experience. Moreover, we
check the authenticity of responses in two ways. One is to
ask our students in the classroom to share a list of people
that they have contacted, who have confirmed responding
to the questionnaire. Students were incentivized to send
this list for extra credits, and we were able to use the list
to match with the respondents’ names in the dataset. The
other way to ensure authenticity is to look at the contact
email since the respondents tend to use their official email
address. Responses from private email or not listed by our
own students were deleted.
Using young consumers for our data collection is appropriate for this study. Statistically, 75% of young consumers
own smartphones, twice that of older adults (Rainee 2014)
and they invest more time and resources in their phones
(Kim, Chun and Lee 2014). Comparatively, they are more
interested in non-visual aspects of phones, especially connectivity to the Internet and communication with others
(Zhou et al. 2014a, b). They also quickly adapt to and use
more features (as well as sometimes abandon elements) relative to older adults (op cit). Therefore, familiarity with and
ability to distinguish features of smartphones are critical to
capturing the relationship to CBBP outcomes.
As a result, we received 1750 responses and 1670 of
them were valid for data analysis. Among the respondents,

the gender distribution is almost balanced (47.2% males
vs. 52.8% females). As for the age category, 91% are
18–24 years old with the remaining 9% being young professionals (aged 25–35). In terms of ownership, 78.7% use
or used an iPhone, 19% Samsung and 2.3% another brand.

Constructs and measures
This section details construct measures. We argue that consumers’ brand association and brand judgment are derived
from brand attributes. These three exogenous constructs play
an important role in determining CBBP, the endogenous
construct. As we pointed out earlier, the CBBE research has
an interesting reality to consider: Famous models tend to be
conceptual (e.g., Keller 1993; Aaker 1991). Several empirical studies focus on the Aaker model, but are restricted by
either product categories (e.g., del Vázquez et al. 2002) or
market (Chau and Ho 2008; Jensen and Klastrup 2008).
Additionally, others’ findings are unstable (Yoo and Donthu
2001; Koçak et al. 2007; Erdem et al. 1999) and have limited
linkages with brand performance (Yang et al. 2015). Given
the critique, we have worked comprehensively to include key
constructs and variables from prior research for our study.
To simplify our model, we have used only first-order constructs and measured all the variables on a Likert scale of
one to six, with higher numbers signaling more endorsement
of the item. For example, brand attributes, such as design,
network, security, interface and software, are important for
consumers, but other attributes (e.g., pricing) were eliminated due to insignificance to the construct and unimportant
for consumers’ CBBE. Figure 2 shows our confirmed model;
Table 1 provides descriptive statistics and correlations, with
no evidence of collinearity between explanatory variables;

average variance extracted (AVE above 53%), indicating a
good convergence of constructs.

Analytical techniques and model validity
We adopt two analytical techniques to search for new findings: structural equation modeling (SEM) and comparative case analysis. The SEM is an appropriate technique
because it allows simultaneous measures of all variables
and comparative models (Samsung vs. iPhone) based on
all-sample observations and comparative sample results.
As a result, we are able to establish a validated model with
both measurement and structural validity. Accordingly, we
use the model to test the findings for the two smartphone
brands and generate comparative findings. The comparative brand analysis details and deepens the SEM-based
findings due to its emphasis on the similarities and differences of the two competing brands in the US market. The
SEM complements the case comparison because of the
established structure and measures serving as a framework
for contextual analysis.

consumers represent an important stakeholder among others (managers, wholesalers and retailers), who can provide
a subjective measure of firm performance (e.g., Hult et al.
2008). Finally, the young consumers that we have studied

Results
This section provides responses to hypotheses and comparative results. Overall, the findings support our argument
that CBBE significantly influences CBBP. This is because

CBBE, based on brand attributes, association and judgment,
have direct and simultaneous impact on CBBP explaining
94% of the variations in CBBP for the all-sample model. The
three CBBE constructs have different degrees of connections
that will be explained in this section. We also compare and
contrast the two brands with the all-data sample. Figure 2
presents a validated model that demonstrates details, similarities and differences from Fig. 1.

Responses to hypotheses
The results support all the exogenous effects on the endogenous construct, CBBP, despite the twist for the effect of
brand attributes. Our findings refute H
­ 1a: Brand attributes
are significantly positive to affect CBBP; instead, the result
is reversed since brand attributes are significantly negative to affect CBBP. As some consumers indicated in their
responses, frequent changes of attributes can undermine
the consistency and stability of the brand. Changes can also
cause confusion when new attributes are not transmitted to
consumers, causing wider differential effects in brand knowledge between brand owners and consumers. Meanwhile, our
findings support H
­ 1b: Brand association is significantly positive to affect CBBP, and H
­ 1c: Brand judgment is significantly
positive to affect CBBP.
The results also support ­H2a: Brand attributes are significantly positive to affect brand association, and H
­ 2b: Brand
attributes are significantly positive to affect brand judgment.
Brand attributes explain over 35% of the variations in brand
association and 27% in brand judgment. The findings suggest that consumer knowledge and experience about brand
functionality are fundamental for perceptions. However, the
insignificant results give us reason to refute ­H2c: Brand association is significantly positive to affect brand judgment. Our
findings generate inconsistent results for the all data sample
(insignificant) and opposite significance for the brand groups
(iPhone positively significant and Samsung negatively significant). These contradictory results suggest that the effect
of brand association on brand judgment is unstable.

Comparative findings: iPhone versus Samsung
We have also conducted sub-sampling analysis between the
two brands to reveal similarities and differences between
them and to the all-data results (See Tables 4 and 5). Comparably, the sub-sample results demonstrate an overall
consistency with the all-data findings. First, both brands
demonstrate a high explanatory power for the variations in
the CBBP model (95% vs. 98% in comparison with 94%
all-data). Their variations were tested significantly different
suggesting the independence of the brands. Second, both
brands demonstrate that CBBP highly depends on consumer
satisfaction, loyalty and perceived value (iPhone 83% vs.
Samsung 79% in comparison with all-data 80%). These consistent results (no significant difference) suggest that, regardless of brands, consumer satisfaction, loyalty and perceived
value explain CBBP, as a latent variable.
Third, both brands show significantly positive effects of
brand attributes on brand association and brand judgment.
The results are consistent with the all-data findings, and the
sub-samples show no significant difference. This means,
regardless of brands, brand attributes have a positive impact
on brand association and brand judgment.
Finally, another consistency with the all-data result is
that brand judgment strongly affects CBBP, being positively significant at the p < 0.001 level like the all-sample
result. These three datasets generate no difference in their
findings. The results provide details as to the impact of
brand perceptions on CBBP by judging, for example,
views on brand quality, superiority and global standing.
In addition to the similarities, the variations between
the sub-samples and to the all dataset should be highlighted. While these results seem to fit with the stereotypes of the two brands, we also claim some inconclusive
findings. First, in terms of CBBP power, all three groups

demonstrate significant differences. Samsung shows
stronger measuring power of CBBP than iPhone. These
variations suggest that despite the overall consistency in
our findings, consumers’ knowledge and experience with
these brands are heterogeneous, thereby influencing their
views on brand performance.
Second, in the brand association and judgment relationship, despite the fact that all-sample data demonstrate no
effect on brand judgment, the two brand groups differ in
opposite ways, showing a positive significance for iPhone,
but a negative one for Samsung. The results suggest that
the connections between association and judgment are more
strongly related for iPhone than for Samsung.
Third, brand attributes–CBBP relationship is significantly
negative for iPhone, but insignificantly negative for Samsung. The results further underscore the distinctions between
the two brands. Despite the need for sleek designs, security,
and software associated with brand attributes, too many features adversely impact CBBP due to the confusion caused.
After all, gaps of brand equity between consumers and brand
providers tend to exist.
Next, despite the complete dataset confirming the positive significance between brand association and CBBP,
iPhone and Samsung data result differently. That is, iPhone
findings are consistent with the whole dataset (p < 0.001),
while Samsung responses are insignificantly negative, and
the two-brand comparison is significantly different. This
means that iPhone has higher brand association for CBBP
than Samsung. One speculation is that Samsung shares the
Android operating system provided by Google with many
other companies. As a result, its brand attributes and association may not be as clearly defined as iPhone. Moreover,
Apple’s ‘few products’ strategy instilled by Steve Jobs has
attracted diehard fans.

In addition, the distinction between the two brands is also
shown in Table 5, a summary of top adjective descriptions
from the respondents. First, consumer perceptions are highly
concentrated for iPhone, but fragmented for Samsung. For
iPhone, the top nine perceptions account for nearly 90%
of the descriptions, including over 65% for the top three
adjectives. In stark contrast, the top three descriptions only
account for approximately 37% of the responses for Samsung. The findings suggest that consumers’ views on iPhone
are broadly consistent, but Samsung is perceived diversely,
and the two brands are treated distinctively.
Second, being ‘expensive’ is consistent for iPhone, but
contradictory for Samsung. Consumers have long held a
brand perception of iPhone being pricey; therefore, it is
unsurprising that this was rated as the number 1 feature.
Contradictory views are detected for Samsung since the top
two descriptions are at two extremes (cheap vs. expensive).
This suggests that consumers have divergent perspectives
concerning the Samsung brand. Regardless, this feature is
considered unimportant and excluded due to insignificance.
Finally, Samsung brand demonstrates a higher ranking in
its innovative endeavor because consumers rank it number
3 in contrast with number 9 for iPhone. This brand image
seems to fit with its endeavor to advance technologies. For
example, Samsung’s patenting activities have surged to 8500
(US Patents and Trademark Office), as the number 1 patent
company in the USA in 2016. Meanwhile, consumers have
perceived Apple’s innovative efforts as slow and non-substantive. For example, some respondents commented: ‘If you
look at what is new in iPhone 8 from iPhone 7, you’d find
no “wow” factor there since all the changes are “cosmetic”.’

Discussion
Finding summary
This paper aims to demonstrate consumer-based brand
equity (CBBE) positively enhances consumer-based brand
performance (CBBP). Based on the comparative analysis
and structural equation modeling of 1670 original questionnaire responses collected from US consumers, we
validated an integrative model of CBBP (Fig. 2) to reflect
on the explanatory power of CBBE. As a result, CBBP
has explained 94% (95% Apple and 98% Samsung) of the
variations in CBBP. That is, CBBE, represented by the key
constructs of brand attributes, brand associations and brand
judgments, significantly affects CBBP, which is represented
by consumer value, loyalty and satisfaction.
We have achieved the first objective by providing evidence that CBBE’s key components, brand attributes, brand
associations and brand judgment, have distinct explanatory
power and direct and indirect effects on CBBP. This is

addressed in detail with three hypotheses. Our findings confirm that brand attributes show significantly negative effects
on brand CBBP. This reverses the original ­H1a since all the
models demonstrate negative direction or significance. This
result affirms a prior study (Sonmez et al. 2013) that frequent
changes of technology-based brand attributes can cause confusion and widen gaps of brand knowledge, thereby causing
negative effects on strategy effectiveness. Meanwhile, our
findings affirm that consumers’ brand associations and judgments are significantly positive to affect CBBP (supporting
­H1b and ­H1c). For brand association, despite the overall consistency, Samsung brand does not demonstrate a significant
support. However, these variations do not detract us from
concluding the significance of the relationship since both
the whole dataset and iPhone models support significant
linkages. The positive relationship between judgment and
CBBP is consistent in all the models with no significant
differences. This finding strengthens Keller’s conceptual
argument (1993) that consumers’ judgment matters to brand
performance.
We have also achieved our second objective, showing that
CBBE key components: brand attributes, brand associations
and brand judgment are significantly related. We demonstrate that brand attributes significantly impact consumers’
views of a particular brand in the form of brand association
­(H2a) and brand judgment ­(H2b). This allows us to assert
prior argument (Erdem et al. 1999) that brand attributes
are a significant factor influencing CBBE. The result also
aligns with Myers (2003), who notes that it is insufficient to
consider brand attributes only as other components should
also be considered simultaneously. Meanwhile, we cannot
confirm that brand association affects brand judgment due
to model uncertainties ­(H2c). Specifically, the model tests
were insignificant for the whole dataset and inconsistent for
the group data (i.e., positive for iPhone users and negative
for Samsung users).

Implications for practice
This paper confirms that the fundamental success of brands
depends on its stakeholders, in this case consumers; therefore, forging strong CBBE relationships matters. First, companies should consider the varied effects on CBBP. This is
because consumers’ knowledge and experience help them
form views about brands and these views have direct impact
on their perceptions of brand performance. Although the
holistic effect from brand attributes, association and judgment strongly affect CBBP (94%), cautions need to be taken
since the three key components serve distinctive and sometimes opposite roles (negative significance of brand attributes vs. positive effects of association and judgment). These
perceptions translate into CBBP in terms of value, satisfaction and loyalty.

D. Yang et al.

Second, companies should evaluate competing brands and
prospective consumers. Consumers, in the process of making
purchasing decisions, build knowledge and experience about
brands and contextualize brand performance. Our findings
demonstrate that iPhone and Samsung, despite their consistent relationship with CBBP, have distinctive CBBE. That is,
consumers may not buy every brand, but they assess many
competing brands to form perceptions of brand equity and
subsequently decide whether they should have an allegiance
with a particular brand. Some consumers (2.3% surveyed)
even owned both brands, thereby gaining knowledge and
experience, and forging opinions on comparative strengths
and weaknesses of relevant brands. Brand companies can
therefore use competing brands and prospective consumers’
views to position their products and services. Such comparative evaluations will provide vital information for the
brand’s future in the industry in terms of competitiveness
and market prospect.
Finally, the key components in CBBE have different
degrees of connections. Impact from brand attributes, association and judgment suggests variations that brand companies
should utilize for brand strategies. For example, companies
should either reduce too frequent changes of brand attributes
or ensure that consumers receive timely communications
about new updates. Such actions can also enhance positive
effects on brand association and judgment and advance strategic considerations to ensure brand success.

Limitations for future research
Our findings warrant further studies. First, we have identified a valid model to examine consumer-based brand performance, but the generalization of the model requires further
tests due to the limitations of the settings. Our model relies
on two global brands in the US setting, which may restrict
us from confirming certain relationships in the model.
Although the high consistency of the results validates the
model, some uncertainties with different-sample results
(e.g., brand attributes to CBBP; brand association to brand
judgment) require further verification in the future.
Second, although we view the results from these global
brands in the USA to be largely representative given the
significance of the brands and the market, testing in other
empirical settings would help understand global brands in
international contexts. For example, consumers may form
different perceptions for the same global brand in other
countries or other global brands in the USA. This future
action will also help enhance the understanding of CBBP in
the global context.
Finally, our findings were based on a large dataset and
strict data collection criteria, but the justification of the data
representation may not eliminate the inherent weaknesses
derived from convenience sampling. The measurement and

structural model fits, and consistency of the comparative
results support the generalizability of the model, but systematic sampling, longitudinal studies, and further tests of the
measures using new data will enhance precision and overcome the limitations of this study.

a Ph.D. degree of Management Science—International IP Management—at the Manchester Business School, the University of Manchester, UK. Her research centers on cross-cultural conflicts and uncertainties in patents and brands on internationalization. She has sole-authored
three scholarly books, including Understanding and Profiting from IP:
Strategies across Borders (2nd ed.), two co-edited books, over 80 peerreviewed journal papers. She is a former consultant for UN World IP
Organization.
Mahmut Sonmez is a scholar and educator in management science and
statistics. He received a Ph.D. in Management Science—Decision Science at the Manchester Business School, the University of Manchester,

UK. His research focuses on strategic decisions against IP uncertainties, comparative studies of developed and developing countries, and
their firms. He has published over 20 peer-reviewed journal papers. He
teaches Business Statistics, and Management Sciences.
Mario Gonzalez is currently Assistant Professor of Marketing. He
received a Ph.D. in Applied Economics from Universidad de Alcala,
Spain. His research focuses on the ways changes in the social context
of marketplaces challenge customer identities. He has published in a
variety of academic outlets. He teaches International Marketing, Marketing Research, and Marketing Strategy.
Yi Liu is currently Assistant Professor of Management. She received
a Ph.D. in Management from Georgia Institute of Technology, USA.
Her research encompasses creativity, leadership, and change management. Her research has appeared in several peer-reviewed journals of
organizational behaviors and management. She teaches Organizational
Behavior, and Human Resource Management.
Carol Y. Yoder is Professor of Psychology. She earned a Ph.D. in Experimental Psychology from Texas Tech University, USA. Her research
focuses on applied cognition, esp. the role of affect on memory and
cognition, and how cultural values impact thinking and decision
making. She has co-authored a textbook on applied cognition, and
three-dozen peer-reviewed journal papers. She currently serves as an
associate editor for the Behavioral Development Bulletin. She teaches
Fundamentals and Applications in Cognitive Psychology, Lifespan
Development, and Food Matters.
