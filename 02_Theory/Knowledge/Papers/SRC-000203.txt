Abstract
Purpose – As staff are vital to successful re-branding, particularly in the charity sector where restricted budgets limit reliance on external marketing, it
is important to understand the impact of re-branding on staff. This study aims to examine the effect of time on staff knowledge, attitudes and behaviour
and, in addition, the interaction of time with seniority, tenure and level of support for re-branding.
Design/methodology/approach – The paper first explores the literature from both the for-profit and non-profit sectors. A quantitative study was
undertaken in nine leading, UK charities that had re-branded two, three and four years ago; n ¼ 345. The data were analysed using one-way and twoway ANOVAs.
Findings – A negative relationship was found between time since re-branding and the three constructs of knowledge, attitudes and behaviour. But this
consistency was not mirrored by a consistency in the impact of interaction effects.
Practical implications – Re-branding is not a one-off event. To sustain its benefits, organizations need to re-visit its outcomes on a regular basis to
ensure staff retain new knowledge, remain positively motivated and maintain their recently adapted behaviours over time.
Originality/value – Thought to be the first empirical paper to explore the effects of re-branding over time. Furthermore, the findings contradict those
from the extant literature that claim that organizational change requires a “settling in” period. By contrast these findings suggest that the positive
effects of re-branding are best felt in the immediate wake of re-branding and thereafter fade over time.

Introduction
Re-branding is a consequence of time. To remain relevant and
desirable, it is necessary to re-visit the organizational
proposition and re-assess its strategic value to stakeholders.
Hence, re-branding signals change, a new beginning and
hopefully a better one (Stuart and Muzellec, 2004).
While re-branding is often managed by external branding
consultants, the implementation is the responsibility of the staff
within the organizations. Their role is pivotal to the success of
re-branding (Free, 1999; Ind and Bell, 2000; Webster, 2002)
and it is they who ultimately own its consequences. Hence staff
are key stakeholders in its effectiveness.

measure the effectiveness of re-branding; the acquisition of
new knowledge, (new straplines, messages and values), more

reason. The significant costs associated with identity makeovers involve the re-evaluation of the organization’s
proposition (Lomax and Mador, 2006; Boyle, 2002; Kaikati
and Kaikati, 2003). Weick (1995, p. 16) describes this process
as “an expression in organizational sense making”.
Initially, such fundamental change contradicts traditional
marketing theory with its emphasis on consistency in building
strong and successful brands (Hankinson and Hankinson,
1999; Aaker and Joachimsthaler, 2000; De Chernatony, 2001).
However long term brand management predicates an ongoing
review of the original proposition to ensure its continued
relevance (Feder, 1998), contemporaneity (Kaikati and Kaikati,
2003) and uniqueness in the face of environmental change
(Knox and Macklan, 1998). Hence re-branding organizations
are those that re-visit their organizational propositions and
re-assess their strategic value to target markets.
Drivers for re-branding may be prompted by a fall in revenue
caused by a decline in voluntary donations in the charity sector.
It may be prompted by mergers, for example, Guinness and
Grand Metropolitan which formed Diageo in 2000 or Imperial
Cancer Research Fund and Cancer Research Campaign
forming Cancer Research UK in 2003; or de-mergers as when
Andersen Consulting distanced itself from Arthur Andersen
Worldwide and became Accenture in 2001. Another trigger may
be a need to adapt to modern values as when the Spastics
Society became Scope to avoid the stigmatising image of
spasticity (Lomax and Mador, 2006); or to provide a single
global presence as when HSBC subsumed eight local brands,
world-wide, under a single brand name (Lomax and Mador,
2006). Alternatively, re-branding may result from the
appointment of a new chief executive, possibly brought into
address organizational problems. In each of these circumstances,
a “paint job” to the visual identity of the organization may be
found wanting (Coles, 2003), as British Airways found to their
cost when they rolled out their new “tailfin” campaign. While
the campaign was designed to represent “visual celebrations of
the world’s diversity” (Hatch and Schultz, 2003, p. 1053),
British Airways failed to follow through with changes to the
organization’s proposition. Only if the visual identity is
accompanied by real change within organizations will the rebrand have any meaning to stakeholders.

The literature on the contribution of staff to re-branding is
limited despite its importance. Thomson et al. (1999)
emphasise the importance of staff understanding the values
the organization represents. They argue that staff need to
internalise these values before they deliver them to
stakeholders. Alternatively, an organization may recruit staff
on the basis that their values are already aligned to those of
the organisation (Hankinson, 2000, 2004), a process known
as value-based interviewing and considered particularly
appropriate to the charity sector (Stainton-Williamson,
2005; Girod, 2005). Ahmed and Rafiq (2003) emphasise
the significance of staff commitment to organizations. They
report that commitment and involvement when coupled with
a clear sense of purpose are a “prerequisite for the much
needed coherence and focus that are ultimately required to
produce successful outcomes” (Ahmed and Rafiq, 2003,
p.1179). A third aspect of how staff contribute to the
branding of an organization lies in behaviour. Here studies by
Thomson and Hecker(2000), Ahmed and Rafiq(2002, 2003)
and Aurand et al. (2005) from the for-profit sector and Ind
and Bell (2000) and Hankinson (2004) from the charity
sector emphasise the importance of “living the brand”, i.e.
behaving in ways that reflect the values of the organization. It
is the acid test of how sincerely staff internalise the
organizational proposition (Hankinson, 2004).
It follows, therefore, that any change to the corporate or
charity brand will, of necessity, require the full participation
and support of staff (Boyle, 2002; Kaikati and Kaikati, 2003;
Coles, 2003).
The time factor in re-branding
Some of the marketing literature on re-branding notes that
organizations need time to change (Ind, 1997; Hankinson and
Hankinson, 1999). Staff need to internalise new brand values,
for example, to commit to those values and to behave
differently (Kaikati, 2003; Coles, 2003; Hankinson, 2004).
This view is also supported by human resource theorists who
argue that the fear of change can slow its adoption and in
some cases lead to counter-implementation (Keen, 1981;
Recardo, 1991; Boddy, 2002). Kotter and Schlesinger (1979)
make a similar point when they note that staff may not
understand the advantages of change and perceive the cost to
be greater than the benefit. Change management theorists
also argue that staff need time to adapt to change (Mintzberg,
1994; Stacey, 1994; Clarry, 1999). These writers note that
introducing change to organizations is an emergent or
adaptive process during which staff learn new ways of
thinking and doing.
The literature describes ways of speeding up the adaptive
process. Visionary leadership is considered a vital ingredient
here. Ind and Bell (2000), Gratton (2004) and Lomax and
Mador (2006) emphasise the importance of senior
management engaging staff in the change process, both in
its planning and its implementation. Boddy (2002, p. 332)
observes that “if people can say ‘I helped to build this’, they
are more willing to live and work with it”.
Asif and Sargeant (2000) and De Chernatony and SegalHorn (2001) note that good communication can also help
speed up the embedding process. The more efficient the
channel of communication, the faster re-branding messages
reach stakeholders (Hankinson and Lomax, 2006).

importance of effective communication to communicate

Staff roles in re-branding
People are a significant part of the services mix, (Booms and
Bitner, 1981). It is surprising, therefore, that the concept and
practice of internal branding, which recognises the
fundamental importance of people in delivering the brand
experience, has received less attention than external branding.
Nevertheless, in the last decade, marketers have laid more
emphasis on the contribution of staff to brand success (De
Chernatony and Segal-Horn, 2001; Bergstrom et al., 2002;
Ahmed and Rafiq, 2003; Lomax and Mador, 2006). There is
a recognition that staff are key to building relationships with
stakeholders, including brand users (Hatch and Schultz,
2001) and are an integral part of the brand itself (Thomson
and Hecker, 2000; Ind and Bell, 2000). As a result, Ahmed
and Rafiq (2003) propose that relationship marketing
provides a more plausible theoretical approach to internal
marketing than the traditional transactional approach that has
previously underpinned the notion of “employees as
customers” (Berry, 1981, p. 26).

This study extends the approach of Hankinson and Lomax
(2006) in which staff responses to re-branding were measured
according to three consistently well documented dimensions
from the branding literature: knowledge, attitudes and
behaviour. Knowledge, for instance, relates to an
understanding of the organization’s proposition, its values
and vision, its logo and slogan. A good understanding of what
the organization does and how it does it, is thought
fundamental to staff commitment to the organization
(Hankinson, 2004; Thomson et al., 1999; Free, 1999).
Attitudes are generally perceived as the emotional link with
knowledge (Thomson et al., 1999; Ambler and Barrow, 1996)
which some also perceive as key to organizational success
(Burman and Zeplin, 2005; Thomson, 1998). Attitudes may
help determine the extent to which knowledge is acted on in
the interests of the organization (Burman and Zeplin, 2005).
Finally, brand behaviour, what Burman and Zeplin (2005)
identify as “brand citizenship” in which staff “live the brand”
(Ind, 2004; Hankinson, 2004), for instance, adopting
considerate and co-operative behaviours to colleagues or
engaging with staff training. Behaviour is the acid test of
brand reality, where knowledge of and attitudes towards the
organisation result in positive brand behaviours (Hankinson,
2004; Bergstrom et al., 2002).
This study explores the effect of time since re-branding on
these three conceptual foci. It also explores the interactive effects
time since re-branding with seniority within the charity, length of
service and the extent to which re-branding is supported.

The evidence so far seems to suggest that re-branding
requires time to embed itself within organizations and that its
effects on staff will be more apparent, longer-term.
However, from the organizational behaviour literature,
Gagliardi (1986) describes a different phenomenon which
may shed light on how quickly and how permanently change
may take place in organizations. He identifies three types of
change. The first type he calls apparent change. Here the
organizational identity changes in terms of its artifacts, for
example its name, logo and symbols but there will be no
significant change to the organization’s deeper values, its
vision and mission. Hence the original organizational
proposition remains intact. The second type of change he
calls revolutionary change which typically exists in merger
scenarios. Here, there may be root and branch change to both
the organizational identity and the proposition as new logos
and symbols and new values are imposed at the expense of the
original. The third type of change, Gagliardi (1986), terms
cultural incrementalism that is the only type of change that
reaches a deeper level by promoting the co-existence of both
old and new values. Gagliardi (1986) argues therefore, that
whereas the second (revolutionary) and third (cultural
incrementalism) types of change may result in longer lasting
change, the first type of change (apparent) may be shorter
lived.
Case studies in the re-branding literature in both the forprofit and charity sectors also demonstrate shorter term
effects as staff become energised by the huge orchestration
that often accompanies a re-branding roll-out and quickly
embrace concomitant change – see Smirnoff (Hankinson and
Hankinson, 1998); Voluntary Services Overseas (Ind and
Bell, 2000); Accenture (Kaikati and Kaikati, 2003). One
explanation may lie in the Hawthorne effect in which any
change to the working environment may influence
performance, simply because staff feel “more special” as the
organization takes notice of them (Boddy, 2002).
From the advertising literature, there is further evidence to
suggest short-term change. Jefkin and Yadin (2000) and Fill
(2002) for example note that the impact of advertising
deteriorates or decays once the advertising has ended i.e. that
recognition and recall of advertising is short-lived, that
memory fades. Hence the need for sustained advertising or
repeated advertising bursts. Bogart (1995) and White and
Dawson (2003) also support the need for repetition, claiming
that the longer the interval between repetitions, the greater
the opportunity for interference from competing messages.
Hence there can be “no recency without frequency” (White
and Dawson, 2003, p. 42).
Re-branding is a comparatively new area of study. It is also
an expensive management initiative that may prompt
criticism, particularly in the context of charities where some
might see it as being funded at the expense of their
beneficiaries. This study investigates a number of leading
UK charities and explores the effect of time since re-branding
on staff knowledge, attitudes and behaviour as well as its
interactive effects.

Methodology

Ethical considerations
The principle of informed consent was paramount. Each
questionnaire was accompanied with a covering letter stating
the purpose of the research and the impartiality and
independence of the researchers. In accordance with the
ethics of both universities, the principles of the British
Sociological Association and the Market Research Society, the
data were held in strictest confidence and the analysis ensured
anonymity of research subjects.

Sample
The research was undertaken among leading UK charities
(Charity Trends, 2003) as larger charities were more likely to
have the resources to re-brand. Those charities that had rebranded in the last four years were identified through a short
telephone interview with the Top 100 charities, as defined by
voluntary income. From a total of 70 responding charities, 38
were found to have re-branded in the last four years. From
this a convenience sample of ten leading charities was drawn.
Two charities had re-branded four years ago, three charities
had re-branded three years ago and four charities had rebranded two years ago and one charity had re-branded in the
last year.

Data analysis
We defined levels of seniority (senior management, middle/
lower management, clerical/administrative), length of service
(0-2 years, 3-6 years, 7 þ years) and support for rebranding
(high, medium and low).
The original sample contained responses from ten different
charities. One charity in our sample had re-branded one year
before the survey. Preliminary analysis suggested that the
responses from this charity followed a very different pattern
from the remainder of the data, reporting significantly lower
scores than others on all of our measures. Consequently this
charity was omitted from the analysis. Descriptive statistics
for the data set appear in Table I (n ¼ 345).
Cronbach’s alpha was calculated for our four constructs,
each showed high levels of consistency between items and
each of the three conceptual foci (knowledge 0.860, attitudes
0.906 and behaviour 0.768) as well as the five support
questions (0.828), an indication of the reliability of the item
statements to produce consistent results with repeated
measures. As a result no question was excluded from the
main analysis.
Independent variables were created by aggregating item
scores from questions around each of the conceptual foci to
form a single composite score for knowledge, attitudes and
behaviour. The five “support” questions were re-scaled into
“high”. “medium” and “low” support categories in which the
distribution was allowed “to define what is high and low as
opposed to the researcher’s arbitrary notions” (de Vaus, 1996,
p. 273) – see Appendices 1-4 for item questions making up
composites.

Questionnaire design
A total of 25 item statements were developed around the three
conceptual foci of knowledge, attitudes and behaviour. A sixpoint agreement scale accompanied each item statement from
“strongly agree”(6) to “strongly disagree”(1) which allowed a
wide spread of responses. There was no neutral mid-point to
avoid participant passivity. Six item statements were
negatively worded to reduce survey participants adopting
directional bias in their responses. In addition, there were two
demographic questions relating to level of seniority and length
of service and four items (with six-point agreement scales as
above) relating to level of support for re-branding (see
Appendix 1). The item statements may be assumed to have
face validity, which though subjective in nature, nevertheless
involves a common-sense interpretation of the three foci of
interest.
Pre-testing the questionnaire
The 31-item questionnaire was mailed to a senior contact in
one of the ten charities in the research sample. The manager
then distributed the survey, through the internal mail, to 50
members of staff. A total of 15 questionnaires were returned
of which one was partially completed, yielding a usable pretest sample of 14 (28 per cent). Reliability analyses and itemtotal statistics revealed no significant problems. This pretesting stage identified the absence of a “summary” question
for the “support” dimension. This issue was remedied by the
generation of an additional item statement for the main
survey.

Survey results
Our first three hypotheses focus on whether knowledge,
attitude and behaviour scores differ across time since rebranding:
H1. The acquisition of new knowledge is less likely in
organizations that have re-branded for longer.
H2. Attitudinal change is less likely in organizations that
have re-branded for longer.
H3. Behavioural change is less likely in organizations which
have re-branded for longer.

These hypotheses are tested using one-way ANOVA; the
results appear in Table II.
There are significant differences in knowledge, attitude and
behaviour scores across time since rebranding occurred
(p , 0:00). Although in each case the differences are
significant, the size of the effect as shown by the eta2 is, at
best, moderate (Cohen, 1988, suggests a value of eta2 ¼ 0:01

knowledge, attitudes and behaviour: charities that had rebranded within two years of the survey had significantly

time since re-branding and seniority, tenure and support.

However, this change in attitudes would appear to be shortlived.

However, the interaction between time since rebranding and

small.

Discussion and conclusions
Our results show a negative relationship between time since
re-branding and the three constructs of knowledge, attitude
and behaviour. But this consistency shown between these foci,
is not mirrored by a consistency in the impact of interaction
effects.
Knowledge
Respondents from charities who had rebranded within two
years of our survey felt that they knew more about the charity
as a result of the process. They claimed to be more aware of
the charity’s vision, values and beneficiaries than those who
had rebranded earlier (i.e. four years before). This acquisition
of knowledge declines as time since the rebranding increases.
Seniority does not appear to have an effect. This may be
because more senior managers feel that they already have a
high level of knowledge of the charity’s values and do not need
the prompt that the re-branding process offers. The main
divide is between managers (senior/middle/lower) and
administrative/clerical staff, but even this is not significant.
Tenure does appear to have an impact. The interaction
effect between time and tenure varies depending on the length
of time since re-branding. For charities which have recently
re-branded, long-time employees feel they gained the most
knowledge. Of those charities that have re-branded in the
mid-term, most recent recruits feel they have benefited most
in knowledge terms. Longer term re-branding shows a similar
bi-polar pattern with mid tenure employees claiming to have
gained most knowledge from the process. It is possible that
our grouping of tenure into three discrete clusters masks the
fact that what it important is how long the employee was in
the organization before it re-branded and linked to this
whether their recruitment was contemporaneous with the rebranding process. Another factor to be considered is the
magnitude of the re-branding. We have used rebranding as a
discrete variable whereas the reality may be that it would be
better viewed as a continuous one. A change of colour on the
logo should be viewed as a different level of shift compared to
a change in organizational values.
Support for re-branding does have a significant effect on
claimed acquisition of knowledge but this is regardless of
time. Level of support of the staff does have an effect but its
effect is not increased or diminished by the passing of time
since the re-branding process. Organizations should therefore

Behaviour
As with the other two constructs, behavioural changes are
more pronounced closest to the point of re-branding. This
makes intuitive sense as employees adapt their behaviour as a
reaction to the stimulus.
Seniority also has an effect here with managers, at all levels
noting behavioural changes. Administrative and clerical staff
claim less effect and even this declines over time. Neither
tenure nor level of support has an effect on behavioural
change. This is surprising, given the results on attitude. It
may reflect the more informal way that charities approach rebranding, perhaps spending less money and time on rebranding seminars and re-training exercises.

Re-branding is not a one-off event. To sustain its benefits,
charities need to re-visit its outcomes, on a regular basis,
to ensure staff retain their new knowledge, remain
positively motivated and maintain their recently adapted
behaviours over time. Lunchtime seminars, away days and
regular training activities should be used to sustain change
brought about by re-branding.
Charities employing outside agencies should ensure that a
senior member of staff is in place to take responsibility
and ensure the continuation of re-branding outcomes
once the brand agency withdraws.
Administrative and clerical staff need to be more involved
with the re-branding process. They also need to be
targeted with communications that increase their brand
knowledge to levels commensurate with those of
managerial staff to counter-act the fading of knowledge
over time.

Future research
To develop an international perspective of the effects of time
since re-branding on staff, this study should be replicated in
the USA, Asia and other European countries.
To determine whether an optimum period exists of the
beneficial effects of re-branding on staff, the study should be
replicated on a sample of charities that re-branded less than
two years ago and compare the results with those in this study.
A similar study should be undertaken in the for-profit
sector and results compared with this study.
Finally, work should be undertaken to develop a typology of
re-branding that reflects its continuous nature. Some rebranding exercises, such as changes to the logo or colours, are
relatively trivial and may not be expected to require the same
level of change that a fundamental re-alignment of vision
requires. It might be expected, for instance that staff
knowledge, attitudes and behaviours would not change
where re-branding is on a superficial level.

In a thoughtful study Philippa Hankinson of Roehampton
University in London and Wendy Lomax and Chris Hand of
Kingston University have conducted quantitative research
examining the issue of time in nine leading UK charities.
Their work involved a literature review of the for-profit as well
as the not-for-profit sector, and secondly the exploration of
the effects of time on staff knowledge, attributes and
behaviours. In doing so, a particular focus was on the
interaction of time with seniority, tenure and level of support
for the re-branding process.

If charged with a re-branding exercise, whether in a charity
or elsewhere, seven golden rules emerge that increase the
chances of being successful.
1 Re-branding is not a one-off event. Find ways of
sustaining it.
2 If employing an outside agency a senior member of staff
still needs to lead the efforts from the inside. Do not
outsource your responsibilities.
3 Involve people such as administrative and clerical staff
and communicate with them.
4 Help your longer serving staff to adjust. Tailor your
communications to their needs.
5 Pay attention and make sure that positive attitudes among
clerical and administrative staff do not fade.
6 Get staff behind the re-branding process from the start
and you will have a much better chance of keeping them
motivated.
7 Focus on getting greater behavioural change, particularly
in the middle levels where people may feel they can hide.

Impacts on knowledge, attitudes and behaviours
The results are interesting – sometimes intuitively sensible, at
other times surprising.
Gaining staff buy-in to a re-branding effort is not something
that diminishes over time. This, perhaps, is one of the most
useful outcomes of this study. The level of support of the staff
does have an impact, but this is not time sensitive. Efforts put
into gaining staff support have lasting value. Something worth
remembering in a world where so much is transitory.
When it comes to staff attitudes, however, time does matter.
The closer people are in time to an enthusiastic re-branding
effort the more positive they feel about it. With distance, the
feeling dissipates somewhat. Seniority matters too. With
seniority comes greater involvement in the decision-making.
With greater involvement comes greater ownership of the
issues. Senior people have a consistently more positive
attitude than junior staff. Maybe the junior staff are just a
bit tired of having yet another thing done to them.
With behaviour, behavioural change is most noticeable near
the time of re-branding. It is a stimulating time, exciting even.
Seniority matters however, with senior staff being more
affected by the change. Surprisingly the level of support and
length of tenure do not have a major impact on behavioural
change patterns, and behavioural change is ultimately what
the exercise is about.

Some of the rules may need slight amendment for different
types of organizations, but clearer guidance is harder to
imagine. The question for others is – will you follow them?
Reading them is the easy part. Having the will to implement is
a greater challenge.
Rather like the rhythms of time, as people we seem to find
the need to go our own way and endlessly repeat the mistakes
of the past, or fail to build upon the hard won knowledge,
often expensively gained through expensive trails and
tribulations elsewhere.
Progression is about building on firm foundations. It is
about taking what is known and running with it, having paid
due regard to its usefulness given unique circumstances. The
simplicity of these lessons should not detract from their
usefulness. To be profoundly beneficial research need not be
opaque.

Moving the agenda forward
So far so interesting, so what next? In this case it is refreshing
to find research that can be readily crystallised into
transferable principles, a blueprint for action.
