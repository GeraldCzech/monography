ABSTRACT

KEYWORDS

Given the importance of volunteers as key assets of formal
volunteering organizations, this paper explores the adoption
and adequacy of Employee Brand Equity (EBE) models to
understand
volunteers’
contributions
to
Nonprofit
Organization (NPO) brand equity. Methodologically this
research embraces a mixed-methods approach by combining
data from exploratory interviews with experts from the NPO
context and from a survey to 227 Portuguese volunteers from
28 formal volunteering organizations. This paper adds to the
literature by applying for the first time EBE dimensions to the
context of formal volunteering institutions. Hence, it provides
a first step to the development of a Volunteer Brand Equity
Model, by characterizing each dimension and validating its
importance in the context of NPOs. From a practitioners’ viewpoint, this paper calls attention to the importance of embracing internal marketing strategies to attract and retain
volunteers in a sustainable way and provides a practical tool
for Volunteer Brand Equity analysis in NPOs.

Introduction
Nonprofit organizations (NPO) face serious challenges in attracting and
retaining volunteers and in obtaining funding opportunities to reach their
goals (Andreasen & Kotler, 2003; Bussell & Forbes, 2002; Drollinger, 2018;
Harp, Scherer, & Allen, 2017; Pope, Isely, & Asamoa-Tutu, 2009). The
literature on the topic calls attention to the importance of brand management as one of NPO’s most important assets to face those challenges
(Chapleo, 2015; Ewing & Napoli, 2005; Garg, Swami, & Malhotra, 2019;
Hankinson, 2001; Huang & Ku, 2016), revealing that a growing number of
NPOs is already adopting marketing strategies to differentiate them from
competing NPOs (Pope et al., 2009; Sargeant, Ford, & Hudson, 2008; Voeth
& Herbst, 2008; Weerawardena, 2018).

In general, researchers in the area provide contributions on the importance of communicating NPO brands to the public (Bruce, Byrne, & Myers,
2018; Hankinson, 2004), and some evidence on how internal marketing
strategies may reinforce brand awareness (Hume & Hume, 2015; Liu,
Chapleo, Ko, & Ngugi, 2015), ignoring however the role of volunteers in
building up NPOs brand equity and conveying brand promises. According to
Bussell and Forbes (2002, p. 14) volunteers “are the most important group of
customers for a voluntary organization”.
So far, the literature does not provide theoretical or empirical insights on
the adequacy of adopting employee brand equity dimensions (e.g. King &
Grace, 2005, 2009, 2010; King, Grace, & Funk, 2012) to explain the contribution of volunteers to NPOs’ brand equity. As such, this paper intends to
bridge this gap by presenting the results of an exploratory pilot study where
the dimensions of Employee Brand Equity (EBE) models (King & Grace,
2009, 2010) were applied to the context of formal voluntary work in NPOs.
In this sense, we seek to understand volunteers’ contributions to build up
NPOs’ brand equity and to advance with a proposal for a Volunteer Brand
Equity (VBE) model. Therefore, this article contributes to the currently
limited literature on the importance of internal marketing strategies to attract
and retain volunteers and, more importantly to the under-researched area of
building brand equity from volunteers’ relationships with their NPO brands.
The remainder of our study is structured as follows. The next section
introduces our theoretical framework and research questions, followed by
our methodological decisions toward a mixed approach, data analysis and
discussion, and finally conclusions and implications of the study.
Theoretical framework
This section presents our theoretical framework in four sections: (a) introduces volunteering work in NPOS, (b) aligns main contributions on brand
management in NPOs, (c) describes main features and dimensions of EBE
models and (d) advances a research model of volunteering brand equity,
based on EBE dimensions.
Volunteering in NPOs

Volunteering work can be classified as either formal (organization-based) or
informal (outside of formal organizations) (Adams and Deane, 2009; Lee &
Brudney, 2012), leading to different types of goals, operations, levels of engagement, and particularly different impacts on individuals (Dovidio et al., 2017).
Previous studies have found differences in expectations and levels of
satisfaction of volunteers when compared to paid workers, as the type of
work and organizations are substantially different (Vantilborgh et al., 2012;

3

Boezeman and Ellemers 2009). Volunteer work tends to be characterized by
a relative sense of autonomy, independence, egalitarianism (Boezeman &
Ellemers, 2009; Vantilborgh et al., 2012; Nichols 2013). However, there is
also evidence of similarities between both. Liao-Troth (2001) concluded that
“volunteers and paid employees in the same location, performing similar
work, and subject to similar work rules, procedures, contracts, expectations,
discipline, and evaluations have job attitudes similar to one another for
psychological contracts (except regarding benefits), affective commitment,
and organizational justice.” (p.435).
Motivations and satisfaction are two dimensions often discussed in the
literature on Volunteering work, which in turn, are also widely discussed in
the EBBE literature. Unquestionably, volunteers present different motivations
and satisfaction levels than employees. However, parallelisms can be made
between both approaches. Motivations are mainly linked to altruism.
Individuals become volunteers and devote themselves to NPOs simply
because they desire to feel useful and occupy free time helping and benefiting
third parties (Anderson & Moore, 1978; McSweeney & Alexander, 1996;
Bussell & Forbes, 2002; Okun, 1994; Gidron, 1983) without being subject
to economic or social constraints (Galindo-Kuhn & Guzley, 2002).
Motivations also emerge from a tendency to attribute value to intangible
rewards (Gidron, 1985) such as social contact, recognition of shared moral
values among peers (Riecken, Babakus, & Yavas, 1994), confidence development whether to perform in professional levels (Gora & Nemerowicz, 1991)
or to gain credits in university contexts (Johnson-Coffey, 1997), networking
and job opportunities (Rifkin, 1995), traveling to foreign countries (Wilson &
Pimm, 1996), opportunity to express and share religious beliefs (Mattis et al.,
2000), friendship relationships that are created while volunteering (Pearce,
1983; Vinokur-Kaplan, Jayaratne, & Chess, 1994) or that motivate volunteering in the first place (Nichols & King, 1998). This can be related to the
process of organizational socialization put forward by King and Grace (2010),
in the EBBE model, according to which employees’ motivation arise from the
set of socialization processes between peers within the organization, promoting a system of shared values, beliefs and objectives (Kelley, 1992).
Regarding volunteers’ satisfaction, the literature emphasizes the importance of
quality communication within the NPO (Bennett & Barkensjo, 2005; Field &
Johnson, 1993; Miller, Powell, & Seltzer, 1990). Factors such as flow and clarity
of information, feedback, and recognition were considered to be very relevant to
volunteers’ satisfaction. Task distribution is also suggested as an active influencer
of volunteers’ satisfaction (Miller et al., 1990), particularly regarding the match
between requirements of the task and volunteer’s ability and convenience to
perform it (Stevens, 1991). EBBE also provides rich insights on the importance
of role clarity as a satisfaction promoter of employees (King & Grace, 2010) which
will be discussed in more detail below. Satisfaction is also dependent on the

effectiveness of volunteers’ participation, that is, the positive impact of volunteers’
actions on their target audience (Miller et al., 1990; Omoto & Snyder, 1995). The
absence of this dimension is recognized as one major driver for unsatisfied
volunteers and abandonment rates (Morrow-Howell & Mui, 1989). Satisfaction
is also discussed in the literature as dependent on group integration and social
interactions (Black & DiNitto, 1995), and on encouragement, emotional and
pedagogical support during training stages (Cyr & Dowrick, 1991).
Brand management in NPOs

Brand management in NPOs is a relatively recent phenomena. Wray (1994)
started by questioning why one of the most valuable assets of organizations,
the brand, was so poorly used by the charitable sector. Tapp (1996) reflected
on the emergent need for NPOs to differentiate from others while Hankinson
(2000) mentioned the importance of using branding to build confidence and
actively work on fundraising. Ali (2001), in turn, emphasized the brand’s
ability to communicate the mission and purpose of charities.
Nowadays one cannot ignore the power of brands in communicating NPOs’
missions and values and in obtaining resources (Liu et al., 2015). For example,
World Wildlife Fund, Red Cross, International Amnesty, are widely recognized
and valued brands. Research shows that strong NPO brands have an important
role in retaining volunteers (Laidler-Kylander, Austin, & Quelch, 2004), in the
same way that successful corporate brands might attract attention of more job
seekers (Wei, Chang, Lin, & Liang, 2016; Gatewood, Gowan, & Lautenschlager,
1993).
Despite the rising debate on the topic, the literature is mainly focused on NPO
brands as visibility enablers in the context of external communication, thus
providing favorable positions in relation to competing institutions and recognition among target audiences. There are also debates around the holistic function
of NPO brands in the creation of greater social impact, which assumes brand
management as strategically relevant for the organization’s own development in
line with its mission, values, and identity. Concepts and strategies of brand
management are being adapted directly from the business context, which is
justified by the usefulness of marketing strategies in NPOs' success, allowing
them to communicate more clearly and efficiently with partners and sponsors of
the business world. In the social economy sector, brands play an important role
in achieving social impact objectives in the long term (Kylander & Stone, 2012).
Employee-based brand equity

The literature on brand equity is massive but largely inconclusive, mainly due
to the complexity of its conceptualization (Christodoulides & de Chernatony,
2010). A review of the literature identifies a plethora of views on brand

5

equity, but no universally accepted definition (Aaker, 1991; Keller, 1998;
Kim, Kim, & An, 2003). According to Aaker (1991), brand equity is “a set
of assets and liabilities linked to a brand, its name, and symbol, that add to or
subtract from the value provided by a product or service to a firm and/or that
firm’s customers” (p.15). Value is thus discussed, on one hand from
a financial view where measurement of brand equity is mainly articulated
in monetary terms (e. g. Atilgan et al., 2005, Kim et al., 2003) and one
a second hand from a customer-based view (e. g. Aaker, 1991; de
Chernatony, Cottam, & Segal-Horn, 2006; Pappu et al., 2005; Vazquez
et al., 2002; You & Donthu, 2001) where, for example, is defined as “the
differential effect that brand knowledge has on consumer response to the
marketing of that brand” (Keller, 1998, p. 45). In spite of its prolific adoption
among researchers, the concept and measurement of brand equity is still
limited, subject of further inquiries and refinements (Christodoulides & de
Chernatony, 2010). For example, both Yoo and Donthu (2001) and Wasburn
and Plank (2002) call for further scale development toward a universally
accepted measure of consumer-based brand equity. Christodoulides and de
Chernatony (2010) question the merge of two theoretically distinct constructs of brand equity – brand awareness and brand associations – into
one single dimension by Yoo and Donthu’s (2001). The authors reinforce
that although both Aaker (1991) and Keller (1998) found a strong correlation
between the constructs, these should be considered as different. Another
limitation is put forward by King and Grace (2009, 2010) who highlighted
that brand equity literature is still strongly dominated by two external
perspectives, as discussed above, and that the benefits that are derived from
internal brand management to brand equity should also be considered, as in
the EBBE model. Brand management literature strongly advocates that
employees’ competences and knowledge promote a competitive advantage
for organizations (de Chernatony et al., 2006) and that for organizations to
perform effectively it is critical for employees to demonstrate positive organizational behaviors (King & Grace, 2009, 2010; Parker, 2007). Thus, there is
a growing recognition of employees as brand ambassadors given their key
role in the building process of firms’ brand equity (de Chernatony et al.,
2006; King & Grace, 2010; King et al., 2012; Tavassoli, Sorescu, & Chandy,
2014). This internal dimension of brand equity is referred to as EmployeeBased Brand Equity (EBBE), later translated simply as Employee Brand
Equity (EBE) and can be defined as “the differential effect that brand knowledge has on employee’s response to internal brand management” (King et al.,
2012, p. 269).
Prior literature has consistently investigated brand equity from the
employees’ point of view (King & Grace, 2005, 2009, 2010; King et al.,
2012). EBE models provide key insights on how knowledge hold by employees about the brand’s promises, values, and mission, may foster positive and

productive behavior of employees coherent with the communicated brand
identity (Brexendort & Kernstock, 2007). King and Grace (2009, 2010)
propose EBBE models divided into three main categories – Internal Brand
Management, Effects of Brand Knowledge and EBBE Benefits – which are
broken down into 13 dimensions, briefly reviewed below.
“Internal Brand Management” emerges as a dimension of information
management, conveying brand knowledge, in the sense of empowering
employees in the process of “delivering” the brand promise. This knowledge
transfer has a positive effect on employees, namely on brand commitment
and awareness of the importance of their roles and functions (King & Grace,
2005). Seven dimensions compose the first category of the model. The
dimension “Information generation” (1) addresses the need to understand
how the brand is perceived by employees, and occurs when employees
interpret a particular aspect of the organization and share their personal
opinion about it (King & Grace, 2009; Lings, 2004). This feedback exercise
allows a collection of information essential for the internal communication
process and is vital for the good functioning of the organization (Naude,
Desai, & Murphy, 2003). “Knowledge dissemination” (2) derives from the
information generated previously and is concerned with the match between
the knowledge about the brand that is transferred to employees and the way
they communicate the brand promise to the external environment (King &
Grace, 2009; Lings & Greenly, 2005). According to the authors, this relationship between external transmission of brand identity and the ability to
generate an image consistent with that identity allows employees to present
positive behaviors toward the brand. Thus, the development of an organizational climate where it is possible to share information about the brand
(often called as “openness” in the literature) is very important for the
improvement of internal brand management practices (King & Grace,
2010; Naude et al., 2003). Related to empowering active participation,
“Employee involvement” (3) plays an important role in reinforcing organizational commitment (King & Grace, 2010), and is manifested in feedback
initiatives, discussion of organizational issues, or participation in decisionmaking situations (Conduit & Mavondo, 2001). The dimension “Employee
attitude toward work” (4), which is indirectly influenced by the previous
dimensions, is the employee’s response and feelings to the way he or she is
treated in the organization. This dimension has a considerable impact on the
organization-employee communication process as it conditions their overall
satisfaction (Naude et al., 2003). “Organizational socialization” (5) refers to
the set of processes that promote employees’ evaluation of values, beliefs, and
goals of the employer organization (King & Grace, 2010). Associated with the
development of expectations (Naude et al., 2003), this dimension normally
entails increases in employees’ motivation, working efforts and organizational commitment (Kelley, 1992). In order to create an open organizational

JOURNAL OF NONPROFIT & PUBLIC SECTOR MARKETING

environment that promotes the development of close relationships with
employees, “Management support” (6) is an important factor for successful
employee performance (Bell, Menguc, & Stefani, 2004; Naude et al., 2003)
and manifests itself through the way the organization supports and values
their employees’ work. Lastly “The H factor” (7) deals with the ability to
foster positive relationships, hence improving the results of internal brand
management practices (King & Grace, 2010).
Regarding the second category – “Effects of Brand Knowledge” – there are
two main effects that result from the correct dissemination of brand knowledge
to employees: a clarification of their role in the organization and higher levels of
brand commitment (King & Grace, 2009). “Role clarity” (8) dimension can be
defined as the level of employees’ understanding and knowledge about his/her
role in the organization as a result of the internal communication process (King
& Grace, 2010). Role clarity creates opportunities to develop happier, more
satisfied and committed employees (Babin & Boles, 1996; Jones, Busch, &
Dacin, 2003). Additionally, it is also important to ensure that employees express
a genuine willingness and motivation to disseminate this knowledge and brand
promise to the external environment. The dimension “Brand Commitment” (9),
represented by the affective bond or sense of belonging that the employee
develops in relation to the brand (King & Grace, 2010), appears as the second
effect of the brand knowledge proposed by EBBE authors. This dimension is
considered to be a determining factor in the development of organizational
success (Morgan & Hunt, 1994), since employee’s sense of belonging influences
his or her predisposition to invest further in their contribution to achieve their
organization’s objectives and goals (Castro, Armario, & Del Rio, 2005; Jones
et al., 2003).
Lastly, the third category of the model – “benefits of EBBE” is discussed as
the set of employees’ attitudes and behaviors that promote their continuity in
the organization (Bloemer & Odekerken-Schroder, 2006; King & Grace,
2010), thus enhancing employees’ satisfaction and performance (Loveman,
1998). According to King and Grace (2009, 2010), the benefits of EBBE
comprise four dimensions: brand citizenship behavior, employee satisfaction,
employee intention to stay and positive employee word of mouth. The
manifestation of “Brand citizenship behavior” (10) emerges from employees’
consistent, spontaneous and genuine behavior with the brand promise, not
imposed by the organization (Burmann & Zeplin, 2005). The second benefit
is related to “Employee satisfaction” (11), characterized by the level of
satisfaction of the employee in relation to his work, according to what the
organization intends and appreciates (King & Grace, 2010). Moreover, this
dimension is associated with other benefits such as employee retention
(Boselie & van der Wiele, 2002) or positive word-of-mouth (Shinnar,
Young, & Meana, 2004). The “Employee’s intention to stay” (12) is defined
as the future willingness of the employee to work in the organization in the

long term (Good, Page, & Young, 1996). The last dimension addresses the
“Positive employee word-of-mouth” (13) which reflects the intention and
genuine interest of the employee, not only in talking positively about his/her
employer but also to recommended it to others. It also illustrates a positive
relationship between the degree of commitment of the employee and his/her
intention to stay in the organization (Bloemer & Odekerken-Schroder, 2006).

Research model
Considering the theme under study and the conceptual framework described
above, this section proposes a research framework to advance potential
dimensions of a future Volunteer Brand Equity (VBE) model (Figure 1).
As mentioned above, despite the differences between paid workers and
volunteers, there are also common factors in terms of psychological contracts, affective commitment and organizational justice (Liao-Troth 2001).
Knowledge hold by volunteers about brands’ promises, values, and missions,
may foster positive and productive behavior of volunteers coherent with the
communicated brand identity. Thus, with appropriate adaptations, it will
make sense to consider volunteers as important brand ambassadors. In this
sense, we seek to understand volunteers “contributions to build up NPOs”
brand equity based on the three building blocks proposed by EBE researchers: Internal Brand Management (Dimensions 1–7), Effects of Brand
Knowledge (Dimensions 8–9) and EBE Benefits (10–13). The model depicted
in Figure 1 represents the adoption of the original dimensions developed for
business contexts to an NPO context. Further explanations on how the model
was adapted will be provided in the methodology section.
The first step to advance toward a new VBE model constitutes our primary
research question (RQ1): is it valid and adequate to apply EBE dimensions to
formal volunteering contexts? Firstly, this question implies a (statistical)
validation of the scales proposed by the original EBBE models applied in
the context of the NPOs of Formal Volunteering. Secondly, it demands
a characterization of each of the dimensions of the model in light of the
volunteers’ perspectives.
Our second research question (RQ2) emerged from the literature review. Given
the importance attributed to volunteers’ knowledge to perform their tasks in
a positive way and in coherence with the institution mission and values,
the second research question clearly asks about the relationship between the
dimensions of “brand knowledge effects” and all other dimensions. Hence, it
questions the relationship between “role clarity” and “brand commitment” and
each of the seven dimensions of “internal brand management” and each of the four
dimensions of “VBE benefits”.

Methods and data
This study adopted a mixed methods approach, combining qualitative and quantitative data collection techniques to allow for a more comprehensive investigation
of the research problem (Creswell, Shope, Plano Clark, & Green, 2006). It included
a self-administered questionnaire to volunteers in formal volunteering organizations (227 valid responses from 28 different NPOs) and three interviews with
experts from the NPO context. The interviews served as a first step to discuss and
validate our research model whereas the survey provided a greater scope of
analysis. The triangulation of qualitative and quantitative data offered a greater
confidence in the conclusions (Bryman, 2006).

Qualitative research design: participants, data collection and analysis

Given the exploratory nature of the study and the scarcity of literature dealing
with the internal dimension of brand equity on NPOs, three in-depth interviews

were conducted with specialists from the third sector. Decisions were made
about potential interviewees that could make solid contributions to the development of a robust VBBE model. Hence, we selected three experts with three
different profiles (Table 1) – one academic-practitioner, one academicresearcher, and one practitioner-volunteer, hoping to gather rich qualitative
data provided by their insights, experience, expertise and thoughts.
Expert 1 is an academic-practitioner, that is to say, is simultaneously
a professor of social economy and social intervention and also co-founder
of a NFPO dealing with social entrepreneurship projects. Expert 2 is an
academic and a researcher in the areas of Sustainable Development and
Social Entrepreneurship particularly examining solutions to foster sustainable
development through collaboration between NGOs and NFPOs. Finally,
expert 3 is a business consultant, an active volunteer for more than 10
years and also, in the last 3 years, coordinator of a Portuguese NGO with
more than 400 volunteers working in five different settings (social exclusion,
children with disabilities, support to isolated seniors, homeless and institutionalized children).
Interviews turn out to be indispensable and vital to discuss the adoption of
EBE models to the context of formal volunteering and advancing toward
a first Volunteer Brand Equity model. Issues related to the adequacy of the
language, the key grounds of EBE models and the usefulness of such models
in NPOs contexts were critically and deeply debated. During the interview,
experts were asked to critically discuss and evaluate the appropriateness of
each one of the three categories and 13 dimensions of the EBE model. Audio
recording of interviews was authorized and fully transcribed. Experts were
selected based on their practical and academic expertise on the subject.
Relevant insights emerged from the interviews, and afterward used to
assist and support the survey design and application. Table 2 provides
examples of changes introduced to the survey to encompass interviewees’

the specificity of voluntary work. For instance, in dimension Volunteer
intention to stay we have considered 2 years, as the average time to remain
in the organization, considered by the experts as a more coherent timeframe
than the 5-year period suggest in the EBBE original model.

Quantitative research design: survey, sampling, and analysis

A self-administrated questionnaire was put forward, using the contributions
of the experts during the interviews and the scales adapted from the original
EBBE models. A non-probabilistic and purposive sampling was used to
guarantee a direct possibility of accessing a group of individuals from the
target audience (Anderson, Sweeney, & Williams, 2007), that is to say, formal
volunteers. Therefore, the study population comprised volunteers who formally carry out their activities within an institution or organization in the
North of Portugal, representing a dimension of 535 918 individuals (National
Statistics Institute [INE], 2012). A pretest was carried out and some changes
were made to provide better understanding of the questions. The survey was
disseminated through an online platform and data analysis was performed
using SPSS.
The first part of the survey aimed at characterizing some dimensions of the
volunteer work, namely the organization they volunteer for (a basic condition for being a formal volunteer), the seniority of the volunteer, the average
time spent in volunteering activities per month, the role of the volunteer in
the organization (coordinator or not). The second part dedicated specifically
to the scales of the VBE model was organized around the 13 dimensions of
the VBE model: generation of information, knowledge dissemination, volunteer attitude, organizational socialization, volunteer involvement, management support, the H factor, role clarity, brand commitment, brand
citizenship behavior, volunteer satisfaction, volunteer intention to stay and
positive volunteer word of mouth. A 7-point Likert scale was used (1 – totally
disagree; 2 – disagree; 3 – partially disagree; 4 – indifferent; 5 – partially
agree; 6 – agree; 7 - totally agree) as proposed by King and Grace (2010) and
King et al. (2012) to address each dimension giving rise to a total of 66
statements.
Results and discussion
This section summarizes results in two domains, according to our research
goals and theoretical framework. Firstly, findings from the interviews are
presented and main changes and adaptations proposed by interviewees are
highlighted. Secondly, data collected from the survey are presented through
descriptive analysis, exploratory factor analysis, and correlation analysis.
Qualitative findings

After a thorough and critical debate around all the dimensions of the EBBE
original models, the panel of experts suggested some changes and language
adaptations to encompass the idiosyncrasies of the volunteering empirical

context (Table 2). Despite these changes, all three categories and 13 dimensions were accepted and validated as useful and necessary to understand the
context of NPOs' brand equity. Proposed adaptations were mainly related to
the language used to address each dimension and the key concepts supporting the model. From the 83 original statements proposed by King and Grace
(2010), 17 questions were eliminated according to the experts’ opinions that
those were not relevant or applicable to formal volunteering institutions. This
is due to the nature of volunteering work. For example, volunteers, in
general, do not have a prior preparation to fulfill certain functions as occurs
in business context. Hence, experts considered that it could be dangerous to
rely too much on the opinion of volunteers who may not yet be prepared or
knowledgeable about the organization’s brand promise and may not come up
with strategically productive suggestions. Additionally, the following changes
were proposed and justified by the experts to enhance the VBE model
(Table 2).
Regarding the dimensions of VBE model we highlight some of the main
contributions derived from experts 1, 2 and 3 (henceforth E1, E2, E3):
(1) Concerning the first dimension “generation of information” suggestions were made about references to the “coordination team” instead
of “organization”. Instead of an abstract concept of “organization”, E1
and E3 suggested to identify the “coordination team” to whom volunteers report and with whom a direct relationship is. According to E1

with a life of its own. But the information conveyed by volunteers is
directed to their leader/coordinator; and if the information is somehow
impacting but does not reach team leaders or coordinators, it has
practically no value”.
(2) Experts draw attention to the fact that willingness to give time does
not imply being prepared to convey a clear and positive opinion about
the organization: “An organization that relies heavily on volunteer
information will drift a bit. Volunteers express ‘everything under the
sun’ and there needs to be some basic coherence in identity”(E3).
(3) Experts considered the dimension “knowledge dissemination” as

Conclusions, implications, and avenues for future research
This exploratory study examined the adequacy of adopting EBBE dimensions
to the context of formal voluntary work in NPOs, contributing to the underresearched area of building brand equity from volunteers’ relationships with
their NPO.
First, by advancing with a proposal for a Volunteer Brand Equity (VBE)
model, this article contributes to the currently limited literature on the
importance of internal marketing strategies to attract, engage and retain
volunteers. This study is the first step in developing a new model for internal
brand management in NPOs, focused on the role and contributions of
volunteers to build NPOs’ brand equity. Hence, the validation of the VBE
model applied to formal volunteering contexts allows us to put forward
a theoretical contribution to an organizational context that to date has
received little or almost no attention from the scientific community.
Additionally, the study provided development of a set of scales that will
allow the application of the VBE model to other volunteering scenarios,
allowing the scientific community to investigate internal brand management
elements, the effects of brand knowledge on volunteers and the benefits of
VBE. The application of VBE model to the Portuguese NPO context highlighted the importance of volunteer brand knowledge effects on the overall
NPO brand equity. Our findings reinforced the key role-played by role clarity
and brand commitment in engaging volunteers with their NPO brand, which
affects their overall satisfaction and motivation to stay in the organization.
Second, regarding the implications of this study for practitioners in NPO
contexts, it is our understanding that the VBE model represents a practical
tool for analyzing internal brand management strategies, evaluating the
effects of brand knowledge on volunteers, and realizing the benefits of VBE
for volunteers and consequently for the NPO brand. In fact, according to
Shah, Rust, Parasuraman, Staelin, and Day (2006), it is this ability to measure
internal results that enhance organizational performance.
Given the importance of brand management for the very development of
organizations (Kylander & Stone, 2012), these results assume considerable
practical relevance. In a holistic way, our findings may be used by practitioners to achieve better results in attracting, engaging and retaining volunteers by: i) improving the volunteer experience to the extent that
organizations will have more information on how to interact with them, in
order to enhance their overall satisfaction; ii) building up closer relationships
with the volunteers by generating and disseminating knowledge about the
NPO brand promise and thus enhancing their role, their feeling of belonging
and the way they identify themselves with the brand.
In terms of limitations of the study, we acknowledge that our survey and
interviews covered responses from the northern Portuguese context of NPOs.

Despite its exploratory nature, to properly develop a new VBE model, future
studies will need to open up the survey to other regions and other countries.
Hence, and following other CBBE (e.g. Buil et al., 2008) and EBBE (e.g.
Poulis & Wisker, 2016) studies which collected and compared data from
consumers in different countries, we invite researchers to apply the
Volunteer Based Brand Equity model to other volunteering settings in
other countries, to allow for further understanding of the phenomena and
also to identify differences among volunteers from different socio-economiccultural backgrounds.
Regarding the interviews, only three experts have been heard in this pilot
study. Although having a high level of experience and a very relevant
theoretical-practical knowledge, their comments on the model may have
introduced failures in the system due to the reduced number of opinions.
We recommend the selection of a broader and more complete panel of
experts in order to further investigate the adequacy of EBE dimensions to
the context of formal volunteering, using the Delphi method in a larger scale,
for example. Furthermore, this paper reports findings of the first stage of the
validation of VBE model. Future research should build upon the scales
validated in this study to form first level constructs of internal brand management, volunteer brand knowledge effects and VBE benefits, similarly to
EBE original models.
Lastly, volunteer and experts responses led us to question the influence
that coordination/management function, seniority of the volunteer and the
number of hours dedicated to volunteering activities, might have on the NPO
brand equity. These variables may constitute potential future research
themes.
