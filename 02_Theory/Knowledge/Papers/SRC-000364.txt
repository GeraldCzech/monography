The effect of charity brand experience on donors’
behavioral intentions: The mediating role of charity brand
personality and donors’ satisfaction
Basant Hassan Ali1 · Nadia Elaref2,3 · Omneya Mokhtar Yacout1
Received: 18 February 2022 / Accepted: 6 November 2022 / Published online: 12 December 2022
© The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer-Verlag GmbH Germany, part of Springer Nature 2022

Abstract
Charity organizations represent an avenue for donors to express themselves by fulfilling their self-esteem and self-actualization needs. Thus, investigating the brand
personality of charity organizations and its effect on donors’ responses is a worthwhile investigation that has received scant attention from marketing scholars. This
research examines the effect of charity brand experience on charity brand personality, donor satisfaction, and donors’ behavioral intentions. A survey was distributed to a sample of donors of five charity organizations in Alexandria, Egypt. The
hypothesized relationships were examined using structural equation modeling. Findings generally supported the hypothesized relationships except for the direct effect
of brand personality on intentions to re-donate. Academic and practical implications
were also presented.
Keywords Brand experience · Brand personality · Donor satisfaction · Donor loyalty

1 Introduction
The not-for-profit sector is currently facing radical change. The number of notfor-profit organizations is increasing tremendously (Mort et al., 2007), leading to
severe competition (Hibbert & Horne, 1996). Charities play an increasingly significant role in many societies by enabling personal empowerment and training,
fighting poverty, combating social diseases, narrowing the deep divide between
the rich and poor, and increasing social, economic, and political inclusiveness.
Many scholars and practitioners have considered the applicability of various marketing strategies in broader societal contexts (Sargeant, 2009). Charity organizations have started to recognize that their brand is a valuable asset that affects
donors’ behavior (Bilgin, & Kethüda, 2022; Gregory et al., 2020; Wymer &
Akbar, 2019)., but many lack the knowledge of developing or managing it (Mort
et al., 2007).
The importance of branding to charity organizations cannot be overlooked,
however. Chapman et al. (2022) note that, unlike conventional products, a triadic and not a dyadic exchange takes place, which includes three players: donors,
beneficiaries, and charities. Understanding donors’ satisfaction with and intention
toward charities and how marketing activities in general and branding activities
in specific affect these intentions and behaviors remains one of the most important challenges of marketing for charities.
For charities, brands offer a practical meaning of conveying the benefits of
goods and services provided by the organization and expressing quality assurance
and consistency (Ritchie et al., 1999). Charity organizations use brand personalities to communicate inherent values and meaning to their stakeholder (Hankinson, 2001). Furthermore, brands facilitate differentiating the offer for donors and
volunteers and thus help target donors (van Dijk et al., 2019). For donors, the
use of branding provides them with feelings of comfort with the brand and contributes to brand preferences or intentions of giving (Schlegelmilch et al., 1997),
reduces donors’ search costs, and helps them to make the right decision in a more
efficient manner (Ritchie et al., 1999). Donors identify with charity organizations
with personalities and values that express their own opinions (Ching et al., 2013;
Ramjaun, 2014), which helps them meet their objectives and expectations (Chang
& Lee, 2008; Ritchie et al., 1999). Thus, the charity brand “mirrors” the values
that highlight the needs and desires of donors, acts like a “lamp” that affects both
the values of its target audience and the host organization and represents a “lens”
that focuses on the values of the organization (Stride, 2006). The brand personality of charity organizations could strengthen the organization’s image (Venable
et al., 2005). Donors identify with charity organizations with personalities and
values that express their opinions (Ramjaun, 2014).
Reviewing the literature on brand experience, two main research avenues can
be identified. The first avenue relates to research aimed at identifying the meaning and dimensions of brand experience. The brand experience reflects the sensations, feelings, cognitions and behavioral responses induced by brand-related
stimuli (Brakus et al., 2009). Most studies examined brand experience for tangible

Donations to charities represent prosocial behavior that may warrant major modifications in the constructs currently utilized. and the relationships examined. This
research builds upon the brand experience model of Brakus et al. (2009) to investigate the effect of brand experience in enhancing re-donation intention (loyalty) to
charities via donor satisfaction and brand personality. Thus, this research applies
the model of Brakus et al. (2009) and Nysveen et al. (2013) in a different context.
Necessary modifications to the dimensions of brand experience, brand personality, and satisfaction were made to harmonize with the context of charity branding.
Furthermore, these relationships are examined in a distinct cultural context. Jung
et al. (2022) noted that studies on not-for-profit marketing in Asia, Africa, and South
America are scarce. Thus, examining not-for-profit marketing in different cultural
contexts leads to better generalizability and more rigorous theory building.
For charity marketing practitioners, studying brand experience and personality
and their relationship to donors’ behavior gives charity brand managers the knowledge to formulate marketing strategies based on their brand personalities. Such
knowledge is beneficial in positioning charities to achieve better outcomes and in
designing better brand experiences. Furthermore, understanding how the brand
experience affects donors’ satisfaction and intentions to re-donate will enable marketing managers to monitor donors’ perception of the charity. Then they can design
programs that enhance satisfaction with and loyalty to the brand.

brand experience and its consequences. The second section will cover the research
methodology, including the research framework, measurement of research variables,
population and sampling, data collection, and statistical analyses. The third section will present the research findings. The fourth section will discuss the research

2 Literature review
2.1 The concept of brand experience
Brand experience is an important concept that triggered the interest of many
researchers. Brakus et al. (2009) argued that the brand experience scale includes
four dimensions: sensory, affective, intellectual, and behavioral. In this conceptualization, brand experience embraces feelings, not just an overall liking of the brand,
whereas attitudes represent only a small portion of the brand experience. It also
includes behavioral responses triggered by specific brand-related stimuli. A brand
experience reflects any direct or indirect contact between the consumer and the
brand (Plummer, 1985). A pleasant brand experience affects the brand’s understanding, enjoyment enhancement, and fostering (Ching et al., 2013). It encompasses the
sensations, feelings, cognitions, and behavioral responses that brand-related stimuli induce. They are a part of a brand’s design, identity, packaging, communication
strategies and environment (Brakus et al., 2009). The brand experience concept
attempts to provide answers on how brand experience can be measured and how it
affects consumer behavior (Cleff et al., 2013).
In addition to identifying the dimensions of brand experience, the model developed by Brakus et al. (2009) specified three main consequences of brand experience:
brand personality, satisfaction, and loyalty. The model was supported by several
researchers (e.g., Nysveen et al., 2013).
Most of the studies examining brand experience focused on commercial and not
charity brand experience (e.g., Brakus et al., 2009; Klaus & Maklan, 2012). A pivotal study was conducted by Nysveen et al. (2013) who added a relational dimension. They proposed that the relational experience dimension could be added in a
service context. They report that it is a strong predictor of customer satisfaction
and loyalty. While the relational dimension added by Nysveen et al. (2013) extends
the use of the brand experience scale by Brakus et al. (2009) to a service setting, it
also offers an opportunity to use the modified scale in charity and non-profit contexts. Charities represent a service where donors highly value relationships with the
charity organization. Furthermore, this dimension as used by Nysveen et al. (2013)
reflects an individual’s connectedness with the community which can be used in a
charity context.

According to Brakus et al. (2009), there are three important consequences of brand
experience; brand personality, satisfaction and loyalty.

2.2.1 Brand personality
Attention to brand experience dimensions allows consumers to judge a brand’s personality dimensions (Keng et al., 2013). Brand personality is defined as the set of
human characteristics that consumers associate with a brand (Aaker, 1997). Based
on the signaling theory (Spence, 1973), organizations use brand personalities to
send signals to their customers about the qualities of their brands which are difficult
to observe or assess (BliegeBird & Smith, 2005) but are still important to the target
segment these organizations work with (Davies et al., 2018). The stereotype content model argues that warmth/sincerity, competence, and status are vital to humans
(Fiske et al., 2002, 2007), and Aaker (2010) suggest using these attributes to incorporate imagery.
Early studies in brand personality aimed at identifying the dimensions of brand
personality for conventional products. Aaker (1997) pioneered the development
of a scale to measure brand personality. Later, Aaker et al. (2001) noted the correspondence between three dimensions of brand personality with three dimensions of
the "Big 5" (Sincerity and Agreeableness; Competence and Conscientiousness and
Excitement and Extraversion). Marketing scholars added other brand personality
dimensions. These dimensions include peacefulness (Aaker et al., 2001), likeableness as well ascendancy (Sung & Tinkham, 2005), sincerity, competence, and status
(Davies et al., 2018).
Other researchers departed from the Big 5 personality dimensions. For example,
Geuens et al. (2009) argued that brand personality comprises five dimensions: activity, responsibility, aggressiveness, simplicity, and emotionality. Their findings indicate acceptable psychometric properties and cross-cultural validity in the USA and
Europe. Freling et al. (2011) conceptualized favorability, originality, and clarity—as
dimensions of a brand’s personality. Later, George and Anandkumar (2018) developed a brand personality scale comprised of seven dimensions. These dimensions
include happiness, reliability, youthfulness, adventurousness, competency, accountability, and appeal. Sound psychometric properties were also reported for this scale.
Conversely, Stinnett et al. (2013) and Ha et al. (2022) used the anthropomorphism perspective to examine brand personality by assigning human characteristics
to nonhuman entities. Dimensions such as gender, race, age, marital status, education level, and pet ownership were used as brand personality dimensions. Assessments by consumers are then compared to multiple data points so that the organization knows how it is perceived by the public.
In charity branding, only a few researchers tried to identify brand personality
dimensions. Venable et al. (2005) were among the early researchers who attempted
to develop and refine a measure for the brand personality of charity organizations.
Three qualitative studies were conducted on donors, the community, and charity
professionals. Sincerity, excitement, competence, sophistication, and ruggedness
were identified as traits of charity organizations. Based on the qualitative studies,
three new dimensions were identified. The first is thoughtfulness which includes
attributes such as loving, compassion, kindness, helpfulness, and caring. The second
is integrity, which include, reputation, commitment to the public good, purposefulness, accessibility, and long-term commitment. The third is reliability, which reflects

cost-effectiveness and financial stability. Using factor analysis, sophistication and
ruggedness dimensions were obtained with two additional dimensions: integrity
and nurturance. Groza and Gordon (2016) used four traits to describe not-for-profit
organization personality. These traits are integrity, nurturance, ruggedness, and
sophistication.
Stebbins and Hartman (2013) examined the dimensions of charity brand personality in smaller, local charitable organizations that do not enjoy the benefits of
national brand recognition. While the brand personality dimensions of benevolence and service transcend the not-for-profit sector, dimensions such as emotional
engagement, voice, and progress differentiate these small charities. Bennett and
Gabriel (2000) aimed to operationalize not-for-profits’ brand personality. The four
main dimensions based on their findings were emotive altruism, efficiency, trust,
and modernity. Voeth and Herbst (2008) developed a brand personality construct
for the not-for-profit sector that includes three dimensions, social competence and
trust, emotion and assertiveness, and sophistication. They note that these dimensions
reflect the social dimension inherent in not-for-profit branding and express the need
to distinguish brand personality dimensions of charities from the traditional conceptualization of brand personalities used in commercial marketing. Recently, Lindenmeier et al. (2021) used two personality dimensions of charities: perceived warmth
and competence.
The studies above show that similar to commercial brand experience, many of the
charity brand personality dimensions relied on the Big 5 classification (e.g., Groza
& Gordon, 2016; Stebbins & Hartman, 2013; Venable et al., 2005). However, many
dimensions were added that reflect the nature of charity branding where the brand
aims at triggering prosocial motives (e.g. Bennett & Gabriel, 2000; Voeth & Herbst,
2008). Furthermore, the same dimension used in conventional branding may have a
different meaning in the context of charity branding. For example, Chrissos Anestis
et al. (2021) noted that ruggedness in the charity branding context may describe an
organization that is strong and acts to defend the rights of its beneficiaries.
Another avenue of brand personality research examined the relationship between
brand experience and brand personality. The relationship between the two constructs
seems logical since personalities are built through experiences (Japutra & Molinillo,
2019). Brakus et al. (2009) were among the first to highlight the key role of brand
experience in developing a brand personality. Based on a sample of U.S. students,
they report that the higher the overall score on the brand experience scale, the more
likely the consumer will endow the brand with personality associations. Several
studies supported the positive effect of brand experience on brand personality. The
results of Keng et al.’s (2013) strongly support the importance of brand experience
in influencing brand personality in Taiwan. Cardoso et al. (2013) studied a local
brand in Portugal which showed that sincerity and competence were associated the
most with the brand personality, followed by sophistication, excitement, and ruggedness. Walter et al. (2013) showed that brand experience positively affects BMW’s
brand personality.
The relationship between brand experience and brand personality of charity
brands received scant attention. Aly et al. (2017) suggested this relationship,
but it was not empirically tested. Later, Chrissos Anestis et al. (2021) used four

2.2.2 Donor satisfaction
Japutra and Molinillo (2019) argued that the three major consequences of brand
experience and brand personality are satisfaction, trust, and loyalty. Notwithstanding the importance of trust, only satisfaction and re-donation intentions will
be considered in this research.
Satisfaction is an essential determinant of long-term consumer behavior (Oliver, 1980) and a central goal of all business activities (Angelova & Zekiri, 2011).
Satisfaction is an outcome that occurs as a result of comparing the rewards and
cost of the purchase (Jamshidi & Rousta, 2021).
The effect of brand experience on satisfaction was supported in various
commercial settings. For example, Kose and Ozer Cizer (2021) reported that
all dimensions of experiential marketing predict customer satisfaction in the
smartphone industry. Similar findings were reported in a shopping environment
(Mumtaz et al., 2011) in the automobile industry (Şahin et al., 2012) and for tablets (Tsai et al., 2010). Huang and Huang (2012) noted that perceived value mediates the relationship between brand experience and customer satisfaction.
Contrary to commercial settings, less research was conducted to examine
the relationship between brand experience and donor satisfaction in charities.
Researchers mainly attempted to identify the differences between the conceptualization and function of donor and conventional customer satisfaction (e.g., Shang
et al., 2019). Donor satisfaction results from sacrificing money or time to benefit others while receiving nothing in return (Strahilevitz (2011). This sacrifice
improves the donors’ self-image, self-esteem, and long-term happiness. Thus, in
the case of prosocial behavior such as donating to charities, satisfaction occurs
when donors realize that their behavior leads to a change in the lives of other people who are in need (Park et al., 2021). Zalluhoğlu et al. (2017) explained this by
identifying two types of satisfaction in the charity context; self-satisfaction and
satisfaction with social benefits offered to others.
Thus, based on the above, the second hypothesis is formulated:
H2: Brand experience has a positive effect on donor satisfaction.

The relationship between brand personality and customer satisfaction is strongly
supported in different commercial settings (e.g., Achouri & Bouslama, 2010; Brakus
et al., 2009; Hou et al., 2009; Lombart & Louis, 2012; Mort et al., 2007; Nysveen
et al., 2013; Park & Lee, 2005). Several scholars examined this relationship in the
charity sector. Similar findings were obtained by Faircloth (2005) and Sargeant et al.
(2008). Stebbins and Hartman (2013) reported that the dimensions of emotional
engagement, voice, progression, tradition, and conservatism had a more profound
effect on giving behavior than other charity brand personalities for smaller charities.
da silva et al. (2020) provided empirical evidence that the two dimensions of interaction and affect predict the donation intention.
It can be concluded that a brand that evokes multiple experience dimensions will
affect the pleasure level of the customer after the sale (Kose and Ozer Cizer, 2021)
and raise customer satisfaction (Brakus et al., 2009; Mumtaz et al., 2011; Tsai et al.,
2010).
Thus, the third hypothesis is formulated:
H3: Brand personality has a positive effect on donor satisfaction
2.2.3 Intentions to re‑donate
An intention is defined as: the individual’s evaluation of the subjective probability
that he or she will perform in a particular way in the future (Sorderlund and Ohman,
2003). Zeithaml et al. (1996) suggested five dimensions of behavioral intentions:
loyalty to the organization, propensity to switch to another company, willingness to
pay more, external response to the problem, and internal response to the problem.
Brand loyalty is the principal behavioral intention, affecting the organization’s
profitability and growth. It denotes a customer’s belief in the priority of a brand
over other rival brands and subsequent behavioral intentions to repurchase, revisit,
recommend and pay a price premium for the brand (Oliver, 1999). It should be noted
that a favorable attitude and a repeat purchase were required to describe loyalty
(Dick & Basu, 1994).
Dick and Basu (1994) argued that customer loyalty is comprised of cognitive,
affective, and conative loyalty reflecting beliefs about the brand, feelings towards it,
and intentions to buy it. Oliver (1997) postulated that consumers start with loyalty
in a cognitive sense, followed by affective loyalty, conative loyalty, and behavioral
loyalty.
Companies try hard to increase loyalty to their brand by providing their customers with positive brand-related experiences (Iglesias et al., 2011; Khalili et al., 2013;
Shim et al., 2015). Brakus et al.’s (2009) sensory, affective, behavioral, and intellectual dimensions of brand experience were strong predictors of loyalty in many studies (Lada et al., 2014; Shim et al., 2015). Ebrahim et al. (2016) reported that brand
personality and experiences’ effects on purchase intentions are indirect via brand
preferences. Recently, Santos and Schlesinger (2021) provided empirical evidence
that brand experience and love directly impact brand loyalty and willingness to pay a
premium price for streaming T.V. services.

In the charity sector, only a limited number of studies examined the relationship between brand experience and intentions to re-donate. For example, Beldad
et al. (2014) conducted a study to examine predictors of repeat donation intention
among residents of two cities in the Netherlands. Positive experience with the
organization affected intention to continue donating to a charitable organization.
Based on the above, the following hypothesis is formulated:
H4: Brand experience has a positive effect on intentions to re-donate.
The positive effect of brand personality on brand loyalty is empirically supported (Achouri & Bouslama, 2010; Akin, 2011; Brakus et al., 2009; Chen &
Phou, 2013; Farhat & Khan, 2011; Lin & Huang, 2012; Shehu et al., 2016a, b).
Rup et al. (2021) argue that brand attitudes mediate the relationship between
brand personality and purchase intentions.
Several scholars examined this relationship in the charity sector. Sargeant and
Woodliffe (2007) examined factors affecting donors’ intentions to re-donate. The
researchers report that a charity brand personality that is unique and different is
related to donors’ giving behavior. In addition, they reported that a higher level of
donor satisfaction with the service leads to donors’ re-donation intention. Similar
findings were obtained by Faircloth (2005) and Sargeant et al. (2008). Stebbins
and Hartman (2013) report that the dimensions of emotional engagement, voice,
progression, tradition, and conservatism had a more profound effect on giving
behavior than other charity brand personalities for smaller charities. da silva et al.
(2020) provided empirical evidence that charities’ brand interaction and affect
predict the donation intention.
Some authors examined the role of mediators and moderators in the relationship between charity brand personality and re-donation intentions. Hou et al.
(2009) reported an indirect relationship via donors’ self-concept. Lindenmeier
et al. (2021) argued that the two personality dimensions of perceived warmth and
competence affect the volunteering intentions of German university students via
actual self-congruence and functional congruence. Shaari et al.‘s (2019) study
on Malaysian millennials examined attitude toward donation as a mediator of the
relationship between university brand personality and intention to donate.
Shehu et al. (2016a, b) reported the suppressing role of monetary incentives in
charity donation marketing activities on the relationship between charity brand
personality and re-donation intentions. Specifically, they report that monetary
incentives may undermine the effect of brand personality on intentions to donate.
Groza and Gordon (2016) reported that the specific intentions the donor will depend
on the different charity brand personalities communicated to donors. Willingness to
donate time was correlated with both nurturance and sophistication of brand personalities. Conversely, willingness to recommend the not-for-profi organization correlated with
both nurturance and ruggedness.
Based on the above, the following hypothesis is formulated.
H5: Brand personality has a positive effect on intentions to re-donate.

The relationship between customer satisfaction and loyalty in various contexts was
empirically supported (Chandrashekaran et al., 2007; Choi et al., 2011; Cooil et al., 2007;
Faullant et al., 2008; Kim et al., 2015; Mittal et al., 1999; Wu et al., 2011).
In charity branding, Weidmann et al. (2013) concluded that donors who are more satisfied when dealing with charity organizations are more likely to continue giving and
recommending the organizations to others. Similarly, Boenigk and Helmig (2013) examined donors’ satisfaction with blood and money donation charities. Donor satisfaction
was found to positively affect donors’ intention to re-donate. Shang et al. (2019) findings supported the positive and direct effect of satisfaction on giving intention and giving
behavior. Nguyen et al. (2008) also reported that satisfaction with the current donation
experience was associated with the intent of the future donation. Martinez et al. (2021)
found that older donors are more likely to report lower overall satisfaction with the service provided by the blood bank and that donor satisfaction affected donor loyalty.
Taylor and Miller-Stevens (2018) highlighted the importance of relational
exchange in the context of donation. They report that donor identification with a notfor-profit organization and satisfaction with it leads to higher intentions to donate.
Melián-Alzola and Martin-Santana (2020) provided empirical support that
donor satisfaction affects donor loyalty directly and indirectly through trust in a
blood donation setting. Later, Melián-Alzola and Martín-Santana (2020) reported
a path from service quality to donor satisfaction to donor loyalty in blood bank
services in Spain.
Zalluhoğlu et al. (2017) argued that there are two types of satisfaction in the charity
context: self-satisfaction and satisfaction for social benefits. Both types were found to
influence donors’ loyalty to not-for-profit organizations.
Based on the above, the following hypothesis is formulated:
H6: Donor satisfaction positively affects donors’ intentions to re-donate.

3 Method
This research investigates the relationship between brand experience, brand personality,
donors’ satisfaction and intentions to re-donate to charity organizations. Thus, the model
used by Brakus et al. (2009) was used in the context of charity branding. Figure 1 shows
the research model.
To test the hypotheses, a descriptive research design was adopted. A survey was developed based on previously developed scales. Donors to five well-known charities in Alexandria, Egypt, were approached to collect the data. Respondents were invited to participate and presented a list of five well-known charity brands in the city of Alexandria. The
respondents were asked to select the brand where they donate more frequently and answer
questions about this particular charity brand.

3.1 Measurement of research variables
Three groups of variables will be measured. The first group includes brand experience,
which is the independent variable. The original scale items by Brakus et al. (2009) and
Nysveen et al. (2013) were used. Three dimensions of brand experience were deemed
important in a charity brand context. These dimensions were affective, intellectual, and
relational experiences. The sensory dimension and the behavioral dimension were not
included in the scale. The sensory dimension may not be applicable in charities, as it
relates to design and aesthetics perceptions (Brakus et al., 2009) and most donations are
collected online or via volunteers, thus donors are not exposed to brand design or aesthetics. The behavioral dimension of brand experience was found to suffer from some
validity problems (Brakus et al., 2009). The relational dimension was originally introduced by Nysveen et al. (2013) to measure brand experience in services Charity organizations are considered services which makes this dimension highly relevant to service
organizations. Furthermore, the relational dimension of Nysveen et al. (2013) reflects the
essence of a charity brand experience. Items such as feeling part of a larger community
and the desire not to be left alone may be highly relevant to a charity brand experience
where donors like to donate to feel connected to others in their communities. The modified scale includes eight items. The respondents will be asked to describe their experiences on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from strongly disagree to strongly agree.
The measurement of the three consequences of brand experiences departed
from the measures used by both Brakus et al. (2009) and Nysveen et al. (2013) to
match the charity branding context. The intention to re-donate to a charity brand
is the dependent variable. It will be measured using three items on a 5-point Likert scale originally introduced by Ranganathan and Henley (2008). Charity brand
personality will be measured using the scale introduced by Venable et al. (2005),
which includes three dimensions (integrity, competence, and thoughtfulness).
Donor satisfaction will be measured using an adapted scale from Oliver (1980). A
five-point Likert scale will be used for measuring the two mediators.

The targeted population of the study is all donors who donate money, time, or goods
to different charity organizations located in Alexandria. Five large and well-known

To test the hypotheses using structural equation modeling, tests of normality were

run to test research hypotheses. Table 3 shows univariate and multivariate tests of
normality.
As shown in Table 3, Mardia’s multivariate skewness and kurtosis were significant, implying that data may not follow the normal distribution. Nevertheless,
when SEM is used, Mardia becomes overly sensitive to large sample sizes, and
there is a high chance of having significant results.
Stevens (2009) and Kim (2017) recommended using descriptive univariate

The values of kurtosis for individual variables vary between 1.2 and 0.27. The

3.8 Testing indirect relationships
The above analysis identified direct paths. Considering the structure of the research
model, indirect effects should also be calculated. According to Baron and Kenny
(1986), for mediation to occur, two conditions should be fulfilled. First, if the indirect effect is significant and the direct effect is not, the mediation is full. Second,
if both the indirect and direct effects are significant, then the mediation is partial.
(Baron & Kenny, 1986).
These conditions were examined then the further investigation was conducted
based on the direct and indirect coefficients, as shown in Table 7. The total effect
equals the sum of both direct and indirect effects (Preacher & Hayes, 2008). The
bootstrapping method was used to obtain the significance levels of the indirect and
total effects. Bootstrapping refers to repeated resampling of the original data (Efron,
1979). In case of a lack of multivariate normality, bootstrapping offers an alternative to asymptotic inference for factor loading, path coefficients, and goodness-of-fit
indices (Finney & DiStefano, 2006).
The first mediator examined was donor satisfaction. The brand experience was
found to affect intentions to re-donate directly(p < 0.026) and indirectly through
donor satisfaction (0.001), thus supporting a partial mediation. Conversely, only
an indirect effect of brand personality on intentions to re-donate was significant
(p < 0.003), thus supporting full mediation.
The second mediator in the model was brand personality, as it mediates the relationship between brand experience and donor satisfaction. The total effect of brand
experience on donor satisfaction was 0.806 (P < 0.002). As shown in Table 6, the
brand experience was found to affect donor satisfaction directly (p < 0.000) and indirectly (p < 0.002) through brand personality, thus supporting a partial mediation.
The total effect of brand experience on donor satisfaction was 0.797 (p < 0.001).

4 Discussion
The role of charities in modern societies cannot be overlooked. Charities provide
services to those in need and raise awareness of societal concerns (Sargeant, 2009).
To explore new income streams, a good understanding of donors’ attitudes and giving behavior is mandatory (Hou et al., 2009). This study empirically examines the
model developed by Brakus et al. (2009) in the charity branding context. The model
investigates the relationship between brand experience, brand personality, and donor
satisfaction on intentions to re-donate. To match the charity branding context, the
scales for brand experience and brand personality were modified. Using a sample of
donors in Alexandria, Egypt, the model was tested using SEM.
The findings support Brakus et al.’s (2009) findings. All hypothesized relationships were supported except the direct path from brand personality to intentions to

A significant relationship between the brand personality of charity organizations and

with the findings of Park and Lee (2005), Brakus et al. (2009), Achouri and Bouslama
(2010), and Chen and Phou (2013). It also provides support to studies conducted in the
charity sector (e.g., da silva et al., 2020; Sargeant et al., 2008; Stebbins & Hartman, 2013).

on intentions to re-donate, implying support for the fourth hypothesis. Many studies reported similar findings (Berry & Carbone, 2007; Iglesias et al., 2011; Walter et al., 2013; Shamim and Mohsin Butt, 2013; Ramaseshan & Stein, 2014; Shim
et al., 2015) while others reported opposite results (Ebrahim et al., 2016; Kwong &
Candinegara, 2014). Rose et al. (2011) noted that for brand experience to affect loyalty, satisfaction should be incorporated as a mediator.
The direct effect of brand personality on intentions to re-donate was insignificant,
leading to the rejection of the fifth hypothesis. This supports the findings of Ebrahim
et al. (2016), but it is inconsistent with the findings of Stebbins and Hartman (2013),
who found that charity brand personality significantly influences donors’ giving
behavior. Many authors suggested mediation effects. For example, Hou et al. (2009)
found that brand personality greatly influences individual donors’ self-perception or
feelings, which in turn affects individual giving intention and behavior directly and
significantly. Lindenmeier et al. (2021) argued that actual self-congruence and functional congruence mediate the relationship between perceived warmth and competence and volunteering intentions. Shaari et al. (2019) identify attitudes as a mediator
of the relationship between university brand personality and intention to re-donate.
The effect of donor satisfaction on re-donation intention was also reported providing support for the sixth hypothesis. This finding is consistent with previous
studies in commercial settings (Chandrashekaran et al., 2007; Cooil et al., 2007;
Faullant et al., 2008; Mittal et al., 1999). Boenigk and Helmig (2013) confirmed
that donor satisfaction affects donors’ intentions to re-donate positively in blood
and money donations.
Indirect relationships between research variables were also examined. The
effect of brand experience on donor satisfaction was found to be direct and
indirect through brand personality and the total effect was 0.707 (P < 0.001).

between these variables in the context of charity branding. Our findings suggest that
research models used in commercial settings could apply in other environments, such
as charity branding. We also suggest that the conceptualization of brand experience
and brand personality may differ from conceptualizations used in traditional marketing contexts. Specifically, we used a brand experience construct comprised of affective, intellectual and relational dimensions and a brand personality construct that
includes thoughtfulness, integrity and competence. These constructs may be more
relevant to a charity brand context than other brand experience dimensions. Their
effect on donor satisfaction and re-donation intentions was positive and significant.
Specifically, our research contributes to the field of charity branding in several
ways. First, we have introduced and validated a scale for the charity brand experience based on the work of Brakus et al. (2009) and Nysveen et al. (2013). Second,
we have provided evidence of the importance of brand experience and personality
on donor satisfaction and re-donation intentions. Third, by conducting this research
in Egypt, we have added to the limited research on charity branding conducted in
developing countries. Conducting research in different cultural settings allows
researchers to understand research models and whether universal or contextual models should be used.
The findings of this research benefit managers of charity organizations interested
in enhancing re-donation behavior. These managers should carefully design, communicate and monitor the donors’ brand experience and personality to fit the prosocial nature of the donation. Brand experiences should reflect relational, intellectual,
and affective components. This means that charity marketing managers should
design experiences stimulating donors’ cognitive and emotional processes. The
brand experience should also convey relational aspects such as belonging to a community or a larger family. Similarly, charity brand personalities communicated to the
donors should reflect integrity, thoughtfulness, and competence. Since these factors
predict re-donation intentions, charities should use market research to trace donors’
perceptions of brand experience, brand personality and satisfaction.

5 Limitations
The data collection was limited to Egypt’s most popular and largest charity organizations. It was observed from the findings of this research that about 26% of donors,
who deal with large and well-known charities, donate more to small and unknown
organizations. Therefore, future researchers should include those small organizations in their studies. The results of this research examined only the effect of the
brand experience of charity organizations in general. While Nysveen et al. (2013),
Japutra and Molinillo (2019), and Chrissos Anestis et al. (2021) examined the effect
of individual brand experience and brand personality in their research, such effects
were not examined in the context of charity organizations. Even though the ­R2 coefficients for both donors’ satisfaction and loyalty were high (0.817 and 0.752, respectively), some model fit indices are barely acceptable. Fit indices such as CMIN, GFI
and AGFI were relatively low. These indices are generally sensitive to sample size
(Sharma et al., 2005). Conversely, other indices such as CMIN/DF, RMSEA, CFI,
and IFI are not sensitive to sample size (Hooper et al., 2008) and they were acceptable. Better fit indices could be obtained by having larger sample sizes. Other variables may be incorporated into the model. Suggested variables include donors’ selfconcept, brand awareness, and brand image.
Another limitation is the use of re-donation intentions and not actual donation
amounts. Understanding the role of charity brand experience and charity brand personality in actual donor behavior could be examined in future research.

6 Conclusion
This research aimed to revisit the brand experience model introduced by Brakus
et al. (2009) in the context of charity branding using a sample of Egyptian donors.
Findings were similar to findings reported in commercial contexts. This indicates
that the model can transcend the context of charities. Nevertheless, such transcendence should be conducted using the appropriate conceptualization and measurement
of research variables.
