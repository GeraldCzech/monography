Faith? Hope? Charity? Religion explains giving
when warm glow and impure altruism do not

Abstract
I explain why many charities, especially religious ones,
spend significantly to improve poor community members'
living standards. Conventional theoretical philanthropy
models with altruism or impure altruism/warm glow cannot
explain poverty-alleviating private transfers in the type
and size of communities where this occurs—for example,
congregations. However, if preferences reflect religious
precepts (you are your brothers' keeper; treat neighbours
like yourself), charitable poverty alleviation can be optimal
even in large communities. Income and price elasticities
for giving prove important. Surprisingly, charitable private
transfers are less likely when donors care for recipients'
living standard instead of the aggregate amount all recipients get.
KEYWORDS

altruism, philanthropy, poverty alleviation, public good, religion,
warm glow

causes free-riding and public good undersupply. The second arises as a transfer directly benefits one

Christian tenets supporting benevolence include “Without charity, I am nothing and I gain nothing…” (1 Corinthians 13:1–13). So, in a pious USA, charitable giving is large: cash giving matches
clothes spending, half spending on health from post-tax income (Bureau of Labour Statistics, 2013).
Religion gets 35%–40% of all U.S. giving, thrice the second-ranked, education (Andreoni &
Payne, 2013; List, 2011). In the UK, it is joint-top with health.
My analysis embraces all major faiths. These all have “pro-social” precepts stressing care for
the poor, which are analogous to preferences for redistribution.2 Judaism's tzedakah (righteousness
via charity) is an obligation. Islam's third of five pillars is zakat—alms. “Dana” (charity) underpins
dharma (righteousness) in Hinduism, Buddhism, Sikhism and Jainism. Hinduism says money not
shared with the poor gives no happiness. The Bhagavadgita (3.19–26) commands “work with others'
welfare in mind” and the Tamil Tirukkural says: “Giving to the poor is true charity, all other giving
expects some return”.
Much of religious giving reduces poverty. In 1989, congregations got half of total US charity
receipts of near $100bn (Biddle, 1992), spending $20bn on members' “specific material needs”.3
Allard (2008) shows religious charities focus more on such needs than secular charities and, since
1997, have offset U.S. governments' reduced transfers to the poor.
Economists rather neglect poverty-reduction-motivated charity. They argue that donors' altruism
towards, for example, transfers, day-care or housing recipients makes these public goods. They then
study the public good, but not the private benefit, aspects of charity. I argue that one must study both
aspects for a full understanding of charity. Formally, this is the crucial difference between my work
and the existing literature. My analysis here and in Fraser (2006) are the only ones I know that bundle
public good and private good provision when there is an opportunity cost of providing one in terms
of the other. I believe that models in the literature that are interpreted as involving private transfers to
the poor do not involve such transfers. They involve, as Bergstrom et al. (1986) title states accurately,
“private provision of public goods”. Unlike in my analysis, they do not have any individual increasing

Some studies justify neglecting charitable transfers on the grounds that state welfare crowds out
poverty-reducing charity (e.g., Roberts (1984)). Few later crowding-out studies examine poverty
reduction.4 Boberg-Fazlić and Sharp (2017) do, finding “strong empirical support for a positive relationship between welfare provision and charitable giving”—that is, they find crowding-in.
The aspects of religions that seem most relevant for charity are precepts obliging help to the poor.
I capture these by including total spending on transfers, separate from that on a pure public good, in
all utility functions. This reflects a taste for redistribution as it yields utility from improving the poor's
welfare, not just from the act of giving itself or giving directly benefiting oneself. It captures List's
(2011, p. 165) idea that different motives to other types of charity spur religious giving. He advances
social insurance: “people give to churches so that in time of need they can more readily receive Church
services or help from fellow churchgoers.” I call such preferences, arguably required by scriptures,
“pious”. Section 4 shows that a charity for such agents can find transfers to the poor welfare-enhancing
in large populations as the transfers' social utility increases with the population.
Superficially, it might seem more natural to reflect concern for poor brethrens' welfare by preferences based on per capita, not total, transfers, as per capita transfers are what determine a poor
person's total income, consumption, and hence welfare. However, I do not assume so here for several
reasons. First, total transfers can be viewed as an “insurance pool” in List's interpretation. Poor individuals' welfare can be affected both by the per capita transfer to them, and also the existence of that
pool. Second, with a fixed population of beneficiaries, a one-one link exists between total and per
capita transfers, so an increase in the former increases the latter. Third, I believe that a redistributive
charity like the Salvation Army and its constituency focuses more on what it does collectively for all
rather than for each individual.
More concretely, I show in Section 5.2 (supported by the Online Appendices) that tastes based
on per capita transfers [which I call “Brown preferences” after Brown (1997)] “do not work” as well
as pious preferences to create an environment in which transfers to the poor can be optimal in large
congregations when such transfers compete with a public good. First, other things equal, Brown
preferences generate a feasible subgame-perfect Nash equilibrium in charitable giving in very small
congregations. However, with typically-sized congregations, they are much less likely to generate
such an equilibrium than when agents have pious preferences. Second, other things equal, for a given
fraction of donations devoted to alleviating poverty by the charity, less is likely to be given with tastes
based on per capita transfers than on total transfers. Lastly, considering the impact on giving of the
proportion of the congregation of fixed size that is poor, I show that we get diametrically opposite
results with Brown and pious preferences. With the former, the amount given to charity by each
donor and the share devoted to transfers decreases as the proportion of the poor in the congregation
increases. With pious preferences, the opposite is true.
My model augments three empirical literature strands. One (Clark & Lelkes, 2006; Scheve &
Stasavage, 2006) links faith to redistribution. Faith insures against mishaps by giving psychic benefits
as, say, health, jobs, or pensions. A welfare state is smaller as these psychic benefits replace state
ones. Another strand (Hungerman, 2005) says that Church welfare substitutes for the state's. Again,
a welfare state is smaller in more pious societies. A third says religious giving insures for an afterlife
(Chang, 2005; Hrung, 2004; Thornton and Helms, 2013).

de Wit and Bekkers' (2017) extensive meta-analysis concludes that “private giving is generally not crowded out by public
funding”. They focus on crowding out in public good supply and ignore transfers.

My analysis predicts that, if tastes reflect religion's precept of sharing with the poor, charitable
transfers occur. This rationalises the first two empirical strands. But, it is not just the pious who give
to the poor; the irreligious might also give if they have prosocial tastes that value transfers to the poor.
My analysis does not preclude this. Nevertheless, like Alesina and Giuliano (2011), List (2011), and
others, I recognize that religion is sufficiently important in the charity landscape to justify special
attention. The Conclusion studies the afterlife.
I show that giving part of charitable donations to the poor can increase giving even if donors get
no altruistic externality from such transfers. An altruistic externality from transfers can raise giving
and welfare, explaining transfers. Section 2 reviews models without religion; Section 3 shows the
difficulty, with “standard” tastes, of reducing poverty via transfers alongside public good supply.
Section 4 shows pious tastes overcome this theoretically and numerically. Section 5 examines other
tastes, including joint-product/warm-glow ones. Section 6 concludes. An Appendix has some proofs;
Online Appendices contain computations, more proofs and discussion.

Becker (1974), Warr (1982), Roberts (1984) and Brown (1997) are among few who model charitable transfers. Becker, Warr and Roberts model situations with a sole poor recipient and so do not
analyse population issues that are central in my analysis. Brown ignores recipients, apart from the fact
that the average donation to them enters her donors' utility functions as an externality. Section 5.2
shows that donors caring for average rather than total donations works against transfers. These authors
ignore that charities only make transfers if this enhances total welfare, which depends on the number
of consumers of any public good that could be funded instead.9
As I focus on a charity's redistribution via direct transfers, my analysis differs from Uler (2009),
who does not study this aspect of giving. She considers how redistribution effected by an income tax
system impacts giving to charity solely for providing a public good, so the new large numbers issue
I consider is not relevant. Uler's tax system entails a flat rate tax on income net of charitable donations (which implies preferential tax treatment of donations) with proceeds returned to all households
equally. She establishes, among other things, conditions under which voluntary giving increases in
the level of redistribution. Although churches as charities, as well as individual donors, receive preferential tax treatment in reality, that treatment does not usually discriminate between a Church's uses
of funds and, thereby, directly influence the issues I focus on. Thus, for brevity, I do not considered it
explicitly in my formal analysis.
Maybe closest to my analysis, Kotchen (2006) models a scenario relevant to charity for a good
with public and private features. For example, buying a grocery item might yield a fixed donation to
a public good. His model's principal differences to mine are: first, its mix of characteristics is fixed
exogenously in a one-stage game. In mine, taking the Church/charity as bundling a public good and
private transfers, their combination arises endogenously from a strategic interaction between charity
and consumers in a two-stage game. Second, while he studies how group size affects equilibrium
provision of the public characteristic, his model is not set up to show how group size impacts whether
or not charitable transfers occur.
Despite a dearth of theory on charitable transfers or religious charities, an impressive empirical
literature exists. Bekkers and Wiepking (2011) review key articles.

5.1 | Warm-glow/joint-product preferences
In a pure warm-glow model, there is one public good and a number of private goods equal to 1 plus the
number of agents in the model. The public good has quantity equal to the total donations of the agents
to provide it from their endowment of the first (and common) private good. Each agent's donation to
providing the public good gives them a warm glow, which is a private good for them only. With pious
preferences, there is one private good (the endowment good) and two public goods (the total transfers,
functioning as a public good, and the Church).

welfare-optimal to alleviate poverty? No. This is seen by applying my approach.

Lastly, I consider numerically the effect on giving of varying the proportion of the poor in a fixed
population with Brown preferences. It can be shown for log-Cobb-Douglas preferences (see Appendix S2 for details) that, when transfers occur, the charity uses a smaller fraction of what is given for
transfers when the proportion of the poor increases and each donor gives less. This is diametrically
opposite to the finding with pious preferences where, as the proportion of the poor in the congregation
increases, a larger share of donations is used for transfers and each donor gives more. These results
follow because, with both Brown and pious preferences, donors can be induced to give more by the
charity increasing the share of donations that is used for transfers. However, with Brown preferences,
the more poor there are to share the total transfers—hence the less the impact on their per capita
income from any given amount of transfer—the less the charity's incentive to use donations for transfers, the less the share of donations that is used for that purpose and the less donors are induced to give.

6

| CONCLUSION

This paper shows that simultaneous charitable provision of private transfers and a separate public
good that compete for resources can optimally occur with what I call “pious” preferences in a large
population, yet this is not so with warm-glow and other preferences. In showing this, I identify a new
large-number effect different from that usually associated with free-riding behaviour when charitable
donations contribute to only a public good. “Pious preferences” are “prosocial” by internalising the
amount given to the poor, reflecting the religious precept to love neighbours like yourself. They are in

the spirit of Becker as everyone internalising aggregate transfers to the poor makes this a public good.
I show also that a charity bundling transfers and public good supply when people have pious tastes
can greatly increase giving compared to a case of non-pious preferences. I think this explains the large
amount of giving to religion and use of much of the receipts for the poor.
My analysis works precisely because aggregate private transfers to the poor function as a public
good. When they do not, as with BBV, Andreoni warm-glow or Brown preferences, where aggregate
private transfers are not analysed explicitly, the analysis does not work in the sense that the second
large-number effect militating against transfers that I identify operates to prevent transfers being optimal in a large congregation.
Explanations for giving to religious charities differing superficially from mine exist. These must
also stress utility from transfers to explain transfers. For example, churchgoers might give as they think
their Church more trustworthy than other organizations. But this leaves using donations for transfers
unexplained. Again, parishioners could feel closer to their own Church's poor members than to others'.
But how do we model a “closer connection”, except as preference between types reflected in the
utility function? For example, greater weight could be put on transfers to their own religion or race
(cf. Alesina et al., 1999). As “11 o'clock Sunday morning continues to be the most segregated hour in
America” (Hartford Institute, 2015), much religious giving allows donors to improve welfare only for
those with whom they identify. People might also think that giving to the poor increases their chance
of heaven. If this matters to them, they must have an in-life evaluation of post-life utility. This must
increase in giving—for either the probability of getting to heaven to enjoy the post-life utility or/and
the post-life utility itself increases with giving in life (as a good giver sits closer to the Deity). But, in
all cases, this is reflected in the utility of transfers.
Several authors (e.g., Andreoni & Payne, 2013; Feldstein, 1975; List, 2011) suggest religious
giving differs from other forms. It is less cyclical (List, 2011), less price-elastic (Feldstein, 1975), and
a donor's piety decides the amount given (McKitrick et al., 2013). So, its motivation is not narrowly
economic. Empathetic agents whose utility responds positively to total giving to the poor capture this.
However, religious precepts might make worshippers feel moral constraints others do not—constraints
not subsumed in utility functions, unlike with pious preferences. The constraints could reflect norms
for giving—for example, tithing. Donors could then suffer a loss for how much their giving deviates
from the “norm”, and balance disutility from giving (via lost consumption) with that loss. I will pursue
this in future.
Lastly, I have ignored administration costs not directly benefiting charity targets. My G is not
funding to maintain the charity's structure. Usually, consumers do not see administration costs as a
public good, much less comparable to a Church building or pastor.
