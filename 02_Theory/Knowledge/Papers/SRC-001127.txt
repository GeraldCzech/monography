Abstract
Research has documented a large number of conditions that affect charitable giving practices. To what
extent is the science of philanthropy self-correcting, and which findings on charitable giving hold up against
the scrutiny of scientific criticism? We answer these questions with an empirical assessment of statistical
irregularities, retractions and replications of published findings. We find that in research on giving
retractions and replications are less common than in research in other fields, and that statistical errors are
more common. About 60% of replications yield similar conclusions as preceding studies. Previous findings
on material benefits and social rewards for giving are less likely to replicate, while findings on material costs
and solicitation are more likely to replicate. In the interest of a robust knowledge base for fundraising, we
call for more replications and we suggest that journals adopt registered reports - in principle acceptance of
publication before results are known - as a solution to publication bias.

Introduction
In 2012, Boyan Slat, a first year aerospace engineering student in the Netherlands, came up with a clever idea
to remove pollution from the world’s oceans: a ship could gather plastic waste floating around, and bring it
back ashore so that it can be recycled. The world’s oceans are a truly global collective good: they are crucial
for planetary health, but no one’s particular ownership or responsibility to protect. All creatures, great and
small, benefit from less pollution and higher ocean water quality.
The current state of science is similar to the plastic waste problem in many ways. We all benefit from clear
waters in science. But more than a decade after the Open Science Collaboration (2015) showed that half of
replications of seminal studies show very different results than their predecessors, it is clear that science is in a
knowledge production crisis. The production of scientific papers has grown at a strong pace, but the
reliability of research has not increased: a recent massive attempt to replicate research findings in the social
and behavioral sciences again found that half of previous research findings was not replicated (Tyner et al.,
2026).
Similar to the ocean clean up initiative, the current paper illustrates the potential of a simple solution that
can identify waste in the ocean of knowledge on why people give. The solution is to screen the large number
of published findings, to identify studies that attempted to replicate previous findings, and compare their
results: replications with similar results as preceding studies reflect robust regularities and reliable knowledge
(see also Hartshorne & Schachner, 2012). Especially independent replications - attempts to replicate findings
by other researchers - are informative. Similar to the concern that the ocean clean up does not eliminate the
causes of the environmental crisis, this initiative is not a solution to the knowledge production crisis. But it
does give readers – scientists and practitioners alike – a simple tool to evaluate the reliability of published
research, and that’s important for our collective scientific health.
An important source of knowledge in science generally are evidence syntheses, such as meta-analyses and
narrative systematic reviews (Siddaway, Wood & Hedges, 2019). One systematic review of the literature on
charitable giving (Bekkers & Wiepking, 2011) distinguished eight mechanisms driving donations. Each
mechanism is a category of interventions, i.e., a set of levers that fundraisers can pull to attract donations.
The regularities emerging from studies that have tested these interventions are that donors give more (often)
when 1) they are aware of a clear need for donations; 2) when they are explicitly being asked to give; 3) when
the material costs of giving are lower and the material benefits are higher; 4) when they care about the
well-being of beneficiaries; 5) when they receive social benefits for giving and avoid social costs for not
giving; 6) when they receive psychological benefits for giving and avoid psychological costs for not giving; 7)
when the cause they support matches their values; and 8) when they perceive donations as more effective
and efficient.

Previous research
With the current paper we aim to raise the bar for what counts as reliable evidence in research on charitable
giving. To guide our attempts to locate reliable findings, we rely on findings from the newly emerged fields

2

of meta science, research on research, and bibliometrics. Here we give a brief overview of the relevant
literature.
First, we broadly define replications as attempts to repeat findings from previous studies. This definition is
not limited to hypothesis-testing research, and includes exploratory and descriptive research. Replications
come in 4 varieties: 1) verification: researchers analyze the same data as a preceding study, with the same
methods and models; 2) robustness analyses: re-analysis of the same data with different methods and models;
3) reproduction: researchers analyze new data with the same methods and models; and 4) generalization:
analysis of new data with different methods and models (Freese & Peterson, 2017).
In a recent attempt to reanalyze findings from 600 papers published in 62 journals in the social and
behavioral sciences (Miske et al., 2026), researchers could locate data and code for only 144 papers.
Reproductions with new data often produce different results (Tyner et al., 2026). The literature review on
mechanisms driving charitable giving that we revisit here (Bekkers & Wiepking, 2011) described some
features of the populations and contexts, but did not include a quality appraisal of included studies.
Meta science findings (Open Science Collaboration, 2015; Holzmeister et al., 2024; Youyou, Yang & Uzzi,
2023) suggest three categories of reasons why replications find different results than preceding studies. A first
category of reasons we label unique findings. Studies reporting findings in unique contexts, for specific
regions or subgroups of populations, or in unusual circumstances are unlikely to be encountered again
when researchers attempt to do the study again in other times, regions, populations and circumstances. A
second category of reasons is the quality of preceding studies. Studies containing statistical errors are less
likely to be replicated. Studies reporting weaker effect sizes, with smaller numbers of observations and lower
statistical power are less likely to be successfully reproduced in new data. A third category of reasons is the
process of publication, peer review, and citation. Studies based on weak evidence but reporting significantly
positive findings - i.e., supporting the hypotheses - are more likely to be published and cited than studies
reporting null-findings or negative results. Such biases distort our view of truly reliable research, and create
the impression that research is more replicable than it actually is.

1. Unique findings
There may be good reasons in the realities we study that explain why repeating a study can produce entirely
different results. Preceding findings may reflect uniquely local patterns that do not reflect general regularities
in human behavior. Testing the same hypothesis with other data can produce radically different results
because the association truly differs between samples drawn from other populations. For charitable giving,
interventions that generally tend to work in experiments among participants in lab studies or surveys does
not guarantee positive results in each and every campaign fundraisers organize. The effects may be very
different in practice. In addition, peculiarities of donor groups, public perceptions of fundraising
organizations, and the causes that they raise donations for will create heterogeneity in treatment effects.
Below we investigate to what extent replications produce results that differ from preceding findings as a
result of differences in study populations and contexts.

3

2. Research quality
Honest mistakes. Sometimes researchers make errors, for example in copying results from statistical software
to the word processors they use to write manuscripts. This is one source of the inconsistencies between
reported p-values and test statistics that we quantify below. Sometimes researchers also forget to include
details in their manuscripts that are crucial for the results. The academic community tolerates honest
mistakes, especially if authors step forward to identify and correct them. Error detection and correction is a
sign of integrity.
Researcher degrees of freedom. Replication failures and weaker associations in replications may also be related
to researcher degrees of freedom in preceding studies (Simmons, Nelson & Simonsohn, 2011; Gelman &
Loken, 2014). Data collection and analysis involve a large number of decisions: the choice of measurement
instruments, treatment of outliers, and other data preprocessing choices, the selection, application and
interpretation of analytical models and statistical tests. Fiddling with the analysis to obtain significant results
is known as p-hacking (Simmons, Nelson, & Simonsohn, 2011). In the presence of incentives to report
strong, significant and positive results, researchers will attempt to obtain them, and withhold negative results
and null-findings. Gross inconsistencies in p-values are more common in statistical tests reported as
significant than in tests reported as not significant, suggesting a systematic bias favoring positive results
(Nuijten et al., 2016). Below we explore how often p-values in research on charitable giving are inconsistent
with test statistics, and whether they contain a similar bias toward positive results.

3. The publication process
Published research is more often reporting positive results than unpublished research. Many relevant
research findings never see the light of day, and those that stay in the shadows tend to tell us mostly which
interventions are not working. Studies published in journals are more likely to report positive findings than
studies that remain unpublished, as a result of author, editor, and reviewer behaviors (Ferguson & Heene,
2012; Franco et al., 2014; O’Boyle et al, 2017; Chopra et al., 2024). In economics, authors at all levels of
seniority as well as editors believe that significant results submitted to journals are more likely to get
published (Chopra et al., 2024). A survey in multiple fields suggests that authors are more likely to submit
significant results for publication, and both reviewers and editors prefer them (Muradchanian et al., 2023).
The excess of positive results supporting the hypotheses in published research – approaching 95% in some
disciplines (Fanelli, 2010, 2012; Schäfer & Schwarz, 2019) indicates that negative or null results are withheld
from publication in file drawers (Rosenthal, 1979). Publication bias implies an asymmetry in the reliability
of findings: positive findings are less reliable than negative findings. If there is no study reporting a
theoretically plausible effect, it is very unlikely that the effect actually exists. The grains of salt added in the
process of publication imply that reported findings cannot be trusted if no one attempted to replicate them.
A recurrent finding in replications is that they tend to produce statistically significant associations much less
often than their predecessors. Replication studies in the social and behavioral sciences find different results
about half of the time (Open Science Collaboration, 2015; Camerer et al., 2018; Tyner et al., 2026).
Replications also tend to find substantially weaker effect sizes than the studies they attempt to replicate, so
4

that meta-analytic effects based solely on published research are likely inflated (Ioannidis, Stanley, &
Doucouliagos, 2017; Kvarven, Strømland & Johannesson, 2020).
Evidence syntheses that summarize results of previous research on charitable giving in systematic literature
reviews and meta-analyses typically report findings in articles published in scientific journals. As a result, the
available systematic literature reviews are likely to provide a distorted picture: because negative findings are
obscured, the conclusions of review articles are biased toward positive results. We do not know how strong
publication bias is in research on charitable giving. Systematic literature reviews that include unpublished
studies typically do not analyze differences between published and unpublished studies. In the analyses
below we explore how often results in studies on giving are supportive of the study hypotheses, and whether
the tendency to report positive results differs between replications and preceding studies, as well as between
studies published in journals and those that remained unpublished.
A commonly used proxy for research quality is publication status: a study published in a journal or book
that employs peer review is commonly believed to be ‘better’ than an unpublished study. Unfortunately, the
peer review system of quality control is not fortified enough to keep out the bad science (Smith, 2010).
Journals continue to rely on unpaid volunteers for reviews, and typically do not specify how reviewers
should evaluate manuscripts, nor provide training in how to detect bad science. Studies that compared
manuscripts submitted and accepted for publication (Stephen, 2022; Hopewell et al., 2014) found that they
do not change much during the review period, indicating that the added value of review is small. Even the
most prestigious journals have trouble keeping the bad science out of their pages (Brembs, 2018). As a result,
the publication of a paper is no guarantee that the research is reliable, even when it is in Science or Nature
(Camerer et al., 2018). The clearest cases of this filtering failure are retractions, where the flaws in a study
become apparent only after publication. Below we examine how often, and for what reasons, research on
charitable giving is retracted; we then identify, review and analyze replication studies in this field.

Data and Methods
This section briefly presents the data and methods we used to identify and code (1) retractions; (2)
consistency of test statistics, and (3) replications. See Appendix A for details.
Retractions. To identify retractions of research on charitable giving, we (RB) searched Google Scholar, using
the six keywords (“charitable behavior”, “charitable giving”, “prosocial behavior”, “philanthropy”,
“altruism”, and “donations”) from Bekkers & Wiepking (2011) plus “retract*”.
Test statistics. To estimate the prevalence of inconsistencies in test statistics associated with p-values in
research on giving, we applied the Statcheck module (Nuijten & Epskamp, 2024) integrated in metacheck
(Lakens & DeBruine, 2026) to a large corpus of papers included in OpenAlex. First, one of the authors (RB)
defined the corpus to include all studies in OpenAlex that cited at least one out of 24 seed publications (see
Appendix B) reflecting the eight mechanisms in giving behavior described in Bekkers & Wiepking (2011).
Next, one of the authors (ML) compiled a corpus of 7,412 studies included in OpenAlex citing these seed
publications, and downloaded the largest possible number of publications from the corpus through Zotero,
obtaining pdf files for 3,702 publications. Next, we (ML) ran Statcheck on these pdfs. Because Statcheck can
5

only process papers that adhere to journal article reporting standards of the American Psychological
Association (APA), it could not process all papers.
Replications. To identify replications, we developed a method to identify replications from large volumes of
research in two stages. In the first stage, we (RB) created a coding scheme and manually screened 1,213
Google Scholar search results for “charitable behavior” for terms related to replication and identified 34
replications. 25 replications analyzed charitable giving as a dependent variable, representing 0.5% of all 4,858
search results for charitable behavior. In the second stage, we (HF, AG, RB) modified this method to screen
a more targeted selection of 340 search results for “charitable giving” mentioning replication, identifying
another 28 replications analyzing charitable giving as a dependent variable. Finally, we added 13 replications
to the analysis that had not shown up in our search results but that we knew existed.

Results
The generic increase in the number of publications researchers produce also holds in the field of research on
philanthropy and charitable giving. The number of studies that researchers produce each year is very large
and has grown strongly in the past decades (see Appendix A). The comprehensive overview of all studies on
conditions that affect charitable giving published in 2007 (Bekkers & Wiepking, 2007) included about 500
studies, assembled from seven keyword searches in Google Scholar that yielded less than 5,000 search results.
Due to increasing productivity in science, repeating this effort for studies that appeared after 2007 would be
humanly impossible. Meanwhile, thousands of studies on charitable giving appear each year. In 2021, no less
than 89,000 studies on donations appeared according to Google Scholar. For the same year, 35,300
publications on altruism appeared, 27,000 on philanthropy, 17,100 on prosocial behavior, 4,270 on
charitable giving, and 467 on charitable behavior. It has become virtually impossible to keep track of the
current state of knowledge. Since the year 2000, when the number of studies on charitable behavior was
only 40 and on prosocial behavior 1,580, the number of publications has grown more than tenfold.

How often is research on giving retracted?
In Google Scholar we searched for retracted publications on charitable giving, using the six keywords from
the mechanisms review plus “retract*” to identify studies that mentioned retracted, retracting, or retraction.
We found 36 studies on charitable giving published between 2007 and 2022 that publishers retracted. We
found no retracted studies on giving that were published before the year 2007.
In science overall, the total number of retracted studies in the database of Retraction Watch (2024) for the
same period was 52,464. The retraction rate of studies on charitable giving was 0.069%. The number of
retractions per year has increased strongly over the years. For studies on charitable giving, in contrast, we see
a downward trend after the peak in 2010 (see Figure 1). For the period until 2014, studies on charitable
giving are 0.010% of all retracted studies; for the period after 2014, they are 0.044%.
Two authors (RB and SKM) coded the retracted studies. 24 retracted studies (62%) were published in
journals, the remaining 15 were conference abstracts. 26 retracted studies (67%) were empirical papers, the
6

remaining 13 were not reporting analyses of data. The majority of retracted empirical studies on giving (n =
22, 85%) did not analyze individual charitable giving as a dependent variable. Instead, these studies analyzed
giving intentions, perceptions of the behavior of others, or corporate social responsibility. We located 31 of
the 39 retracted studies in the Retraction Watch database, which contained the country of the first author,
and the stated reason for retraction. The majority of retracted studies on charitable giving were from China
(n = 17, 55%), similar to the proportion for the entire database of retractions (52%). Almost half (14 of 31)
retracted studies on giving stated ‘peer review concerns’ or ‘breach of policy by author’ as a reason for
retraction. In the entire database of retractions, these reasons are much less common (17% of all cases).

All retracted empirical studies that analyzed charitable giving as a dependent variable were published in
psychology. Three of them – Chatterjee et al., 2013), Sanna et al. (2011) and Wang et al. (2012) – were
retracted for fraudulent data. The retraction of Decety et al. (2015) deserves mentioning because the authors
requested it after a reanalysis of the study data by other authors (Shariff et al., 2016) produced different
results. The preceding findings were due to a coding error. In this case, independent scrutiny of data shared
by the authors enabled the correction of the study findings.
Most retracted studies were rarely cited, if ever: in Google Scholar, 15 retracted publications had citation
counts of 0. Three quarters were cited less than 20 times. In all, studies on charitable behavior are rarely
retracted; retracted studies are rarely cited, and the retraction rate is declining. These findings may be

7

interpreted two fundamentally different ways. One interpretation is that research on charitable giving is
trustworthy and reliable. Apparently publishers find little cause for retraction of research on giving. Another
option is that research on giving has not received much scrutiny, and is therefore less likely to be retracted.
Retractions tell us something about the peer review process. The flaws in the retracted studies were serious
enough to warrant withdrawal, but reviewers did not catch them before publication. As stated above, almost
half of the retractions stated "peer review concerns" or "breach of policy by author" as the reason, compared
to only 17% in the entire database. Peer review apparently does not reliably keep bad science out, and in
some cases, the problem lies in the review process or in author misconduct.
Availability of data greatly facilitates error detection. Yet transparency about data in the field of research on
charitable giving is low. In 2023, less than half of empirical research papers in five journals (Voluntary Sector
Review, Nonprofit Management and Leadership, Voluntas, NVSQ, and the Journal of Philanthropy and
Marketing) provided any references to the data analyzed (Bekkers, 2023). Articles in NML and NVSQ were
most likely to contain links to datafiles or information about data availability, but such information was
given in only one out of five articles.

How often does research on charitable giving misreport p-values?
Of 667 publications that Statcheck could process, 306 (46%) produced some form of error. In 86 papers
(13%) decision errors were found, where reported significance levels were different from the computed
significance levels implied by the test statistics. Papers typically report multiple p-values. Of all 6,999
p-values checked, 776 (11%) were errors, and 128 were decision errors (1.8%). Both proportions are rather
high compared to previous estimates for communication science (8.8% and 1.3%, respectively) and social
psychology (8.7% and 1.5%, respectively; Vermeulen et al., 2015). Also they are higher than for psychology as
a whole (9.7% and 1.4%, respectively; Nuijten et al., 2016).
89 of the 128 decision errors (70%) were favoring the study hypotheses, such that the authors incorrectly
claimed a statistically significant relationship, when the test statistics actually indicated a non-significant
relationship. This proportion is also higher than in communication science (65%; Vermeulen et al., 2015).
We do not have a good explanation why the error rates in studies on giving are higher than in (social)
psychology and communication science, and why a higher proportion of the errors are favoring study
hypotheses. These two findings may reflect both honest mistakes as well as researcher degrees of freedom.
Honest mistakes may have occurred in transcribing results from software to manuscripts. Researchers
studying charitable giving may have received less training in statistics. It is also possible that studies on giving
contain more errors because peer reviewers and editors are less able to detect such errors. The pronounced
skew toward errors that favor the hypotheses suggests researcher degrees of freedom.

What characterizes studies attempting to replicate previous findings?
In research on charitable giving, replications are rare: they represent less than 1% of the total number of
publications, which is less than in most other disciplines of the social and behavioral sciences (Bekkers,
8

2024c). Among the 66 studies that replicated studies on charitable giving as a dependent variable, 39 studies
(59%) found results that were similar to the preceding study, 27 did not: 17 studies reported null findings,
and 10 studies reported findings in contrast to the preceding study. These results are similar to results of a
recent attempt to replicate a large number of findings from the social and behavioral sciences (Tyner et al.,
2026).
Unique findings. The majority of studies that sought to replicate a previous study on charitable behavior
attempted to generalize the findings by using different data and conducting a different analysis than the
preceding study (n = 41, 64%). Among these studies, the majority of replication results (n = 25, 61%)
supported the preceding findings. Among the 25 studies that attempted to replicate a previous finding with
either the same data or the same method, a similar proportion produced findings in line with previous
results (n = 14, 56%). The difference is not significant (χ2 = .159, df = 1, p = .690).
Publication process. 48 studies that attempted to replicate preceding findings on charitable giving were
published in journal articles or book chapters (73%), 18 were not: 7 were dissertations, master theses or
bachelor theses; 11 were working papers, preprints, or conference contributions. Among the 39 studies
replicating previous findings that found results in line with the replicated study, a clear majority (n = 29,
74%) was published. Among the 27 studies that found null or negative results, a similar proportion of
studies (n = 19, 70%) was published. The difference is not significant (χ2 = .128, df = 1, p = .721).

What can we learn from replications of research on giving?
We present the reliable findings from replications by the primary mechanism examined in Table 1. Among
the 66 studies on charitable behavior and charitable giving that attempted to replicate previous findings (see
Appendix C) 60 examined at least one of the eight mechanisms. Attention to the mechanisms was unevenly
distributed: material costs and benefits (19 replications), psychological costs and benefits (15), and efficacy
(7) were most likely to be the primary mechanism examined.
In the replications we identified, there is no reliable evidence supporting the regularities as stated in Bekkers
& Wiepking (2011) on awareness of need, altruism, reputation, and values. The lack of reliable evidence for
these mechanisms is not a result of a lack of research. There are plenty of studies, but there are no
replications with similar results as the claims presented in Bekkers & Wiepking (2011) from studies that
measured donations.
Awareness of need. The highly cited “identifiable victim effect” (Jenni & Loewenstein, 1997), suggests that
people find it easier to process information about the needs of a single individual than about the needs of a
large number of individuals. Yet replications in field experiments (Lesner & Rasmussen, 2014;
Thomas‐Walters & Raihani, 2017) do not find evidence for the original claim that opportunities to donate
to a single, identifiable victim produce more donations than opportunities to donate to multiple, statistical
victims. A recent registered report (Vu et al., 2024) also did not find support for the identified victim effect.
In one field experiment, contemplation of needs of victims in prayer did not increase donations (Greenway,
Schnitker, & Shepherd, 2018).

Altruism. Results of replication studies show that most people do not donate for purely altruistic reasons. In
laboratory experiments with very clear instructions to students show that some of them indeed allocate
funds provided to them in ways that maximize the well-being of others (Gandullia et al., 2020) as one would
predict from pure altruism (Ottoni-Wilhelm, Vesterlund, & Xie, 2017). However, in ordinary situations,
most donors do not respond to changes in the need for contributions when nonprofit organizations lose or
gain government grants (De Wit & Bekkers, 2017, 2020). When customers can benefit a charity at no cost to
themselves, they tend to do so, but they do not pay more to yield a larger benefit for the charity (Jung et al.,
2017). A large field experiment in Alaska appealing to benefits for recipients (List et al., 2021) found that
they raised less donations than appeals to the benefits for donors.

10

Reputation. The often cited result from an experiment among a lab study among a small sample of students
that cues of being watched would increase donations (Haley & Fessler, 2005) is not reliable. A field
experiment in Japan found it only when previous donations were larger (Oda & Ichihashi, 2016). It did not
emerge from multiple follow-up experiments among larger numbers of participants, with null-findings in
both online experiments (Bekkers, 2011; Saunders et al., 2016) as well as in field experiments (Ekström,
2012; Jung et al., 2023).
The claim that public recognition increases donations was neither replicated in a large set of field
experiments in US shops (Jung et al, 2023), nor in an online experiment among Facebook users in France
(Denis, Pecheux, & Warlop, 2020). Announcing that in-kind donations of books would be publicized did
not increase donations in a field experiment in the UK (Cotterill, John & Richardson, 2013). Observability
of donations did not increase gifts in a field experiment in cinemas in Germany (Dannenberg,
Johansson-Stenman & Wetzel, 2022). Observability reduced giving among muslims in Morocco (Lamberaa
& Riener, 2015), a finding that the authors attribute to giving norms discouraging public display of charity.
In a registered report replicating a study on donor preferences, participants in an online experiment
preferred charities that would keep donations anonymous (Chan & Feldman, 2025).
Values. There is strong evidence that they are correlated with donation behavior (Bekkers &
Ottoni-Wilhelm, 2016; Nilsson, Erlandsson & Västfjäll, 2020). Yet most field experiments - an exception is
Grodeck & Schoenegger (2023) - have not found positive effects of moral appeals on donation behavior
(Frohly et al., 2026). This pattern of results suggests that people with more prosocial values give more, but
that reminding people of prosocial values does not make them give more. The highly cited finding that
higher social class individuals display lower levels of giving behavior (Piff et al., 2010), potentially as a result
of higher greed, narcissism and lower prosocial values, has met with failures to replicate (Korndörfer, Egloff
& Schmukle, 2015; Stamos et al., 2020).
We did find some evidence supporting the regularities on solicitation, material costs and benefits,
psychological benefits, and efficacy. At the same time, we also found evidence qualifying or in two cases even
consistently rejecting the regularities reported in preceding studies.
Solicitation. Direct solicitations yielded more donations in two large field experiments in the US. At the same
time, the same experiments showed that a quarter of persons did not open the door (Dellavigna, List &
Malmendier, 2012). About one third of consumers in a supermarket in Boston avoided exposure to
fundraisers (Andreoni, Rao & Trachtman, 2017) when they knew they would ask them to donate. The
latter finding was not replicated in Alaska, where entering the supermarket through another door exposed
shoppers to more extreme cold (Trachtman et al., 2015). Opera house donors in Germany gave 42% less
when they knew a second solicitation would be coming (Adena & Huck, 2019). Students in Switzerland
gave 33% less when solicited twice vs once (Grieder, Schmitz & Schubert, 2021). In a field experiment in
Denmark, sending reminders to regular donors increased donations, but also unsubscriptions (Damgaard &
Gravert, 2018). An analysis of panel survey data in the Netherlands (Bekkers, 2026) revealed that conversion
rates of direct solicitations declined strongly in the past 20 years.

Material costs and benefits. For material costs, we found clear support of the regularity that price reductions
encourage donations (Bekkers, 2015; Davis et al., 2005; Zhang, 2023). Tax price studies (Fack & Landais,
2010; Almunia et al., 2020) and experiments (Huck & Rasul, 2011) tend to find smaller estimates than in
previous research (Karlan & List, 2007; Peloza & Steel, 2005). For material benefits, a study in Pakistan that
sought to repeat preceding findings that material benefits would encourage donations actually found the
opposite result: unconditional gifts reduced giving (Naeem & Zaman, 2009, published in 2016). An
experiment on thank you gifts found that they reduced donations among existing donors in the US
(Newman & Shen, 2012; also see Fang & Bian, 2022).
Psychological costs and benefits. In a ‘stress-test’ of preceding findings (Eckel & Grossman, 2003), the framing
as price reductions in the form of matches by third parties rather than as rebates produced more giving in a
replication study (Davis et al., 2005). A field test with earned money among survey participants in the
Netherlands found that matches attract more donations than rebates (Bekkers, 2015). Similar results
emerged from a study in Japan (Sasaki et al., 2022). Finally, a field experiment in Germany among donors to
an opera house (Huck & Rasul, 2011) also found that matches increase donations.
The finding that people give more when they think about one unit first, and how many to give next (Hsee et
al., 2013) found support in two replications (Marcinkiewicz, 2016; Diederich et al., 2022; also see Diederich
et al., 2023; Epperson et al., 2025). Two studies measuring donations in hypothetical situations (Karlsson et
al., 2020; Maier et al., 2023) also found support for the unit asking effect.
A series of replications (Kessler & Meier, 2014) suggest that effects of cognitive load manipulations
(Loewenstein & Small, 2007) on giving are limited to situations in which people are not exposed to other
information.
Effects of mortality salience on giving have been much weaker in independent replications (Bruine de Bruin
& Ulqinako, 2021; Roberts & Maxfield, 2019) than previously reported (Jonas, Schimel, Greenberg &
Pyszczynski, 2002). One study in Norway measuring donation preferences found no effect (Sætrevik &
Sjåstad, 2022).
Field experiments with social information treatments have resulted in much smaller (Van Teunenbroek &
Bekkers, 2019) or no effects (Murphy et al., 2015) than previously reported (Croson & Shang, 2008; Shang
& Croson, 2009).
Efficacy. In line with preceding results, replications have found that donors are responsive to information
about the overhead costs of fundraising organizations, and give more when overhead costs are lower
(Gneezy, Keenan & Gneezy, 2014; Portillo & Stinn, 2018; Charles, Sloan & Schubert, 2020). This finding
also emerges from a study measuring willingness to donate (Hung, Berrett, & Ma, 2025). In addition, one
experiment attempting to replicate findings on seed money - an intervention successfully applied by Boyan
Slat to fund the Ocean Cleanup - found that participants give more often when their donations are more
likely to help reach a fundraising target (Verhaert & Van den Poel, 2012).

12

Discussion
Limitations: what are we missing in this analysis?
To identify the largest possible number of replications, we used Google Scholar because this is the most
comprehensive database of research (Martín-Martín et al., 2021). Compared to other databases such as
Scopus and OpenAlex, Google Scholar includes research reports from a larger variety of sources such as
dissertations, working papers, and preprints. The results indicate that these sources are particularly valuable
because they tend to report less positive results. Relying on less comprehensive databases, we would have
failed to find them, thereby sustaining the biases in the publication process that create the illusion that most
studies support the hypotheses.
Despite the comprehensiveness of our search strategy, the findings reported above are limited to a relatively
small proportion of the entire body of published research on conditions that affect charitable giving. There
will be studies that attempt to repeat previous findings on conditions that are affecting giving behavior that
we did not find with the keywords “charitable behavior” and “charitable giving”.
Furthermore, because our search strategy only produced studies that explicitly mentioned terms related to
replication of research, the results may not fully represent the population of studies that can be considered
to repeat previous findings. Authors repeating previous research may not always present their studies as
such, especially if they work in a field where novelty has a high premium. In addition, when their results are
different from the preceding study, they can anticipate pushback from the preceding study authors if they
were to be invited to review the repeated study findings.
Both limitations imply that our overview is incomplete. It is quite likely that we are missing insights on
giving just because they used slightly different terms. Furthermore, we did not estimate effect sizes in
replications and preceding studies. Our findings on publication bias have sobering implications for
systematic reviews and meta-analyses that rely solely on published research. They should be read with
caution.
In our assessment of replications, we did not quantify effect sizes and compare them with the replicated
studies. Our impression is that effect sizes in replication studies are smaller than in the studies they
replicated, as in mass replication efforts (Open Science Collaboration, 2015; Camerer et al., 2018; Tyner et
al., 2026). Particularly in studies testing the mechanisms of solicitation, material costs, reputation, and
psychological costs and benefits replications found weaker effects than preceding studies.
In the analysis of p-values we were limited by the software we used. We could only analyze studies for which
we could download the full text and to manuscripts that adhered to APA reporting guidelines. Also, we
could not analyze p-values from tables. Ideally we would also have analyzed p-values reported in
unpublished research, and in journals that do not require compliance with APA guidelines. The error rate
among all studies is likely to be higher than among those that we could analyze.

13

Future research: What’s next?
It will not come as a surprise that the first recommendation for future research is that the current study
should be repeated. The current results do not represent the entire field of research on philanthropy. A
complete science of philanthropy cleanup should revisit all research on philanthropy, including studies on
the organization and the effects of philanthropy. The field of research on giving is growing so fast that it is
no longer feasible to collect, summarize, review, and analyze all studies by hand. Future work can use
computational social science tools (Ma et al., 2023) to automate the identification and classification of
replications. The database that we created for the current project by manually coded features of replications
and preceding studies can serve as training data in the development of rule-based algorithms.
Our second recommendation is that researchers should replicate findings on charitable giving more
frequently and consistently, and should actively look for replications of promising findings before acting
upon them in practice. Without the replications covered in the current paper, readers would have continued
to believe that watching eyes, public recognition of donations, identified victims, and thank you gifts
increase donations.
A third recommendation is that future evidence syntheses should compare effect sizes in replications and
preceding studies. In our sample, published studies did not present more positive results than unpublished
studies. However, replications tended to find weaker main effects than the studies they replicated. To learn
which interventions have reliable effects, it is important to have the complete picture. Registered reports are
a solution to this problem. After a stringent peer review of the thoroughness of the design and methods,
authors of registered reports can be sure of publication of their findings, regardless of whether they are
positive, negative, or null. In psychology, only 44% of registered reports provide support for the study
hypothesis, while 96% of traditional studies support the hypothesis (Scheel, Schijen & Lakens, 2021).
A fourth recommendation is that peer review of research on charitable giving should enhance the scrutiny of
statistical results. The field of nonprofit and philanthropy research has recently begun to discuss open
science practices that may help to improve the reliability of knowledge (Bekkers et al., 2025). Nonprofit &
Voluntary Sector Quarterly introduced a data and code review policy for manuscripts reporting results from
experiments, requiring computational verification that the data and code produce the results reported in the
manuscript (Bekkers, 2020). We recommend that journals publishing research based on other sources of
data should also scrutinize numerical data and code before publication. In the disciplines that are more often
sharing data and code, replication rates are higher (Miske et al., 2026). Our analyses of inconsistencies in
p-values indicated that almost half of published studies we could process include errors. Even though
authors, reviewers, editors, and journal production staff can easily identify them, these errors were not
caught in peer review. This kind of error is just one example of a large number of questionable research
practices that pollute the body of knowledge on giving. Using metacheck (DeBruine & Lakens, 2026) at the
preprint stage or in peer review can prevent these errors.

14

Conclusion
We find that both retractions and replications are rare in research on conditions that affect charitable giving.
Furthermore, we find that reporting errors in statistical tests in research on charitable giving are more
common than in other fields, and that they are more often favoring the hypotheses. Even if the errors are
honest mistakes, their prevalence indicates that quality control mechanisms in research on charitable giving
are working less effectively than in other fields.
We answered our question which interventions have reliable effects on charitable giving behavior by a
rigorous removal of all studies for which we have no evidence from replications by other scholars confirming
the preceding results. We found that about 60% of replications yield similar conclusions as preceding studies,
in line with recent replication attempts in the social and behavioral sciences (Tyner et al., 2026). Reductions
of material costs, exposing people to direct solicitations, unit asking, matching, and lower overhead costs
have reliably positive results in independent replications. We find that published findings on reputation and
material benefits in charitable giving do not hold up against the scrutiny of scientific criticism through
replications. Finally, we find no evidence for publication bias in replications. It is good to know that the
science of philanthropy is self-correcting: researchers published replication failures, for instance with respect
to reputation and material benefits. Increasing the scrutiny of research on charitable giving and publishing
replications regardless of results in registered reports will further improve the robustness and reliability of
research on charitable giving.

Identifying inconsistent test statistics and decision errors in research on charitable giving
We fully report the data and methods used to identify inconsistencies in test statistics reported in research on
charitable giving in Littel (2025), https://osf.io/9v5km. We (RB) created a stratified purposive sample of 24
articles, reflecting 2 to 4 key publications for each of the eight mechanisms in charitable giving Bekkers and
Wiepking (2011). We provide the list of seed articles below. Next, we (ML) used the snowball function of
the ROpenalex package (Aria et al., 2024) and identified 7412 papers that cited at least one of the seed
articles. The pdfs of 3702 of these papers could be downloaded automatically by Zotero. We (ML) applied
the R package metacheck (DeBruine & Lakens, 2026) to convert the pdfs into XML files, enabling targeted
search for test statistics and assessment of their consistency with the statchek module (Nuijten & Epskamp,
2024). A limitation of statcheck is that it can only process manuscripts adhering to reporting guidelines for
statistical tests issued by the American Psychological Association. The APA guidelines are used in journals
within the discipline of psychology and related fields. General interest journals such as Science, the
Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS), Nature, Nature Human Behavior, and journals of
disciplines other than psychology, notably economics, public administration, biology, and sociology, do not
require authors to report statistical tests according to the APA guidelines. Articles in these journals are more
likely to report p-values in tables with symbols such as asterisks (*) for various significance levels. Statcheck
cannot process p-values reported in this manner. As a result, statcheck could only analyze a minority of
studies (n = 659, 18%) citing one of the seed articles.
In the pdfs in which statcheck detected precise p-values and test statistics, 306 (46.4%) contained some kind
of erroneous test statistic or p-value. An error was classified as a decision error when it made the difference
between being above or below the common .05 threshold. For example, if the reported p-value is .045 while
the computed p-value is .055, this constitutes a decision error since one of them is statistically significant at
the conventional p = .05 threshold, while the other is not. A decision error was classified as favoring the
study hypothesis when the reported result of the statistical supports the hypothesis while the computed
p-value does not.

Identifying replications
To identify replications, we first used two rule-based search strategies on Google Scholar. To keep the scope
of our efforts within feasible bounds, we started our search for replications in studies indexed under the
keyword “charitable behavior” because it produced the smallest number of results. Within this set, we
followed an inclusive approach. We added the wildcard character * after the stem of replicate, searching for
“replicat*”, so that we would find studies that contained “replicated”, “replicate”, “replicates”, “replicating”,
and “replication”. Having learned from this search that a large proportion of search results merely
mentioned replication but were not actual replications themselves, we used a more restrictive approach for
the keyword “charitable giving”. We added only those replications that had not shown up in the previous
search. Finally, we added replications we knew about that had not yet been identified through the rule-based
searches. Figure A2 gives an overview of our search efforts.

Replications of studies on “charitable behavior”
In November 2024, we (RB) searched Google Scholar for “replicat*” to identify terms related to replication
in research reports on “charitable behavior” from the years 2005-2023, producing 1,213 results. Citation
counts, bibliographic details of replications and further details are on tab “"charitable behavior" replicat” of
https://osf.io/5thj9/files/jxkgs.
We stratified the results by year of publication and screened them in four steps: 1) we manually inspected all
titles and text fragments; 2) for studies that we deemed likely to repeat a previous study we visited the URLs
and read the abstracts; 3) if the abstract suggested that the study repeated a previous study, we downloaded
the full text; 4) we searched the full text and coded the meaning of the sentences containing “replicat*”.
Among the 1,213 search results we identified, 34 results actually replicated a previous study. We removed 2
working papers of which the published version also showed up in the search. In our main analysis we leave
out 7 studies that did not analyze charitable giving behavior as a dependent variable, but instead examined
intentions to give, or used it as an independent variable. Appendix B below lists the 25 unique results with
prefix [A].
Mentions of terms related to replication increased strongly, but the number of actual replications hardly
increased (see Figure A3). We interpret this pattern as a growing frequency of mentions as an increasing
awareness of the importance of replication, and a growing gap with the actual practice of replication.

For each year, RB counted how terms related to replication were mentioned. For 1,179 search results on
“charitable behavior” and “replicat*”, the abstracts and full text search results indicated that the studies did
not repeat a previous study by other scholars. Across all years, about 31% of the search results were ‘internal
replications’, building on previous findings by the same authors (“Study 2 partly replicates the findings of

research, typically in the discussion section (“future research should replicate our findings”). For 6% of the
search results terms related to replication appeared in the literature review section (“Previous replication
attempts have been unsuccessful”). Five percent (5%) of search results were studies that did not measure
charitable giving. Most of the other mentions occurred in sentences offering substantive examples (e.g.,
replication of DNA), discussion of the robustness of study findings (“subsample analyses replicating our

were much more likely to be replications than studies that mentioned terms related to replication elsewhere
in the text.

Appendix D. Further Exploratory Analyses
Small study bias. Studies with a small number of observations can produce strong effects, purely as a result
of chance, while in reality no reliable effect exists. Small samples favor positive results (Loken & Gelman,
2017). Many studies in the social and behavioral sciences are underpowered (Ioannidis, Stanley &
Doucouliagos, 2017; Arel-Bundock et al., 2022; Maxwell, 2004). Replications tend to have larger numbers
of observations (Tyner et al., 2026).
The median number of observations in 59 replicated studies for which we could find this information was
405. We find no evidence that a smaller number of observations is associated with replications finding
different results than preceding studies. In fact, the median number of observations in 36 replications that
produced results in the same direction as the replicated study was 248, lower than the median number of
observations of 573 in 23 replications that produced null findings or results running counter to the
replicated study. This result is due to differences between experiments and other studies. Results of the 32
replications of results from 48 experiments were in line with the replicated study (63%). Of the 18
replications with a non-experimental design, 9 results were in line with previous findings (50%). The
difference is not significant (χ2 = 3.825, df = 1, p = .050).
The median number of observations in 42 replicated experiments was 181, in the 18 other studies the
median number of observations was 3572. Median regression indicates that the number of observations in
replicated studies are not associated with results of replications having similar or different results, when the
difference in study designs is taken into account (coefficient: -117 (SE: 1410.279), t = -0.08, p = .934).
Citation counts. A commonly used proxy for research quality is the number of citations that a study has
received, which partly reflects the impact factor of the journal that published the study. However, more
highly cited research is not more replicable than less cited research (Youyou, Yang, & Uzzi, 2023). In fact,
studies in the Open Science Collaboration (2015) that were not successfully replicated were cited more often
than studies that did replicate well (Serra-Garcia & Gneezy, 2021).
We find no evidence that citation counts differ between replicated studies that replications found similar
results for and replications with null-findings or negative results. The median number of citations in Google
Scholar for 39 previous studies that replications found similar results for was 27; for the 27 other studies the
median number of citations to the previous study was 33. The median number of citations to experiments
(27) was similar to that of studies with non-experimental designs (33). Median regression indicates that the
number of citations for replicated studies is not different for replications having similar or different results,
when the difference in study designs is taken into account (coefficient: 8 (SE: 16.570), t = 0.48, p = .630).
Changes after peer review. A common questionable research practice is ‘harking’, hypothesizing after results
are known (Kerr, 1998). A recent study in Germany suggests that about half of published hypotheses in the
social, behavioral and economic studies analyzing large population survey data are formulated after results
are known (Poppa et al., 2025). A study in the field of management research (O’Boyle, Banks &
Gonzalez-Mulé, 2017) suggests that peer review - especially at journals ranked as ‘more prestigious’ introduces questionable research practices such as adding or deleting data points, covariates, and hypotheses.

In our set of replications, most studies that were available as a working paper or preprint did not change
substantially when they were published. Sometimes dissertation advisors appear as co-authors on journal
