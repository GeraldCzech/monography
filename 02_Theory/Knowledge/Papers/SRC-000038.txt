ABSTRACT

KEYWORDS

This study investigates intention to donate to nonprofit organi­
zations with the perspective of brand heritage, brand trust, and
the temporal focus of donors. The study is conducted on the
long-established Turkish nonprofit organization Kızılay. Data is
gathered via an online survey (n = 473) with judgmental sam­
pling. The findings indicate that the brand heritage of
a nonprofit organization affects brand trust and intention to
donate. Likewise, trust in a nonprofit organization positively
influences donation intention. Brand trust partially mediates
the relationship between brand heritage and intention to
donate. While individuals’ temporal focuses on the past and
future moderate the relationship between brand heritage and
brand trust, as expected, the present focus does not. The find­
ings are expected to contribute to the branding processes and
donation encouraging activities of nonprofit organizations.

Introduction

The global non-governmental organizations (NGOs) and charities market was
valued at approximately US$255,705.9 million in 2019, with a growth of 4.4%
since 2015, with more than 10 million institutions and organizations. The
market is expected to reach a value of US$ 294,313.5 million in 2023 (globe­
newswire.com). Despite concerns that the COVID-19 pandemic would reduce
donations to nonprofit organizations, donations in the US, which is one of the
most prominent actors in the global charities and NGOs market, increased by
10.6%, with a 7.3% increase in the total number of donors in 2020 compared to
the previous year (afpglobal.org/fepreports). According to the Annual Report
on Philanthropy in America (2020), individuals, foundations, and companies
have donated an estimated $471.44 billion to more than 1.54 million charities
in the United States (givingusa.org). Considering that the rate of donors who
regularly subscribe to a donation program every month around the world is
45% and online donation behavior has increased by 12.1%

in 2023. It is also evident that the market has a growth potential due to

Organizations that operate in a wide variety of fields, from health to

expecting any financial benefit in return are defined as nonprofit organiza­

the increasing demand for a broader range of social services in recent years
have led to a significant increase in the need for volunteers. In this regard,
organizations’ brand-oriented efforts are essential for their target audiences.
Napoli (2006) found that the most successful nonprofit organizations tend to
have more brand-oriented focuses. Brand-focused strategies have influenced
the mind-set and tactical decisions of many nonprofit executives (Kylander &
Stone, 2012). Brand heritage is one of these focuses. Creating heritage associa­
tions about a brand in the minds of consumers can evoke positive emotions
about the brand (G. M. Rose et al., 2016). In addition, brand trust (G. M. Rose
et al., 2016), brand credibility (Pecot et al., 2019), and the perceived value of
a brand (Wuestefeld et al., 2012) are positively affected by brand heritage.
Therefore, managers of relatively young organizations should attempt to reach
the appropriate brand heritage by making connections with the organization’s
history. This may include connecting with heritage-rich groups and organiza­
tions, supporting local heritage and cultural events, or matching an organiza­
tion’s heritage with that of its philanthropists, volunteers, or founders (Curran
et al., 2016). In addition, brand story elements such as the date of foundation,
technology, and omni-temporality have been used to create brand associations
by heritage brands (Pfannes et al., 2021).
Considering the importance of fundraising for nonprofit organizations with
a clearer understanding of the role of branding in donor behavior, factors such
as the brand heritage that these organizations have brought from the past to
the present, and the public’s trust in these organizations are critical elements.
Mort et al. (2007) also emphasized that the essence of brand heritage in
nonprofit organizations needs to be understood and requires the attention of
researchers as a field of study.

Brand heritage is an environmental asset that plays a vital role for any
organization, whether for-profit or not (Balmer & Balmer, 2013). On the
other hand, there is no empirical study evaluating this vital role in the
context of nonprofit organizations. The present study takes into account
the dimensions of track record, longevity, core values, and use of symbols,
which are put forward by Urde et al. (2007) as the foundations of brand
heritage for nonprofit organizations together for the first time. Urde et al.
(2007) introduce the concept of brand heritage from a business perspective.
In this study, however, the brand heritage is conceptualized from the donor
perspective of a nonprofit organization. Participants of the study evaluate
a nonprofit organization’s expertise and actions (track record), tradition
(longevity), adherence to its corporate values (core values) and use of
symbols.
The concept of brand heritage has been related to time. For instance, Pecot
et al. (2019) conceptualize brand heritage as a temporal perception.
Individuals may also pay varying amounts of attention to the past, present,
and future. This distinction can be explained by the concept of temporal focus.
Temporal focus, which may influence attitudes and behaviors and develops
during childhood through interaction with classmates, teachers, and parents
(Shipp & Aeon, 2019). In this study, instead of history important to the
identity dimension in Urde et al. (2007)’s model, temporal focus of individuals
is used in explaining brand heritage. The effect of brand heritage assessments
on brand trust and intention to donate is expected to increase as donors’
temporal focus increases. On the contrary, for donors with a low temporal
focus, brand heritage associations of a nonprofit organization will be of
relatively little value.
Brand trust’s role as mediator and (past and future) temporal focus’ role as
moderator within the context of brand heritage in nonprofit organizations are
examined for the first time in this study. In this regard, the present study is
expected to contribute to the literature on brand heritage and nonprofit
organizations.

Theoretical background and hypotheses development
Brand heritage

The idea that brands have heritage is based on the initial work of Smith and
Steadman (1982), with the use of the historical perspective as a theme that
supports the brand’s image in advertisements (Hudson & Balmer, 2011).
Brand heritage is characterized as a dimension of brand identity (Aaker,
2010). A brand having a performance that meets the past expectations of
customers, its longevity, its continuity, and consistency over time, the use of
symbols reflecting its past, and especially the organizational belief that history

is important for brand identity constitute the foundations of brand heritage
(Urde et al., 2007).
However, these five pillars that explain the concept of brand heritage receive
criticism for only reflecting the organization’s perspective. Perspectives of
managers, shareholders and customers on brand heritage may vary. For this
reason, it is recommended to take customers or stakeholders into considera­
tion while creating brand heritage communication studies (Rindell et al.,
2015).
Components of the brand heritage model put forward by Urde et al.
(2007), have been employed in brand heritage studies that consider con­
sumer perceptions. In such studies, track record (Merchant & Rose, 2013;
G. M. Rose et al., 2016; M. Rose et al., 2017), longevity (Merchant & Rose,
2013; Pecot et al., 2019; G. M. Rose et al., 2016; M. Rose et al., 2017) and
use-of-symbols (M. Rose et al., 2017) dimensions have been evaluated by
customers. Considering the uses in previous customer-focused studies, in
this study, brand heritage can be defined as consumers’ evaluation of the
track record, longevity, use of symbols, and core values of a brand. Akin to
previous studies, in this study, four facets of the brand heritage are exam­
ined from a consumer perspective.
Brand heritage can be used to create connotations by having a strong pointof-difference that can distinguish the brand from competing brands (Keller,
2013). Since competitors cannot easily imitate, using brand heritage as
a positioning tool can provide companies with a competitive advantage against
emerging brands. Furthermore, it increases the brand’s value for customers
and strengthens customer relations by providing origins and originality. The
strong heritage of a brand makes it easier for the business to find new employ­
ees and retain existing ones (Curran, 2017).
Customers’ confidence in the future weakens during periods of economic
hardship. Under such unpredictable and harsh conditions, consumers look for
products to feel safe. The use of brand heritage responds the consumers’ search
for safety (Hakala et al., 2011). Brands with high levels of heritage perception
increase customers’ perceptions of quality and allow both existing and new
companies in the market to set higher prices (Pecot et al., 2018).
Brand heritage creates a link between the past, present, and future.
Customers’ perceptions of a brand’s heritage could be examined in three
temporal dimensions. Brand stability, longevity, and adaptability form the
basis of perceived heritage (Pecot et al., 2019). Marketing management that
wants to utilize brand heritage can focus on one of these dimensions in line
with the aims of the business. For example, a brand that wants to be perceived
as innovative by its customers may attach significance to adaptability. On the
other hand, a brand that has recently introduced many innovations to its
customers can benefit from these innovations in the long term by focusing on
stability (Pecot et al., 2019).

However, the influence of the brand heritage on consumer behavior can
vary. It is shown that brand heritage has a weaker effect on the intention to
purchase products in individuals who seek personal growth and pleasure,
place little value on safety, and avoid focusing on negative consequences
(G. M. Rose et al., 2016). Therefore, brand heritage in brand positioning is
expected to positively affect the purchasing behavior of more risk-averse
individuals.
Although using a brand as a heritage object generally has positive results,
some customers feel skeptical about it and may even resist it (Mencarelli et al.,
2020). Furthermore, heritage brands can be perceived as old-fashioned in
products aimed at children, adolescents, and young adults. In durable con­
sumer goods and high-technology product categories, some consumers may
perceive heritage brands as incompetent and outdated in innovation (Aaker,
2010).
Research on the phenomenon of brand heritage in nonprofit organizations
(Curran et al., 2016) appears to be quite limited. Therefore, this study inves­
tigates brand heritage in the nonprofit organizations context by donors’
perspective. More specifically, this research examines not the brand heritage
of nonprofit organizations but rather the donors’ perceptions of the brand
heritage of nonprofit organizations.
Brand trust

In terms of brands, the phenomenon of trust can be based on the theory of
brand personality. Brands having various personality traits like individuals can
lead consumers to trust specific brand clusters, similar to how they trust
people in their daily lives (Belaid & Temessek Behi, 2011). Since trust between
parties is an essential phenomenon from the perspective of relationship
marketing, the first studies on trust in the field of marketing focused on this
area. The ultimate goal in marketing is to establish a strong bond between
customers and the brand. Brand trust is generally thought to play a critical role
in creating such a bond in the relationship (Delgado‐Ballester & Luis
Munuera‐Alemán, 2001; Morgan & Hunt, 1994). Over time, the concept of
brand trust has been examined in terms of consumers and businesses. These
studies discuss several constructs to create brand trust, such as brand image,
customer satisfaction, and perceived quality. Additionally, studies have
revealed that brand trust positively affects customer loyalty, customer accep­
tance of brand extensions, and brand equity (Koschate-Fischer & Gartner,
2015).
One of the most cited definitions of brand trust was made by Chaudhuri
and Holbrook (2001). According to this definition, brand trust is “the will­
ingness of the average consumer to trust a brand’s ability to perform the specified
functions” (Chaudhuri & Holbrook, 2001, p. 82). Munuera-Aleman et al.

(2003) define brand trust as “Strong expectations about the reliability and
intentions of the brand in situations that involve risk for the consumer”
(Munuera-Aleman et al., 2003, p. 37). This definition conceptualizes brand
trust as a phenomenon that consists of two dimensions: brand credibility and
brand intentions. Brand credibility is based on consumers’ belief that the
brand will deliver on its value proposition. On the other hand, brand inten­
tions are based on the belief that the brand will protect the consumer’s
interests when unexpected problems arise during the consumption of the
product.
From a nonprofit organization perspective, building trust with donors is
essential. Trust is the strongest indicator of the relationship between the
nonprofit organization and the donor (Wiggill, 2014). However, a large num­
ber of studies have examined the relationship between trust and giving beha­
viors by donors, and the results have varied substantially. A meta-analysis
study showed that organizational and sectoral trust were more strongly asso­
ciated with giving than generalized or institutional trust (Chapman et al.,
2021). Donations are made with a broad promise created by the mission and
brand of nonprofit organizations instead of their performance in a particular
activity (Bryce, 2007). Due to the fact that the provided services are intangible
and the nonprofit organization’s activities are generally not evaluated by the
donors with objective criteria requires donors to trust the promises made by
the organization (Sargeant et al., 2006). Brand trust in nonprofit organizations
is affected by individual-related (attitude toward philanthropy) and contextual
(service quality) variables (Sargeant & Lee, 2002). An organization’s size,
importance to the nation, reputation, transparency in reporting, and aware­
ness increase trust in the organization (Furneaux & Wymer, 2015). Studies
conducted on trademarks reveal that brand heritage positively affects brand
trust (G. M. Rose et al., 2016; Wiedmann et al., 2011; Zeren & Kara, 2020).
Hypothesis 1 is hypothesized with the assumption that such a relationship may
also be valid in nonprofit organizations.

H1: Brand heritage positively affects brand trust in nonprofit organizations.

Intention to donate

Donations and aid are seen to have an important place among nonprofit
organizations’ sources of income. For this reason, nonprofit organizations
compete with each other in order to collect donations from donors and ensure
the sustainability of the business (Omura & Forster, 2014).
Donation behavior emerges depending on the individual characteris­
tics of the donors and the activities of nonprofit organizations.

Donation behavior is associated with many personal factors, such as
showing empathy, having warm relationships with others (Webb &
Wong, 2014), religiousness, and self-construal (Skarmeas et al., 2011).
Additionally, nonprofit organizations informing donors about their
activities, providing entertaining content, and being credible can posi­
tively affect donor behavior (Florenthal et al., 2020). Reporting to the
donors that their donations are used for those in need creates positive
feelings in them and increases their intention to donate again in the
future (Merchant et al., 2010).
Studies on brand trust and purchase intention (Alif Fianto et al., 2014;
Becerra & Badrinarayanan, 2013; Chae et al., 2020) reveal the positive effect of
brand trust on intention. With respect to nonprofit organizations, brand trust
is also an essential prerequisite for developing strong brands. Indeed, building
trust that the nonprofit uses donations for its cause is one of the most
important factors affecting donation behavior (Aytaç & Çarkoğlu, 2019).
Generally, donors have to rely on the nonprofit organization due to the
intangibility of the service provided by the organization and donors’ lack of
objective evaluations regarding whether or not donations will be used for the
cause (Sargeant & Lee, 2004). Accordingly, trust in a nonprofit organization
positively affects individuals’ intention to donate (Alhidari et al., 2018; Bilgin
& Kethüda, 2022; Goktas et al., 2019; Ha et al., 2022; Schultz et al., 2019).
Hypothesis 2 is hypothesized based on previous findings in the literature.

H2: Brand trust in nonprofit organizations positively affects the intention to
donate to these organizations.
Brand heritage can have a positive effect on consumers’ intention to purchase
products (G. M. Rose et al., 2016; Yaqub et al., 2020). In terms of nonprofit
organizations, heritage brands that are perceived to have carried out their past
activities properly and successfully (track-record dimension) are more likely to
raise donations. Moreover, in donor perspective use of symbol dimension of
brand heritage is about brand knowledge. An increase in the salience of
a nonprofit organization’s brand positively affects donation intention
(Gregory et al., 2020). In addition, the longevity of a nonprofit might create
nostalgic associations in the memories of donors. Nostalgia is shown to
positively influence donation intention (Ford & Merchant, 2010). Hence, the
present study examines the effect of nonprofit organizations’ brand heritage
on intention to donate. Hypothesis 3 is hypothesized accordingly.

H3: The brand heritage of nonprofit organizations positively affects indivi­
duals’ intention to donate.

According to the Brand Resonance Model, brand heritage is one of the brand
imagery associations that can be used by brands. Such associations might
influence consumer judgments (such as credibility) and feelings regarding
brand. Eventually, consumers form diverse types of loyalty to the brand.
With regard to nonprofit organizations, stronger heritage associations gener­
ate trust. In addition to having a direct influence on intention to donate, brand
heritage might indirectly increase donation intention via brand trust. A study
on airline companies (Zeren & Kara, 2020) revealed that brand heritage affects
brand purchase intention through brand trust and brand loyalty. The present
study examines the mediating role of brand trust in brand heritage’s effect on
the intention to donate to nonprofit organizations. Hypothesis 4 is hypothe­
sized accordingly.

H4: In nonprofit organizations, brand trust mediates the effect of brand
heritage on the intention to donate.

Temporal focus

Individuals differ in their attention to the past, future, and present. Some
people might focus on the memories and experiences they had in the past. On
the other hand, some individuals may concentrate on what they will do in the
future. Individuals’ temporal perspective has been conceptualized in various
studies. One of the most frequently used conceptualizations on this subject has
been the Zimbardo Time Perspective Inventory (ZTPI). According to
Zimbardo and Boyd (1999), individuals’ time perspective can be evaluated in
five dimensions (past-negative, past-positive, present-hedonistic, presentfatalistic, future). Past-negative items are about the individual’s negative
evaluations of the past. In contrast, the past-positive factor mainly focuses
on the positive and nostalgic past. The present-hedonistic dimension indicates
a risk-taking attitude toward time and life. The present-fatalistic items, how­
ever, are related to a helpless and pessimistic attitude toward the future and
life. The future factor is described by the planning and achievement of future
objectives.
Zimbardo and Boyd (1999) utilize the expression “temporal orienta­
tion” to describe individual viewpoints on time. Moreover, the temporal
orientation approach suggests that individuals have a dominant perspec­
tive of time. Akin to temporal orientation, Shipp et al. (2009) propose the
concept of temporal focus. The concept of temporal focus is defined as
“the allocation of attention to the past, present and future” (Shipp et al.,
2009, p. 2). According to Shipp et al. (2009), individuals may have more
than one-time frame of focus and could shift their attention between

different time periods. At one extreme, individuals who focus highly on
all three time periods are called “hypertemporal,” whereas those who
don’t focus highly on any time are called “atemporal” (Shipp et al.,
2009, p. 7).
The concept of temporal focus has been investigated under employability
(Cerdin et al., 2020), managerial psychology (Tuncdogan & Dogan, 2019),
consumer behavior (Cruz-Cárdenas et al., 2021; Tan et al., 2019) and corpo­
rate philanthropy (Nguyen et al., 2021). Individual temporal focus might affect
how information is encoded and retrieved from memory, how goals are used,
and how expectations are formed, among other cognitive processes (Zimbardo
& Boyd, 1999). Previous studies have revealed that the level of temporal focus
may have an influence on an individual’s attitude, behavior, and decisionmaking processes (Shipp et al., 2009). For example, the features that customers
desire in a business loyalty program may differ depending on their temporal
orientation. While past-focused consumers value traditions and rituals, pre­
sent-focused individuals seek immediate benefits from loyalty programs
(Hendler et al., 2022).
When it comes to the link between nostalgia, temporal focus, and brand
heritage, Merchant and Rose (2013) state that nostalgia is one of the several
antecedents of brand heritage, and vicarious nostalgia impacts brand heritage,
leading to stronger brand attachment. Several conceptualizations of brand
heritage (Rindell et al., 2015; Urde et al., 2007) emphasize that brand heritage
is related not only to the past but also to the present and future of a brand’s
identity. Rindell et al. (2015) further extend the concept of brand heritage with
a consumer perspective and stress the importance of customers’ awareness of
time spans and key time periods regarding a company’s brand heritage. This
perspective is company-specific and focuses mainly on the past. Temporal
focus, however, is a more general personality characteristic and deals with
individuals’ past, present, and future focus.
One of the dimensions mentioned in Urde et al. (2007)’s corporate brand
heritage model is history important to identity. Namely, a heritage brand’s past
actions and values influence its present and future. The authors further
elaborate on this notion and give the example of a Swiss watch company
that uses the company’s chronological history for advertising purposes. In this
paper, consumer-oriented perspective on brand heritage uses individual tem­
poral focus instead of the concept of history important to identity.
Construal Level Theory posits that when the psychological distance between
an entity and an individual increases, the individual tends to form more
abstract construals regarding the entity (Trope & Liberman, 2010).
Conversely, a decrease in the conceptual level results in more concrete and
detailed thinking by the individual about the entity. Construal Level Theory
proposes that psychological distance might consist of temporal distance,
spatial distance, social distance, and hypothetical distance. In comparison to

low-level ones, high-level construals are more abstract, coherent and super­
ordinate representations (Trope & Liberman, 2010).
By and large, studies indicate that present-focus and orientation is mostly
related with short-term actions and decisions. Present-oriented consumers
react discounts more positively than future-oriented consumers. That is, the
effect of discount size on perceived product quality, perceived financial risk
and purchase intention is moderated by individual temporal orientation
(Mukherjee et al., 2017). In addition, future-focused customers like advertise­
ments, which display forthcoming goods and emphasize key product features.
Present-focused consumers, however, favor near-future advertisements that
emphasize secondary product features (Martin et al., 2009). Present-oriented
customers have a stronger favorable purchase intention and brand attitude
when the cause-related marketing advertisement incorporates the proximal
rather than distant company response (Tangari et al., 2010). However, such
study indicates no significant change for future-oriented consumers. Similarly
in a social media advertisement study, it is shown that using low emotionally
intense emojis (low construal level) may result in higher purchase intentions
for present-oriented consumers (Huang et al., 2022). The influence of tem­
poral distance (short-term vs long term) in ad also is shown to have varying
degrees of buying intention regarding smart glasses between present and
future oriented individuals (Balbo & Jeannot, 2015). Compared with presentoriented consumers, future-oriented consumers are more likely to positively
evaluate energy-efficient products when savings are positioned in the far
future rather than the near future (Tangari & Smith, 2012).
Using brand heritage elements in advertisements may give rise to an
increase in construal level of the brand (Scarpi, 2021). Therefore, authors
anticipate no influence of present focus on the effect of brand heritage. On
the other hand, Pecot et al. (2018) suggest that when evaluating a branded
product, the consumer’s past focus may be important to the impact of brand
heritage. Indeed, their study confirms that consumer past focus might play
a moderating role in the influence of brand heritage on brand consistency.
Future orientation is shown to influence positively social behaviors. For
instance, future orientation plays a moderator role on the link between
individual cultural orientation and prosocial giving (Hong & Byun, 2020).
Future orientation also decrease the influence of social exclusion on prosocial
behavior (Baorui et al., 2022). According to Meyer and Orth (2023), however,
since the phenomenon of brand heritage is mostly related to the past, its effect
on attitudes and behavior may weaken when individuals’ future focus
increases. By using the stereotype content model, it is shown that the influence
of brand heritage on warmth and competence is negatively moderated by
individual future focus for wine and hotel brands (Meyer & Orth, 2023). In
the context of nonprofit brands, brand heritage may provide positive cues for
donors regarding the nonprofit’s success in dealing with unexpected future

events such as earthquakes and floods. As a result, drawing on the previous
literature and the construal level theory, past and future temporal focus is
thought to strengthen the effect of brand heritage on brand trust. Present
focus, however is anticipated to have no influence on the relationship between
brand heritage and brand trust. The following hypotheses are developed
accordingly:

H5: Individuals’ past focus moderates positively the relationship between
brand heritage and brand trust.
H6: Individuals’ future focus moderates positively the relationship between
brand heritage and brand trust
H7: Individuals’ present focus does not moderate positively the relationship
between brand heritage and brand trust.

Methodology
Design, sampling, and data collection

In order to test the hypotheses, a cross-sectional research design is used. Data
is collected regarding Kızılay (Türkiye Red Crescent) organization using the
judgmental sampling method via an online questionnaire. Kızılay is a Türkiyebased nonprofit organization that has been active since 1868. Kızılay’s mission
is defined as taking a proactive role in providing assistance to the needy and
the vulnerable, improving cooperation in society, providing safe blood, and
reducing vulnerability during disasters and in ordinary times (www.kızılay.
org.tr). Considerations such as the fact that the organization has a relatively
long and deep-rooted history, leading it to be more prone to evaluations in
terms of brand heritage among the participants, are influential for choosing
the Kızılay case for the study. For instance, Kızılay Week is celebrated between
October 29 and November 4 in schools in Türkiye. During this period,
students are informed in schools about the history and activities of Kızılay.
Covariance-based structural equation modeling and confirmatory factor
analyses are carried out in order to test the research hypotheses and evaluate
the validity of measures. A minimum number of 10 cases is suggested per
indicator to carry out such analyses (Kline, 2011). The questionnaire included
43 scale items. As a result, data is collected with the aim of exceeding 430.
Data is gathered via online questionnaires created in Google forms. The
survey form was shared on the researchers’ social media accounts. The data
collection process continued throughout June and July 2021. For ethical

Measures

At the beginning of the questionnaire, respondents of the study are asked for
their consent. Then, the opinions of respondents about Kızılay are asked.
Kızılay’s track record, longevity, core values, use of symbols, and brand trust
are respectively evaluated by respondents. In the next part of the question­
naire, respondents are asked about their intention to donate to Kızılay. Then,
the temporal focus of the participants is measured. Finally, questions about the
demographic profiles of respondents are given. The scales used to measure the
variables are shown in Table 2. Two bilingual experts translate all items into
Turkish and then back-translated into English. After this procedure, a pilot
study is conducted on 30 respondents to detect any possible wording or
measurement problems.

Discussion and conclusion

The findings of this study reveal that within the context of nonprofit organiza­
tions, brand heritage affects brand trust and intention to donate, and brand
trust affects intention to donate. These findings are consistent with the current
studies in the literature. The findings that brand heritage positively affects
brand trust (G. M. Rose et al., 2016; Wiedmann et al., 2011; Zeren & Kara,
2020) are also supported within the scope of nonprofit organizations in this
study. Similarly, brand heritage is also shown to affect the intention to donate
to nonprofit organizations, as seen in studies (G. M. Rose et al., 2016; Yaqub
et al., 2020) that demonstrate how brand heritage affects purchase intention
for brands.
Not every brand that has a narrative on something from the past can have
a heritage value. A deep bond of trust should be established with the con­
sumers for this (Balmer & Balmer, 2013). This way, trust is critical for a brand
with brand heritage to create an intention to donate. The results obtained in
this study reveal that brand trust has a partially mediating effect on the
relationship between brand heritage and intention to donate. Ha et al.
(2022) revealed that brand-related factors (such as brand trust, brand famil­
iarity etc.) have significant effects on behavioral intention. They also demon­
strated that brand trust can be seen as a mediating variable in the context of
branding process for nonprofit organizations. Therefore, nonprofit organiza­
tion’s efforts to create and maintain brand trust will be vital to influence
intention to donate based on brand heritage. Because brand trust is
a variable that convey the effect of brand heritage on the intention to donate.
On the other hand, stronger heritage associations may generate and
strengthen the trust. Thus, nonprofit organizations were suggested to consider
utilizing brand heritage to enhance trust and thereby increase consumers’
intention to donate.

Pecot et al. (2019) discussed the scope of brand heritage as a purely
temporal concept based on a set of brand associations. Heritage is an
element that carries something from the past to the present and later
shapes future expectations (Rindell et al., 2014). In this respect, the tem­
poral focus of consumers is important in terms of brand heritage. In this
paper, Urde et al. (2007)’s conceptualization of brand heritage is modified
by using temporal focus instead of history important to identity. Temporal
focus is a variable that significantly influences different types of consumer
responses, such as brand preference and willingness to pay under various
conditions (Tan et al., 2019). Therefore, temporal focus can be evaluated as
a moderating variable in terms of brand heritage and heritage-dependent
variables. Regarding the results obtained in this study, while the indivi­
duals’ temporal focuses on past and future have a significant effect on the
relationship between brand heritage and brand trust, as expected, the focus
on present does not. There are two past and two future groups, categorized
as low and high. In the high past focus group, the relationship between
brand heritage and brand trust appears to be stronger. Similarly, the
relationship between brand heritage and brand trust seems to be more
substantial in the high future focus group. In summary, the findings of
this study revealed that consumers’ temporal focuses can be utilized to
develop relationship between brand heritage and brand trust. Therefore,
nonprofit organizations that want to increase and strengthen brand trust
through their brand heritage can target consumer profiles with a high past
and high future temporal focuses. It can be suggested that nonprofit
organizations consider using messages focused on past and future events
to evoke the link between brand heritage and brand trust. For example,
Kızılay’s longevity might create nostalgic associations in the memories of
donors. So, Kızılay may utilize messages focused on past via longevity to
strengthen the link between brand heritage and brand trust. On the other
hand, as Hendler et al. (2022) stated that past-focused consumers value
traditions and rituals. If nonprofit organizations such as Kızılay, try to
create shared traditions and rituals among communities, it could be ben­
eficial for revealing the connection between brand heritage and brand trust.
For future-focurs consumers, it is suggested that using advertisements
which display forthcoming or planning goods and services or how non­
profit organization (will) dealing with unexpected future events such as
earthquakes, floods etc.
This study is expected to help nonprofit organizations determine whether
they have brand heritage and make efforts to strengthen their brand heritage,
create more resources, lead to more active participation by the volunteers
(through donations as well as other volunteering activities), improve organi­
zational performance, and develop other strategies that might lead to achiev­
ing competitive advantages.

Limitations and future research

Firstly, the study is conducted with participants reached online through
judgmental sampling. It limits the generalizability of the findings.
Furthermore, survey data is gathered partially from the members of
Kızılay, specifically from the Kızılay brand, instead of several nonprofit
organizations. Therefore, the findings should not be seen as clear evi­
dence of any causal relationships but rather as an advancement of the
theoretical understanding of brand heritage, particularly in nonprofit
organizations.
Secondly, other brand heritage related factors, such as customers’ percep­
tions of quality, which might have a moderation effect between brand heritage
and intention to donate, can be considered in the future.
Lastly, the participants are offered no incentives due to financial constraints,
leading to low participant interest in the survey. Therefore, future studies may
provide participants with incentives and adopt research designs that allow for
experimental designs or qualitative research instead of collecting data through
surveys.
