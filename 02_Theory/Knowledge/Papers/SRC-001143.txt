Bhati, A., & McDonnell, D. (2024). Message Content and Diversity as an Effective 
Nonprofit Fundraising Strategy on Social Media. Nonprofit and Voluntary Sector 
Quarterly, 0(0). https://doi.org/10.1177/08997640241303920

Message content and diversity as an effective nonprofit fundraising strategy on social media 

Abstract  

This article explores the relationship between solicitation messages posted by nonprofit 

organizations on their Facebook profile and attracting donations during online fundraising 

events. Recent studies suggest that certain types of messages are more effective at spurring 

donors to give, but the evidence base is founded mainly on experimental designs. We use 

observational data for 752 nonprofits that participated in one or both of the Omaha Gives 

fundraising events in 2015 and 2020. Using the total amount of money donated to each 

organization as our outcome, we find nonprofits that post more diverse solicitation messages 

raise more money during online fundraising events. Consistent with previous studies, social 

media network size, activity, and engagement are once again important factors in explaining 

fundraising success. Our results provide deeper insights into the nuances of social media 

strategies for attracting donors and raising funds. 

Keywords  

Fundraising, social media, online giving, giving days, Facebook

Introduction 

It is common knowledge that nonprofit organizations harness the power of social media 

platforms to connect with potential donors, share their work, recruit volunteers, and raise 

donations (Campbell et al., 2014; Guo & Saxton, 2014, 2020; Lovejoy & Saxton, 2012; Waters 

et al., 2009; Waters & Jamal, 2011). In the past decade, fundraising using social media has been 

one of the fastest ways of collecting donations from individuals globally (Global Trends, 2020). 

Similarly, academic studies have found evidence that an active social media presence - such as 

nonprofits posting requests for donations on Facebook - can lead to peer pressure among 

“friends” of a donor or followers of the nonprofit and acts as a motivation for them to give (Bhati 

& McDonnell, 2020; Castillo et al., 2014; Harris et al., 2021; Lacetera et al., 2016; Saxton & 

Wang, 2014).  

However, still there is a gap in our understanding about what type of social media activities, and 

soliticiation requests in particular, are most successful at generating donations (Saxton & Waters, 

2014; Xiao et al., 2022). For instance, studies have shown that not all messages are equal in 

attracting donations through traditional fundraising practices (Huang et al., 2016; Guidry et al., 

2014; Saxton & Waters, 2014). This leaves important questions unanswered: what type of 

messaging on social media generates the highest level of donations? Should nonprofits adopt a 

diverse or consistent approach to their solicitation messages when raising funds through social 

media platforms? To answer these questions, we use data from 752  nonprofits that participated 

in the Omaha Gives online fundraising eventsin 2015 and 2020. We link administrative data on 

these organisations, including total donations received, to their social media usage, in particular 

their Facebook posts in relation to Omaha Gives in the lead-up to the events. We find a 

2 

 
 
moderate, statistically significant relationship between message type diversity and fundraising 

success: nonprofits that post a diverse range of messages on social media – e.g., thanking donors 

for their support, sharing information about the organization’s mission, demonstrating impact on 

beneficiaries – to solicit donations raise more in funding than organizations that post 

homogeneous solicitation messages. We also find, compared to posts aimed at general 

solicitation, posts focused on gratitude or goal attainment are associated with increased 

donations. Finally, social media network size, activity, and engagement are once again important 

factors in explaining fundraising success. Our study is one of the first to show the influence of 

message type and diversity on fundraising success during giving days using observational data 

on a large sample of nonprofit organizations. 

Literature Review 

Since the early 2000s, nonprofit organizations have evolved in their usage of Information 

Technology from an era where only a handful of organizations had a website and communication 

was mainly “one-way” (Hackler & Saxton, 2007; Kang & Norton, 2004), to more reciprocal 

(“two-way”) engagement (Bortree & Seltzer, 2009; Briones et al., 2011; Lovejoy & Saxton, 

2012). As nonprofits continue to use social media platforms, scholars have focused on how 

nonprofits are using them to engage with stakeholders. Lovejoy & Saxton (2012) identified a 

three-tier “hierarchy of engagement”: the first tier as providing information about the 

organization or cause, followed by developing a community, and finally calling on followers to 

act on behalf of the organization. Subsequent studies have implemented this framework to 

advance the understanding of social media usage by nonprofits in diverse contexts (Bürger, 

2015; Campbell & Lambright, 2020; Svensson et al., 2015; Zhou & Pan, 2016). Overall, these 

3 

 
 
studies argue a majority of organizations still use social media platforms primarily for 

disseminating information, with limited communication and engagement focused on community 

building or calls to action.  

Another important stream of research examines the relationship between social media usage, 

such as the frequency of posting messages and subsequent engagement with followers, and 

attracting donors and donations (Bhati & McDonnell, 2020; Elvira-Lorilla et al., 2023; Enjolras, 

2023; Lee, 2021; Olinski & Szamrowski, 2021; Saxton & Wang, 2014; Suh, 2022; Sun & 

Asencio, 2019). Although these studies provide valuable insights about the relationship between 

social media usage and fundraising, they do examine which type of fundraising messages are 

most successful at generating support. This question is essential as nonprofits are fiercely 

competing to get attention from potential donors (Guo & Saxton, 2018).  

A small number of studies examie whether the content of social media messagesis associated 

with attracting donations. In an experimental study, Baek & Yoon (2022) found that participants 

expressed a higher intention to give when fundraising appeals on social media triggered a 

particular emotion. For example, participants show higher intention to give when they are shown 

ads focusing on egoistic benefits (i.e., benefits to the donor). Similar results were found by 

Daniel, Curley & Walk (2024) examing social media fundraising messages. They found “donors 

most positively responded to the philanthropic or altruistic attempts to raise funds” (p. 79). In 

another experimental study using participants from Amazon MTurk, Xiao et al. (2022) found 

that messages containing details on outcomes produced by donations elicited higher intention to 

donate. These studies suggest that message content influences prosocial behavior in the relation 

4 

 
 
 
of social media. However, the research on this topic is nascent and founded on experimental 

studies and/or small samples. Our study addresses this gap by using observational data on 

nonprofit’s social media messages and their subsequent success during online fundraising events.  

Data and Method 

Our data consists of 983 observations for 752 nonprofits that participated in one or both Omaha 

Gives fundraising events in 2015 and 2020. 231 of these 752 organisations participated in both 

events (2015 and 2020) and 521 nonprofits participated in one Omaha Gives. Omaha Gives was 

a 24-hour fully online, community-wide event in the Omaha Metro area organized by Omaha 

Community Foundation (OCF). The event took place for eight consecutive years between 2013 

and 2020 and raised over $58 million for local nonprofits (OCF, 2021). In Omaha Gives 2015 

and 2020, a total of 1,720 nonprofits participated (2015 – 704 and 2020 – 1,016). However, only 

752 organizations either received any donations or had a social media presence that we could 

measure. All participating organizations created a public profile at omahagives.org where donors 

can donate as little as $10 through the website. Throughout the 24 hours, the total amount raised 

is publically accessible using the Omaha Gives portal, and participating nonprofits can 

continuously appeal to potential donors through their social media platforms.  

The data underpinning this study records total donations received by the organization, its 

program service area and budget size, as well as Facebook information on the number of likes, 

posts, shares, and message content and diversity. While the social media landscape evolves at 

pace, Facebook remains an important platform for nonprofits (Carboni & Maxwell, 2015; 

Campbell & Lambright, 2020). We collect social media data using a three-stage process. First, 

5 

 
 
 
we collected all  Facebook posts by each organization from their profile in the months leading up 

to each event (including the day it took place). Second, we filter posts to those containing terms 

related to Omaha Gives, such as “Omaha Gives,” “omahagives,” “#omahagives,” “omaha gives,” 

“omahagives!,” and “May 20.” Third, Facebook posts were hand coded into distinct message 

types drawn from previous literature on fundraising messaging (Table 1). Each post was assigned 

a single code based on the message types listed in Table 1; in instances where a post appeared to 

fit multiple categories, codes were assigned after considering the post’s primary focus (Lovejoy 

& Saxton, 2012; Saxton & Waters, 2014). Further, any discrepancies between coding were 

discussed and code rules were refined until an agreement was reached by the researchers.

Analytical Approach 

Our dependent variable is the total donations ($) raised by a participating nonprofit during an 

Omaha Gives event. Drawing on  past studies, we operationalize ten independent variables: five 

relating to organizational characteristics and five concerning social media use. Budget size is 

7 

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
measured using an ordinal variable with three categories: (1) budget size less than $250,000 

(Small); (2) budget between $250,000 - $999,999 (Medium); and (3) budget more than $999,999 

(Large). Organization age is calculated by subtracting year of each Omaha Gives event from the 

nonprofit’s year of formation. Prior participation in Omaha Gives is measured as a binary 

categorical variable - 1 if the organization participated in past Omaha Gives and 0 if it is its first 

time. Program service area is a nominal variable with nine categories representing areas of 

activity, including Human Services, Religious, Education, Arts & Culture, etc. Finally, we 

employ a binary variable to measure the difference in donations for the 2015 and 2020 events (1 

= donations raised during 2020, 0 = donations raised during 2015).  

We specify three numeric measures of social media usage: (1) total likes on nonprofits’s 

Facebook page (network size), (2) total number of posts related to Omaha Gives (network 

activity), and (3) total shares of posts relating to Omaha Gives by the organization’s Facebook 

followers (network engagement). We operationalize fundraising message content as a categorical 

measure capturing the most common (modal) Facebook post type based on our classification: 

Beneficiary, Goal, Gratitude, Mission, Social Media,  Solicitation, and Tie (which captures 

instances where a nonprofit has more than one most common post type e.g., same number of 

Mission and Gratitude posts). For example, a nonprofit has total of 8 Facebook posts, 4 were 

coded as Gratitude (50 percent of total posts), 2 were coded as Goal, 1 was coded as Mission and 

1 was coded as Social Media; in this instance Gratitude is the most common post type. Finally, 

we measure fundraising message diversity as a continuous variable ranging between 0 (very 

concentrated post types) and 1 (very diverse post types). For example, a nonprofit that only posts 

Solicitation messages will have a score of 0, while an organization that posts an equal number of 

all post types will have a score of 1. 

8 

 
We employ ordinary least squares (OLS) regression with cluster-robust standard errors on a log-

transformed version of total donations: the transformation ensures that our regression 

coefficients fall within the valid range of the dependent variable (i.e., nonzero, positive values 

for total donations). Cluster-robust OLS, which adjusts statistical significance tests in light of 

some organisations appearing more than once in the data, is preferred to panel data models with 

organisation fixed-effects as only 231 of 752 organisations provide observations for more than a 

single year and thus there is little variation over time in the data. Fixed-effects models only 

examine within-unit variation in the outcome; in this instance the model coeficients could only 

be interpreted in terms of how organisations change over time, not differences between them. We 

estimate our models in an iterative process, adding in the organizational characteristic variables, 

followed by our three numeric measures of Facebook use, and finally, our measures of Facebook 

post type – we examine the statistical significance of adding in each set of variables using 

Likelihood Ratio tests. We test for multicollinearity among the independent variables in each 

model by calculating the variance inflation factors (VIF): mean VIF is never higher than 1.5 and 

no variable has a VIF greater than 2.5 in any model which is below the thresholds of suggesting 

multicollinearity is not a problem (Allison, 1999).

The mean and median donations raised per nonprofit organization was $11,969 (SD=$29,644) 

and $3,395 respectively, with this amount ranging from $2 to $419,624. The mean age of 

participating nonprofits was 32 years (SD = 33). Approximately 50 percent of organizations had 

an annual budget of less than $250,000, while 28 percent had a budget size of $1 million or

Predicting Donations using Message Content and Diversity 

We present the results of a set of nested statistical models predicting total donations as a function 

of our independent variables. The models are estimated on a consistent sample of 752 

organizations (983 observations) with no missing values for the variables of interest. We 

describe the regression coefficients as the percentage change in total donations for a given 

change (absolute or percentage) in the independent variables e.g., an x percent increase in the 

number of Facebook post shares is associated with a y percent increase in total donations.1 We 

interpret Model 3 coefficients to make substantive claims about the association between each of 

the independent variables and the outcome – this model was chosen as it constitutes a better fit

Age is not associated with fundraising performance during Omaha Gives: the predicted level of 

total donations does not vary substantially across organizations of different ages (e.g., newer vs. 

older). There is a clear gradient in the association between budget size and total donations: 

medium (between $250,000 and $1mn) and large (over $1mn) organizations are predicted to 

raise 79 percent (exp(0.580), p<.001)) and 265 percent (exp(1.296), p < .001) more than small 

organizations respectively. Nonprofits participating in Omaha Gives 2020 raised 29 per cent less 

in expected donations compared to organizations taking part in the 2015 event. Finally, there is 

considerable variation by program service area: compared to human service organizations, 

neighborhood nonprofits raised over 100 percent more and health nonprofits 17 percent less. 

There are statistically significant, positive relationships between each of the numeric measures of 

social media usage and total donations. We find a 2.2 percent increase in donations for a 10 

percent increase in the number of likes on participating nonprofit’s Facebook profile. Similarly, a 

10 per cent increase in the number of posts or the times a post is shared is associated with 

predicted increases in donations of 3 percent and 1.4 percent, respectively. There are variable – 

both in terms of the magnitude of the coefficients and their statistical significance - differences in 

expected donations by Facebook post type: compared to organizations that most commonly use 

general solicitation posts, organizations that rely most heavily on messages relating to gratitude 

or goals raised 59 and 78 percent more in donations respectively, while those that relied mainly

do not feel the need to donate as they have already taken “action” by changing their profile 

picture. Nonprofits that have more than one most common type of post raised 10 percent more 

those that mainly post general solicitation posts.

Discussion  

This study adds to the fundraising literature by demonstrating the relationship between the 

content and diversity of social media messages and attracting donations. Our findings suggest 

that mixing different types of fundraising messages on social media is positively associated with 

higher levels of donations: nonprofits with more diverse fundraising posts raise 98 percent more 

in donations during Omaha Gives. We also examined whether the main type of message posted 

on Facebook - such as goal setting, gratitude, discussing the mission of the organization and 

general solication – was associated with fundraising success. The picture is mixed but compared 

to general solicitation messages, nonprofits that mainly posted messages of gratitude or goal 

attainment raised more during the events; for other post types there was no significant difference 

with general solicitation messages in the amount raised. The variable overall is statistically 

significant, indicating that message type is an important explanatory factor of fundraising 

performance during online giving events. This coheres with expectations from the literature, as 

diverse messaging  targets different types of potential donors who might be motivated to give 

based on emotional or efficacy framing (Bekkers & Wiekping, 2011). 

We highlight a number of other key findings. First, we find a range of differences – in degree of 

association and statistical significance - in expected donations by Facebook post type; overall the 

variable is globally significant, suggesting there are statistically significant differences across the 

post types overall. Compared to posts where the general aim is solicitation, those that focus on 

17 

 
 
 
 
thanking or displaying gratitude towards donors are associated with an increase in donations (Li 

et al., 2020; Waters & Feneley, 2013). One of the organizations in our dataset posted following 

Facebook message thanking their donors: 

Thank you to over 126 different individuals who have made contributions already and 

received their personal calls of thanks from our Executive Director! 

In addition, many organizations posted the names of their donors. Coupled with our findings, this 

suggests that donors are more likely to give in online fundraising events when organizations 

nurture relationships by showing gratitude. As argued previously by Lacetera et al. (2016),  

mentioning specific donors in their gratitude posts can create peer effects among the 

organization’s/donors’ social media networks. 

Second, our results suggest that posts calling on followers to take action on behalf of the 

organization, such as changing their profile picture on Facebook, are associated with a 58 percent 

decrease in donations. This finding resonates with Lovejoy & Saxton’s (2012) three-tier 

“hierarchy of engagement” model – information, community, and action. Often it is assumed that 

“action” is the most important part of messaging as it urges donors to do something like share 

content or change their profile picture. However, in the context of fundraising during online 

giving days, such calls to action appear least effective among the variety of message types and 

strategies a nonprofit could adopt.  

18 

 
 
 
 
 
This study uses data from a single social media platform (Facebook) and thus overlooks 

fundraising activities conducted on other platforms such as X (formerly Twitter), TikTok, etc. 

We suspect that the effect of message diversity and type is consistent across platforms, but we do 

not show this empirically. We also do not observe offline/non-social media fundraising activities 

of the organization such as its supporters in the community, board members’ engagement in 

fundraising, or solicitation through mail or emails. We encourage future researchers to develop 

more robust models capturing both offline and online fundraising activities of nonprofit 

organizations. 

Conclusion 

Overall, we found that mixing different types of social media posts is more likely to achieve 

fundraising success. We also found that organizations that mainly posted about gratitude or goal 

attainment raised more during the event as compared to general solicitation messages. This 

suggest that message time is important factor predicting fundraising success using social media. 

In addition, we showed how the benefit of more diverse messaging is the same for nonprofits of 

varying budget sizes, lending weight to the argument that social media platforms can be effective 

mechanisms for generating donations for smaller organizations with limited staffing.
