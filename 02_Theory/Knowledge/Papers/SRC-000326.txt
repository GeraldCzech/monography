ABSTRACT

KEYWORDS

Nonprofit organizations highly depend on the public’s trust for
legitimacy and support, to ultimately further their missions.
Despite its high importance, little is known about the trustbuilding processes of the general public. In this study, we develop
and test a multilevel study design to provide deeper insights into
the public’s trust in nonprofit organizations and its relevant determinants. We used a comprehensive data set of 4,072 dyads, with
survey data from 1,686 individuals and organizational data related
to 102 German NPOs to investigate the different trust-building
components: (1) individual public trust evaluation in NPO, (2)
individual (trustor) context, and (3) organizational (trustee) context.
The results of the analysis reveal unexpected differences in the
importance of the different components for explaining the public’s
trust in nonprofit organizations. Results show that the individual
context is as relevant as the individual public trust evaluation, but
the organizational context is far less important.

Introduction
“Despite the diversity among NPOs, there is one thing that they have in common–public trust is their most valuable asset” (Kearns, 2014, p. 265). On the one
hand, many nonprofit organizations depend highly on the public’s trust because
only a well-trusted organization receives social support in the form of monetary
and voluntary contributions (e.g., Alhidari et al., 2018; Furneaux & Wymer, 2015;
Hager & Hedberg, 2016; O’Neill, 2009). From a public’s perspective, the output
of nonprofit organizations is difficult to quantify unambiguously (Willems,
Jegers, & Faulk, 2016), and the mission orientation leads to stakeholders relying
heavily on relational trust in their interactions with nonprofit organizations
(Kearns, 2014). On the other hand, the nonprofit sector maintains its status as
the most trusted one (Edelman, 2019; Laidler-Kylander, Quelch, & Simonin,
2007). Due to the non-distribution constraint, according to which nonprofits are
not allowed to distribute profit to private parties, the public ascribes them higher
levels of trustworthiness compared to other institutions or sectors (Hansmann,

1980). A growing number of scandals in nonprofit sectors worldwide, however,
challenges the notion of inherent nonprofit trust (Prakash & Gugerty, 2010).
Recent incidences in some of the most respected organizations such as WWF
International’s allegations of human rights violations or Oxfam’s Haiti scandal
severely threaten public’s perceptions of nonprofit organizations (Chisolm, 2008;
Rimington, 2018; Willems & Faulk, 2019). Not only do these scandals harm the
reputation and trustworthiness of the single organization concerned, they cause
negative spillover effects also to other organizations in the sector (Archambeault
& Webber, 2018; Slatten, Guidry, & Austin, 2011).
Given its high importance, research on trust in nonprofit organizations has
gained growing interest among scholars over the past years. Beginning with
discussions on the general significance and role of trust in the nonprofit sector
(Hansmann, 2003; Ortmann & Schlesinger, 2003; Rose-Ackerman, 1996), nonprofit scholars recently focused on specific stakeholder groups centering donor
trust, and its relationship with outcomes such as donor commitment
(MacMillan, Money, Money, & Downing, 2005; Naskrent & Siebelt, 2011;
Sargeant & Lee, 2004), or donation behavior (Hager & Hedberg, 2016;
Sargeant & Lee, 2004). In another recently published study, Knapp, Sprinkle,
Urick, and Delaney-Klinger (2019) evaluate the trusting behavior among the
internal stakeholder group of employees of nonprofit organizations.
In contrast, less is known about the public’s trust in nonprofit organizations
(Bryce, 2007), and “[…] prevailing theoretical discussion and empirical study of
the effects of trust suffer from confusion over different dimensions of trust”
(Hager & Hedberg, 2016, p. 164). Herein, the very same approaches to address
both donor and public trust have been used, which equates the general public
with nonprofit stakeholders that are directly related to the organization through
actual experiences and transactions, and stronger relationships, respectively
(Boenigk, 2014). Sargeant and Lee (2002, p. 81), however, find empirical evidence that “trust may operate at two levels distinguishing donors from nondonors”, leading to a corresponding need for differentiated marketing and
management approaches (Sargeant, 2009). This study goes beyond this rather
narrow perspective, and a (more or less) established trust relationship between
a donor, volunteer, or beneficiary and a nonprofit organization, but focuses on
the public as a specific non-relational trust reference.
The latest trend in trust research relates to the development of strategies and
tools for organizational trust-building (Bekkers, 2003; Farwell, Shier, & Handy,
2018; Prakash & Gugerty, 2010). Particularly, nonprofit governance and accountability approaches are emerging that seek to send signals of quality and trustworthiness to an increasingly skeptical public (Slatten et al., 2011). Feng, Neely, and
Slatten (2016) and Becker (2018), for example, provide empirical evidence for the
benefits of voluntary nonprofit accountability in the form of using accreditation
tools, in terms of evoking higher levels of public trust toward nonprofit organizations. However, still little is known about what determines public perceptions of

trust in organizations (Jones & Shah, 2015), and the organizational determinants
have been most prominent in prior research. This study seeks to better understand
the public’s trust in organizations, thereby questioning the effectiveness of organizational efforts alone to build and restore the public’s trust. From the fundraising
literature, for instance, we know that various individual factors, including religion,
gender or education are important determinants for pro-social behavior toward
nonprofit organizations (Bekkers & Wiepking, 2011; Wiepking & Bekkers, 2012;
Wymer, 2012). In this study, we thus include the context of both parties involved in
the trust process, namely the trustor (individuals of the general public) and the
trustee (nonprofit organization), thereby providing a more comprehensive perspective on trust.
Considering these gaps in nonprofit trust research, we aim to provide
deeper insights into the trust-building processes among the general public
and the relevant determinants that explain the public’s trust in nonprofit
organizations. Thus, our first research question is:
RQ 1: How is trust built among the general public, and which components are
most important to explain public’s trust in nonprofit organizations?
To answer this question, we conducted a large multilevel study with survey
data from 1,686 respondents and organizational data related to 102 nonprofit
organizations. Assuming that both organizational and individual determinants are somewhat important, our second research question is:
RQ 2: Which of the individual and organizational determinants explain the
public’s trust in a nonprofit organization most?
In the next section, we provide a literature review that leads in to our
proposed public trust framework, with its different trust-building determinants.
The framework builds on the combination of two theoretical perspectives:
attribution theory (Kelley, 1967; see also Geigenmüller & Greschuchna, 2011)
and social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1979; see also Fehrler & Kosfeld,
2013), and allows for a holistic view on the public’s trust in nonprofit organizations. We then show the most trusted nonprofit organizations and test various
influential determinants of public trust in a nonprofit organization with the use
of multilevel modeling. Finally, we discuss the findings and provide nonprofit
scholars and marketers with deeper insights into the mechanisms behind the
public’s trust in nonprofit organizations, and approaches to successfully build
and maintain public trust.

Theoretical foundation and hypotheses
Public trust framework

Previous studies from organizational science mostly conceptualized and
measured trust in (nonprofit) organizations as a two-dimensional construct.
The first dimension relates to positive expectations of trustworthiness
(Morgan & Hunt, 1994). In this domain, scholars refer to generally positive
trust-related expectations such as ability/competence, benevolence, and
integrity/honesty of an organization. The second dimension is the willingness
to accept vulnerability, which comes with an element of risk. Widely used
across the research community, we draw on a definition that explicitly
focuses on the first dimension. Specifically, we refer to Morgan and Hunt’s
conceptualization of trust as “existing when one party has confidence in an
exchange partner’s reliability and integrity” (1994, p. 23). We also integrate
Feldheim and Wang’s (2004) notion that public trust is based primarily on
each interaction between the individual stakeholder and the nonprofit organization but ultimately is an aggregate, reflecting the attitudes of the overall
public toward a nonprofit organization. To sum up, trust is thus based on the
anticipated capacity of the nonprofit organization to satisfy each individual
stakeholder’s expectation regarding reliability, credibility and general reputation (Aurier & N’Goala, 2010).
Based on this definition, Figure 1 shows the developed framework of
public trust in a nonprofit organization in an overview. Our conceptual
framework builds on the assumption that the public’s trust in nonprofit
organizations depends on the aggregation of each individual’s trust evaluation, as well as organizational and individual trust-building determinants. It
follows that to provide a holistic view of public trust in nonprofit organizations, each of the aforementioned components (or methodologically speaking: levels) needs to be considered. In the following subsections, we discuss
the underlying rationale for the framework and derive specific hypotheses.
Importance of three different components for explaining public’s trust

Most studies on trust in nonprofit organizations center the individual
trust evaluation toward a nonprofit organization, focusing on the individual as a donor (e.g. MacMillan et al., 2005; Naskrent & Siebelt, 2011). On
the contrary, in our framework the individual stems from the general
public with no strong direct relationship with the organization through
actual experiences and transactions. If no strong relationship between an
individual and a specific organization exists yet, attribution theory is one
common theoretical foundation that argues that specific attributions about
the organization, in particular about its trustworthiness, are the most
relevant factor to build up trust (Geigenmüller & Greschuchna, 2011). If

the individual’s expectations of the organization’s trustworthiness are
positive, and he or she is willing to enter into an uncertain exchange
relationship with the organization, trust is primed to develop (Schilke &
Cook, 2013; Schoorman, Mayer, & Davis, 2007). Therefore, the core of
public trust in a nonprofit organization is an aggregate of trust evaluations that each individual has made toward the nonprofit organization.
Thus, our first hypothesis is the following:
Hypothesis 1. The individual trust evaluation has the largest influence on the
public’s trust in a nonprofit organization, compared to the individual (trustor)
and the organizational (trustee) context.
In addition to the trust evaluation, there is evidence about the significance of the individual (trustor) and organizational (trustee) context in
determining the public’s trust in nonprofit organizations. Focusing on the
individual context, the underlying rationale is that trust should vary across
relationships and within persons (Schoorman et al., 2007), and trust
evaluations vary strongly within individuals. According to social identity
theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1979), the individual context including sociodemographics relates closely to the individuals’ social identity, which helps
individuals define themselves according to their group affiliations, such
that it also helps them evaluate their relationships. It then follows that

individual social identity affects trust relationships with organizations,
insofar as individuals value those nonprofit organizations better that
embody their social identities (Ashforth & Mael, 1989). We, therefore,
hypothesize, as follows:
Hypothesis 2. The individual (trustor) context has a positive influence on the
individual trust evaluation of a nonprofit organization.
Social identity theory further explains that individuals possess greater trust
in nonprofit organizations if they could highly identify with the organizational context, and its values and missions. Herein, attribution theory among
cue utilization theory (Kelley, 1967; see also Geigenmüller & Greschuchna,
2011) provides a valuable framework for explaining the process of how
individuals attribute trustworthiness to nonprofit organizations. That is,
individuals assess a variety of intrinsic and extrinsic cues, and thereby derive
expectations about the future organizational trustworthiness. In situations
where a strong relationship has not developed yet and prior experiences with
the nonprofit organization are lacking, extrinsic cues in the form of NPOrelated attributes such as accountability or mission focus provide essential
orientation (Geigenmüller & Greschuchna, 2011). Therefore, our third
hypothesis is the following:
Hypothesis 3. The organizational (trustee) context has a positive influence on
the individual trust evaluation of a nonprofit organization.
Influence of individual and organizational determinants on public’s trust

For both the individual and organizational context, prior research, respectively, claimed specific determinants to be of particular relevance in the
context of affecting public’s trust; they are explained in the following.
Individual determinants
Individual determinants refer to the specific personal background of each
person, reflected in sociodemographics. With an extensive literature review,
Bekkers and Wiepking (2011) and Wiepking and Bekkers (2012) identify
a wide range of evidence of the importance of sociodemographic characteristics for pro-social behavior toward nonprofit organizations, and so in trustrelated relationships, including age, gender, education, household size,
income, religion, and citizenship. Against the background of general trusting
behavior, an early study from Stolle (1998) shows, for example, that more
educated people tend to generally trust more, and that different age groups
across different countries trust less or more, respectively (Nieminen et al.,
2008; Stolle, 1998). In another study, Delhey and Newton (2005) provide

evidence for the impact of the religious background on the individual trusting propensity. We, therefore, hypothesize, as follows:
Hypothesis 4. Individual determinants in the form of sociodemographics
influence the individual trust evaluation of a nonprofit organization.
Organizational determinants
Academic research has particularly highlighted three organizational determinants – accountability, mission, and history & size – that are relevant to
public trust. Accountability: In Germany, nonprofit organizations are not
obliged to report their annual financial statements publicly, though such
forms of accountability are crucial to maintaining the public’s trust
(Greiling, 2007). Feng et al. (2016) and Becker (2018)furthermore show
that external mechanisms, such as accreditation systems, can enhance public’s trust in nonprofit organizations. Therefore, organizations increasingly
devote efforts to demonstrating their transparency and trustworthiness with
various labels, certifications, and other accreditation tools (Bekkers, 2003;
Farwell et al., 2018; Furneaux & Wymer, 2015). A prestigious, nationally
endorsed donor accreditation might affect trust relationships.
Mission
A nonprofit organization’s mission identifies its long-term objective and determines its strategic direction (McDonald, 2007), and is thus also relevant to
public trust. Missions can vary greatly across organizations, with substantially
different meanings and relevance for various stakeholders (Willems, Jegers, &
Faulk, 2016). For example, religiousness in a mission can have distinct impacts
on people’s trust (Fehrler & Kosfeld, 2013; Wiepking & Bekkers, 2012). Social
identity theory suggests that when people have a special interest in a particular
topic, such as the environmental sector or children’s welfare, they identify more
strongly with organizations that make these topics part of their mission and are
more likely to support organizations with missions that reflect those connections
(Fehrler & Kosfeld, 2013; James & Wiepking, 2008).
History and size
The history and size of the nonprofit organization (i.e., organization’s age
and revenues) also appear crucial for trust. The organization’s age provides
information about how established and historically anchored it is, such that
age likely informs the organization’s trustworthiness (Wiedmann, Hennigs,
Schmidt, & Wuestefeld, 2011). Growth by an organization also signals to
external stakeholders that it has been successful and that others trust and
support this organization. In addition, organizational size in the form of total
revenues (depending on the specific nonprofit organization; e.g. public funds,

private donations, or profitable private-nonprofit-partnerships) provides
information about the efficiency and success of the nonprofit organization
(Furneaux & Wymer, 2015). Such information then support conclusions
about the current state of the nonprofit organization and its future forecasts.
Taken all organizational determinants together, we therefore predict:
Hypothesis 5a-c. Organizational determinants – (a) accountability, (b) mission, and (c) history & size – influence the individual trust evaluation of
a nonprofit organization.

Method
Data collection and sample

Our data set includes survey data from 1,686 respondents and organizational
data related to the 102 best known nonprofit organizations in Germany (see
Appendix). The first part of our data set, the individual survey data, is
extracted from a larger brand equity study (Boenigk, 2014). In that study,
nonprofit brand equity is conceptualized as having three dimensions, and
trust is one focal concept besides two other concepts, awareness and commitment. In this study, we solely focus on the public’s trust toward nonprofit
brands. Data collection took place in 2015. The selection included both
national and regional nonprofit organizations and covered a broad spectrum
of organizational missions. In a first step, respondents (the general public)
could indicate, from the full list of 102 nonprofit organizations, which ones
they already had heard of (awareness level). In a comparative evaluation, they
then evaluated the trustworthiness of up to three of the nonprofit organizations they were familiar with, which resulted in 4,072 evaluations toward 102
nonprofit organizations. Considering the relative context in which evaluations are made is important because individuals trust in nonprofit organizations relative to other organizations, and on the basis of comparisons.
Comparative evaluations provide a more realistic perspective, such that
when a reputational scandal harms the trustworthiness of one nonprofit
organization, negative spill-over effects often exist for other, similar organizations (Archambeault & Webber, 2018; Slatten et al., 2011). In the framework, we hence integrated a comparative element. These methodological
considerations are consistent with recommendations for comparative evaluations in organizational and marketing science (Olsen, 2002). To limit the
trust evaluations to three per individual was necessary, due to time limitations and the recognition that most respondents knew only a few
organizations.
The second part of our data set relies on a document analysis to gain
further information about the organizational context of the 102 nonprofit

brands. To examine the organization’s accountability, mission, history, and
size, we evaluated externally available data (DZI, 2014), including the webpages and annual reports of all nonprofit organizations and documents that
offer insights into the organization’s accountability.
Measures
Trust evaluation: individual public’s trust in NPO
Two main approaches were most often used to measure trust in organizations: (1) measurement of expectations and opinions about the trustworthiness, (2) measurement of trusting behaviors (Shamir & Lapidot, 2003,
p. 469). In our case, the behavioral measurement approach is problematic
because a typical characteristic of the general public is that there is no
willingness to extra-role engagement with a specific nonprofit organization.
We hence measured individual trust in an organization, using a scale
developed by Sargeant and Woodliffe (2007), which is commonly used in
nonprofit trust research. The trust variable was operationalized with three
items in a reflective measure−(1) I trust this organization to always act in
the best interests of the cause. (2) I trust this organization to conduct its
operations ethically. (3) I trust this organization to use donated funds
appropriately−using a 7-point Likert scale anchored at “strongly disagree”
(1) and “strongly agree” (7). To assess the measurement quality of our main
construct, we ran confirmatory factor analysis using AMOS. The results in
Table 1 provide evidence of the measure’s sound psychometric properties.
The Cronbach’s α value exceeds with .93 the recommended threshold value
of .70, indicating high internal consistency variables (Nunnally & Bernstein,
1994). The composite reliability and average variance extracted also were
with .93 and .82 above the suggested threshold values of .60 and .50,
respectively (Bagozzi, Yi, & Phillips, 1991).
Individual context and determinants
To assess the individual context, we included several sociodemographic
characteristics in the survey (see Table 1). Age was measured in years; gender
was operationalized as a dummy variable (0 = female, 1 = male). For
education, we used several categories, ranging from “no degree” to “PhD”
at the highest level. Household size reflected the number of persons in the
household. For income, we determined monthly net income in Euros, and we
operationalized religion with a dummy to indicate if the respondent had any
religious affiliation (0 = no religious affiliation, 1 = religious affiliation).
Citizenship was another dummy variable, according to whether the respondent was of German nationality or not (0 = German, 1 = foreigner).

Organizational context and determinants
At the organizational level, we measured accountability according to whether
the organization carries the so-called “DZI Seal-of-Approval”, using
a dummy variable (0 = no DZI in 2014, 1 = DZI in 2014). The DZI Seal-ofApproval is a Germany-wide renowned accreditation and widely regarded as
a strong quality signal that the nonprofit organization deals carefully and
responsibly with funds it receives (DZI, 2014), with an appropriate amount
of overall overhead cost (not higher than 30% of a raised Euro). As such, it
constitutes an important quality label for organizations from the nonprofit
sector to show their transparency and increase public trust. We measured the
organization’s mission focus with several dummy variables, categorizing the
wide variety of possible missions following the United Nations Sustainable
Development Goals (United Nations, 2019) (0 = not fitting this mission
category, 1 = fitting this mission category). Table 1 contains a list of the
measurement items and variables, as well as their descriptive statistics. Each
nonprofit organization had at least one specific mission focus and fit
a specific mission category, but it also could be assigned to several categories.
Finally, we measured the history of the organization in years since its
founding, and we used the organization’s total revenues (mainly from
2013) to quantify its size. Because normal distribution was not reached,
data relating to total revenues were log-transformed.
Data analysis

In our conceptual framework, public trust is the dependent variable, which
is influenced by determinants from both the individual and organizational
context. The multitude of comparative trust evaluations (at level 1) creates
an appropriate data structure for performing a multilevel analysis of trust
of various organizations by individuals (Gonzalez-Roma & Hernandez,
2017; Kozlowski & Klein, 2000). Herein, the dependent variable of public
trust is nested in the individual and organizational context, which represents the multilevel, or more precisely the cross-level, structure of our
data set.
To address our two research questions, we took a step-wise approach to the
data analysis. First, we specified the levels at which public trust in nonprofit
organizations is situated and verified if significant variances occur at each of the
three levels (level 1, and contextual levels 2a and 2b). In this process, we calculated
the intra-class correlation (ICC 1), which expresses the relative proportion of level
variances by calculating the proportion of significant variance at the organizational or individual contextual level, compared with the overall variance of the
individual public’s trust in NPO. The calculation of the ICC 1 values is based on
null models without fixed-effect independent variables. ICC accordingly depicts
the amount of significant contextual level variance, divided by the amount of

12

A. BECKER ET AL.

residual variance for the dependent variable. In the context of effectiveness, ICC
has been frequently used to quantify within-group agreement (Boyer & Verma,
2000), yet its use is not limited to the agreement function and can be used also in
other contexts (Rasbash, Charlton, Browne, Healy, & Cameron, 2005). It follows
that with these calculations, we can test which level in our framework explains
most. Thus, we are able to answer H1, H2, and H3.
Second, we analyzed the specific cross-level effects with a multilevel regression analysis. More generally speaking, we assessed if and which individual and
organizational determinants were influencing the individual’s trust evaluation of
a nonprofit organization. Herein, a multilevel approach is appropriate because it
allows for the simultaneous examination of the effects between variables on
different levels. Using these considerations, we tested the hypotheses with two
separate multilevel regression analyses, one for each cross-level combination
(with MLwiN: Rasbash et al., 2005). Although similar results could be obtained
from a single, integrated analysis, such as a nonhierarchical multilevel structure
(MCMC approach in Rasbash et al., 2005), we decided to report the two separate
analyses, because the main findings do not differ, but with this approach, we
could adopt a much broader, accepted, and standardized estimation (including
the reporting format).
For the first regression analysis, the 4,072 trust evaluations are nested in
1,686 individuals, and we include individual determinants reflecting their
age, gender, education, household size, income, religion, and citizenship.
With this analysis, we test Hypothesis 4. Then, in the second analysis, the
4,072 trust assessments are nested in 102 nonprofit organizations, and we
include the organizational determinants – accreditation, mission, and history
and size. This analysis relates to the test of Hypothesis 5a-c.

Concluding discussion and future research
Overall, the key findings of our study reveal considerable differences in the
importance of the different trust-building components, or levels, for explaining
the public’s trust. Given the comparatively high ICC 1 values of 49.09% and
93.62%, public trust in a nonprofit organization depends mostly on the specific
relationship between the individual and the nonprofit organization. This might
stem from the fact that a person’s trust evaluation mainly builds from characteristics of his or her actual trust relationship and experiences he or she has with each
single organization. This view is widely supported in trust research, given the high
importance of trust at the so-called individual level (Fulmer & Gelfand, 2012).
However, with an ICC 1 value of 50.91%, a person’s individual background
appears to determine assessments of the trustworthiness of a nonprofit organization substantially. It follows that when a person evaluates several relationships
with different organizations; his or her background influences such evaluations
similarly. The high importance of the individual context for the public’s trust
might be due to the fact that trust varies widely according to each person’s
characteristic qualities (see also Lewicki, Tomlinson, & Gillespie, 2006). Our
results are based on an investigation of mostly sociodemographic characteristics,
and we found age, gender, income, and household size to be relevant as older,
female, and low-income people and those from larger households are more likely
to trust nonprofit organizations. Yet, given the comparatively high ICC 1 values of
49.09% versus 50.91; the public’s trust is nearly equally based on a multitude of
individual beliefs and expectations toward the organization.
The varying effects among people’s sociodemographic characteristics regarding
their interactions in trustful relationships might stem from different mechanisms
such as solicitation, value, cost mechanisms, and awareness of need that work
differently among persons. In the context of pro-social behavior toward nonprofit
organizations, and philanthropic behavior, in particular, existent studies typically
show a positive relationship with age (for an extensive literature overview, see
Bekkers & Wiepking, 2011). While there is no certain agreement about the

individuals being targeted by the nonprofit organization might impact his or her
trusting behavior. In the context of giving behavior, Bennett (2012) finds the
sense of an individual’s affinity with other low-income individuals and general
beliefs about how unfairly society treats the poor exerted significant impacts on
giving behavior; whereas this effect was influenced by the person’s educational
level. Lastly, individuals from larger households tend to pose higher levels of
trust toward nonprofit organizations. Large households usually contain (more)
children, and having children increases the awareness of need, and families are
also more likely exposed to solicited offers (Wiepking & Bekkers, 2012).

organizational context influences trustors only on a small scale in a similar way.

commercial business or public organizations, possess per se higher trust values
(Edelman, 2019), and nonprofit organizations’ perception relating to trustworthiness is rather similar among the general public. Within the context of low
organizational context relevance, we found that well-established organizations,
and organizations with a specific mission as well as those that are accredited,
achieve comparatively higher levels of trust. This might stem from the fact that
well-established organizations have generated greater awareness and familiarity
among the public which results in higher trust. Besides, the importance of the
respective nonprofit organization’s mission, and particularly when it relates to
supporting children’s welfare, may stem from mechanisms such as higher awareness of need, higher solicitation, or greater consistency between the individual’s
and organization’s values. Lastly, accreditation and other attempts to show
a nonprofit organization’s transparency and trustworthiness might be important
for the general public as such certificates reduce risks in trustful exchange

Research implications

As a point of departure, this study complements and specifies undifferentiated
notions in previous research about the public’s trust in nonprofit organizations,
and beyond that, in organizations in general. Thereby, our study offers a more
complex perspective on trust and contributes to trust research in three main ways.
First, although nonprofit organizations depend particularly on trust, the academic
discussion is rather unilateral, largely focusing on a single stakeholder group.
Compared to mainly donor trust, public trust has been only focused on more
recently. Considering its increasing practical relevance, more scholars are starting
to investigate the public’s trust in nonprofit organizations. Still, from the few
studies, most investigations take a conceptual perspective (e.g., Bryce, 2007;
O’Neill, 2009), or focus on the rather dominant organizational context (Farwell
et al., 2018; Furneaux & Wymer, 2015). In contrast, we provide empirical insights
into the trust-building processes among the general public, including both trust
parties’ context. As our results show, public trust essentially demands a broad and
more comprehensive perspective, revealing that this complex phenomenon
emerges not just from a single determinant but rather results from the interaction
of multiple causes.
This leads to our second contribution, the investigation of public trust in
nonprofit organizations from a so-called multilevel perspective. To the best of
our knowledge, this study is the first to address, conceptually and empirically,
the issue of trust in nonprofit organizations across different levels of analysis. In
line with other studies (e.g., Jones & Shah, 2015; Schilke & Cook, 2013), our
study offers empirical evidence, gathered from a large sample of individuals and
nonprofit organizations in Germany, that the public’s trust in nonprofit organizations is situated at different levels and that certain contextual determinants
are relevant to this trust. The trust concept thus should not be limited to a single
conceptual or operational level. Several levels in combination can be relevant. As
the public’s trust in nonprofit organizations depends mostly on the specific
relationship between the individual and the nonprofit organization, this finding
has major methodological and conceptual consequences for studies that focus
on trust as an organizational-level construct. Multiple individual opinions are
needed to assess public trust in an organization, and specific relational and
individual elements need to be considered to develop a full, thorough understanding of an organization’s trustworthiness. Yet few trust studies use multilevel investigations, or explicitly examine trust in individual and organizational
contexts (Jones & Shah, 2015). Our findings among other studies from organizational science, in turn, highlight the need for in-depth analyses (see also,
Fulmer & Gelfand, 2012; Mayer, Davis, & Schoorman, 1995).

Third, in nonprofit management and marketing research, organizational
efforts to build and restore public trust are prominent. Besides the organizational context, the importance of individual characteristics for pro-social
behavior toward nonprofit organizations has been highlighted. Little is
known about the interrelation between these contextual components and
the public trust evaluations, and most previous studies focus on the latter
component alone. The expectations at this level support the subjective nature
of trust, as it is embedded in attribution and social identity theories. Both
these theories also are context dependent, such that different findings and
propositions likely arise from different contexts. We clarify how some contextual elements explain this diversity. Further research also could incorporate more details about specific backgrounds and context while applying
attribution and social identity theories to nonprofit organizations.
Managerial implications

The study results provide bases for some useful recommendations for managers and marketers of all kinds of organizations, but nonprofit organizations
in particular. A key recommendation is that nonprofit marketers should
regard public trust as a complex entity, influenced by determinants on several
levels. To capture the full potential of efforts to increase public trust in their
organizations, marketers should apply a more integrated approach in practice, with tactics at both the organizational and individual contextual levels.
Noting the low variance in the organizational context and the widely varying
individual opinions about any single nonprofit organization, marketers
should take sociodemographic characteristics into account and refrain from
applying any one-size-fits-all strategy to increase trust.
At the organizational contextual level, our findings suggest that accreditation
that indicates the transparency and trustworthiness of nonprofit organizations can
increase trust in those. Therefore, nonprofit organizations should apply for the
DZI Seal-of-Approval or corresponding seals and certificates in other nations. For
nonprofit organizations whose mission relates to children’s welfare, marketers
should highlight the mission in their marketing efforts. Similarly, if the nonprofit
organization has been well established and reached an advanced age, this status
should be communicated to the public. This organizational context thus offers
a range of trust-building actions, but they are relatively less important than the
factors that constitute the individual context. We recommend that nonprofit
marketers carefully consider whether to devote effort, financial resources, and
time to apply for certificates or develop new marketing campaigns.
Finally, at the individual contextual level, nonprofit marketers should
recognize the potential benefits of segmenting their stakeholder groups. In
particular, they should consider how to develop targeted marketing campaigns for different stakeholders that will increase their trust in the

organizations. A sociodemographic segmentation should focus mainly on
individual stakeholders’ ages, gender, net income, and household size. The
stakeholder groups that exhibit the greatest potential are younger men,
wealthy people, and those from single-person households.

Limitations and further research

Some limitations of this study suggest directions for further research, beyond
those indicated by our findings. First, we find that the individual context is
highly relevant for explaining trust in nonprofit organizations, because
50.91% of variance relates to this level. But this study focuses on sociodemographic characteristics at the individual level, so we cannot explain all
the variance. That is, we identify the importance of this level but can only
account for one relevant contextual component, such that we were forced to
neglect others. Recent studies, for example, highlighted the relevance of trust
contextualization within broader social and institutional trust (Farwell et al.,
2018; Hager & Hedberg, 2016), in that each trust evaluation of a nonprofit
organization is influenced by these broader forms of trust. Further research
could include additional variables at this level, such as an individual’s unique
trust-related and psychographic characteristics.
Second, nonprofit organizations with religious missions are comparatively less
trusted by the general public than organizations without such a mission (coefficient = −0.386). At first glance, this strong effect may seem surprising, especially
because many nonprofit organizations operate in affiliation with churches and
other religious organizations (Weisbrod, 1988). We posit that a series of church
scandals in Germany might have created negative spillover effects on churchrelated organizations. In addition, differences between the general public and
certain stakeholder groups might result in different effects; for example, people
with a religious background reacted differently to a nonprofit organization with
a religious mission than those with a non-religious background. This finding is
interesting for further research; a thorough investigation was beyond the scope of
this study. We encourage researchers to investigate the fit between individual and
organizational contextual elements, by examining specific individual–organizational trust matches. These limitations and avenues for research together indicate
the relevance of additional studies on trust in various contexts; we consider it
a very promising area for trust research. This initial evidence should, we hope,
stimulate and contribute to the much needed discussion about an integrated view
of trust in nonprofit organizations.
