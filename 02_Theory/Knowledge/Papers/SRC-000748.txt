Abstract
Purpose – The present empirical study intends to address a significant topic for business
management, such as the influence of market orientation (MO) on the internal and external
environments of an organisation. More precisely, the main objective of this paper focuses on the effect
of such an orientation on employees – organisational commitment and OCBs – as well as on
customers – satisfaction and perceived service quality.
Design/methodology/approach – Structural equation models are employed to analyse the data
collected from the spanish retail banking sector in order to verify the hypotheses corresponding to the
causal relationships proposed in our research model.
Findings – The results achieved in this research represent significant contributions both from a
theoretical perspective as well as for management in the service industry. From a theoretical
perspective, these findings reinforce the research position stressing the role of the employees in the MO
relationship and the operational level of the business incorporating new evidence into this emerging
field of study.
Research limitations/implications – Limitations of the study can account for the anomalous
relationships that have been identified. First, the cross-sectional nature of the study could have been an
obstacle to revealing short-term answers. In addition, the empirical study focused on one particular
case in the service industry – banking – hindered the generalization of results.
Originality/value – Provides further research on the role of market orientation as a major source of
achieving sustainable competitive advantage.
Keywords Market orientation, Employee attitudes, Customer satisfaction, Customer services quality
Paper type Research paper

Consequences of MO for employees
Recent literature in marketing shows clearly that when it comes to achieving success in
an organization, it is of vital importance for its organizational culture and climate focus
on satisfying the customer’s needs, from top management down to the lowest level
employees. Kennedy et al. (2002) states, consistent with earlier investigations of MO,
that the true concept of marketing can only be achieved when customer-orientation is
considered to be a working philosophy for all of a firm’s employees. For this reason, the
effect of MO at the operational level of employees has begun to appear as a latent
concern in current research. Recent research such as those by Kennedy et al. (2002) and
Brown et al. (2002), points out that the imbalance between the volume of research
exploring the relationship between MO and a company’s performance and the research
concerning the effect of MO, as well as the operational level of the employees
constitutes, in itself, proof of emergent interest in this stream of research.
A review of the literature reveals the existence of two different approaches with
respect to the relationship between MO and the employee. The first approach does not
recognize its potential importance, since it propounds that the presence of this
relationship is not necessary. It does so either because it argues for the primacy of the
external customer over the employee in the operation of the marketing concept
(Webster, 1994; Dickinson et al., 1986), or because it supports the “micropolitical”
perspective, which states that the development of MO involves the higher hierarchical
levels of the organization and not the lower levels (Piercy, 1989; Whittington and
Whipp, 1992). The second approach defends the need to study the mechanisms for
effectively implementing a business strategy. From this perspective, and from the
point of view of the employees’ role, Kolhi and Jaworski (1990) and Jaworski and Kolhi
(1993) are considered to be pioneers in proposing and validating the positive and direct
effect of MO on organizational commitment and corporate spirit. Selnes et al. (1996)
reaches similar results. Additionally, Ruekert (1992) proposes and verifies that there is
a significant positive correlation between the level of MO and employee’s satisfaction
and organizational commitment. Other researchers have led to similar results
(Hoffman and Ingram, 1992; O’Hara et al., 1991). However, a circumstance that could
cause misleading results is that these studies analyse data only provided by
management. In order to overcome this limitation, Siguaw et al. (1994) collect their
information directly from the sales force, concluding that as the level of MO increases
so does the level of organizational commitment.
It is necessary to understand the role played by an organization’s employees in
effectively establishing the MO. Postulating the contrary would lead to an important
limitation in understanding and establishing suitable policies for its development.
Accordingly, Piercy et al. (2002) indicate that the analysis of the interrelationships
between the company’s strategies and policies for MO, and the characteristics of the
employees in terms of beliefs, attitudes, and behaviours is currently an important area
of research, albeit only very occasionally studied. These authors explore this
relationship in two differentiated research stages. In the first stage, an exploratory

Kennedy et al. (2002) have developed the customer mind-set (CMS) construct to
verify that customer orientation affects the workplace and employee performance.
CMS is proposed to be positively associated with desirable outputs such as
satisfaction, organizational commitment and employee performance. Findings point to
the existence of a desirable relationship between MO and the commitment of
employees to their organization, as well as to their satisfaction level with their job.
Nevertheless, these studies note the need for more research in this field in order to
study the consequences of MO in more depth. With the general objective of looking
more deeply into this field of study, this research focuses, within the internal
environment, on the organizational commitment of the employees. As has been shown,
studies by Kolhi and Jaworsky (1990), Ruekert (1992), Siguaw et al. (1994) and Selnes
et al. (1996), as well as the more recent works of Piercy et al. (2002), Kennedy et al. (2002)
and Brown et al. (2002) have supported the positive influence of MO on employee
commitment to the firm; in many cases mediated by customer orientation and, only in
particular occasions, displaying a deficient form. However, it should be underlined that
the majority of the earlier studies in which some of these relationships have been tested
focus almost exclusively on sales personnel (Siguaw et al., 1994; Babin and Boles, 1998;
Hartline and Ferrell, 1996; MacKenzie et al., 1998; Singh, 1998). Despite this, it is
reasonable to extend these conclusions to the rest of the employees within a service
organization, given the high level of interaction between employees and customers in
service companies.
Organizational commitment refers to the feeling of belonging, employees have
towards the company, which consequently influences their ability to perform above
and beyond expectations in achieving the company’s goals. Employees feel as if the
company makes up part of their “family” and are willing to continually provide
supportive behaviour to uphold the company’s values (Meyer and Herscovitch, 2001).
This concept comprises different aspects related to the individual’s attachment to the
organization, along with the worker perceiving costs associated with leaving the
organization, and an obligation to stay in the company. Therefore, three perspectives of
commitment arise
(1) affective or attitudinal;
(2) continuance or calculative; and
(3) normative (Bayona et al., 1999; Shore et al., 1995; Reichers, 1985).

Organizational citizenship behaviours
One of the most notable consequences of commitment to the organization is the
performance of employees in their work environment. Over the last few years,
researchers have recognized the importance of distinguishing two types of
performance: that known as “in-role” and that known as “extra-role”. In-role
performance represents results connected with work output, including sales volume,
commissions, etc. On the other hand, extra-role performance is fundamentally related
to aspects of the individual’s behaviour (MacKenzie et al., 1998). Among them,
organizational citizenship behaviours (OCBS) are emphasized in this research given its
notable relevance in the literature under review.
There is much evidence supporting this distinction. In the first place, research has
demonstrated that upon evaluating their employees, managers keep both types of
performance in mind, and these greatly influence decisions such as promotion, rewards
and compensation (Mackenzie et al., 1991). Similarly, Podsakoff et al. (2000) hold that
management considers “extra-role” performance as part of the individual’s job. In
addition, both types of performance have an important influence on the overall
financial performance of the organization (MacKenzie et al., 1998). Finally, such distinct
types of performance have also different antecedents and consequences (Organ, 1990).
Therefore, the weight of the empirical evidence suggests that “in-role” performance is
an antecedent to job satisfaction, while “extra-role” performance is seen as a
consequence (Organ, 1988, 1990). The logic supporting this causal relationship is based
on the fact that employees satisfied with their job have a feeling of gratitude towards
the organization and a desire to reciprocate, which manifests itself in their behaviour.
On the other hand, “in-role” behaviour should be considered as an antecedent to
organizational commitment, while “extra-role” behaviour is perceived as its
consequence. This relationship has been widely analysed among sales staff. Thus,
the individuals who are more committed to the firm want to give more of themselves
with the intention of contributing to the overall well-being of the organization (Cardona
et al., 1999; MacKenzie et al., 1998; Munene, 1995; Organ and Ryan, 1995).
Individual behaviour in the workplace has attracted a great deal of attention from
researchers over the past decade. A common objective of these studies has been to define
a type of individual behaviour which is believed to contribute to the long-term success of
the service company, and which had previously been neglected when assessing
employee performance (Dyne et al., 1994). Thus, it is important for service companies to
give more attention to discretionary and voluntary behaviours of contact employees,
which should lead to the effective working of organizations (Yoon and Suh, 2003).

The most important aspect of the OCB construct with regard to employee performance
is the framework of organizational rights and responsibilities. Organizational rights
are granted to employees by the organization and organizational responsibilities are
behaviours that are expected from individuals as a result of being part of a larger
group such as a business organization (Bienstock et al., 2003).
There are many difficulties in distinguishing between “in-role” and “extra-role”
performance. First, the management’s perception may not coincide with that of the
employees regarding their performance, thus, regarding what their responsibilities are;
secondly, the employees’ level of satisfaction affects their perception of their
responsibility and their performance level (Morrison, 1994). Nevertheless, given that
the majority of the empirical studies under review start from Organ’s initial definition,
this study follows this approach.
Organ (1988) defines OCBS as the behaviour among employees of an organization
who seek to directly promote the effective functioning of the organization, independent
of their personal productivity goals of each of them. Netemeyer et al. (1997)
summarized the essential elements of this type of behaviour. According to these
authors, OCBS is a type of behaviour that goes beyond that which is formally
prescribed by the organization.
Multiple aspects of services require non-mandated employee behaviours that can be
critical to customer perceptions of service. While some behaviour can be explicitly
defined, many others are more abstract and are dependent on employee attitudes and
motivations (Bienstock et al., 2003). Also, OCBS is a type of discretional behaviour,
which is not directly rewarded or recognized in the formal structure of organizational
rewards. However, OCBS is extraordinarily important for the organization’s
effectiveness and success. In short, a review of the literature indicates that
researchers have primarily established two motivational bases causing OCBS:
employees’ attitude toward their job, and personality disposition (Bolino, 1999).
According to Podsakoff et al. (2000), there are various forms of OCBS. The initial
proposal of Netemeyer et al. (1997) considers four major dimensions: sportsmanship,
civic virtue, conscience and altruism. Sportsmanship is defined as the good will an
employee displays in tolerating circumstances that are less than ideal without making
“mountains out of molehills”. Civic virtue manifests itself in the concern of the
employee in participating in the life of the company – for example, attending functions
that are not specifically required. Conscience is understood to be the type of behaviour

impress management. In some circumstances, OCB might be considered a tactic of the
employee aimed at influencing those managers who are responsible for deciding future
promotions and rewards. Such behaviours turn “good soldiers” (Hui et al., 2001) into
“good actors” within the firm.

Consequences of MO for customers
Slater and Narver (1994) state that MO must be understood as a business culture
centred on the continual creation of value for the customer. Many studies emerge from
these initial proposals, suggesting that MO involves gathering information about the
needs of customers and implementing strategies accordingly, and always considering
the competition (Chang and Chen, 1998a, b; Krepapa et al., 2003). Nevertheless, in early
studies in this field, MO is considered and measured exclusively through management
perceptions. Recent approaches suggest that since MO in its very concept involves a
strong customer orientation, a business should be considered as market oriented only
when its customers actually perceive it. In consequence, the appropriate level of a
firm’s MO stems from what its customers think it should have (Krepapa et al., 2003;
Steinman et al., 2000). Therefore, MO and relationship marketing are tightly connected
approaches since both emphasize that in order to obtain a sustainable competitive
advantage the company should attract and retain customers by continually satisfying
their needs (Steinman et al., 2000).

In the service industry, the competitive advantage is fundamentally related to the
company’s intangible assets (Kaplan and Norton, 2001); thus, when the customer is
highly committed to the service delivery process, it is necessary to develop close
relationships between the company and the customer. This relationship increases the
customer perceived value, and consequently such a relationship must be encouraged
by the firm’s MO (McNaughton et al., 2002).
The study of the consequences of MO has almost exclusively focused on its final
effect on the company’s performance, but the way in which this relationship develops
remains unexplored and requires further development (McNaughton et al., 2001;
Uncles, 2000; Deshpandé, 1999). This situation is even more critical in the service
industry, given that the inherent intangibility of services may considerably affect this
relationship (McNaughton et al., 2002). Therefore, it is necessary to identify the
mediating variables in the MO-performance relationship. Until now, the dominant logic
has considered that MO establishes the bases of creating value for the customer, and
that this strategy enables the achievement of a sustainable competitive advantage
resulting in better performance (Jarwoski and Kolhi, 1993; Deshpandé et al., 1993).
However, this argument does not explain why a business might come to deliver value
for its stockholders by trying to create greater value for its customers (McNaughton
et al., 2002).
Chang and Chen (1998a, b) make an extremely important contribution by
identifying one of the mediating variables in this relationship. These authors develop a
model in which an indirect effect of the MO-performance relationship is represented
through adequate service quality. Literature on service marketing establishes that
service quality is one of the variables that have a significant impact on the company’s
performance (Rust et al., 1995; Zeithaml et al., 1996). Similar evidence is provided by
literature on MO (Slater and Narver, 1994; Doyle and Wong, 1998; Chang and Chen,
1998b; Matsuno and Mentzer, 2000; Pelham, 2000; Dobni and Luffman, 2003). Thus, it
is relevant to look into the type of relationship existing between MO and service
quality.
In this respect, an immediate effect of enhancing a company’s service quality is an
increase in the level of customer satisfaction. Improving service quality for the
customer encourages repurchasing, attracts new customers, reduces non-essential
services and, therefore, increases income while reducing costs. In short, developing
effective and efficient service offerings will improve the company’s performance
(Chang et al., 1999). Consequently, the strength of the relationship between MO and the
firm’s performance will strongly depend on the effectiveness and efficiency of the
marketing effort. A direct indication of the level of effectiveness in the service industry
is the level of service quality delivered (Chang and Chen, 1998a, b). The effort of the
market oriented company closes the “gap”, identified in service literature, between the
company management and its customers (Zeithaml et al., 1990).
Along with the arguments, the contingency theory contributes to clarifying the
relationship between MO and the level of service quality. This theory establishes that
there needs to be a congruency between organizational internal elements, as well as
between its internal and external elements, if an organization wants to survive in the
market and be profitable (Galbraith, 1977; Zeithaml et al., 1988). In consequence, no
universal principal can be applied to all organizations. The need for this congruency
between the organization’s internal and external environments brings together MO

Nevertheless, it is reasonable to think that service quality is not the only

Once MO has been developed, the company’s ability to derive superior performance
is attributed to the subsequent skills it builds which allow for a better understanding of
the needs of its target market (Gounaris et al., 2003). A market oriented approach may
remain elusive unless the company has managed to effectively communicate the values
of MO and the behaviours towards the customer that comes along with it (Harris and
Ogbonna, 1999). It is necessary to understand that one important issue is winning the
“hearts and minds” of operational employees if MO is to succeed (Piercy, 1997).
In short, the success of the impact of MO on the customers’ perceived service quality
cannot be understood simply in terms of the characteristics of service, but also in terms
of the customer’s experience of his encounter with the service provider (Gounaris et al.,
2003).
Service quality is a widely studied concept in literature, especially in the field of
service marketing. Nevertheless, it is not a concept that can be easily defined given the
evolution it has undergone over the last few years. Most contributions on the topic
agree in defining it as a comparison between the customers’ expectations and their
perception of the service received (Parasuraman et al., 1988; Grönroos, 1990; Bigné et al.,
1996), pointing out that this is treated as a continual process in time (Grönroos, 1990).
Additionally, despite being a highly subjective concept, similar to an attitude (Bitner,
1990; Zeithaml, 1988), there have been different approaches for measurement purposes.
To begin with, the consensus existing on the multidimensionality of perceived service
quality disappears upon establishing specific dimensions that define it (Llosa et al.,
1998).
One of the most relevant contributions in the area is a research conducted by
Parasuraman et al. (1985), after which authors propose the measurement instrument
called SERVQUAL (Parasuraman et al., 1988). Despite the criticisms it has received
(Teas, 1993; Cronin and Taylor, 1994), SERVQUAL has been selected to measure
service quality in numerous empirical studies.
The consideration of quality as a long-term subjective construct suggests the need
for an organizational culture in which the values, norms and symbols favouring the
continual performance of service quality permeates the whole firm. Accordingly, Lewis
and Gabrielsen (1998) argue that perceived quality requires a customer-oriented
culture behind the responsible organizational behaviour of delivering such an excellent
quality. Söderlund (1995) affirms that the dimensions of perceived quality can be used
as dimensions of MO as long as they are ways of directing the firm towards the
customer.

The literature indicates that service quality and customer satisfaction are two different
variables albeit closely related (Bansal and Taylor, 1997; Spreng and Mackoy, 1996;
Dabholkar, 1993, 1995a, b; Taylor and Baker, 1994). Evidence of their strong
relationship is proved by how often consumers find it difficult to differentiate them
(Dabholkar et al., 1996). Parasuraman et al. (1988) state that the difference between
them lies in the fact that service quality is a global evaluation, while satisfaction tends
to be related to a specific purchase. This means that satisfaction involves a single and
non-continual relationship with the business (Larrea, 1991), while service quality
should be analysed from a long-term perspective. Anderson et al. (1994) consider that
satisfaction requires previous consumption experience and depends on price, whereas
quality can be perceived without previous consumption experience and does not
normally depend on price, although in circumstances where there is little available
information or where quality evaluation is difficult, price can be an indicator of quality.
With respect to the relationship between the two variables, there is a lack of
consensus in literature and among research about the casual link between the two
construct. While some authors claim that customer satisfaction is an antecedent of
perceived quality (Bitner, 1990; Carman, 1990), others scholars agree in placing service
quality as an antecedent to satisfaction (Cronin and Taylor, 1992; Zeithaml et al., 1996;
Bitner and Hubbert, 1994; Spreng and Mackoy, 1996).
As we explained earlier, for Parasuraman et al. (1994), this controversy derives from
the type of transaction that is being evaluated, namely, whether it is a specific
transaction or the result of cumulative experience. However, these authors argue that,
from the theoretical point of view, the customer’s perceived service quality is an
antecedent to satisfaction when both constructs are measured in the context of a
specific transaction. The recent studies by Beerli et al. (2004), Dabholkar et al. (2000),
Caruana (2002) and Sureshchandar et al. (2002) put forward the same argument.
According to these arguments, the following hypothesis is proposed:
H4. Perceived service quality is positively related to customer satisfaction.
Linking the internal and external environments of the company
As previously indicated, the contingency theory points out the need for the internal and
external environments of a company to be congruent in order to achieve market
profitability (Galbraith, 1977; Zeithaml et al., 1988). Literature on service has
sufficiently documented that customers final perceptions of the service quality
delivered by the firm is conditioned by the behaviours and attitudes of its personnel
(Lings, 2004; Hartline and Jones, 1996; Bitner, 1990; Avkiran, 1999). Furthermore,
earlier research indicates that only when the needs of the customer service personnel

Peters, 1994; Grene et al., 1994). According to this reason, a significant contribution of
this research is to explore the relationships among the variables involved in the
internal and external environments of the business.
The positive contribution made by OCBS to the organizational performance is
reasonably accepted in service literature (Podsakoff and MacKenzie, 1994, 1997;
Podsakoff et al., 2000), yet it is still necessary to look more deeply into this field. In this
respect, one of the most valuable results for management in the service industry has
been to understand the contribution of OCBS towards perceived service quality. To
date, this relationship has received important theoretical support (Morrison, 1996),
although only little empirical support (Kelly and Hoffman, 1997).
The majority of researchers have addressed the interrelationship between
employees and customers starting from the affective and cognitive antecedents of
perceived service quality. Thus, many studies have focused on what is known as
customer-oriented behaviour. Customer-oriented behaviour is a central pillar of MO
where the value of the employees is not discussed. However, it is certainly possible to
discuss whether employees’ customer-oriented behaviours are sufficient to generate
better perceptions or even customer delight. Thus, potential antecedents to perceived
service quality should be studied in more depth. In short, academics should study how
to achieve an adequate level of perceived service quality (Bell and Menguc, 2002). In
this respect, there are two fundamental reasons for supporting the positive relationship
between OCBS and service quality as perceived by customers and their level of
satisfaction. In the first place, OCBS can have an immediate or direct effect derived
from the interaction between the employee and the customer. In the service encounter,
employees are performers rather than workers, and their behavioural performance is a
major part of service quality that customer perceive (Yoon and Suh, 2003). Secondly, a
positive effect can be achieved through the impact on the organization’s internal work
factors, including in this group the work environment, service climate and consistency
in the service process. Furthermore, within these last factors lies the difference between
this variable and customer orientation (Bell and Menguc, 2002). Therefore, OCBS
directly and indirectly contribute to create higher levels of perceived service quality
(Morrison, 1996).
Regarding direct effects, OCBS captures many of the essential behaviours described
in service marketing literature that is necessary to achieve higher benefits. Service
marketing literature indicates the need for employees to develop both suitable role
behaviours and additional discretional behaviours in order to achieve high levels of
service quality. In addition, employees displaying behaviours defined by OCBS are
capable of promoting high levels of service quality through their tendency to help
customers make a better decision. The theory of socialization also helps explain this
relationship. Here, the process of the individual’s socialization in the organization
encourages the acclaimed dimensions of OCBS to be brought up-to-date in behaviours
pursuing the best results for the customer (Netemeyer et al., 1997). The second reason
to justify the relationship between OCBS and perceived service quality is based on
their impact on the internal aspects of the organization. Thus, OCBS promotes
teamwork, ease communication between employees and management, improve the
work environment and achieves high rates of employee retention. In consequence,
employees who develop this type of behaviour usually help other employees to deliver

Downloaded from http://www.emerald.com/ejm/article-pdf/39/5-6/646/446576/03090560510590755.pdf by WU Wirtschaftsuniversitet Wien user on 23 July

service quality, i.e. they are good candidates for encouraging and carrying out quality
initiatives in the organization (Hui et al., 2001).
Based on the above arguments, the following hypotheses are proposed:
H5. OCBS is positively related to perceived service quality.
H6. OCBS is positively related to customer satisfaction.

Methodology
Scope of the study
In order to test the hypotheses proposed previously, an empirical study that is
quantitative in nature has been conducted. The financial sector, specifically in the
Spanish retail banking industry, has been selected for the empirical research. The
changes occurring in this area (greater competition and more demanding customers
have made service quality a current competitive weapon) and the service itself (strong
employee-customer contact) have promoted the application of general marketing
concepts. In the same line as the studies of Hallowell (1996) and Narver and Slater
(1990), behaviours in the offices of a single financial institution are explored. Similarly,
two units of analysis are contemplated. The first level refers to the bank branch where
the opinions of the executives (on MO) and of the employees (on their commitment to
the organization and their civic behaviour) is collected. The second level is composed
by customers and their perceptions on service quality and satisfaction with the service
provider.
Sample and data collection
The financial institution that is the subject of our study has currently 319 offices in two
major provinces in Spain. Following the convenience sample procedure and in
accordance with the company’s top management it was decided to select only those
offices located in cities with over 10,000 inhabitants, which redefined the sample to 106
offices. In each of these offices, information regarding the opinion of the director, of the
employees and a sample of customers provided the latter considered the office in
question as their principal bank and the one in which they carried out most of their
banking transactions (Table I), was gathered. A total of 184 employees and 3,623
customers were interviewed during March, April and May, distributed among the 106
branches according to their size. Customers were approached by the interviewer before
contact with the employee took place. Data was collected for 1 h, 5 days a week, at a
different time each day, with a minimum requirement of 30 responses from each

Measurement scales
Measurement scales are chosen on the basis of two criteria: consistency with the
theoretical approach followed, and psychometrical properties already validated in
other empirical research. Although there is currently a lack of consensus about the
most suitable scale to measure MO (Uncles, 2000), the present study adopts the 15-item
MKTOR scale (Narver and Slater, 1990) basing this decision on the following
arguments:
.
it is suitable to the goals of the investigation since it focuses on the customer and
the competition (Verhees, 1998);
.
it is appropriate for evaluating the company’s current commitment to its
customer base (Gauzente, 1999); and
.
it has better reliability, validity and unidimensionality (Farrell and Oczkowski,
1997).
To measure the organizational commitment of the employees, the Jaworski and Kolhi
(1993) scale of seven items is selected instead of other alternatives due fundamentally
to the fact that there are no appreciable differences among the different tools and the
Jaworski-Kohli scale has fewer items. Similarly, the scale proposed by Netemeyer et al.
(1997) is selected to measure employees’ OCBS. These authors, starting from the work
of MacKenzie et al. (1993), develop a scale of 12 items to collect the four dimensions
they identify in this construct: “sportsmanship” (three items), “civic virtue” (three
items), “civic conscience” (three items) and “altruism” (three items).
Initially the SERVQUAL scale (Parasuraman et al., 1988) in the modified version
that the authors proposed in 1991, was chosen for measuring service quality.
Nevertheless, since service quality is an endogenous variable in the empirical study,
following the recommendations of Zeithaml et al. (1996) only the perception scale
(SERVPERF) and not that of expectations is estimated. In conclusion, the perception
scale that we used contains 22 items grouped into five dimensions: “tangibility” (four
items), “reliability” (four fouritems), “responsiveness” (four items), “security” (four
items) and “empathy” (five items). Finally customer satisfaction was evaluated with
the scale proposed by Maloles (1997). This one-dimensional scale consists of nine items
that has been slightly reworded to adapt it to the banking industry.

Selection of statistical techniques in any research study depends on the objectives
being pursued. In the present study, since the main objective is to establish cause-effect
relationships among the different variables involved, structural equation modeling

Measurement model
The scales’ reliability and validity are assessed conducting a confirmatory factor
analysis. Specifically, Crombach’s a is estimated to test internal consistency, whereas
R 2 accounts for each item’s reliability, and, finally, composite reliability and extracted
variance of each construct are also calculated in order to test reliability of the measures.
Convergent validity is examined looking at standardized loadings of the items, along
with their critical ratios, while discriminant validity is assumed to exist between two
constructs when their squared correlation is lower than their extracted variances
(Tables II and III).
The results of the aforementioned process are displayed in Tables III and IV. Hence
with respect to the MO scale, Crombach’s a and CFA results suggest redefining items
MO2, MO6, MO7, MO11, MO14, and MO15. Values of the a coefficient (superior to 0.7),
the R 2 of the items (superior to 0.4), the extracted variance (superior to 0.5) and the
reliability of the construct (superior to 0.6) reveal that this scale is reliable and has
adequate internal consistency. Similarly, the standardized loading values (superior to
0.4) and the critical coefficients of the items (superior to 1.96) indicate the presence of
convergent validity. Lastly, it is possible to affirm the presence of discriminant validity
given that the variances extracted by the components of the scale are superior to the
squared correlation coefficients among these components.

validity.

Structural model
The structural model contains five latent variables, four endogenous variables
(COMMIT, OCBS, SQ and CUSTSAT) and one exogenous variable (MO). Following the
recommendations of other investigations (Babin and Boles, 1998; MacKenzie et al.,
1998) and given the great number of items, values for each of the constructs involved
have been averaged into a single indicator.
The proposed model (Figure 2) requires the homogenization of the data regarding
the three groups of individuals considered in the study: branch directors, employees
and customers. Data has been grouped by branch, reducing the sample to 94 sets of
responses. A detailed analysis determining the average and the variance of every
indicator preceded this grouping. Therefore, every piece of office information related to
its director (on the MO), the employees (average value of the employees of an office for
the COMMIT and OCBS variables) and the customers (average value of the customers
of an office for the SQ and CUSTSAT variables) has been included.
The multi-normality test recommends a maximum likelihood estimation method for
the estimation of the structural model. According to the results achieved, it is necessary
to eliminate the relationship between OCBS and CUSTSAT due to lack of statistical
significance. The new re-specification of the model shows a more satisfactory
adjustment as can be seen in Table V.
Discussion and implications for management
The results achieved in this research represent significant contributions both from a
theoretical perspective as well as for management in the service industry. Regarding
the consequences of MO on the internal environment of a company, results confirm a
positive test of hypotheses H1 and H2, although the first of these in a weaker than
expected form (Levy, 1997). These results prove the importance of analyzing the
mechanisms facilitating the establishment of a business strategy as well as the role of
the employees in this task. From a theoretical perspective, these findings reinforce the
research position stressing the role of the employees in the MO relationship and the
operational level of the business incorporating new evidence into this emerging field of
study.

The weakness of the first relationship might be explained because all organizational
change – which the development of a market oriented culture implies – requires a lot
of time. Hence the employee must forget part of his past conduct and learn new forms
of behaviour, MO acting as a source of socialization (Kelley, 1990). Therefore, the
temporal scope for the dissemination of MO in the firm may influence the weakness of
this relationship. Additionally, just because the employees know the so-called “rules of
the game” does not mean that they agree with them at all times. This could lead to a
certain discrepancy between perceived and desired values (of both the employee and
the business) about the existing orientation of the organization. In any case, findings
reveal the importance of coordinating a company’s internal activities with the aim of
continuously creating value for the customer. In other words, it is necessary to develop
an internal and external orientation towards this aim that makes the establishment of
the true MO philosophy possible. According to these arguments, and as an important
implication for business management, the need for greater concern on the part of
business management to encourage a suitable organizational climate and behaviour
that is truly oriented towards the employees’ market is fostered. In this way,
organizational practices should be emphasized since they are an instrument that
transfers corporate values and norms (Gatewood and Riordan, 1997).
In addition, the attitude of the people who make up the organization – management
and employees – represents an essential factor in the development of a “true MO”
insofar as the company leaders have the power to shift organizational systems and
structures in favour of the employees. The latter then create the climate aimed at
offering higher value to the customer. The role of management is fundamental to
overcome organizational inertia as well as employee’s resistance to changes intended to
motivate everyone in the company towards customer satisfaction. Furthermore, the
employee must have a positive attitude about learning norms and values, although a
large part of this attitude can be controlled by the management via recruitment,
communication, motivation and market oriented training. In summary, not only is a
MO culture required but also an appropriate climate must be created to support its
development through socialization of the employees – absence of discrepancy between
company and employee. A positive attitude will develop towards the job vis-à-vis
trying to achieve customer satisfaction.
Concerning the consequences of MO on the business’ external environment,
hypotheses H3 and H4 have been positively verified. Again, there are significant
contributions from these results on a theoretical level as well as in terms of implications
for service industry management. From a theoretical perspective, this study looks more
deeply at the positive correlation between MO and perceived service quality, showing
the need for connecting these two different organizational environments.
As shown previously, competitive advantage in the service industry is
fundamentally related to intangible assets (Kaplan and Norton, 2001). For this
reason, given the high level of customer commitment to the service process, it is utterly
necessary to develop close relationships between the business and its customers
whereby enabling the business to increase the value perceived by their customers.
Findings confirm that the firm’s MO encourages this relationship. Value creation for
the customer has a direct consequence: customer satisfaction and loyalty. Indeed,
customers may become true agents for the firm in the marketplace through referrals
(Reichheld and Sasser, 1990). A basic requirement for achieving this perception in the

Downloaded from http://www.emerald.com/ejm/article-pdf/39/5-6/646/446576/03090560510590755.pdf by WU Wirtschaftsuniversitet Wien user on 23 July

customer is that MO be disseminated throughout the prevailing culture of the firm.
Additionally, this chain of relationships entails an increase in the company’s
profitability which establishes the mediating role of perceived service quality and
satisfaction in the complex relationship between MO and business performance.
A market oriented business is continually studying how to improve its services in
order to reach a sustainable competitive advantage. This implies that on an operational
level the firm has to respond rapidly and anticipate the future needs and preferences of
its customers. Consequently, MO contributes to continuous education and to an
accumulation of knowledge that enables the creation of higher value. In the service
industry this relationship is even more accentuated. Thus, service industry businesses
that are more market oriented and possess greater knowledge of their customers
largely structure their service subject to their needs and preferences (Chang et al.,
1999). This results in the creation of an exceptional value for these businesses.
Additionally the positive relationship between MO and perceived service quality
suggests that the efforts carried out by business managers to focus on their customer
activities are rewarded by customers’ evaluation of the received services. Nevertheless,
it must be pointed out that this relationship could be stronger whether the appropriate
level of a firm’s MO actually is that which the customers thought it should be.
There are important implications for service managers. Hence businesses that are
more market oriented will obtain higher levels of customer perception of service
quality, achieving a sustainable competitive advantage. There is a need to increase the
level of knowledge of the customers’ needs and preferences so as to adjust service
assets to customer expectations. In order to do so, MO sets forth the essential
guidelines to be established in the firm at an operational level, increasing the efficiency
and effectiveness of its marketing actions.
Finally, on the topic of the relationship between the internal and external
environments of the organization, this study does not confirm the proposed working
hypotheses. Hypothesis H6, proposing the relationship between OCBS and customer
satisfaction, is not significant; also hypothesis H5 presents an inverse sense to that
proposed. There are several possible arguments that can help to justify these results.
In the first place, the high rates of employee turnover among the different branches
figuring in our study means that long-standing employees share offices with those who
have been only recently hired. This situation does not only affect the customers who
see the contact person in their branch continuously changing, but also affects the
“service climate” in the office and the relationships among the employees. For this
reason, efforts made by employees in their job may not be rewarded with an
appropriate perception by customers of service quality.
To study this situation in more depth, two large groups of bank branches were
distinguished: those whose perceived service quality scores were high versus those
with middle or low scores. It can be observed how in the second group of bank
branches the relationship between OCBS and perceived service quality was negative
due, essentially, to the “helping behaviour” dimension. These results coincide with

relationship between OCBS and perceived service quality is quite notable. The service
industry must be aware that encouraging extra-role behaviour in the firm constitutes a
significant mechanism to deliver service quality. Certainly, the organization has little
control over this type of behaviour, but it is possible to encourage a “service culture” to
increase the commitment of the employees to the company. Hence, mechanisms such as
rewards (economic or otherwise), personnel recruitment process and empowerment are
entirely valid tools to facilitate the achievement of such goals.
Limitations of the study
Limitations of our study can account for the anomalous relationships that have been
identified. First, the cross-sectional nature of the study could have been an obstacle to
revealing short-term answers. The design of this investigation, by nature causal and
cross-sectional, cannot assure in a strict way the correctness of associative variance,
temporality and absence of spurious association. In addition, the empirical study
focused on one particular case in the service industry – banking – hindering the
generalization of results. In the same way, although the model’s global adjustment is
adequate, the existence of many other variables that were not considered and that
could influence the relationships in study should not be forgotten. Finally, SEM
supposes the linearity of the causal relationship being studied, and the development
strategy of the model limits conclusions about the empirical base under consideration.
These limitations also provide some ideas for conducting future research projects.
One alternative is to measure MO by different informants, e.g. management and
customers. This would help to identify discrepancies in their perceptions. In this line,
the distinction between MO as a culture and MO as a behaviour could be interesting
since they have been considered as complementary measurement scales – as
suggested by Varela González et al. (1996) and Álvarez et al. (2000). Also, the design of
new measurement scales is relevant for those constructs whose indicators turned out to
be not consistent enough. Next, and considering the emphasis placed by the
development of a suitable organizational climate, empirical studies may contemplate
the formal mechanisms used by management to influence employee behaviour, e.g.
training, selection, empowerment, reward, etc. Moreover, employees and managers’
perceptions of such organizational practices would facilitate the analysis of results and
the creation of more precise conclusions. It would also be interesting to study the
different facets of organizational commitment – for example, affective, continuance

Furthermore, it would be convenient to investigate the motives that cause an

“political” motivation. Along these same lines, it might be possible to use sources of

longitudinal investigations and the study of the proposed model in different service
industries can yield interesting insights on the topic.
As a final comment, work by Lewis and Gabrielsen (1998) reveals that in the
service sector today – and specifically in the financial sector – relationship
marketing is overcoming the deficient role of the traditional marketing-mix as a
competitive base. The importance of long-term relationships with customers
requires a culture encouraging responsibility in all of the members of a business
towards the creation of value for the customer, or, an MO. In the same line, we
address the idea of Harris (1999) stating that the most important barrier in the
maintenance and development of MO lies in the members of the business, both
management and employees. Organizational processes and structures are not true
obstacles to the development of MO as an organizational behaviour since they can
be modified by the management. At the same time, employees can develop market
oriented behaviours via socialization. Socialization of the employee is a continual
process that can be found at different stages in the individual’s life (Louis, 1980).
The existence of a specified level of market oriented behaviour is evidence of the
socialization of the members of the business although this behaviour also serves as
a source of that socialization.
