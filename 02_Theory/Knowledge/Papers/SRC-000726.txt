Abstract
Effective altruists argue that charitable giving is over-influenced by empathic responses and should
instead be guided by cost-benefit analyses about the effectiveness of giving. To provide insight into the
extent to which empathy and effectiveness are associated with charitable giving in the population, two
meta-analyses are conducted, synthesising 416 effect sizes from 124 papers sampling 74,797
participants. Here we show that effectiveness, r = .34, SE = .04, 95%CIs [.28, .40], and empathy, r =

S

.25, SE = .02, CIs [.21, .29], both positively relate to charitable giving overall. However, prediction

ES

intervals reveal significant heterogeneity. Moderation analyses reveal one crucial caveat to the overall

PR

association between effectiveness and charitable giving: although the mean effect is relatively large

IN

when effectiveness is measured, r = .42, CIs [.35, .48], manipulated effectiveness has a weak effect, r =
.03, CIs [-.10, .17]. Our findings suggest that while people may self-perceive as effective altruists, they

TI

C

LE

give like empathic ones, a disjunction that calls for deeper causal investigation.

Introduction
Understanding why and to what extent people engage in charitable giving has long been a
preoccupation of social scientists.1 One reason for this is that charitable giving serves an important
function in sustaining a healthy and inclusive society. Estimated at over $470 billion per year in the U.S.
alone2, charitable giving funds help, care, and services in ways that cannot be readily substituted

ES

S

through commercial or government activities. As such, charitable giving is designed to enrich lives, and

PR

in many cases to save lives. Another reason charitable giving has preoccupied scholars is its symbolic
value in signifying the degree to which people engage in kindness and compassion to others.

IN

Understanding charitable giving helps us understand who we are as humans, and how we express our

LE

humanity.

C

One of the biggest debates about charitable giving – both within and outside academia – is

TI

whether people rely too much on their “heart” when giving, and not enough on their “head”. In his book

A

R

Against Empathy3 – and in high-profile opinion pieces4 – Paul Bloom makes the case that empathy is a
poor moral guide to charitable giving. In line with the empathy-altruism hypothesis5,6, Bloom agrees
that empathy plays a role in activating the motivation to do good. However, it also leads us to make
ineffective or unfair charitable decisions, focusing on issues that respond to our immediate emotional
triggers rather than grounding our compassion in rational deliberation about which causes do most good
(i.e., effectiveness considerations). Consistent with this notion, effective altruism scholars propose
decision-making frameworks for judging how effective one’s giving will be such as “How many people
benefit, and by how much?” and “What are the chances of success?”7-9 Although earlier critiques
positioned empathy and effectiveness as competing motives, recent evidence suggests they may be
mutually reinforcing, particularly when empathic concern is broadened to include distant others and is
guided by reasoning about impact.10-13

ARTICLE IN PRESS

To assess research on the association between charitable giving, effectiveness, and empathy, a
key distinction lies between studies that rely on self-reports of these constructs and studies that
experimentally manipulated them. While the debate about the role of empathy in charitable giving has
gained increasing attention in recent years, prior syntheses have excluded experimental designs, making
this comparison unclear. When it comes to effectiveness, this distinction becomes especially salient
given high-profile experiments suggesting that donors have relatively little appetite to seek out

S

information about welfare impact14, and that when it is provided effectiveness information has limited

ES

impact on charitable giving15 and choice of charities16.

PR

The goal of this paper is to add an important missing piece to the debate: meta-analyses of the

IN

extent to which charitable responses are, in fact, associated with empathy and perceived effectiveness.
By combining the voices of thousands of participants sampled by hundreds of researchers across several

LE

decades, one is in a stronger position to distinguish signal from noise when examining the associations

TI

C

between charitable giving, empathy, and effectiveness. We emphasise that the goal of this paper is not

R

to compare empathy and effectiveness directly. There are too few studies that measure both constructs

A

within the same sample, and the fields from which these constructs originate use different
operationalisations. Instead, our aim is to provide a descriptive and independent synthesis of the extent
to which each construct is associated with charitable giving, and under what conditions.
Before doing so, some definitional clarification is required. Empathy is a multi-dimensional
construct, one that is often used interchangeably with constructs such as sympathy and compassion. A
particularly important distinction is made between “emotional empathy”, which Bloom refers to as
“feeling what you believe other people feel – experiencing what they experience” (p.3)3 and “cognitive
empathy”, which is variously referred to in the literature as social intelligence, theory of mind, and
perspective taking. In Against Empathy, Bloom makes it clear that his case is primarily about the limits
of affective empathy. So although we meta-analyse all operationalisations of empathy here, we note that

ARTICLE IN PRESS

it is affective empathy that is most closely aligned to the debate that inspired the current paper. As
illustrated below, empathy has been operationalised in many different ways (e.g., cognitive versus
affective; dispositional versus situational; experimentally versus correlationally) and drilling down into
the patterns for each helps illuminate the different ways in which empathy may be associated with
charitable giving. Empirical synthesis is overdue: the first meta-analysis on the link between empathy
and prosocial behaviour was published nearly 40 years ago, and incorporated only a handful of studies

S

on charitable giving.17 Subsequent syntheses have not examined experimental work, focusing

ES

exclusively on correlational links between measures of empathy and questionnaire measures of

PR

dispositional pro-social behaviour18,19 or between measures of empathy and resource allocations in lab-

IN

based games such as the Dictator Game and the Prisoner’s Dilemma20.
In extracting data for the effectiveness meta-analysis, we excluded the related literature on

LE

efficiency, that is, the proportion of money that goes to beneficiaries as opposed to being absorbed by

TI

C

administrative costs.21,22 We made this decision in line with the arguments of effective altruism

R

advocates, who make it clear that the efficiency of a charity should not be a primary criterion for

A

judging whether it is effective (i.e., a charity for which 99.99% of income is funnelled directly towards a
pointless mission is efficient but not effective)7. Instead, we meta-analyse studies that measure or
manipulate variables sometimes referred to as “response efficacy”, “perceived donation efficacy”, or
“perceived impact of donation”: constructs that are measured through items such as “How much good
do you think you can do by donating money to this organisation?” or “To what extent do you think that
your donation would make a positive difference?”
Finally, we restricted our analysis to charitable giving, as distinct from other forms of prosocial
behaviour. We defined charitable giving as “voluntary contributions of money that benefit people
outside the giver’s family”.1 We had two reasons for restricting the analysis to charitable giving. First, it
is an operationalisation of giving that most closely aligns to the effective altruism movement, which is

ARTICLE IN PRESS

best known for providing moral context around which charities to support financially. Second, by
restricting the range to one domain of prosocial behaviour, we reduce statistical noise and unwanted
heterogeneity associated with comparing effect sizes across different behaviours.23 Patterns of altruism
across domains (e.g., donating money versus blood versus time) are psychologically distinct24, so
focusing just on charitable giving leaves us in a better position to make like-for-like comparisons
between studies.

S

Here, we show through two meta-analyses that empathy is a robust correlate of charitable giving

ES

and reliably increases generosity when experimentally evoked. Perceived effectiveness, on the other,

PR

hand predicts giving strongly at the self-report level and weakly under experimental manipulation. We

IN

conclude that people give like empathic altruists even when they see themselves as effective ones, a
pattern that sharpens the empirical context within which debates about effective altruism unfold.

interpretation, we report values transformed back to Pearson r correlations. Given that the majority of
articles included in both meta-analyses reported multiple dependent effect sizes, we employed a three-

tests of significance are 2-tailed unless otherwise specified.
We examined publication bias in two ways: visual inspection of the Funnel Plots (where

presence of dependent effect sizes. As can be seen in Fig. 1, no clear or systematic asymmetry is shown

Discussion

S

We meta-analysed 416 effect sizes from 74,797 participants to answer two fundamental

ES

questions: To what extent is empathy associated with charitable giving?; and To what extent is

PR

effectiveness associated with charitable giving? Consistent with the empathy-altruism hypothesis5,6,

IN

empathy shared a small-to-medium sized association with charitable giving (r = .25). This association
was statistically robust across a wide range of samples and operationalisations of charitable giving,

LE

although significant heterogeneity was observed. The association was strongest when empathy was

TI

C

operationalised in terms of its emotional components, but remained reliable even among studies that

R

measured the cognitive components of empathy or operationalised empathy as a dispositional tendency

A

to experiencing distress, unrelated to a particular target. Importantly, there was no statistically
significant difference in the average effect regardless of whether empathy was measured or manipulated.
So not only did people’s self-reports of empathy predict their charitable giving, but experimenters’
invocations of empathy successfully increased charitable giving. In sum, the empirical research
indicates that people are indeed influenced by empathy when making decisions about charitable giving.
Although charitable giving is a valid and well-studied expression of altruism, it is only one form
among many. It remains an open question whether the patterns we observe here generalise to other
forms of altruism that carry higher personal costs, such as organ donation, stem cell registration, or
direct physical aid. For instance, Rhoads and colleagues found that self-reported empathic concern was
not a consistent predictor of real-world extraordinary altruism in these domains across six populations of

ARTICLE IN PRESS

altruistic individuals.31 Charitable giving may be more strongly influenced by empathy because it is (a)
low-cost, (b) normatively endorsed, and (c) easily enacted. Additionally, some other forms of prosocial
behaviour (e.g., volunteering, organ donation, sperm donation) provide more direct payoffs to the
“giver” and so may be less empathically motivated.32-34 We encourage future research to test whether
the role of empathy in charitable giving is driven by these contextual features, and to explore whether
different psychological profiles underlie high-cost versus low-cost altruism.

S

In our second meta-analysis we found a medium-to-large association between perceived

ES

effectiveness and charitable giving (r = .42). Despite this, manipulations of effectiveness did not have a

PR

statistically significant effect on charitable giving. In short, we identify an apparent “effectiveness

IN

paradox”: people self-report in ways that imply effectiveness is very important to them but there was no
statistically significant effect of actual effectiveness information presented in experiments.

LE

Why would there be such a discrepancy between the correlational and experimental evidence for

TI

C

the role of effectiveness? One possible answer is that the corpus of experimental studies is simply too

R

small to draw reliable conclusions (this part of the meta-analysis was built on 29 effect sizes, far fewer

A

than available for the correlational analyses). We acknowledge this limitation, although it is possible
also that the small number of studies is a consequence rather than a cause of the null result. In the
context of a high-profile debate about effective altruism – one that straddles across disciplines within
academia but also has extended beyond academia – one might think that experimental tests of
effectiveness manipulations would be low-hanging fruit for researchers in this space. The surprisingly
small number of studies available to the meta-analysis suggests potential for a file drawer problem:
researchers do not seek to progress their null results of publication because they may be (mis)construing
them as “failed” experiments that are not diagnostic of real-world behaviours.
With a modest body of work to draw on, it is also possible that the size of the overall association
is unreasonably diluted due to subtle manipulations. Speaking against this explanation, however, most

ARTICLE IN PRESS

of the effect sizes contained in this section of the meta-analysis were face-valid and relatively blatant.
For example, 19 of the 29 effect sizes came from studies in which participants were taught about
effective altruism in much the same way as effective altruists themselves present their arguments to the
public (e.g., the cost of training a guide dog versus the cost of preventing blindness in developing
countries35; comparing the donation response to the “Baby Jessica” incident in Texas with the much
larger number of lives that could be saved through other causes4). In most of these paradigms,

S

participants are then directed to choose donation amounts for a charity described as being highly

ES

effective (versus no information), often using charities that directly correspond to the effective altruism

PR

lessons provided in the experimental stimuli. We conducted a posthoc meta-analysis of the effect sizes

IN

in which participants were taught about effective altruism. The meta-analysis of just these effect sizes
revealed a larger overall effect (r = .10) although this was still non-significant, p = .230, t = 1.24,

LE

95%CIs[-.07, .27]. The fact that blatant manipulations such as these have relatively muted effects on

C

charity decisions – manipulations which explicitly seek to direct participants to more effective choices

A

effectiveness paradox.

R

TI

and willingly invite demand characteristics – suggests a more psychological explanation for the

One such psychological explanation is that people have limited (and imperfect) insight into the
drivers of their own behaviour. Decades of research in social psychology have described how selfperception is vulnerable to bias, often skewed in ways that preserve an image of ourselves as both
prosocial and logical.36 For example, it seems reasonable that people want to think of themselves as
driven by genuinely altruistic motives rather than by the “warm glow” or emotional utility that
donations provide. Faced with this self-expectation, it is possible that people rationalise their giving
behaviours as effective even when they are primarily driven by less analytical motives (i.e., the causal
path flows “backwards” from giving to effectiveness). This could help account for why correlational
effects for effectiveness would be larger than experimental effects.

ARTICLE IN PRESS

Another possibility is that people see effectiveness as relevant in an abstract sense, but when
making specific charitable decisions they de-prioritise effectiveness in a mindful and explicit way. It has
been argued previously that many donors see their giving behaviour as a signal of personal choice, and
for this reason actively embrace the notion that it should be based on what is personally meaningful to
them rather than what might be most effective in the world.16 In the words of Caviola and colleagues,
“For many, a donor choosing a charity is more like a gourmand choosing a restaurant than a doctor

S

prescribing a treatment”.37

ES

Further clues for the effectiveness paradox can be found in the parallel literature on pro-

PR

environmental behaviour. That literature reveals strong and consistent evidence that people’s proenvironmental behaviours are positively associated with perceptions that their individual actions can

IN

make a difference in terms of mitigating climate change.38 However, there is no consistent evidence that

LE

experimenters can increase pro-environmental behaviour by promoting individual efficacy.39,40 In other

TI

C

words, the pattern resembles what was uncovered in the current manuscript: strong correlational

R

evidence for the role of effectiveness but non-significant experimental evidence. In making sense of this

A

pattern, some theorists have disputed the classic assumption that ratings of effectiveness flow on from
an analytical process: an effortful, mindful, and rational process of weighing up the inputs and outputs
of an action. Rather, a case has been made that effectiveness perceptions are formed through nonanalytical processes: vulnerable to motivated reasoning, infused with emotion, susceptible to social
desirability and identity-expressive concerns, and difficult to change through instruction.41 If this
reasoning is translated into the current context, it might help explain why the correlational evidence for
effectiveness as a correlate of charitable giving does not translate into useful interventions. Specifically,
self-reports of effectiveness may reflect primarily non-analytic considerations (e.g., emotion, identity,
rationalisation) whereas experimental manipulations work from the flawed assumption that people

ARTICLE IN PRESS

engage deliberative mental algorithms about effectiveness, processes that can be honed and improved
through explicit instruction.
As for any synthesis of existing evidence, limitations of our meta-analysis are a distillation of the
limitations of the field. We re-emphasise that meta-analytic syntheses cannot make a definitive
statement about whether instruction about effectiveness can have a causal influence on charitable
giving. Failure to detect experimental effects does not equate to disproof of causality, and experimental

S

null results can arise from numerous factors (e.g., manipulation weakness, psychological reactance,

ES

entrenched priors). Weak experimental effects may also be due to contextual features such as the

PR

absence of side-by-side comparisons, variation in donor norms, or limited ecological validity in some

IN

lab paradigms. Rather than ruling out an influence of information about effectiveness, the data highlight
a tension in the literature: despite robust correlational evidence suggesting that perceived effectiveness

LE

predicts giving, experimental attempts to shift perceptions of effectiveness frequently fail to influence

TI

C

donation behaviour. This is a pattern that raises questions rather than one that resolves them, and we call

R

for more nuanced and ecologically valid experimental work to explore potential causal pathways (e.g.,

A

longer-term, field-based interventions, or studies that leverage existing variation in perceived
effectiveness). Future research should also explore how different dimensions of study quality (e.g.,
ecological validity, risk of bias, design strength, attrition) may affect conclusions in this area. In sum,
our effect sizes should not be treated as literal estimates of variance explained in real-world contexts,
but rather as indicators of the extent to which these constructs are currently leveraged successfully in
both correlational and experimental work. This distinction highlights the current limits of effectivenessbased interventions and the need for more ecologically valid research in this domain.
Table 1 reveals another clear limitation in the literature: an extreme restriction of range in terms
of cultural samples. Strikingly, all but 6 of the 151 effect sizes in the effectiveness library were drawn
from so-called WEIRD nations. Going forward, there needs to be much stronger representation from

ARTICLE IN PRESS

non-Western samples if the field can get a comprehensive understanding of this global issue. On the
upside, over 80% of the effect sizes were drawn from non-student samples, and 32% measured actual
donation behaviour as opposed to an intention or some other proxy of behaviour. Although many
researchers’ choices are presumably constrained by lack of funding, it does suggest a reasonable
quorum of evidence drawn from real behaviour among community samples.
Finally, we note that although empathy and effectiveness are often discussed in parallel – both in

S

public discourse and in theoretical debates – there are currently too few studies that measure both

ES

constructs within the same design to allow formal statistical comparisons between them. This limits the

PR

extent to which we can directly evaluate their relative contributions to charitable giving. We therefore

IN

encourage future research to explicitly examine both constructs within the same studies, using consistent
measures and parallel designs, to allow more direct comparisons of their predictive value.

LE

In sum, the current analysis sheds light on the current state of evidence regarding two

TI

C

fundamental questions: To what extent is charitable giving associated with empathy? And to what extent

R

is charitable giving associated with effectiveness? The data provide a reliable answer to the first

A

question: empathy is a moderately strong predictor of charitable giving, and interventions that draw on
the power of empathy are generally successful. The data provide an intriguing answer to the second
question: people self-report to be effective altruists, but there is little evidence that their charitable
decisions are, in fact, influenced by effectiveness information. We emphasise that answering these
questions do not constitute a “test” of the arguments summarised above about effective altruism and the
limitations of empathy: the arguments are complex and not reducible to simple empirical associations
between constructs. However, answering these questions will go some of the way to laying a
descriptive, empirical context within which philosophical arguments about effective altruism – and
practical interventions to improve the impact of charity efforts – can be situated.

Search strategy
Literature searches were initially run in November 2019 and the search was refreshed in
December 2022. The search was conducted across four databases: the two largest multidisciplinary
academic databases (Scopus and Web of Science) plus content-relevant databases in business
(ABI/Inform) and psychology (PsycINFO). For both libraries we used search terms nested around
charitable giving (donation*, donate, donor*, philanthrop*, charitable, "not for profit*", "non profit*",

S

nonprofit*, NGO*, "non governmental", “third sector” , "charit*") and the factor of interest:

ES

effectiveness (“effective altruism”, “effective giving”, "donation effective*", “charity effective*”,

PR

“nonprofit effective*”, efficacy, competence, "impact of gift", "donation impact", “nonprofit impact”,

IN

“charity impact”), and empathy (empath*, sympath*, compassion*). Searches were based on title,
abstract, and keywords. Results were supplemented through snowballing from reference lists and open

LE

calls for unpublished data distributed through X and mailing lists of the Association for Research on

TI

C

R

Personality and Social Psychology. To help identify relevant grey literature, we also screened the first

A

20 pages (200 results) in Google Scholar (consistent with standard practice)42 using the same search
terms identified in the database searching.
Inclusion criteria and screening
To be eligible for inclusion, studies needed to include a quantitative measure of charitable giving
(as part of correlational or experimental designs), be reported in English, and published 1980 or later.
Correlational studies allowed us to estimate the strength of naturalistic associations, while experimental
studies enabled us to examine causal effects of manipulating empathy or effectiveness on giving
behaviour. We excluded purely qualitative, theoretical, or descriptive studies because they do not
provide effect sizes or statistical associations necessary for meta-analysis. Similarly, we did not include
studies based on simulation models, ethnographic observation, or administrative records without direct

ARTICLE IN PRESS

measurement of the psychological constructs of interest, as these designs do not allow for computation
of comparable quantitative associations.
In these meta-analyses, we coded for several moderators. In doing so, we defined intention
likelihood as a self-reported assessment of the likelihood or willingness to donate to a charitable cause
(e.g., “How likely are you to donate to this organisation?”). Value was defined as the monetary value
participants reported they would donate or actually donated. We distinguished between measured

S

giving, where donation behaviours or intentions were assessed without experimental manipulation of the

ES

constructs of interest, and manipulated giving, where donation behaviours or intentions were measured

PR

after an experimental manipulation aimed at influencing empathy or perceived effectiveness. Self-report

IN

measures refer to participants’ stated responses to survey items about their donation intentions or
behaviour (e.g., likelihood of donating or intended donation amount), whereas behavioural measures

LE

involve participants making actual donation decisions, typically involving real or incentivised financial

TI

C

contributions.

R

There were no limits on age or nationality of participants. Papers that were not full-length

A

articles (e.g., book reviews, books, conference abstracts, corrections, legal judgements) were not
included. The second and third authors screened texts for eligibility on title and then again on abstract,
resolving discrepancies through discussion. Both screeners had experience conducting and publishing
meta-analyses previously. Screening on the full text was conducted by the second author. For articles
less than 10 years old that did not report bivariate effect sizes, we emailed authors to request this
information.
Analytic strategy
Following the screening process, data on the association between effectiveness and charitable
giving were analysed from 47 papers, comprising 81 independent samples and 157 effect sizes (N =
32,116). Data on the association between empathy and charitable giving were analysed from 77 papers,

ARTICLE IN PRESS

comprising 105 independent samples and 259 effect sizes (N = 42,681). Where an experiment both
manipulated and measured effectiveness (for example as a manipulation check) both effect sizes were
included (one in the “manipulated” category and one in the “measured” category).
We employed a three-level meta-analytic approach to calculate the overall effect size and
perform moderator analyses. To determine the appropriate level at which to model statistical
dependence among effect sizes, we compared alternative multilevel random-effects structures and

S

modelled dependence at the paper level; model comparisons and sensitivity analyses are reported in the

ES

OSF supplement. The total variance is partitioned into three components: sampling variance (Level 1),

PR

within-paper variance reflecting dependence among studies in the same article (Level 2), and betweenpaper variance (Level 3), thereby appropriately accounting for the clustering of effect sizes. To conduct

IN

LE

TI

C

The type of bivariate effect size extracted depended on study design, but we typically extracted
Pearson’s correlation coefficients (r) for correlational studies or Cohen’s ds for experimental studies. In
situations where these values were not provided by the study, we used test statistics (e.g., means and
standard deviations, F-values, frequency data) to calculate effect sizes. All effect sizes were transformed
to Pearson r correlations for the meta-analysis (see OSF for effect size conversion formulas).
A leave-one-out analysis did not identify any studies that influenced the overall findings. To
check for extreme effect sizes that may disproportionately influence the results, we screened for those
