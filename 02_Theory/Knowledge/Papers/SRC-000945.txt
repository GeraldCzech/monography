Abstract
Green brand equity (GBE) is an emerging topic that is attracting growing attention
from both scholars and managers. However, most empirical research in this field
has predominantly employed quantitative methods. This study aims to identify the
factors and criteria driving GBE ranking the relative importance of these factors and
criteria. The Delphi method and the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) were combined
to address research gaps. The participants included individuals with at least five years
of experience in consuming green agricultural products in Vietnam. The research
results indicate the relative importance of the factors for GBE from high to low as
follows: Subjective norms, emotional perceived values, functional perceived values,
green trust, corporate social responsibility, attitude toward green products, and
perceived behaviour control. The study also provided 24 criteria related to these
factors and demonstrated their weights concerning GBE. This could potentially be
the first study examining the relationship of all components within the theory of
planned behaviour with GBE while ranking their relative importance compared to
CSR and perceived value.
Keywords Green brand, Green brand equity, Brand management, Corporate social
responsibility, CSR, Perceived value
1 Introduction
Growing environmental concerns have ushered in the era of “green strategy”, pushing
sustainability issues to the forefront of numerous corporate agendas [1]. Companies are
facing increased public sensitivity, stricter regulations, and growing pressure from stakeholders regarding responsible business practices and natural environment conservation
[2]. Therefore, companies and governments are increasingly focusing on integrating
environmental responsibility into their marketing and brand management strategies [3].
There is evidence suggesting that consumers have a positive attitude toward products
and brands perceived to have little or no negative impact on the environment [4–6]. The
literature has also provided evidence that customers are willing to pay a premium for
brands that implement environmentally friendly policies [5].

In this context, companies are actively adjusting their brand equity management
strategies towards green brand equity (GBE) [6]. GBE is a recent trend that has been
attracting the attention of researchers, managers, and policymakers. Several studies have
shown the significant importance of GBE, which can positively influence consumers in
various ways, such as positive word-of-mouth [7], brand attitudes [6], purchase intentions [8], and even brand passion [4].
There have been several studies on the factors driving GBE across different industries and contexts. For instance, [9] studied the electronics industry in South Korea,
[10] focused on the cosmetics industry in Taiwan ROC, [6] examined shopping malls
in India, [11] explored refrigerators and paper towels in Turkey, [12] analyzed telecommunications and home appliances in Pakistan and Italy, [13] investigated hotels in India,
[14] researched fast-food restaurants in Pakistan, [15] explored banks in India, and [16]
looked into the banking sector in Vietnam. These studies predominantly employed
quantitative methods such as Structural Equation Modeling (SEM) and regression.
They emphasize drivers like brand image, attachment, satisfaction, trust, loyalty, perceived quality, brand awareness, and brand associations. Some studies have focused on
less commonly examined factors, such as green perceived value [13] or corporate social
responsibility [16], but have not compared their relative importance with other dimensions of GBE. Moreover, these studies primarily employed the SEM method and concentrated on sectors other than agricultural products.
Agricultural products are among Vietnam’s key and well-known export sectors, contributing significantly to the country’s GDP and overall export turnover. However,
according to Vietnam’s Ministry of Industry and Trade [17], agriculture is also one of
the main contributors to climate change, accounting for up to 18% of the nation’s total
greenhouse gas emissions. Emissions from Vietnam’s agricultural sector are projected
to reach 120 million tons of CO₂ by 2030. To fulfill its commitment at the 26th Conference of the Parties to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change
(COP26) to achieve net-zero emissions by 2050, greening the agricultural sector has
become an urgent priority. Therefore, it is crucial to research effective solutions for
enhancing GBE in Vietnam’s agricultural industry.
The existing research on GBE so far has certain notable gaps. Firstly, there appears to
be a lack of studies examining all the components of the Theory of Planned Behaviour
(TPB) as a foundation to explain the drivers behind GBE development. Most studies to
date have primarily focused on elements of GBE based on the definition of consumerbased brand equity of Keller [18] or explored the impact of marketing-mix factors on
the structure of GBE. However, there is evidence suggesting that GBE is constituted by
multiple factors from both the brand and individual consumer perspectives [19]. From
a perceived benefit standpoint, consumers’ intention to purchase green products might
stem from within themselves, not solely from stimuli provided by businesses through
marketing activities [20]. Secondly, there appears to be a lack of qualitative studies or a
combination of qualitative and quantitative approaches to provide more insights than
what conventional quantitative methods can typically explain. Particularly, there is an
apparent absence of studies integrating Delphi and Analytic Hierarchy Process, and
deriving GBE drivers based on the TPB structure. Despite the widely acknowledged
role of perceived value types in general marketing research [13], studies that specifically
examine the separate impacts of functional and emotional values on GBE, and compare

2 Theoretical background
2.1 Green brand equity

Brand equity can be considered from either a financial or consumer standpoint. When
viewed from the consumer’s perspective, it is referred to as consumer-based brand
equity [21]. Under this approach, brand equity is defined as the differentiated effects of
brand knowledge on consumer perception through brand marketing activities [18]. It
reflects the differences in customer choice between branded and non-branded products
[22].
Green brand equity is viewed as the perceptions, beliefs, and understanding in the
minds of consumers regarding a brand’s sustainability and environmental friendliness [23]. GBE encompasses a set of perceptions and behaviors of customers towards a
brand’s environmental responsibility commitments, which elevate the brand’s value [24].
Similar to brand equity, GBE refers to the added value gained by a brand. Consumers
are believed to favor a product with a higher brand value over another with the same
function. For green products, this added value relates to the product’s attributes meeting consumers’ environmental preservation needs. According to Bekk et al. [7], products
with higher GBE are given priority by consumers. Studies indicate a close relationship
between sustainable business practices and GBE [25].
2.2 Theory of planned behaviour

The Theory of Planned Behaviour by Ajzen [26] assumes that behavior can be predicted
or explained by intentions to perform that behavior. This theory posits that intention is
a function of three influencing factors: attitude toward the behavior, subjective norms,
and perceived behavioral control.
Attitude toward the behavior refers to an individual’s evaluation of the outcomes
derived from performing a specific behavior, indicating the degree of favourability or
unfavourability towards an individual’s behavior [26]. Accordingly, environmentally conscious consumers are expected to hold a positive attitude towards green brands to meet
their consumption objectives [19]. Some researchers have found that environmentally
responsible consumers often exhibit positive attitudes towards environmentally friendly
brands [21, 27, 28].
Subjective norms represent an individual’s perception of the importance that significant others attach to whether the behavior should or should not be performed. Thus,
an individual’s intention and behavior can be influenced by the opinions of significant
others, such as parents, spouses, friends, or colleagues [26]. Some studies related to GBE

have also suggested that positive word-of-mouth can impact visitation intentions, continued word-of-mouth, and willingness to pay a premium, aiding companies in attracting
new consumers [29–31]. However, as of now, there seems to be a lack of experimental
evidence showing that subjective norms drive intentions and behaviors toward consuming green products or supporting green brands.
Perceived behavioral control is an individual’s perception of the ease or difficulty in
performing a specific behavior, depending on the availability of resources and opportunities to carry out that behavior [26]. Therefore, it can be anticipated that human
behavior and intentions toward consuming green products may be influenced by their
perceptions of convenience, availability, opportunities, or challenges when purchasing
those products. There is evidence indicating that establishing green distribution channels creates convenience for consumers, consequently positively impacting trust and
loyalty toward green brands [2]. However, to date, the literature appears to provide very
little empirical evidence on the relationship between perceived behavioral control and
GBE.
Although widely applied in green consumer behavior research, TPB has faced several
criticisms. A key limitation is the attitude-behavior gap, where consumers may hold
positive attitudes toward green products but fail to act accordingly [31]. Additionally,
components such as subjective norms and perceived behavioral control have shown
inconsistent results in predicting actual behavior [32–34]. As a result, many studies
have proposed extending TPB by incorporating factors like perceived value, willingness
to pay, or green identity to enhance its predictive power [33]. Moreover, TPB needs to
be contextually adapted, as cultural and social factors, such as material values or social
media influence, can significantly alter green consumption intentions and behavior [35,
36].
2.3 Green trust, green perceived value

Green trust is the willingness to trust a product, service, or brand based on belief or
expectation of its environmental effectiveness [23]. Several studies have demonstrated
that trust in green brands is a fundamental driver of GBE [11, 17, 18]. Trust in the effectiveness of a green product regarding its environmentally friendly features and natural environment preservation is a key criterion for customers when purchasing it [23].
Therefore, green trust can strengthen the intention to purchase green products [35].
Furthermore, trust in a green brand indicates a higher likelihood that its customers will
support it, and provide positive feedback, thereby enhancing GBE [36]. Papista et al.
[5] identified the multidimensional drivers of GBE, including green trust, green brand
image, green satisfaction, green brand experience, green customer value, and green marketing mix (7Ps). Among these, the role of green trust is particularly emphasized. When
consumers believe that a brand is genuinely committed to environmental sustainability,
it has a significant and positive impact on brand equity [37, 38].
Green perceived value is the comprehensive assessment of the extent to which consumers perceive their green needs to be met during an experience [39]. Customer perceived value is viewed as an exchange between perceived costs and perceived benefits
[38]. Brands can offer both functional and emotional value to consumers [40]. Functional value refers to the practical and utilitarian benefits that a product provides, such
as quality, performance, and cost-efficiency. It reflects how well a product fulfills its

intended purpose [40]. In contrast, emotional value captures the affective and psychological gratification derived from the product, such as enjoyment, comfort, or positive
feelings evoked by its use. While functional value appeals to rational judgment, emotional value resonates with personal feelings and experiences [40]. These two dimensions
often interact but remain conceptually distinct, each contributing uniquely to consumer
decision-making. Li et al. [24], and Papista et al. [5] demonstrate that perceived value by
consumers is highly significant in constructing and developing GBE. Therefore, creating
greater value for customers will enhance the relationship between consumers and green
brands, and conversely, customer reactions to green brands contribute to shaping GBE
[24].
Although green perceived value stands as a crucial factor in the strategy for GBE
development, the number of studies on this driver remains limited to date. Particularly,
there appears to be a lack of research evaluating the impact of emotional values, and
functional values on GBE while ranking their importance concerning other factors.
2.4 Corporate social responsibility

McWilliams and Siegel [41] define Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) as actions
that produce positive social outcomes and go beyond the interests of the firm and legal
requirements. CSR is understood as a comprehensive construct encompassing multiple dimensions, including corporate citizenship, business ethics, sustainability, environmental responsibility, and both social and financial performance [42]. Rather than
being merely philanthropic, CSR is increasingly viewed as a strategic move to enhance
brand value by shaping social identity and encouraging consumer engagement [43]. Key
elements such as a positive brand image and consumer trust, which are strengthened
through CSR initiatives, serve as essential building blocks for sustainable brand value
[44, 45].
CSR has a clear and positive impact on brand value in general—and particularly on
GBE in the context of environmentally focused initiatives. Gilal et al. [4] found that consumer perceptions of CSR significantly boost brand trust and passion. CSR activities
aimed at environmental sustainability, such as green advertising or zero-packaging initiatives, help increase brand awareness, trust, satisfaction, and loyalty, thereby strengthening green brand equity [44, 46]. Moreover, the relationship between CSR and GBE
is mediated by factors like brand awareness, brand image, brand loyalty, and purchase
intention [47]. These findings underscore CSR’s role as a strategic tool in building and
maintaining brand equity, especially when CSR is coherently integrated into the brand’s
core values and aligned with stakeholder expectations across global markets [48].

3 Methodology
3.1 Research design

This study utilizes a combination of the Delphi and Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP)
methods. The integration of these two methods is considered an effective way to make
decisions in complex situations [49], aiding in reducing individual biases while enhancing the efficiency and transparency of the decision-making process [50]. It is also seen
as the amalgamation of powerful tools for decision-making across various fields, including business, policy, and science [51]. The application of the Delphi and AHP methods
is particularly appropriate given the complexity and exploratory nature of the research

Page 5 of 20

Hue and Huyen Discover Sustainability

topic. GBE is an emerging area with limited empirical evidence, especially regarding
the relative importance of various influencing factors. The Delphi method enables systematic expert consultation to identify and refine key factors, while AHP allows for the
structured comparison and prioritization of these factors based on participant judgment. Combining these methods not only enhances the validity of the findings by reducing individual bias but also improves the transparency and rigor of the decision-making
process.
Figure 1 illustrates the research design of this study. Firstly, we examined relevant literature on GBE across different countries and industries. Subsequently, we identified
gaps and specified the particular research objectives. Drivers that have been minimally
explored or absent in the literature but have theoretical foundations or scientific bases
were selected for examination. Then, we proceeded to develop specific criteria (within
the factors), based on the TPB and the overall literature review.
Following that, we invited six experts to contribute their opinions on the criteria set
to evaluate their suitability for the agricultural sector within the context of Vietnam.
Among these experts, three were retail store managers specializing in the sale of green
agricultural produce (acting both as consumers and sellers), while the remaining three
were researchers and lecturers in the field of brand management, holding doctoral
degrees. All experts possessed a minimum of five years’ experience in consuming green

3.2 Delphi

The Delphi method is a technique introduced by Dalkey and Helmer [52] to gather opinions from a group of experts systematically on a specific issue to be resolved. Subsequently, Murry and Hammons [53] developed the modified Delphi method suggesting
the use of structured questionnaires to focus participants on the issue being discussed
and save time [54]. This study utilizes the modified Delphi method, allowing the
researcher to begin with structured questions and also initiate some open-ended questions [55].
In the first Delphi round, the questionnaire comprised 25 questions. It began with
an explanation of the GBE concept and a screening question to select survey participants, ensuring they have at least five years of experience in consuming green agricultural products. Following this were demographic questions. Then, questions about
attitudes towards green products were adapted from [19], and questions about green
trust were adapted from [9] and [23]. The remaining questions on social norms, perceived behavioral control, functional perceived values, and emotional perceived values
were self-developed by the authors based on the TPB, Literature, and expert feedback.
The structured questions were designed using a 5-point Likert scale, from highly important (5 points) to not important (1 point). Finally, there was an open-ended question for
participants to provide further explanation and propose additional criteria for developing GBE.
Participants in the first Delphi round comprised 39 individuals. The mean value and
Content Validity Ratio (CVR) will be used to analyze the data, assisted by Excel software.
The CVR is an index used to assess the level of expert agreement regarding the necessity of each item or criterion. Each expert rates an item as “essential,” “useful but not
essential,” or “not necessary.” According to Lawshe [56], CVR is calculated based on the
number of experts who rate an item as “essential.” If the CVR value exceeds a specific
threshold, depending on the number of experts (for example, a Minimum CVR of 0.31
is required for a panel of 35 or more), the item is considered to Have acceptable content

3.3 Analytic hierarchy process

To assess which factors drive GBE and their relative importance, numerous criteria are
required for evaluation. Therefore, this evaluation process can be considered a multi-criteria decision-making process, and the application of the AHP technique is suitable [49].
According to Saaty [49], AHP has several benefits, including (1) focusing on determining
the importance of each criterion, thus helping overcome shortcomings of many other
methods and facilitating their integration to leverage the advantages of each method; (2)
potentially enhancing reliability by checking consistency in experts’ evaluation results;
(3) being straightforward, considering both quantitative and qualitative factors, as well
as both major and minor criteria.
When integrating Delphi with AHP, Khorramshahgol & Moustakis [57] recommend
avoiding an excessive number of experts as interview subjects; typically, 5–15 people are
most appropriate. We collected responses from ten participants who participated in the
initial two rounds of Delphi for this AHP round. The questionnaire was designed based
on the results obtained from the two Delphi rounds. The data were analyzed using MS
Excel.
According to Saaty [49], AHP can be implemented through the following five steps:
3.3.1 Step 1: Structuring the Hierarchy

The first step involves constructing a hierarchical structure of the decision problem. This
hierarchy typically includes the overall goal at the top level, followed by the evaluation
criteria and sub-criteria, and finally the alternatives at the lowest level. The hierarchical
decomposition helps decision-makers handle complex problems in a systematic way.
3.3.2 Step 2: Pairwise comparisons

In this step, decision-makers conduct pairwise comparisons of elements at each level of
the hierarchy concerning their impact on the elements at the higher level. The fundamental 1–9 scale of Saaty [49] is used to represent the intensity of importance or preference. These judgments are used to construct a pairwise comparison matrix A = |aij |,
where aij indicates the relative importance of element i compared to element j.
3.3.3 Step 3: Deriving priority vectors

To determine the relative weights of the elements, the pairwise comparison matrix is
normalized, and the priority vector w is derived. This is typically done by calculating the
principal right eigenvector of the matrix:

4.3 Analytic hierarchy process

Table 2 presents the results of ranking the importance level of factors driving GBE and
the consistency ratio of the assessment results from the participants. The CR is less than
0.1, ensuring consistency in the respondents’ answers [49]. Thus, subjective norms, a
component of TPB and seemingly unexplored in GBE literature, were evaluated as the
most crucial factor. Following this are emotional perceived values, functional perceived
values, green trust, corporate social responsibility, attitude toward green products, and
perceived behaviour control, in descending order of importance.
Table 3 displays local weights, global weights, CR, and the ranking results of the criteria’s importance. The five most crucial criteria are SNO1, SNO2, EPV1, FPV1, and
GTR1, which belong to subjective norms, emotional perceived values, functional perceived values, and green trust factors. Although the new CSR criteria added in the second Delphi round are not ranked as high compared to other criteria, they still achieved
consensus among the participants. The consistency ratio results indicate that all factors

fall below the acceptable threshold (CR < 0.1), ensuring the reliability of the pairwise
comparison process in the AHP model [49]. Some factors, such as attitude toward green
products (0.085), functional perceived values (0.084), and corporate social responsibility
(0.084), show relatively higher CR values, which may be attributed to the large number
of criteria within each group, making it cognitively demanding for experts, especially
for more abstract constructs like CSR or technically complex ones like functional values. Nevertheless, these CR values remain within acceptable limits. In contrast, factors

experts. This may be due to the clearer and more distinct nature of the criteria within
these groups, which are commonly understood and consistently interpreted. Overall,
these results strengthen the reliability of the AHP model’s outcomes.

5 Discussion and implication
5.1 Discussion

This study contributes significantly to the theoretical aspects. Firstly, it might be pioneering in comprehensively examining the factors of the TPB (attitude toward green
products, subjective norms, perceived behavior control) and demonstrating their crucial roles concerning GBE in comparison to other factors. Secondly, the ranking of the
importance of various GBE-driving factors through the integration of the Delphi and
AHP methods represents a rare approach to addressing gaps in the literature on GBE.
This combination of qualitative and quantitative methods has provided additional information and explanations regarding the essential roles of subjective norms, emotional
values, and corporate social responsibility in GBE development strategies. Thirdly, the
distinct exploration of the relationship between emotional values, functional values, and
GBE is a fresh approach compared to existing literature, highlighting and explaining the
significance of each value type in consumer perceptions. Until now, there have been only
a few experimental studies primarily employing linear structural models to explore the
relationship between perceived value in general and GBE. Lastly, this is also one of the
very few studies examining the relationship between CSR and GBE (not general brand
equity).
Subjective norms emerged as the most important factor as ranked by the participants.
The criteria under subjective norms, SNO1 (My family encourages me to use green
products) and SNO2 (My friends encourage me to use green products), were ranked
first and second, respectively. This finding is intriguing because surprisingly, despite the
wide use of TPB to explain intention and behavior in business, there seems to be scarce
experimental research examining the relationship between all three TPB components
and GBE. This study has highlighted the significance of the opinions of reference groups
such as family, friends, colleagues, and the public around each consumer. This can be
understood in the context of Asian countries like Vietnam and the diversity of agricultural products, which are used daily and often rely heavily on word-of-mouth recommendations. These societies are known for their high-context nature and collectivism,
where decision-making and actions are preferred based on the opinions of significant
others.
Among the items of subjective norms, the influence of family and friends (SNO1,
SNO2) emerged as the most influential in green consumption behavior, as they share

Page 13 of 20

Hue and Huyen Discover Sustainability

(2025) 6:932

close personal relationships with the consumer. In contrast, the influence from the
broader community (SNO4) has the lowest global weight (0.032), suggesting that social
pressure from the general public has less impact. This may be attributed to the fact that
in emerging markets with rapid economic growth and high consumption levels, such
as Vietnam, public awareness of environmental protection remains limited, and the
broader community has yet to significantly influence individuals’ green consumption
intentions.
Emotional perceived values and functional perceived values are the second and third
most influential factors driving GBE, with the emotional criterion “These brands make
me more confident about the health of my family” ranking third in global importance.
This suggests that affective benefits, particularly those tied to consumer well-being and
environmental reassurance, play a central role in shaping perceptions of green brands.
In comparison, the functional dimension, as represented by “Green features satisfy my
needs,” ranked slightly lower but still reflects a strong utilitarian motivation.
Some prior studies have demonstrated a positive relationship between perceived values in general with GBE [13, 58, 59]. This study reinforces previous findings on the role
of perceived value in GBE within the agricultural sector and the Vietnamese context,
which differs from the industries and settings previously investigated, such as the hospitality sector in [13]. However, to date, there has been limited research isolating psychological and functional values for experimental evaluation. For instance, Winit and
Kantabutra [60] discussed that psychological and functional benefits positively impact
brand equity in general (not GBE). Sweeney and Soutar [40] positioned functional value
as the primary driver in their PERVAL framework, particularly in contexts where product quality, performance, and price dominate decision-making. Emotional value, while
acknowledged, was generally secondary to utilitarian concerns in their study. However,
it is important to note that their work focused on broader consumer value judgments
across product categories and not specifically within the green branding context. This
study reveals a shift in emphasis within the green market, where emotional perceived
values play a more dominant role than functional values in influencing green brand
equity. This contrasts with traditional models like [40], which prioritize functional value
and do not empirically address this hierarchy in a green branding context. In emerging markets such as Vietnam, where environmental concerns are increasingly pressing,
consumers tend to value brands that offer not only functional benefits but also emotional experiences tied to health, safety, and pride. This highlights the need to adapt
conventional value frameworks to the socio-environmental realities of sustainable
consumption.
Green trust was ranked as the fourth factor. Although there have been numerous
experimental pieces of evidence concerning the relationship between green trust and
GBE, we aimed to pioneer in examining the relative importance of this factor in comparison to the components of TPB and perceived values. Prior studies have demonstrated
that green trust has a positive, direct impact on GBE or serves as an intermediary in the
relationship between attitude toward green sponsorship or GBE in the dairy industry
[61], between CSR or green brand association, or green brand satisfaction with GBE in
the banking sector [16]. The findings of this study further reinforce those earlier results,
but in a new context: the agricultural sector in Vietnam. However, when compared
with other factors like subjective norms and perceived values, green trust appears to

Page 14 of 20

Hue and Huyen Discover Sustainability

(2025) 6:932

be relatively less influential and should be considered in conjunction with other antecedents of GBE. To effectively enhance green trust and GBE, priority should perhaps be
given to strengthening subjective norms (through education, family, schools, and public
advocacy) and perceived values (by improving customers’ real-life experiences).
Corporate social responsibility was ranked fifth in importance among the seven
factors. Similarly, its items, CSR1, CSR2, CSR3, and CSR4, were also ranked at moderately high levels, positioned at 11th, 19th, 23rd, and 24th respectively in the overall
ranking. The role of CSR in GBE has been explored by a few recent studies [16, 61]. A
unique aspect of our study is the integration of the CSR factor with other factors and
their weighted rankings. The research results have indicated that CSR continues to play
a significant role in the GBE development strategy of agricultural businesses. However,
businesses should give greater priority to subjective norms, perceived values, and green
trust, particularly in emerging markets like Vietnam, where public concerns about greenwashing, the practice of presenting products or activities as more environmentally
friendly than they truly are, are increasing consumer skepticism [62]. Greenwashing has
been shown to trigger negative brand perceptions and pose both economic and social
risks [63, 64]. To address consumer skepticism toward this phenomenon, research
suggests that companies must build green trust through transparency and honesty in
their sustainability claims [64]. Organizations can also play a role in educating consumers about greenwashing and how to recognize genuine sustainability efforts, helping to
reduce misunderstandings and foster trust [64]. In the context of a market showing signs
of greenwashing in Vietnam, consumers might remain skeptical about corporate CSRrelated activities, placing higher importance on green values and green trust. Nevertheless, the research findings still demonstrate that CSR is an indispensable activity for GBE
development, which needs improvement concurrently with other driving factors.
Other components of the Theory of Planned Behavior, namely attitude toward green
products and perceived behavioral control, were ranked as the least important factors
(with local weights of 0.107 and 0.104, respectively). However, they still contribute to
promoting GBE, as their consistency ratios (CR = 0.085 and 0.058) remain below the
accepted threshold of 0.1 [49]. Moreover, in the second round of the Delphi process, all
items under these two factors exceeded the Minimum required CVR threshold of 0.37,
as recommended by Lawshe [56]. This indicates a reasonable level of expert consensus
regarding their role. This result is partly supported by prior research. For instance, Ha
[36] illustrated that attitude toward green products positively influences GBE in the
electronics industry in Vietnam through intermediary factors like green brand image
and green trust. Shanti and Joshi [13] demonstrated that a green attitude contributes to
GBE in the hotel industry in India. Bekk et al. [7] indicated that brand attitude directly
impacts positive word-of-mouth communication in Germany. However, there seems to
be a lack of studies examining the relationship between perceived behavior control and
GBE. Hence, this study contributes new knowledge to the GBE literature and demonstrates that consumers’ ability to find and purchase green products easily and feasibly
can promote GBE.
5.2 Implication

This study makes a theoretical contribution by empirically examining all three components of the TPB and comparing their relative importance to other drivers of GBE.

Page 15 of 20

Hue and Huyen Discover Sustainability

(2025) 6:932

The integration of Delphi and AHP methods offers a rare mixed-method approach that
enhances both qualitative and quantitative understanding. It also distinguishes between
emotional and functional values, often merged in prior research, thus expanding the
theoretical framework of perceived value in green branding. Moreover, it is among the
few studies to explore the link between CSR and GBE rather than general brand equity.
These findings suggest research implications: future studies should adopt longitudinal
designs to capture the evolving influence of GBE drivers over time, and further investigate the distinct roles of emotional and functional values in shaping green consumer
behavior, especially in emerging markets.
Based on these findings, the study offers several practical and policy implications
aimed at enhancing GBE in emerging markets like Vietnam. First and foremost, there is
a need to strengthen social norms through education and communication. Regulatory
bodies should integrate sustainability and green consumption into national school curricula while launching large-scale awareness campaigns through schools, youth organizations, women’s unions, and mainstream media. These channels have the potential to
amplify pro-environmental social norms—identified in this study as the most influential
factor in green purchasing behavior.
In addition, a transparent, credible, and accessible national green product certification system should be established. Such a system would enhance consumer trust in
green brands while reducing concerns about greenwashing. For businesses, especially
small enterprises and those in agriculture, investing in multiple GBE-related drivers
may pose financial challenges. Therefore, governments should provide targeted support
such as subsidies, tax incentives, and access to green financing for businesses adopting
sustainable practices like cleaner production, biodegradable packaging, or eco-friendly
supply chains. To ensure practicality, official cost–benefit assessment guidelines should
be issued to help businesses prioritize investments in the most impactful drivers, rather
than attempting to implement all seven simultaneously.
Moreover, to strengthen community-level impact, which was found to be relatively
weak in this study, local campaigns such as “Green Consumption Week,” “Eco-friendly
Farmers’ Markets,” or “Sustainable Brand Awards” should be organized regularly. These
initiatives can help foster a collective green mindset and gradually elevate public awareness. Overall, the above actions require coordinated efforts among government agencies, businesses, civil society organizations, and local communities, with the long-term
goal of building a sustainable consumption culture and improving the quality of life in
Vietnam’s green growth context.

6 Conclusion
This study aims to identify the factors and criteria for developing GBE in the case of
the agricultural sector in Vietnam, ranking the level of importance of these factors and
criteria. The research results indicate the relative importance of factors in descending
order as follows: Subjective norms, emotional perceived values, functional perceived values, green trust, corporate social responsibility, attitude toward green products, and perceived behavior control. The criteria within these factors have achieved consensus and
have been ranked in terms of importance by the participants.
This work makes significant contributions to both science and practical application.
It may be one of the few studies that examine the relationship between components of

Page 16 of 20

Hue and Huyen Discover Sustainability

(2025) 6:932

the TPB and other factors with GBE, while also providing a weighted ranking of these
factors. It might also be pioneering in combining the Delphi and AHP methods in GBE
research, addressing certain research gaps, and offering new insights. This study has also
attempted to explore factors rarely or never addressed in empirical GBE studies, such as
emotional perceived values, functional perceived values, and CSR, providing profound
insights and meaningful implications in this field. Moreover, the focus of this research is
the agricultural sector, which is a pivotal economic sector in Vietnam and several other
countries. It has a significant environmental impact but has been overlooked in prior
GBE studies. Finally, the study offers practical recommendations for educational institutions, civil society organizations, corporations, and governments to improve GBE.
Specific suggestions encompass the incorporation of sustainability and eco-friendly
consumption into educational programs, the implementation of coordinated communication initiatives to enhance community awareness GBE, and the establishment of a
national certification system for green products.
Certain limitations persist in this article. Although the sample sizes for the Delphi
and AHP rounds meet methodological requirements, they remain relatively small and
are geographically limited to Hanoi, which reduces the generalizability of the findings.
The expert panel also lacks diversity, notably with no representation from environmental NGOs or farmers, potentially leading to a commercial bias. In addition, one of the
main limitations of this study is that the CSR criteria introduced after the first Delphi
round were not pilot-tested beforehand. Future studies might increase the number of
participants in the Delphi and AHP phases, with a more diverse panel that includes
representatives from various stakeholder groups such as environmental NGOs, farmers, policymakers, and consumers. Additionally, some other factors related to GBE were
not examined in this study, such as green brand loyalty, brand associations, green brand
image, and green satisfaction. Subsequent research could include new measures, especially considering the relationship between intention and behavior in purchasing green
products, as TPB suggests that factors do not necessarily lead directly to action but often
go through an intention phase. In addition, future scholars may also consider adopting
longitudinal research designs instead of relying solely on cross-sectional data, as done in
this study, to track how the influence of various factors on GBE evolves. This approach
would help overcome the limitations of cross-sectional studies and offer deeper insights
into the development of consumer attitudes and behaviors as sustainability initiatives
unfold. Moreover, cross-national or cross-sector studies are encouraged to examine how
cultural, economic, or industry-specific differences may shape the drivers of green brand
equity, thereby enhancing the generalizability and contextual relevance of future findings. Finally, the scope of this study is focused on consumers of green agricultural products in Vietnam. Therefore, applying the research findings to other industries, fields, or
countries requires caution. Future studies might explore GBE from the perspectives of
other stakeholders, such as shareholders, and company employees, while also exploring
other sectors significantly impacting the environment.
