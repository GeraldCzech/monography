Abstract
Many charities, especially religious ones, spend signi…cantly to improve poor community
members’living standards. Conventional theoretical philanthropy models with altruism or
impure altruism/warm glow cannot explain poverty-alleviating private transfers in the type
and size of communities where this occurs - e.g., congregations. However, if preferences
re‡ect religious precepts (“you are your brother’s keeper”; “treat neighbours like yourself”),
charitable poverty alleviation can be optimal even in large communities. Income and price
elasticities for giving prove important. Surprisingly, charitable private transfers are less
likely when donors care for recipients’living standard instead of the aggregate amount all
recipients get.

A facile explanation for a dearth of explicit theoretical analyses of charitable provision
of private goods is that, as in the emblematic Bergstrom, Blume and Varian (1986) paper,
focus is on "voluntary provision of public goods". Authors might not have got to studying
charitable provision of private goods with public goods yet. Another argument is Hochman
and Rodgers’(1969): "charitable giving, motivated by altruism, creates a public good out of
the charity" (Andreoni, 2006). Donors’altruism towards recipients of, e.g., money transfers,
daycare or housing makes these, or their institutional arrangements, public goods. But,
while the resulting theoretical models consider the public good aspects explicitly, the private
goods provided simultaneously, which yield distinct bene…ts, are not.
The crux of my model is my assumption that the total amount given to alleviate poverty
entering agents’utility functions as an argument separate from the amount used for a pure
public good embodies religious precepts like being your brother’s keeper. This captures List’s
(2011, 165) idea that di¤erent factors to other types of charity motivate religious giving.5
He advances social insurance: "it might be [...] that people give to churches so that in time
of need they can more readily receive church services or help from fellow churchgoers." Total
charitable funding for poverty alleviation being an argument in both donors’and recipients’
utility functions re‡ects satisfaction both from being your brothers’keeper in times of need
and knowing that they are yours similarly. I call such preferences "pious". With pious
preferences, a utilitarian charity can …nd poverty alleviating private transfers cost-e¤ective.

V Conclusion
Becker, in his "A Theory of Social Interactions" (1974), quotes The Random House Dictionary of the English Language’s (1967, unabridged) de…nition of charity: "the benevolent
feeling, especially towards those in need or in disfavour." He includes the recipients’ wellbeing in the donors’utility functions to study explicitly the trade-o¤ between a recipient’s
income and a charitable person’s giving. However, explicit analyses of charitable transfers to
the needy are conspicuously absent in much of the theoretical charity literature in the subsequent 40 years. I have shown a di¢ culty of incorporating such transfers in models based
on utility functions common in that literature. This di¢ culty arises as a large-population
e¤ect makes poverty-alleviating private transfers cost-ine¤ective if the funds could instead
be used for a public good. I also show that this di¢ culty is overcome if we posit preferences close to the spirit of both Becker’s and Hochman and Rodgers’original conceptions.
Such preferences internalise the amount the community gives to the poor and so also re‡ect
the religious precepts that one is one’s brothers’keeper and should love thy neighbours like
yourself. Hence I label them "pious preferences" for short.
Several authors (e.g., Feldstein, 1975, List, 2011; Andreoni and Payne, 2013) suggest,
from empirical …ndings, that religious giving is di¤erent from other forms. For example, it is
30
Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3164327

less susceptible to vagaries of the economic cycle (List, 2011), is less price elastic (Feldstein,
1975, and followers) and the amount given depends on the devoutness of the giver (The
Barna Group, 2009; McKitrick et al, 2013). Such …ndings suggest that the motivation for
religious giving is not narrowly economic. I have captured this aspect of religious giving by
modeling agents who empathize with their poorer brethren by having utility that responds
positively to the total amount given to the latter. As "eleven o’clock Sunday morning continues to be the most segregated hour in America" (Hartford Institute for Religion Research,
2015), I believe that this empathy is also important for reasons I have not stressed: religious giving allows donors to improve the well-being only of people that they identify with.
So, it is bound up with the e¤ect that Alesina et al (1999) identify, wherein giving within
essentially homogeneous communities substitutes for and undermines more widely distributed state provision. This is an issue to be pursued in further research.The same is true
of the exploration of any complementarity or substitutability in the relationship between
joint-product/warm-glow giving and pious preferences.
