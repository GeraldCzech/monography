Abstract
Purpose – This work explores how recipients’ identity influences givers’ choice of charitable gifts, their
propensity to deviate from gifting registries and how it impacts social ties.
Design/methodology/approach – We conducted five experiments involving diverse charities to explore the
above effect, investigate the mediating role of a warm-glow effect and examine the moderating effect of social
closeness and anonymity on gift preferences.
Findings – Givers deviate from the registry in favor of alternatives that are congruent with the recipient’s
identity due to the givers’ desire to gift a warm-glow effect. Socially close givers are more likely to deviate from
the gift registry to enhance social ties. Recipients receive a higher warm-glow effect when distant givers give
unrequested identity-congruous gifts, thus improving social relationships.
Practical implications – This study will help organizations determine which identities a specific charity should
highlight in its campaigns. Marketers can encourage consumers to prefer charitable gifts by emphasizing the
relational and warm-glow benefits.
Originality/value – This is the first study to examine the effect of the recipient’s identity on givers’ preference
for charitable gifts. Specifically, while giving charitable gifts, givers are likelier to donate to a charity whose
identity is congruent with the recipient.
Keywords Gift-giving, Charitable gifts, Prosocial behavior, Warm-glow effect, Identity-based motivation

1. Introduction
Imagine that your friend invites you to his/her birthday party and shares their gift registry,
which consists of a charitable gift (CG). While donating, you encounter another charity that is
more congruent with your friend’s identity. Will you deviate from the gift registry? In this
research, we examine how preference for CGs is influenced by the recipient’s identity.
CGs do not directly benefit the recipient since they are donations to a charitable
organization that a giver gives on a recipient’s behalf (Maciejewsky, 2008). About 47% of
recipients showed an inclination toward CGs, which indicates their popularity (Simpson,
2022). Despite this, very little is known about them, factors that influence their preference, and
how they impact interpersonal relationships. Academic research has thoroughly studied the
gifting phenomenon (Givi et al., 2022) as well as charitable donations (Bekkers and Wiepking,
2011). However, research on charitable gifting is at a nascent stage due to its newness. Since
donating to charity on your own versus as a gift for someone is a distinct phenomenon, research

findings of the former may not be applicable to the latter. Therefore, we investigate how givers
decide upon CGs to elicit appreciation from recipients.
CGs are expected to provide emotional benefits to the recipients through satisfaction and
joy (Maciejewsky, 2008). Certain recipients prefer them as they enable them to indulge in
altruistic behavior by contributing to society (Andreoni, 1990) while fostering a moral identity
(Aquino and Reed, 2002). However, related research has also shown that recipients do not
always appreciate CGs when gift-givers mispredict recipients’ preferences (Ok et al., 2020).
Due to contradictory findings, the literature is inconclusive regarding CGs’ appreciation and
factors that impact their efficacy. Therefore, there is a gap in our understanding of the
asymmetries between givers’ and recipients’ preferences for CGs.
Asymmetries in the preference between givers’ and recipients’ gifting preferences hamper
social ties (Chan and Mogilner, 2016), which emphasizes the importance of gift registries.
Despite this, givers sometimes prefer freely chosen gifts to convey their intimacy with
recipients (Ward and Broniarczyk, 2016), which may displease recipients and adversely
impact social relationships (Waldfogel, 1993). Therefore, extant research has focused on
identifying factors that help givers match recipients’ preferences. One such factor that related
literature emphasizes is the role of identity. While one school of thought posits that recipients
appreciate gifts that are congruent to their identity (Gino and Flynn, 2011), whereas another
school of thought posits that they prefer gifts that match givers’ identity (Paolacci et al., 2015).
Such contradictory findings preclude our understanding of how identity impacts preference
and/or appreciation of CGs. Therefore, our work attempts to study how recipients’ identity
influences givers’ choice of CGs, their propensity to deviate from CG registries, and how this
divergence affects social ties.
Based on identity-based motivation research (Oyserman, 2009), using five studies, we
show that gift-givers deviate from a CG registry if they encounter an option that is highly
congruous with the recipient’s identity to gift them a warm-glow effect. Moreover, close givers
possess a higher propensity to do so. Lastly, recipients experience greater warm glow when
receiving an identity-congruent gift from distant givers than close givers, resulting in enhanced
relationship closeness. This work contributes in several ways. Firstly, it studies CGs-an underexplored practice in gift-giving literature. Secondly, we highlight the influence of identity on
charitable gifting preference. Thirdly, we demonstrate the effect of a warm glow in the giftgiving context. Lastly, we also explore how CGs enhance interpersonal cohesiveness. Our
findings offer executable tactics to organizations to promote CGs to support the needy.

2. Theory development
2.1 Gift-giving and role of identity
Although gift-giving is largely undertaken to develop and maintain social ties (Chan and
Mogilner, 2016), oftentimes, there exists a mismatch between givers’ and recipients’
preferences, which can be detrimental to relational closeness (Ward and Broniarczyk,
2016). Therefore, extant literature has attempted to explore the reasons behind this
mismatch (Givi and Galak, 2017) to minimize the associated disappointment (Waldfogel,
1993). However, gifting research has not yet conclusively demonstrated how gifts that
match a recipient’s identity are evaluated. Specifically, some studies (Paolacci et al., 2015)
report that gifts that match a giver’s characteristics are well-received, while others (Gino
and Flynn, 2011) contend that gifts that match a recipient’s identity elicit gratitude and
improve social ties. Therefore, extant research has generated mixed findings concerning the
role of identity in gifting. According to identity-based motivation research (Oyserman,
2009), individuals are likely to prefer identity-congruent products to identity-incongruent
ones since they communicate who they are and aspire to be through their consumption
choices (Das, 2014). Moreover, individuals not only use identities to make their own
decisions but also judge the actions and purchases of others based on their own identities
(Pratap et al., 2016).

Gifts play a fundamental role in reinforcing personal and social identities (Klein et al.,
2015). A recipient’s identity can considerably influence the gifting decisions of the giver.
Givers are likelier to choose gifts that align with the recipient’s self-identity, such as hobbies or
interests, rather than their own preferences to communicate their knowledge about the
recipient and strengthen social ties (Gino and Flynn, 2011). Additionally, the relationship
between the giver and the recipient was an essential factor in gifting decisions (Bagozzi et al.,
1999). For example, givers are more likely to choose gifts that reflect their preferences when
giving to close friends or family members but are more likely to select gifts based on the
recipient’s preferences when giving to acquaintances or colleagues (Ward and Broniarczyk,
2016). These findings suggest that a recipient’s identity and the relationship between the two
parties can influence gifting choices.
2.2 Charitable gifts and identity
Social psychology research suggests that people are more likely to donate to causes that
align with their identity, values, and beliefs (Aquino and Reed, 2002; Chapman et al.,
2020). Also, empirical evidence is available to demonstrate how various sources of
morality (such as moral emotion, moral judgment, and moral identity) can simultaneously
and cooperatively influence prosocial behavior (Septianto and Soegianto, 2017). Matching
the donor’s identity with the charity’s mission and values can increase their connection with
the cause, leading to greater donation intentions (Sargeant and Lee, 2004). For example, a
study found that people who identified as environmentalists were likelier to donate to an
environmental cause when the solicitation message emphasized the compatibility between
the cause and their identity (Chapman et al., 2020). Therefore, charities that consider the
identity of their donors in their fundraising appeals are likely to be more successful in
securing donations.
Charitable gift-giving is a popular form of philanthropy that has recently gained attention
(Cavanaugh et al., 2015). CGs are donations made by givers on a recipient’s behalf to support a
cause instead of spending money on a gift that directly benefits a recipient. As they lack
tangibility and are not technically for the recipient, factors influencing their appreciation
remain unclear due to previous literature’s mixed findings. Maciejewsky (2008) suggests that
recipients appreciate such gifts as they provide emotional benefits from helping others.
Additionally, such gifts can strengthen social connections and enhance the perception of the
gift-giver as a caring and socially responsible individual (Aquino and Reed, 2002). These
findings highlight the potential benefits of such gifts in enhancing social cohesion and such
gifts may effectively promote individual and collective well-being. While others (Cavanaugh
et al., 2015; Ok et al., 2020) indicate that recipients do not appreciate such gifts, especially
when such gifts are unrequested. Therefore, CGs, especially unrequested ones, may be
inappropriate for pleasing recipients.
Research on CGs is nascent and has not thoroughly examined the factors leading to their
appreciation. Existing literature has examined charitable donations and the significant role of
identity in charitable donations (Chapman et al., 2018, 2020). However, these findings may
not apply to CGs, as the two constitute separate phenomena. Additionally, the gift-giving
domain also indicates the influence of identity on gift preference and highlights that givers
prefer to give gifts that match recipients’ identity to signal relational closeness (Gino and
Flynn, 2011). Consequently, identity may potentially exert a significant influence within the
context of charitable gifting. Therefore, we aim to examine the role of identity in charitable
gifting.
In this research, we suggest that if an unrequested CG is highly congruent with a recipient’s
identity, it is likely to be appreciated, according to the findings of identity-based motivation
research and our previous discussion. Hence, CGs that match a recipient’s identity may
diminish the negative impact of receiving an unrequested gift and are more likely to be
appreciated. However, since the recipient will not use the gift, one can question the relevance

of an identity-congruent charity in recipients’ appreciation of such gifts. Therefore, we discuss

2.3 Warm glow as a mediating variable
People feel good about themselves when they help others, referred to as the warm-glow effect
(Isen, 1970). The warm glow effect is where individuals experience positive emotions from
prosocial behavior. Later, Andreoni (1990) suggested that people experience a psychological
benefit called a “warm glow” when they donate to a common good instead of spending on
themselves. This effect may be due to the activation of reward centers in the brain that are
associated with positive emotions (Moll et al., 2006). This phenomenon has been empirically
studied in prosocial and pro-environmental behavior (Hartmann et al., 2017). Hartmann et al.
(2017) compare altruistic and warm glow motivations for pro-environmental behavior and
conclude that warm glow has a more significant impact than altruistic on driving proenvironmental behavior. Overall, these findings suggest that the warm-glow effect may play
an important role in motivating individuals to engage in prosocial behavior and contribute to
the well-being of others.
According to Andreoni (1990), this warm glow arises due to individuals’ satisfaction from
knowing their actions have helped others. Moreover, Bekkers and Wiepking (2011) found that
individuals are more likely to donate when they can be publicly acknowledged. Overall, the
warm glow effect has important implications for the design of effective fundraising
campaigns.
Using identity-based motivation research, we hypothesize that givers are more likely
to deviate from the recipient’s explicitly requested gift registry when a CG closely
resembles the recipient’s identity. We predict this propensity to deviate because givers
want to gift a warm-glow effect to the recipient. Further, we hypothesize that close (versus
distant) givers are more likely to deviate from the requested gift registry when presented
with a high identity-congruent CG. Moreover, when presented with high identitycongruent CGs, givers are more likely to deviate from the requested gift registry when the
gift is given under a non-anonymous (versus anonymous) condition. That is, givers prefer
to deviate only when they receive attribution. Lastly, the giver-recipient relationship
strengthens when the deviation from the registry happens in favor of a high (versus low)
identity-congruent CG. This is more likely to be evident in the case of socially distant
givers. More formally:

H1a. Givers’ likelihood of deviating from the requested CG registry is significantly higher

H1b. The above effect is mediated through a warm-glow effect.
H2. When encountering a gift that is congruent with the recipient’s identity, the likelihood

compared to distant givers.
H3. When encountering a gift that is congruent with the recipient’s identity, the likelihood
of diverting from the requested CG registry is significantly higher among nonanonymous givers than anonymous givers.
H4. Recipients receiving unrequested identity congruent CGs from socially distant (vs.
close) givers feel a higher warm glow effect, which enhances their relationship

3. Methodology and pretest
To test our hypotheses, we used an experimental design as it has been commonly used in the
gift-giving context (Cavanaugh et al., 2015; Givi and Galak, 2017).
3.1 Pretest
A pretest was conducted to establish that gift-givers preferred charitable gifts that are highly
congruent with a recipient’s identity. The participants (N 5 68) were randomly assigned to
either high-identity congruent or low-identity congruent charitable gift conditions.
They read about their colleague who is passionate about football and trains school children
for free in football. Later, they read that they were invited to this colleague’s birthday party. In
the high identity-congruence condition, they read that while browsing online for a suitable gift,
they came across a sports-related charity and decided to donate to it (on behalf of this
colleague) as a gift. On the other hand, in the low identity-congruence condition, they donated
to a music-related charity. Lastly, participants were asked to rate their purchase intention for
the given charity and the extent to which the CG was congruent with the recipient’s identity.
A one-way ANOVA with purchase intention as a dependent variable showed a main effect of
identity such that high-identity congruent CG was significantly higher than its counterpart
(Mhigh 5 4.77, Mlow 5 3.79; F(1, 66) 5 6.79, p < 0.011; Cohen’s d 5 0.63). Participants in the
high identity-congruent gift condition perceived the charitable organization as highly
congruent with the recipient’s identity (Mhigh congruent 5 5.09, SDhigh congruent 5 0.62; Mlow
congruent 5 3.43, SDlow congruent 5 0.74) than the low identity-congruent condition. Therefore,
the pretest demonstrated that gift-givers prefer to donate to a charity that closely matches the
recipient’s identity.
4. Study overview
We demonstrate that gift-givers will deviate from a CG registry if they encounter a more
identity-congruous option, and this propensity is mediated by givers’ tendency to maximize
the warm-glow effect experienced by the recipient (Study 1A and 1B). Compared to distant
givers, we predict that close givers are likely to choose a higher identity congruent gift (Study
2). We also predict that givers are not likely to deviate from the registry if they do not receive
the attribution for the gift (Study 3). Finally, we show that recipients experience a higher
degree of warm glow on receiving identity-congruent gifts (vs. less congruent), strengthening
social ties (Study 4).
5. Study 1
5.1 Study 1A
Study 1 intended to test Hypothesis 1a and Hypothesis 1b. We had participants engage in a
consequential gift-giving situation that involved giving a CG to an office colleague. We
employed a single factor between-subjects design by varying the degree of identity
congruence of the CG.
5.1.1 Method. Initially, 123 students at a Southeast Asian university participated in the
study. We had a final sample of 105 students (40% female, Mage 5 23.6 years), as 18
participants were excluded for failing an attention check (common for all studies) that asked a
question: “Everyone likes to watch television programs. Recently, sports television programs
have seen a major increase in ratings. Many sports start with the letter ‘B’. However, we ask
that from the list below, you select a sport that does not start with the letter ‘B’. Thank you for
doing this task. a. Basketball, b. Football, c. Baseball, d. Bowling.”
Participants read about their colleague who is passionate about guitar and gives free guitar
lessons to school children. Later, they read that they were invited to their colleague’s birthday
party, and the colleague requested them to donate to a specific charity (Option A-Charity for
learning art and culture). In the high identity-congruence condition, the participants read that

while browsing online for the same charity, they encountered another charity (Option BCharity for learning musical instruments, especially guitar). In the low identity-congruence
condition, participants read the same scenario. The only difference was that while browsing for
the given charity, they encountered another charity (Option B- Charity for learning singing,
especially classical singing). The participants rated their relative preference between the two
gifts on a 7-point scale (1 5 Option A, 7 5 Option B).
Furthermore, they completed a three-item manipulation check adapted from Paolacci
et al.(2015) assessing the gift’s identity congruence with the recipient (e.g. “The gift reflects
recipient’s interests,”; Cronbach’s α 5 0.74). We also tested for the warm-glow effect using
four items on a 7-point scale adapted from Hartmann et al. (2017) (e.g. “With the receipt of this
gift, the recipient will have a pleasant feeling of personal satisfaction by doing something
about the cause”; Cronbach’s α 5 0.86). After responding to the primary dependent variable of
the study, participants were always asked to complete an attention check. (Appendix and Data
available at https://osf.io/s9kbp/?view_only507093b2c81ac4b79b4011877d0cf4c54).
5.1.2 Results and discussion. Manipulation check confirmed that the participants in the
high identity-congruence condition perceived the CG as highly congruent with the recipient’s
identity (F(1, 103) 5 118.70, p < 0.001, Mhigh 5 5.85, SDhigh 5 0.65; Mlow 5 4.52,
SDlow 5 0.58) than the low identity-congruence condition. An ANOVAwith gift preference as
a dependent variable showed a main effect of identity congruence (F(1, 103) 5 23.87,
p < 0.001, η2 5 0.18), such that givers indicated a higher preference to give to a charity that
closely matched with the recipient (Mhigh 5 5.66, SDhigh 5 0.99; Mlow 5 4.64, SDlow 5 1.14).
This result supported Hypothesis 1a.
We conducted a mediation analysis (5,000 bootstraps) with recipient’s identity-congruence
(low 5 0, high 5 1) as the independent variable, the warm-glow effect as the mediator, and gift
preference as the dependent variable (PROCESS MODEL 4; Hayes, 2017, Figure 1) and found
a significant indirect effect of identity-congruence on gifting preference through warm-glow
(β 5 0.79, 95% CI 5 [0.51,1.05]). The participants felt that recipients would receive a higher
degree of warm glow while receiving a high (vs. low) identity-congruent CG (Mhigh 5 5.79,
SDhigh 5 0.98; Mlow 5 4.43, SDlow 5 0.87). This result supported Hypothesis1b.

6.2 Results and discussion
Nine participants failed the comprehension check for social closeness, and twenty-five failed
the attention check. We excluded those participants who failed any of these checks (Han and
Newman, 2022), leaving a total of 213 participants (42% female, Mage 5 23.51 years) for

social closeness as independent variables. There was a main effect of identity F(1,
209) 5 109.53, p < 0.001, η2 5 0.34) such that participants were more likely to choose high
identity-congruent gift as (Mhigh 5 5.50, SDlow 5 1.13, vs. Mlow 5 3.92, SDlow 5 1.12)
compared to low identity-congruent gift. However, there was no significant difference in the

The findings of Study 2 lend support to Hypothesis 2. The closer the relationship between
the giver and the recipient, the more likely the giver will try to match the gift with the
recipient’s identity.

7. Study 3
Study 3 intended to test Hypothesis 3. Gifts signal relationship closeness, and givers are
motivated to please the recipient. However, when givers do not receive the attribution (the
giver’s name is unknown) for the gift, then gift-givers are less likely to choose an identitycongruent charitable gift. Anonymity (the giver’s name is unknown) demotivates gift-givers to
choose an identity-congruent gift because anonymity removes the relationship signal. When
givers receive attribution (the giver’s name is disclosed) for the gift, they are encouraged to
choose an identity-congruent gift, which signals relationship closeness. We expect participants
in non-anonymous conditions (the giver’s name is known) to deviate from the charitable gift
registry if they encounter an option that is highly congruous with the recipient’s identity. We

9. General discussion
Studies 1–3 demonstrated that gift-givers deviate from the registry if they encounter an option
that is highly congruous with the recipient’s identity. Study 1A and Study 1B found that this is
due to the gift-givers’ desire to gift the recipient with a warm-glow. Study 2 demonstrated that
socially close givers tend to deviate from the gift registry to signal their relationship closeness.
These results are similar to those of Ward and Broniarczyk (2016), demonstrating how close
givers deviate from the registry to exhibit their social closeness with the recipient. Study 3
examined the role of anonymity, which impacts the preference for identity-congruent gifts,
such that when givers are anonymous, they are less likely to deviate from the gift registry,
highlighting the importance of attribution in gifting. Finally, Study 4 demonstrated that
recipients experience a warm-glow effect on receiving unrequested identity-congruous CGs.
This effect is magnified in the case of distant givers, which strengthen social ties. These
findings contrast earlier research (Paolacci et al., 2015; Ward and Broniarczyk, 2016) on
gifting asymmetries and their adverse effects on social proximity by demonstrating that gifting
deviation does not always hamper giver-recipient relationships.
10. Contributions
10.1 Theoretical
Our work contributes to the existing gifting literature, warm-glow theory, and interrelationship dynamics. Firstly, we explore a new gift type-a CG. Prior research has focused on
tangible gifts that directly benefit recipients (Chan and Mogilner, 2016; Paolacci et al., 2015)
and making charitable choices for the self (Bekkers and Wiepking, 2011). We explore
situations that involve charitable donations for others. Sherry’s (1983) seminal work indicated
altruism and agonism as two primary motives of gift-giving. Our work demonstrates that giftgivers deviate from the gift registry to give the recipient a warm glow to strengthen
relationships. This indicates that charitable gift-givers are partially motivated by agonistic
motives.
Secondly, we focus on factors that influence givers’ decision-making in selecting a CG.
Prior studies have primarily focused on the receiver’s appreciation and misappreciation of
such gifts (Cavanaugh et al., 2015; Ok et al., 2020). Our research contributes to the existing
literature by illustrating the influence of a recipient’s identity on a giver’s decision-making
process. Previous research on gift-giving has yielded contradictory results regarding the
significance of identity. Paolacci et al., (2015) contend that recipients favor gifts that
correspond to the identity of the givers, while Gino and Flynn (2011) demonstrate that
recipients favor gifts that correspond to their own identities. Our work attempts to resolve this
conflict by demonstrating that not only do recipients prefer CGs that match their own identity
but also that givers are aware of it.
Thirdly, our study offers significant advances to the warm-glow theory. Hartmann et al.,
(2017) empirically investigated the warm glow effect in the context of pro-environmental
behavior and found it to be a powerful mechanism that encourages sustained participation in
prosocial action by reinforcing positive emotions. Experiencing positive feelings like a warm
glow can induce learning effects that influence future behavior toward altruism, kindness, and
generosity. Our research, for the first time, demonstrates the significance of the warm-glow

effect in the context of gift-giving. The results indicate that givers deviate towards charitable
gifts that are congruent with the recipient’s identity in order to gift them a feeling of
warm-glow.
Lastly, we develop a better understanding of how charitable gifts affect interpersonal
relationships. We show that givers deviate from the registry to gift a warm glow to enhance
cohesion in interpersonal relationships. This is indeed the case as recipients, too, feel closer to
the givers when they deviate from the gift registry toward gifts that match the recipient’s
identity. Moreover, this effect is enhanced for distant givers, as recipients probably have fewer
expectations from them. This signals the giver’s thoughtfulness, enhancing the emotional
connection between the giver and recipient, which may develop enduring and meaningful
relationships.

10.2 Managerial and societal implications
This research provides substantial implications for policymakers and charitable organizations
by identifying an executable strategy for promoting CGs. Charitable organizations use
message-framing techniques to solicit donations, but the optimal application of these
techniques remains largely obscure. Our research can potentially assist them in developing
impactful strategies, thereby stimulating donations.
First, our study consistently demonstrates that identity plays a significant role in charity
selection, which can benefit charitable organizations. Fundraisers can determine which
identities a specific charity should highlight in its campaigns to increase donations. For
example, in the gifting context, givers are consistently motivated to prefer the recipient’s
identity-congruous gifts to elicit appreciation and strengthen interpersonal relationships.
When charitable donations are requested in a gift registry, organizations must strategically
evoke the recipient’s identity with the gift registry. Marketers can frame identity-based appeals
to resonate with the donor’s identity and increase responsible consumption behavior (Kumar
and Dholakia, 2022).
Second, this research suggests that an emotional construct such as a warm-glow effect can
further assist fundraisers in employing emotional appeals in their marketing campaigns. Since
CGs lack tangibility, the warm-glow effect can add a certain degree of tangibility. Marketers
can encourage consumers to choose CGs by communicating the warm-glow benefits by
creating narratives like, “Your gift cultivates hope. Experience the pleasure of contributing to
society.” to evoke empathy and highlight the social impact of CGs. Marketers can also share
stories from other gift receivers about how CGs make a tangible difference in the initiative.
Marketers can also express gratitude to the gift recipients for CGs and emphasize that their
efforts are recognized, which can promote feelings of fulfillment. Receivers receiving a warm
glow will establish an emotional connection with the cause and might continue supporting the
organization.
Third, charitable organizations can benefit by framing charitable giving as a gift (Wang
et al., 2023). Donations as CGs have the potential to enhance the prosocial behavior of the
givers and augment their overall contribution. Such donations can communicate the symbolic
meaning of social closeness between the giver and the receiver. This research suggests that
receiving an unrequested identity-congruous charitable gift from close givers does not hamper
the relationship. However, receiving unrequested charitable gifts from distant givers that align
with the recipient’s identity can be overpowering.
Our research also offers societal implications. CGs foster a sense of generosity and
empathy, assisting organizations in leveraging the network of their fundraisers, who encourage
their family and friends to donate to the cause. Therefore, CGs can become a vital component
of their financial ecosystem. We show that identity-congruent CGs satisfy recipients, which
may enhance their propensity to seek more donations on other occasions. Second, CGs
cultivate responsible behavior. Individuals can be motivated for prosocial behavior by
augmenting their knowledge of the warm-glow effect (Hartmann et al., 2017). Observing the

11. Limitations and future research directions
Firstly, we studied the impact of the recipient’s identity in decision-making while overlooking
the giver’s identity. Ward and Broniarczyk (2011) indicated that givers feel an identity threat
while giving identity-contrary gifts. In this research, we did not check if givers experience an
identity threat if they encounter an identity-incongruent CG that is highly congruent with the
recipient’s identity. Therefore, researchers can investigate the interaction between the giver’s
identity and the type of charity when it is incongruent with the former.
Secondly, in this research, the gift-receiver sacrifices a tangible gift for a CG. Since CGs
offer a glimpse into an individual’s moral identity, it may be fascinating to investigate the
factors that motivate an individual to request them. Some people may genuinely empathize
with the beneficiaries of these gifts and wish to assist them. In contrast, others may do so to
project their self-image. Scholars (Chapman et al., 2020) have shown how status motives
make people sensitive toward prosocial behavior. Examining whether the costly signaling
theory significantly influences recipients’ inclination to request CGs may be beneficial.
Lastly, we show that receivers are not offended when givers deviate from the registry.
However, we did this by providing the respondents with gift registries that only included CG
contributions. CGs, whether congruent or incongruent, can be explored to evaluate whether
they produce offense when receivers indicate a desire for a tangible gift rather than a CG.
