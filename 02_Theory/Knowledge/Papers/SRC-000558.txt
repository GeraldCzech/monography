These six questions split into identity-based prediction, organizational predictors of intention and retention, and
past behavior plus evidence gaps.

Identity-Based Prediction
Role identity predicts volunteer intention and often later behavior, especially when identity is behavior-specific
rather than a general “helpful person” self-view (White et al., 2017; Grube & Piliavin, 2000). In a 3-year study, role
identity predicted later volunteer performance through intentions, and it fully mediated links from attitude, social
norms, past behavior, and parental modelling to performance (Marta et al., 2014).
• Behavior-specific identity predicted intention across money, time, and blood giving (White et al., 2017).
• Identity-based models from blood donation generalize partly to volunteering, because the same role-identity

logic applied across blood, time, and money outcomes (White et al., 2017).
• Generalization is context-limited: in COVID mutual aid, prior volunteer role identity predicted later helping only

indirectly through beneficiary closeness and community identification (Wakefield et al., 2021).

Organizational Predictors
Organizational reputation affects willingness to volunteer, and organizational experience shapes retention-related
outcomes (Schloderer et al., 2014; Groza & Groza, 2021). Retention is also predicted by identity, commitment-like
attachment, pride, and supportive role conditions rather than by identity alone (Grube & Piliavin, 2000; Bang et al.,
2022).

Past Behavior and Gaps
Past volunteering behavior predicts future volunteering in the available longitudinal evidence, but usually as one
predictor among identity and TPB variables (White et al., 2017; Marta et al., 2014). More broadly, volunteer
retention appears to emerge from repeated role enactment, identity validation, meaningfulness, and organizational
belonging (Thoits, 2012; Thoits, 2021).
• Past behavior predicted later volunteering time at follow-up (White et al., 2017).
• More time in the role appears to strengthen volunteer identity, which can sustain repeated service (Thoits,

2012; Van Ingen & Wilson, 2017).
• There is no direct evidence in these 20 papers that social media engagement predicts volunteering intention

toward nonprofits.
Overall, role identity is a robust predictor of volunteer intention and persistence, organizational reputation
matters for volunteering support and retention, and social media engagement remains unaddressed in the
supplied evidence.
