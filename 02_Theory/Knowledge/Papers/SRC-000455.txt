1 | INTRODUCTION
Positioned between the market and government, nonprofit organizations (NPOs) play a crucial
role in mobilizing private motivation for the common good (Salamon, 2003). With more than
10 million NPOs worldwide, including nongovernmental organizations (NGOs), foundations,
and charities, these organizations perform a wide variety of social, economic, and political services. Their areas of activity are broad, including the defense of civil rights, education, health,
economic development, arts and culture, sports and recreation, international aid, and civic and
social activities (Boris & Steuerle, 2006).
While all NPOs are inherently nonprofit, they present different legal constitutions and confront disparate challenges in their brand management due to their distinct legal constitutions
and fields of action.
Table 1 reveals significant differences among various categories of NPOs; however,
researchers frequently use the term NPO to refer to foundations, charities, and NGOs without
distinguishing between them. As a result, there is a scarcity of literature specifically devoted to
building NGO brand equity.
NGOs are value-based organizations (Stride, 2006) defined, according to Salamon and Anheier (1992) and Vakil (1997), as prosocial, self-governing, private, not-for-profit organizations
that have a distinctive characteristic: promoting a particular cause, which often pertains to
social and political issues, such as humanitarian aid, welfare, freedom, justice, and equal rights,
with the aim of improving the lives of vulnerable people. Another aspect that differentiates
NGOs from NPOs is that while NGOs are often involved in international advocacy for social
and political change, NPOs focus more on providing direct services to meet specific needs in
their local community (Salamon & Anheier, 1992; Unerman & O'Dwyer, 2006; Vakil, 1997).
NGOs' moral objectives necessitate particular management principles (Jones et al., 2007)
that distinguish them from other NPOs in the eyes of donors, as they require higher moral capital and social legitimacy than other NPOs to fulfill their moral aims (Kane, 2001). Furthermore,
for donors to provide donations, they need to identify with an NGO's brand values. The degree
of alignment between a donor's moral values and an NGO's moral objectives affects the level of
support the former is willing to provide to the organization (Goenka & van Osselaer, 2019;
Gregory et al., 2020; Sargeant et al., 2008; Wymer et al., 2021). For instance, donors who support
Greenpeace or World Vision do so because they share their values and goals, protecting the
environment or children; however, such moral identification is not as necessary when donating
to other NPOs, such as the National Academy of Fine Arts (Boenigk & Becker, 2016; Gregory
et al., 2020; Venable, 2005; Voeth & Herbst, 2008). Given these differences, we contend that
NGOs' management differs from that of other types of NPOs, especially regarding how they
build donor-based brand equity.
The most widely adopted definition of brand equity is that of Aaker (1991): a set of brand
assets and liabilities linked to a brand, its name and symbol, which adds to or subtracts from
the value provided by a product or service to a firm and/or its customers. Similarly, NGO
donor-based brand equity refers to the differential effect that NGO brand has on donor
NGO preference and intention to donate. Aaker (1991) considers four dimensions of brand
equity, brand awareness, brand association, loyalty, and perceived quality, and studies the influence of each on brand equity.
Positive brand equity cause a product to be chosen over its competition by recognizing and
differentiating the brand, causing positive associations that facilitate loyalty to it (Aaker, 1991;
Yoo & Donthu, 2001).

is critical for NGO survival (Ha et al., 2022). In a growing and competitive “NGO market”
where attracting and maintaining donors is an ongoing concern (Michel & Rieunier, 2012),
building strong donor-based brand equity is highly relevant. An NGO with strong brand equity

is likely to attract donors who are more engaged and committed to the focal organization, leading to increased donations and greater loyalty.
To explore the factors that influence the formation of NGO brand equity, we conduct a comprehensive review of the literature on branding in NPOs and NGOs. We examine 511 papers
from the Scopus and Web of Science (WOS) databases published between 2000 and 2022 (see
Appendix 1 for more details). We then classify the literature by each brand equity dimension
from Aaker's (1991) most widely used model, identify the existing research gaps concerning
NGO particularities and propose new avenues for future conceptualizations of a novel NGO
brand equity model from the donor perspective.
Our study therefore makes two important contributions to the NGO literature. First, it contributes to the NGO management literature by deepening the understanding of NGO brand
equity building. Second, it assists managers involved in NGO marketing by reassessing the
dimensions of brand equity and suggesting adaptations and mechanisms that will allow them
to strengthen their NGO's donor-based brand equity and develop appropriate marketing strategies that further enhance it.

2 | LITERATURE R EVIEW
Although the concept of brand equity has been extensively investigated in the business literature, we found very few studies that explicitly address NPO brand equity models and none that
explicitly refer to NGO brand equity. The main focus in the NPO brand literature is thus a
donor's intention to donate and how some NPO brand attributes affect and/or influence this
intention positively. NPO brand equity models in the literature are also adaptations of brand
concepts concerning for-profit organizations, mainly Aaker's (1991) model. However, these
NPO models are difficult to generalize, as they typically rely on tailored measurement scales
specific to the unique characteristics of the focal NPO (Bennett & Gabriel, 2003; Ewing &
Napoli, 2005; Faircloth, 2005; Michel & Rieunier, 2012; Venable, 2005; Voeth & Herbst, 2008).
Therefore, there is not yet a generally accepted brand equity model suitable for NGOs.

3 | R ES U L T S A N D D I S C U S S I O N
To fulfill our objective to generalize an NGO donor-based brand equity model, we group and
discuss the following literature review based on Aaker's (1991) consumer-based brand equity
dimensions, which are the most widely used (Ishaq & Di Maria, 2020; Romero et al., 2023). The
dimensions included in Aaker's model are brand awareness, brand association, loyalty, and perceived quality. First, we review how these dimensions have been analyzed in terms of their
impact on donor behavior in the context of branding. Subsequently, we discuss the limited
number of comprehensive models developed in the NPO and NGO literature on brand equity.

3.1 | Brand awareness
Aaker (1991) considers brand awareness a component of brand equity that ensures a brand is
available for consumer consideration, either through recall or recognition. In the for-profit literature, the theories of mere exposure and signaling suggest that brand awareness alone

influences consumer decision-making, either by triggering an impulse purchase or by serving as
a cue for perceived quality (Erdem & Swait, 1998).
However, the conceptualizations of NPO brand awareness that focus only on brand recall or
recognition are insufficient in the NPO context; signaling is not enough, as donors require more
than mere brand recognition. Therefore, most authors incorporate the dimension of brand
familiarity, that is, the magnitude of knowledge about a brand (Keller, 2003) that determines its
prominence in a donor's memory (Gregory et al., 2020). However, there is some controversy in
the empirical results obtained thus far (Table A1 in Appendix 2).
NPOs are characterized by their moral engagements with social causes; hence, for NGOs, in
contrast to other NPOs, positive brand familiarity should be related to their cause and its moral
objectives. To impact NGO brand equity in a positive way, donors must not only recognize an
NGO brand but also be familiar with its purpose and approve of it.
This captivation is strengthened when a donor is directly affected by an NGO's cause
(e.g., cancer patients/relatives and cancer-focused NGOs); this can also facilitate the donor's
trust in the NGO (Czepiel, 1990; Sargeant & Lee, 2002).

3.2 | Brand associations
Brand associations refer to the brand perceptions of individuals in their memory and are categorized as product, organizational, symbol, or personality associations (Aaker, 1991). Within
brand associations, brand personality refers to “a set of human characteristics associated with
the brand” (Aaker, 1997, p. 347), allowing the consumer to identify with the brand
(Aaker, 1997; Keller, 1993). Brand image thus includes functional and symbolic brand associations (Aaker, 1991; Hankinson, 2001; Keller, 1993; Michel & Rieunier, 2012). Michaelidou et al.
(2015) argue that brand personality and brand image therefore play different roles in the NPO
context. Brand personality targets consumers (donors) and their self-concept when choosing an
NPO; brand image refers both to the NPO itself and the image it projects to society.
NPO brand personality and brand image have been extensively reviewed in the NPO literature by different clusters of authors. Several studies have developed customized scales for these
dimensions based on their focal NPO (Bennett & Gabriel, 2003; Faircloth, 2005; Michaelidou
et al., 2015; Michel & Rieunier, 2012; Sargeant et al., 2008; Venable, 2005; Voeth &
Herbst, 2008). Their objective has been to determine the predictive value of these associations
for donors' intentions to contribute to an organization.
However, the customization of the associations to specific personality types makes it difficult to generalize these models to NGOs. For example, sophistication is a favorable trait in a
cultural NPO, but, for a childcare NGO, sophistication is far less important than integrity or
childhood values.
Some authors have researched broader associations, such as integrity, reputation, commitment
to the public good, and financial stability. These associations highlight how critical donor trust is
in the nonprofit context. According to Sargeant and Lee (2002), trust is the foundation of charity
and voluntary organizations, one of the most important reasons that individuals donate (Hager &
Hedberg, 2016). NPO trustworthiness therefore not only attracts donors but also prevents the negative influence of scandals and damaging press in the nonprofit sector (Faircloth, 2005; Hager &
Hedberg, 2016; Hou et al., 2009; Sargeant et al., 2008; Venable, 2005) (Table A2 in Appendix 2).
Among NGOs, a more nuanced approach is needed that recognizes the distinction between
the functional attributes of a brand (its cause) and the symbolic values thereof (its beliefs).

Donors need to link both the cause and the moral values of an organization. Therefore, among
NGOs, brand associations should refer to their moral values and objectives rather than specific
personality traits. NGO moral compliance facilitates donor connection and the image that a
donor projects by partnering with an NGO. As NGOs are value-based organizations
(Stride, 2006), donors choose one or more NGOs with a brand image that portrays the values
they identify the most strongly with. Therefore, an NGO's donors will develop stronger selfidentifications with than with other NGOs less related to their personal values.

3.3 | Loyalty
Loyalty has multiple definitions (Jacoby & Kyner, 1973) but is generally defined in terms of two
aspects: Behavioral loyalty and attitudinal loyalty. Behavioral loyalty refers to the buying pattern of an individual, measured by repeat purchase, whereas attitudinal loyalty increases behavioral loyalty and affective commitment, influenced by the emotional aspects of an individual.
Although brand loyalty is crucial for building consumer-based brand equity (Oliver, 1997;
Yoo & Donthu, 2001), there is limited research on brand loyalty in the NPO context. One possible explanation for this is that donors often contribute to multiple NPOs at the same time.
Moreover, in humanitarian crises, donors may make donations without feeling committed to
an organization's long-term goals or causes (Gregory et al., 2020). Another reason is that the act
of donating is not in principle something that is necessary recurrent; therefore, it does not
require continued action that expresses loyalty. Nevertheless, brand switching and donor attrition are key issues that NPO managers must address.
In the NPO literature, the term “commitment” is often used instead of “loyalty” (Boenigk &
Becker, 2016) to describe a donor's attitudes and behaviors toward an organization that stem
from a strong belief in and acceptance of the organization's goals.
Sargeant and Lee (2004) argue that emotional attachment to an NPO brand creates a sense
of commitment that may lead to brand loyalty. Conversely, Bennett (2009) notes that donors
who have low involvement with an NPO are more likely to switch to other organizations.
We therefore suggest that for an NGO, before demonstrating a commitment, a donor must
have a favorable attitude toward its focal cause; only later will this donor choose a certain
NGO. Indeed, people are inclined to support NGOs that align with their personal values and
beliefs (Wymer & Akbar, 2019). If the moral objectives of an NGO are congruent with donors'
values, they are more likely to consider donating to that organization. However, if donors do
not feel connected to an organization's mission or values, they are unlikely to donate except in
emergency situations (Gregory et al., 2020; Sargeant & Woodliffe, 2007; Wymer et al., 2021).
Therefore, donors may remain loyal to a cause that resonates with them and, as a result, remain
loyal to multiple NGOs that advocate similar cause(s), such as Save the Children and World
Vision (Sargeant, 2001; Table A3 in Appendix 2).

3.4 | Perceived quality
According to Aaker (1991), perceived quality is a key dimension of brand equity, defined as the
overall assessment of the superiority or excellence of certain products or services based on a
user's perceptions (Myers & Allan, 1981; Zeithaml, 1988).

Perceived quality is rarely discussed in the NPO brand equity literature, although donors'
positive perceived quality is related to their retention and engagement (Sargeant, 2001). The
nature of the products offered by NGOs makes their quality difficult for donors to verify, as they
are not beneficiaries of these NGOs' actions, which usually occur abroad. Thus, donors must
make decisions based on certain cues as they have limited means by which to verify the quality
of NGOs' services (Prakash & Gugerty, 2010), affirming the importance of trust in these
organizations.
Some of the heuristics indicators of NPO quality in the personality and brand image
research discussed above include the traits integrity, reliability, commitment to the public good,
reputation, and financial stability (Bennett & Gabriel, 2000; Katz, 2018; Michaelidou
et al., 2015; Michel & Rieunier, 2012; Sargeant et al., 2008; Venable, 2005; Voeth &
Herbst, 2008), which indicate how an NPO is carrying out its work and whether a donor can
trust it. Using the SERVQUAL framework (Parasuraman et al., 1988), Sargeant (2001), and
Jones and Shandiz (2015) thus show that the best dimensions for assessing service quality are
reliability (a donor trusts an organization will do its job and that its funds will be used properly)
and tangibles (physical facilities, staff, and communication).
On the other hand, an NPO's efficient use of donations and effectiveness in achieving its
objectives (Sargeant & Woodliffe, 2007) create the perception of its performance. Thus, an
NPO's performance can also indicate donors' confidence in the completion of its promoted work
(Gregory et al., 2020; Sargeant et al., 2004).
For NGOs, demonstrating their quality, associated with their ethical performance, is therefore more necessary, as donors demand higher standards of moral capital from them. An NGO's
legitimacy, unlike that of other NPOs, is based both on its clear accountability for its actions
and its moral accountability regarding the beneficiaries of its cause (Slim, 2002). Given the difficulty of assessing the quality of the products provided by NGOs, we suggest that accountability
and reporting tools effectively demonstrate organizational reliability and integrity when
assessing perceived NGO quality. In this manner, donors can evaluate NGO performance and
compare the qualities of different NGOs (Sargeant & Woodliffe, 2007; Wymer et al., 2021)
(Table A4 in Appendix 2).

3.5 | Nonprofit brand equity models
In addition to each of the dimensions of Aaker's brand equity model, we have examined the
NPO brand equity models in the literature. We found no single model that is broadly accepted
in the NPO context; rather, different brand equity models are adapted to the specific characteristics of the focal organization, hindering their generalization.
Faircloth (2005) has proposed a nonprofit brand equity model from the perspective of volunteers and donors that includes brand awareness (recall, recognition, and familiarity). Under the
association dimension, he differentiates between brand personality and brand image. Interestingly, Faircloth discovered a negative correlation between brand awareness and brand equity,
contrary to his expectations. This finding indicates that if familiarity implies a negative perception of an NPO, the rejection of the organization is more evident. Similar outcomes have been
reported by other authors; still others have found the opposite, depending on their research
context (Dogan et al., 2021; Hou et al., 2009; Kashif et al., 2018; Paço et al., 2014).
From a theoretical perspective, Laidler-Kylander and Simonin (2009) propose an NPO brand
equity model consisting of four dimensions: consistency, focus, trust, and partnership. These

From the donor identification perspective, Hou et al. (2009) have studied the effects of brand
equity on the intention to donate by considering the mediating effect of the self-concept of individual donors. Their results indicate that brand personality and brand awareness strengthen the
self-concept of individual donors, which in turn influences their intention to donate in a direct
and meaningful way.
Juntunen et al. (2013) have developed a nonprofit brand equity model that incorporates
both internal and external stakeholders. Used to measure the impact of brand image and brand
awareness on the brand equity of the Military Driving School of the Finnish Defense Forces
(FDF), this model provides evidence of their positive influence. Kashif et al. (2018) alone have
explored the moderating impact of brand credibility on the donor-based brand equity model via
a rather comprehensive approach, but only in the context of Islam in Pakistan.
Finally, Boenigk and Becker (2016) have proposed a novel measure of brand equity in
NPOs, a nonprofit brand equity index from the perspective of volunteers and donors, the primary resource providers. Their index consists of three dimensions, brand awareness, brand
trust, and brand commitment, which they employ to establish a brand equity index for 40 German NPOs. Nevertheless, these authors do not analyze the overall effect of brand equity on
donor intention.
In summary, the current research on brand equity in the context of NPOs and NGOs is limited and inconclusive, with conflicting findings and a lack of generalizability. Additionally, the
research models used in this area do not incorporate relevant dimensions of brand equity, such
as loyalty and perceived quality, which impact brand equity according to the consumer literature and are also important in the NPO and NGO context.

4 | C ON C L U S I ON S
Through a critical review of the literature on NPO and NGO brand equity, this research
explores the sources of donor-based brand equity for NGOs.
We find a generalization of the term “NPO” in the literature that encompasses all types of
NPOs without considering important differences among them. This heterogeneity of the NPO
sector might be a possible explanation for some of the inconclusive findings in this literature.
We contend that there are significant differences between NPOs and NGOs regarding brand
equity. First, donors must identify with the cause and the social objectives of an NGO to trigger
their intention to donate and their future commitment to this organization. Second, donors
demand greater moral capital from NGOs than other types of NPOs, as the former address more
sensitive causes. Thus, donors establish preferences for NGOs that reflect their personal values
and allow them to project a positive image of themselves by associating with them. These peculiarities impact how NGOs build and legitimize their brands compared to other types of NPOs.
Hence, we highlight the limited and inconsistent NPO brand equity models and note that
no single NPO brand equity model has been widely adopted, likely due to their customization
to the unique characteristics of each focal organization.
Most brand equity models in the NPO literature have also adapted consumer-based brand
equity models and thus primarily focus on brand awareness, brand personality, and

addressed in the NPO and NGO contexts. For NGOs, these models fail to consider the impact of
donor identification with NGO cause and moral objectives on the development of brand equity.
These gaps in the literature have significant implications for developing effective strategies to
build brand equity. We suggest that future NGO donor-based brand equity models should move
beyond merely assessing awareness and include the more nuanced and complex concept of positive knowledge, as well as new dimensions, such as attitudinal and behavioral commitment, to
better reflect the meaning of loyalty.
Furthermore, it is essential to explore the factors that contribute to the perceived quality of
an NGO. This topic raises various marketing, organizational, and managerial issues, which
need to be considered when developing a comprehensive model of NGO brand equity. By doing
so, we can better understand the various elements that contribute to the formation and
strengthening of brand equity and develop strategies that enable NGOs to build a strong
and positive brand image.

4.1 | Managerial implications
The findings of our study have several practical and managerial implications. First, our results
suggest that simply increasing brand awareness is not sufficient to establish strong donor-based
brand equity. Instead, it is important for NGOs to disseminate their values and activities to
ensure that donors become familiar with their objectives and to focus on building their reputation based on these values. Therefore, NGOs should consider targeted communication strategies
that effectively communicate their values to potential donors to build their brand equity.
Second, our study highlights the importance of segmenting donors based on their identification with the moral values of an organization. This implies that communication strategies for
each target group should be tailored to effectively communicate the values and activities of an
organization to each specific group. A one-size-fits-all approach may not be as effective as
targeted communication strategies for each group.
Finally, this study emphasizes the importance of NGO accountability in building strong
donor relationships by demonstrating NGO quality. Fundraising campaigns should consider the
importance of communicating performance and achievements for building strong relationships
with donors. This may involve providing regular updates on an organization's activities,
highlighting the impact of donations, and recognizing donors' contributions to the organization's goals. Overall, these implications underscore the importance of building and maintaining
strong relationships with donors to establish and maintain strong NGO brand equity.

5 | LIMITATIONS A ND FUTURE RESEARCH
We acknowledge some limitations of our study, which merit further research. First, although
there are important conceptual differences among NPOs and NGOs, in some cases, it is difficult
to differentiate between the two. More granular distinctions in these types of organizations will
be interesting to explore. Second, as our study is exploratory in nature, our results do not provide direct insights into the effects and interrelations of the dimensions proposed for constructing a donor-based brand equity model. This is a topic that could be explored further in

future research. Despite these limitations, we believe that our review provides valuable insights
and a solid foundation for future research in this field.
Our findings also suggest several research avenues that can help NGOs build stronger
brands and increase their social impacts.
First, our results suggest that brand associations should be more related to an NGO's values,
objectives and reputation than to specific personality traits, which are more relevant in other
NPOs. Hence, we suggest that tangible associations such as personnel or physical facilities,
brand logos, or media content might also be worth studying.
Second, research should further investigate how donors perceive NGO quality, an important
dimension that is absent in the NPO literature. We propose the use of accountability tools to
demonstrate organizational reliability and legitimacy to assess perceived NGO quality. Some
works have studied the effects that voluntary NPO public disclosure have on donors (Saxton
et al., 2014); however, these results have not been incorporated as elements to assess perceived
NGO quality using brand equity.
Third, regarding brand loyalty, it is important to investigate NGO brand loyalty when
donors may support multiple various NGOs simultaneously. In addition to behavioral commitment through donation, we propose examining donors' attitudinal commitment, as their emotional connection with a cause may enhance their loyalty to an NGO. Therefore, we suggest
extending the concept of commitment by considering both behavioral and attitudinal
dimensions.
Fourth, moral self-identification with NGO causes should be explored to understand how to
trigger donor intention. Certain elements, such as NGO cause, size, and whether an NGO is
local or international, should be investigated; these may impact donor self-donor identification
with an NGO brand.
Finally, few studies have explored how marketing mix influences brand equity formation.
We thus suggest that future research examines how to build stronger NGO brands, exploring
the impact of the marketing mix, including social media and cause-related marketing.

A P P END I X 1 : METHOD
To analyze the NGO and NPO brand equity literature, the WOS Core Collection and Scopus
databases were used as the main sources for the research. The string defined for the literature
search was “NPO OR nonprofit OR charity* OR NGO OR nonforprofit OR nonprofit AND
Brand*” to collect any brand article related to any of the nomenclatures used for NPOs
and NGOs in the literature. We limited the search to articles and reviews in English for the
research areas of business, economic, and social work. This search provided 205 articles in
WOS and 306 in SCOPUS.
We then examined all article titles and abstracts to excluded duplicates and those that did
not address NGO or NPO brand equity or brand equity dimensions. After analysis and exclusion, our sample for the systematic review included 61 articles. The article selection and
