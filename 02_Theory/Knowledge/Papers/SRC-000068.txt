Abstract
The purpose of this study is to examine the impact of donor-related factors (e.g.,
religiosity, and attitude toward helping others), m-payment system-related factors
(system trust, and effort expectancy) and charity brand-related factors (a donor-brand
engagement model) on the intention to donate via mobile payment applications (m-
payment apps) and intention to recommend this function to others. Furthermore,
importance-performance map analysis (IPMA) was used to list factors in order of
priority to provide managerial insights. 523 donors of prior donation experiences from
9 charity organizations in Tehran participated in this study designed to gauge their
intention to donate via m-payment apps and intention to recommend to others. PLS
structural equation modeling was used to test the hypotheses of this study. The results
indicated that donor-brand engagement, helper’s high, and donor involvement fall into
the “maintain quadrant for intention to donate via m-payment apps. Additionally, as
religiosity has the lowest importance but relatively high performance, investing in this
variable seems to be a wasted effort. Also, m-payment system-related factors have
relatively low importance. So, they are not sufficient to motivate donors to donate via
m-payment apps. Consequently, charity managers who are interested in expanding
online donation via m-payment apps should prioritize improving the performance of
the brand-related factors. Although religiosity may affect offline donation, its influence
was insignificant in this study. It seems religious donors in this study seek visible or
public displays of charity donation. This can be justified by pointing to the conspicuous
consumption and need for social status.

Keywords Brand engagement . Charity reputation . Donation intention . Mobile payment
apps . Religiosity

1 Introduction

The ubiquity of mobile payment applications (m-payment apps) has fundamentally
reshaped the process of some payment services. After the emergence of the 4th and 5th
generation of mobile internet and smartphones, m-payment apps have proliferated over
the recent years in Iran as a developing country. There are a variety of financial and
payment services available in these apps. These services include bill payment, card to
card transfer, inventory inquiry, and travel services, which enable users to send money
remotely by using a mobile device. Charity is a new feature of m-payment apps in Iran
as an online donation platform. In the charity section of m-payment, there is a shortlist
of charities to which the users can choose to donate. After the selection of the respective
charity, donors donate money using a bank card number and related password.

The function of charity donation is essentially different from the other services
which are available in these m-payment apps. In other services, users receive financial
benefits. As the charity organizations offer intangible services which the consumer
(consumer in our study refers to the donor) does not consume (Meijer 2009), the factors
that influence the charity donation intention via m-payment apps differ from those
impacting the adoption intention of m-payment apps to receive the other services.

Bekkers and Wiepking (2011) identified eight different reasons for charitable
donations. Their categorization is an extension of types of “costs and benefits” asso-
ciated with motivation factors for a charitable donation. They indicated that even the
physical comfort such as weather conditions affects people’s money donation possi-
bilities. Moreover, in the context of consumption motives, the donation may be
classified as an exchange. Due to the intangible services of charities for donors as
consumers, social exchange plays an important role in consumers’ decision of donation
(Venable et al. 2005). Donors’ behavior can be affected by pro-social self-attribution
values, social approval, humanitarianism, or material benefits (Mitchell and Clark
2019). For example, when donations are announced in public or when donors are
directly observable, reputation as the social consequences of donations is rewarded to
the donors.

In addition, identification is related to donors engaging in supporting a non-profit
organization (Kleinschafer, et al. 2018). A person’s identity is made up of narrower
identities including self-identity and social identity. Social identity refers to individuals’
tendency to be identified with groups in their social environment, while self-identity is
one’s characteristics as a particular individual.

So, payment methods which influence tangible and intangible costs and benefits,
and donors’ identification would serve as a motivation factor for charitable donations.
Considering the various existing mechanisms for donation decision-making, payment
method (online payment vs. cash payment or unknown vs. observable) would contrib-
ute to donation intention.

There are some studies about online donation intention via online platforms and
technology-mediated services, such as websites, blogs (Li 2017), social network sites
(Chen 2018), and charity applications (Choi and Kim 2016). When individuals visit
charity websites or blogs, the atmospheres of charity websites arouse visitors’ emotions
and affect their donation intention and behavior. Charity websites or blogs can use
appropriate colors, shading, text, imagery, and sound to encourage affective responses
(Bennett 2006). Moreover, charity fundraising via online social networking sites and

crowd-funding is often about projects that may be initiated by a particular person, and
initiator reputation can raise the number of potential donors (Liu et al. 2018). So donors
can be influenced by the expectations of others and the desire to be a member of a
community (Rodriguez-Ricardo et al. 2018; Choi et al. 2019). Crow-funding through
social networks provides charities with an additional advantage (Cho et al. 2019).
Besides, in seeking information about philanthropy, likely donors can interact with the
organization on social networks, and charity organizations use this effective interaction
to achieve their goal of collecting money for charity (Chen 2018). Also, donors can
download the charity application, and by using functions of the installed apps, donate
indirectly to a charity. In other words, donors donate in exchange for the functions of
free applications (Choi and Kim 2016). But, factors impacting charity donation inten-
tion via m-payment apps have not yet been exactly clarified.

In the charity function of m-payment apps in Iran, there is no more information than
the name of the branded charity. Managing charities as brands assists donors in
differentiating between the charities and understanding what a charity stands for, and
helps communicate value to their supporters (Wymer and Akbar 2019). In the context
of charities, donor-brand relationships are value-based (Wymer and Akbar 2017).
Brands are significant since they can acquire valuable donors and supporter relation-
ships (Hudson 2008, p. 72). Charities are supported by donors whose values are in line
with the charity’s value. So, traits associated with the brands can impact donors’ giving
intention and behavior (Sargeant et al. 2008). A well-known charity brand to the target
groups of donors is a strong brand that leads to a higher probability of donation
intention (Wymer et al. 2016).

In m-payment apps, donors can choose from a shortlist of branded charities.
Kull and Heath (2016) have shown that restricted cause choices (from a
shortlist) strengthen consumer-brand relationships. When there is only a
shortlist of charity brands on the apps and no further information given, the
brand knowledge must come before the brand can be chosen. Moreover,
according to the Zajonc’s theory of mere exposure “mere repeated exposure
of the individual to a stimulus is a sufficient condition for the enhancement of
his attitude toward it
(Zajonc 1968). So, the more the donor hears from the
brand the bigger its chance to be chosen.

A shortlist of branded charities can strengthen donor–brand relationships. When a
charity focuses on how the donors’ need for social exchanges is met through donation,
there will be a high level of engagement between the donors and the charity (Mitchell
and Clark 2019). On the other hand, the charity’s ambition to build strong relationships
leads to donor-brand engagement (Smitko 2012). Higher donor engagement rates have
a major impact on contributing to society and continuing their membership in a charity
organization (Jarvis et al. 2017). Furthermore, studies offer charity reputation and
donor involvement as drivers of donor-brand engagement (Bennett 2013, Algharabat
et al. 2018). Involvement is defined as “an active interest in, engagement with, and
commitment to a group or product (Hassay and Peloza 2009). When the donors have
active involvement with the charity, then the level of donors’ engagement will be high
(Mitchell and Clark 2019). Thus, brand engagement can ensure donors’ giving through
affective commitment. Therefore, this research tests charity reputation and donor
involvement as the antecedents and affective commitment and intention to donate via
m-payment apps as a consequence of donor-brand engagement responding to the need

for more research that explores antecedents and consequences of donor-brand engage-
ment in the context of donating via m-payment apps.

Moreover, donor-related factors can be important factors influencing donation via
m-payment apps. Since the name of donors remains unknown while donating via m-
payment apps, moral and internal emotions arouse the donors’ behavioral intention, and
donors are less influenced by externally-oriented arousal (Urbonavicius et al. 2019).
Accordingly, helpers’ high is an internal emotion that motivates donors. Helpers’ high
gives donors moral satisfaction and positive emotions as consequences of giving
behavior (Bennett and Savani 2004). Also, a sense of inner reward and gratification
from a positive attitude toward helping others are primary and important motives for
donation activities (Briggs et al. 2010). Likewise, the literature shows in comparison to
those who are not religious, the more religious the donors are, the more likely they are
to donate to charity (Lwin et al. 2014; Tiltay and Torlak 2020). So donor-related factors
including helpers’ high, attitude toward helping others and religiosity may affect the
donors’ intention to donate via m-payment apps.

M-payment apps allow consumers the quick and easy transfer of money without the
need for cash. Remote money transfer helps consumers to reduce operation time and
location restrictions of cash transfer (Oliveira et al. 2016). In the m-payment transac-
tion, the information passes back and forth to process the payment. This information
includes personal financial information. Users should trust that the transaction process-
es are meet their expectations, and their financial and sensitive information will be
shared only with the respective parties. So, in the context of monetary transactions via
m-payment, the users’ trust in m-payment systems plays an important role in predicting
their intention to use m-payment services (Bailey et al. 2020). On the other hand, the
users’ intention to use m-payment systems depends on how easy it is to do so (Cao and
Niu 2019). Moreover, technology-mediated charity donation focuses on easiness and
convenience factors (Chen and Givens 2013). So, effort expectancy, ease of use of a
system (Sivathanu 2019), can affect donors’ intention to use m-payment for donating
money to charities. Thus, system trust and effort expectancy can be considered an
appeal of donation via the m-payment system.

However, in the literature there is research into donation intention via some online
platforms. Still, donation via m-payment apps has not been studied, especially in
developing countries. M-payment apps are different from other online donation plat-
forms. In addition to being an unobservable donation method, m-payment apps are
lacking in the atmosphere of charity websites, social network site interactions, and the
free functions of application. So this study investigates how donor-related factors, m-
payment system-related factors, and charity brand-related factors would affect the
intention to donate via m-payment apps and intention to recommend it as a post-
adoption behavior.

An understanding of users’ donation intention via m-payment apps is important for
charity organization managers to prioritize the factors in order of importance and to
help them make the right marketing decisions and strategies to achieve their goals in
collecting donations. Besides, users’ m-payment app adoption intention is significant
for app providers. In addition, intention to recommend a technological innovation is
recognized as post-adoption behavior that is a tool for the success assessment of a new
technology which arouses the interest of providers and app marketers (Miltgen et al.
2013; Oliveira et al. 2016). Thus, this research is going to focus on the factors that

The combination of donor-related factors, system-related factors, and brand-related

2 Literature review and hypothesis development

The purpose of this study is to add to the knowledge of donation intention via m-
payment apps. Therefore, it suggests a brand engagement model in the context of
charitable donation and the relationship between the model and the intention to donate
via m-payment apps and the intention to recommend this technology to others. Also,
the literature was extended by examining the influence of donor-related factors, and
system-related factors on the intention to donate via m-payment apps. The relationships
are shown in Fig. 1. The focal constructs section presents definitions and previous
research findings on these issues, and then the hypotheses are proposed.

2.1 A donor-brand engagement model

Brands are used to recognize the products and services of one producer and organiza-
tion from those of competitors (Sargeant et al. 2008). Charity brands are also employed
so that people can recognize the difference between a charity and its competitors. Due

to the increasing number of charities and their competitiveness, brands are important in
differentiating charitable organizations and achieving competitive advantage (Michel
and Rieunier 2012; Randle et al. 2013). As there is no tangible product to sell, branding
is critical for charities to communicate their services and values to donors as their
consumers (Stebbins and Hartman 2013). Positive attitudes towards a charity brand
leads to a higher level of donation behavior (Randle et al. 2013).

Brands are not passive entities, but the relation between brands and consumers is
considered an interactive dynamic relationship (Algharabat et al. 2018). The concept of
Consumer-brand engagement refers to consumer-brand relationship, which is psycho-
logical and interactive in nature. It encompasses a consumer’s pride and passion for the
brand, and rises from the strength of the consumer-brand relationship (France et al.,
2016). The term “engagement
is used to express this developed relationship, which
leads to a growth in the accounting rates of return and profitability, enriches perfor-
mance outcomes, and improves consumers’ significant contributions (Khan et al.
2016). Since brand-engaged consumers play an essential role in improving the perfor-
mance of companies and organizations, brand-engagement should be given precedence
in branding strategies (Stathopoulou et al. 2017). The engagement is also important for
charities to connect donors emotionally to charities and their activities.

Some researchers have characterized the consumer-brand engagement as a three-
dimensional variable including cognitive (a consumer level of using his/her mind to
consider consumer-brand interaction carefully), affective (the emotional bond between
consumer and brand), and behavioral (the level of time, effort or energy that the
engaged consumer is prepared to invest in consumer-brand interaction) (Stathopoulou
et al. 2017) or absorption (sense of being fully concentrated and happily engrossed in
brand interactions, in which time passes quickly), dedication (sense of significance,
enthusiasm, inspiration, pride, and challenge), and vigor (the willingness and the ability
to invest effort in such interactions) (Dwivedi 2015). Therefore, engaged donors invest
a high level of resources to the charity (Vigor), while they take pride in belonging to the
charity and have a strong identification with it (Dedication) and are fascinated by the
charity’s activities (Absorption) (Bennett, 2013).

According to the threefold classification of Carvalho and Fernandes (2018), this
study addresses the existing donors of branded charities as “engagement subject
the
brand of charities as “engagement object , and the charitable donations as the “engage-
ment context . Thus, this study introduces the term donor-brand engagement taking into
consideration the relationship between donors and brands as an important element in
donation intention.

As charity organizations do not offer tangible products, there needs to be a strong
and dynamic relationship between donors and brands. Therefore, a donor-brand en-
gagement model related to donating via m-payment apps has to be explored. In this
study, donor involvement and charity reputation are drivers of the donor-brand en-
gagement model and the donors’ affective commitment and the intention to donate via
m-payment apps are the consequences (Fig. 1).

2.1.1 Drivers of donor-brand engagement

Different antecedents and consequences have been identified in the literature of
consumer-brand engagement. The antecedents of donor-brand engagement within the

Charity donation intention via m-payment apps: donor-related,...

context of charitable donation via m-payment apps are donor involvement and charity
reputation in this study. These constructs were adopted considering how important they
are in creating donor-brand interactive relationships and individual experience in the
context of charities as the service-dominant organizations (Bennett 2013; Algharabat
et al. 2020). France et al. (2016) categorized the antecedents of consumer-brand
engagement as firm-led and consumer-centered. In this study, donor involvement and
charity reputation are investigated respectively as donor-centered and charity-led ante-
cedents of donor-brand engagement.

Involvement is defined as the extent to which a consumer feels that a targeted object
is consistent with his/her concerns, importance, values, and interests. A convergence of
a consumer’s personal relevance and a brand leads to the consumer-brand engagement,
as a result of which the consumer is prepared to invest time, energy, thoughts, feelings,
and actions in the brand (Carvalho and Fernandes 2018). Involved consumers feel a
very strong liking for the brand. This strong feeling puts the involved consumers deep
into a particular relevance to that brand and converts them to engaged consumers
(France et al. 2016).

Based on what has been investigated by Bennett (2009), strong feelings of personal
involvement with the charity could enhance the donors’ inclination toward charitable
donations. Becoming involved with a charity increases intangible benefits by increas-
ing the donors’ knowledge of and support for the charity’s activities (Stebbins and
Hartman 2013). In the context of donation, involved donors go beyond just giving
donations and a superficial attachment and become engaged with the brand and even
become a member of the charity brand community (Hassay and Peloza 2009). Like-
wise, Algharabat et al. (2018) have shown that involvement positively affects
consumer-brand engagement in the context of non-profit organizations. So, the follow-
ing hypothesis is posited in the context of charitable donation via m-payment apps in
Iran:

H1. Donor involvement positively affects donor-brand engagement.

Involvement is studied as an effective factor in the process of identification. According
to Bloemer and Odekerken-Schröder (2003), involvement is a situation in which
consumers’ values, needs, and self-images are recognized by an object which can be
the brand or the service provider. The perceived relevance to the object leads to
affective and emotional ties, and identification with an organization (Bennett 2013).
On the other hand, affective commitment is a psychological attachment to an object and
is associated with feelings and identification. Affective commitment captures the
consumers’ desire to maintain ties with the brand (Hassay and Peloza 2009; Vivek
et al. 2012). Studies have shown that the affective form of commitment is developed by
individual involvement (Bloemer and Odekerken-Schröder 2003; Rather et al. 2018).
Likewise, involvement has been recognized as an indicator of donor commitment to
charitable donation (Hassay and Peloza 2009). Furthermore, Merchant, Ford and Rose
(2011) have shown that emotional benefits affect the donor’s commitment. Affective
commitment is an emotional factor, which evolves out of personal involvement, which
contributes to a significant level of commitment (Rather et al. 2018). Thus, in the
context of donation via m-payment apps, the following hypothesis is posited:

H2. Donor involvement positively affects the donors’ affective commitment.

Based on the resource-based view, charity reputation can be a valuable, rare, and
inimitable resource, which can create a competitive edge. Regarding the charity
organizations that offer intangible service, the donors do not consume it; reputation
plays a prominent role in the organization’s success (Meijer 2009). In addition, an
individual’s perspective on the organization’s reputation is a part of his/her experience
of the organization. Reputation strengthens a close psychological bond between indi-
viduals and organizations, which exerts considerable influence over the perceived
relationship quality (Bennett 2013). Hence, the good opinion that people have about
the organization, mainly based on the experience they have had with the organization,
leads to high levels of engagement. Accordingly, Bennett (2013) has shown in the
context of offline donation that donors are more willing to engage with the charities
which have a good reputation. Thus, in the context of donation via m-payment apps,
the following hypothesis is posited:

H3. Charity reputation positively affects donor-brand engagement.

2.1.2 The consequences of donor-brand engagement

Examination of the consequences of donor-brand engagement has an important value
in helping charities reach their goals of collecting donations. Marketing researchers
have noticed that consumer-brand engagement is an antecedent of the consumers’
affective commitment (Vivek et al. 2012). According to relationship marketing theory,
perceptions of the strength of the relationship shape the consumers’ behavior and
intention in that relationship (Jeng 2016). When consumers experience positive emo-
tions as a result of a strong relationship with the brand –conceptualized as consumer-
brand engagement— there would be a tendency to behave in a way that leads to a more
heightened consumer-brand relationship (Fang 2017). Affective commitment is an
important factor that serves as the strength of the relationship, which is defined as a
psychological desire to remain attached to the partner-brand relationship (Vivek et al.
2012; Jeng 2016). In the context of branded charities, affective commitment as a
psychological inclination toward the continuity of a relationship guarantees the donors’
efforts to maintain the relationship. Thus affective commitment can be a consequence
of donor-brand engagement. So, the following hypothesis is posited:

H4. Donor-brand engagement positively affects the donors’ affective commitment.

The investment of the preferred self of the consumer is an outcome linked to engage-
ment (Kumar and Nayak 2019). Engaged donors find their interaction with the charity a
meaningful one. Their interaction with the charity is meaningful, mainly because their
investments are rewarded physically, cognitively, or emotionally. In this case, donors
are affectively committed to the charity and are prepared to invest resources, including
thought, emotion, time, money, and energy. So, a meaningful interactive relationship
between donors, as consumers of charities, and the brand of charities would make the
donors more willing to invest their resources. Thus, the charities emotionally engage

consumers when appealing for donations (Merchant et al. 2011). Algharabat et al.
(2018) have indicated that engagement with a non-profit organization Facebook page
(the brand) raises the probability of members’ willingness to donate. In addition,
Bennett (2013) suggested that an actively engaged donor intends to offer more support
in the future. Hence, in the context of donation via m-payment apps, the following
hypothesis is posited:

H5. Donor-brand engagement positively affects intention to donate via m-payment
apps.

2.2 Affective commitment and intention to donate via m-payment apps

According to relationship marketing, commitment is a key-mediating construct which
links evaluative variables to intentional variables (Fullerton, 2005). Donor commitment
has been recognized as a predictor of charitable donation (Sargeant et al. 2006; Alhidari
et al. 2018). In studies of marketing relationships, commitment is usually operational-
ized as affective commitment (Fullerton 2005). So, donor affective commitment can be
critical for the charity when trying to raise funds. As affective commitment is based on
the shared values, identification, and attachment (Fullerton 2005; Erciş et al. 2012),
affectively committed donors are inclined to support or give money to the charity to
which they feel attached and with which they identify, and they desire to maintain the
relationship. On the other hand, Meng et al. (2016) have emphasized the significant
positive effect of affective commitment on users’ behavioral intention in the context of
online crowd-funding. So, the rationale behind the impact of affective commitment on
donation can be logical for intention to donate via m-payment apps. So, the following
hypothesis is posited:

H6. Affective commitment positively affects intention to donate via m-payment
apps.

2.3 Donors-related factors and intention to donate via m-payment apps

Donating to a charity requires certain motivations and incentives. There are different
internalized and externalized motivations for charitable donation intention. However,
when donors remain unknown, their primary motivation may be restricted to internal-
ized motivation. Donors do not reveal their identity while donating via m-payment
apps. So, since there is no external recognition, donation intention is influenced by
internalized motivation (Urbonavicius et al. 2019). Donation intention via m-payment
apps could be influenced by moral emotions, internalized moral values, or feelings that
are experienced or felt internally.

The donors’ strong desire to achieve “do the right thing , justifies seeking moral
satisfaction. Also, people show a positive attitude toward helping others to experience
positive inner emotions (Human and Terblanche 2012). Attitude toward helping others
is defined as a relatively enduring judgment about helping, supporting, or assisting
other people, which include a wide range of donation behaviors (Rodriguez-Ricardo

F. Maleki, S. M. Hosseini

et al., 2018). Attitude toward helping others judges individuals on their personal norms,
internalized values, and beliefs in a charitable act (Moriuchi and Chung 2018). Attitude
toward a particular behavior is a reliable index of behavioral intention. The aspiration
of helping others has been acknowledged as an important motivator for charitable acts
(Webb et al. 2000). Positives attitude toward helping others could be derived from the
donors’ basic beliefs, inner empathy, and personal norms (Wymer and Akbar 2019).
So, a sympathetic attitude toward helping others may be predictive of charity donation
intentions via m-payment apps.

H7. Attitude toward helping others positively affects intention to donate via m-
payment apps.

On the other hand, people look for experiencing “the warm glow of giving . “The inner
warm glow refers to the construct of helper’s high as an experience following
charitable donation, an experience that leads to pleasurable emotions and personal
satisfaction that make the donor’s life a better one (Bennett 2018). Considering helper’s
high, a charitable donation is interpreted as the monetary purchase of moral satisfaction,
and an internal sense of well-being (Bennett 2006). So, the helper’s high is just like
giving something to yourself. Helper’s high, the feeling of calmness and inner self-
worth as a consequence of helping others, is said to be a donor-related reason for
charitable donations. As a result, this study proposes the following hypothesis.

H8. Helpers’ high positively affects the intention to donate via m-payment apps.

Moreover, religion as a social-cultural system with specific morals and spirituals for its
followers plays an important role in giving attitude, intention, and behavior (Noor, et al.
2015; Tiltay and Torlak 2020). Religiosity is defined as the belief in God and the extent
to which a person lives out his or her religious rules and principles (Muralidharan and
La Ferle, 2018). Religion and charitable donation intention and behavior have been
studied in charity studies inseparably (Rajan et al. 2009; Lwin et al. 2014). Religiosity
is considered a spiritual reason based on the spiritual values of individuals for charitable
activities. The loyal persons to religious values are more willing to donate (Tiltay and
Torlak 2020). Peoples’ religious values, which become their self-worth, affect their
donation intention (Arli and Lasmono 2015). In Iran, as a Muslim country, donation
patterns have been shaped by religion. The importance of religion and rituals are
undeniable in Iranian people’s lives. Receiving a return on charity in the afterlife can
be a driver of donation motivations (Skarmeas and Shabbir 2011). So:

H9. Religiosity positively affects intention to donate via m-payment apps.

Moreover, all major religions have taught the importance of giving behaviors and have
encouraged helping others (Arli & Lasmono, 2015; Tiltay & Torlak, 2020). So, more
religious individuals feel that they have a duty to others (Hopkins et al., 2014). There
are mandatory and optional charitable contributions in Islam for all Muslims who meet
the necessary criteria of wealth (Rizal & Amin, 2017). So, this study assumes that in
Iran, as a Muslim country, religiosity affects donors’ attitudes toward helping others.

can predict the level of the donors’ intention to donate via m-payment apps.

2.5 The donors’ intention to recommend donating via m-payment apps

The intention to recommend a technological innovation has been considered an
evaluation of the success of new technology. Recommending a technology to others
is a positive word of mouth in which providers and marketer are interested (Miltgen
et al. 2013). Studies have shown that consumers with a higher intention to use the
technology are more likely to actually use it and then recommend it to others (Oliveira
et al., 2016). Hence, the intention to recommend a technology is a type of post-adoption
behavior, which is vital if the technology is to stand out among its competitors
(Verkijika 2020). Charitable donation services are a recent addition to m-payment apps
in Iran. The acceptance and usage of this function can have more than individual
acceptance (Miltgen et al. 2013), as word of mouth and recommending intention can be
applied to spread information, ideas, opinions, experiences, and even behavior to as
many people as possible. Thus, this study proposes the intention to recommend
donating via m-payment apps as the consequence of the intention to donate via m-
payment apps.

H13. The intention to donate via m-payment apps positively affects the intention to
recommend donating via m-payment apps.

3 Methodology

3.1 Measurement instrument

The measurement instrument consisted of two sections of demographic questions
(items for gender, education, age, and income) and open-ended questions about
research variables. The second section included 47 items to measure the variables of
the research model. The preliminary questionnaire was translated into Persian. To be
sure of the face and content validity of the translated questionnaire and to make items
free from complexity or ambiguity (As a procedural remedy for common method bias),
content validity index (CVI), and content validity ratio (CVR) were conducted through
a panel of experts. The questionnaire items were measured with a 5-point, Likert-type,
from strongly disagree (1) to strongly agree (5). Since the data was collected from
single respondents in a one-time survey, the common method bias (CMB) had to be
assessed. So, procedural (Preventive techniques) and statistical methods were used to
control the potential common method bias (Podsakoff et al. 2003). The respondents
were assured that they would remain anonymous. For psychological separation, as a
way to control CMB (Jordan and Troth 2020), the respondents were asked some
questions between the independent and dependent variables. Measurement scales were
standard and established measurements were adapted from previous studies (see
Table 1). The questionnaire was pre-tested among 30 respondents from the study
population to avoid misunderstandings.

Charity donation intention via m-payment apps: donor-related,...

3.2 Sampling and data collection

Data collection was done through the questionnaires that were distributed face to face
and online. Due to their popularity, twelve charity organizations in Tehran, to which
donors can donate money via m-payment apps, were contacted for data collection. Nine
of these organizations cooperated with this study. The questionnaire started with two
screening questions. First, the donors were asked about the frequency of their donations
to the charity. Since past behavior is a predictor of future intention to donate
(Michaelidou et al. 2015), donors who had made three or more donations to the same
charity over the past year were included. According to the interview with charity
managers, three or more donations to the charity are equated with brand engagement
and commitment to the charity. Then, the respondents were asked if they had ever
donated money via m-payment apps. Respondents were asked to answer questions for
the charity which they currently supported. Also, if the respondent passed the screening
questions and completed the questionnaire, he/she was asked to extend the survey
participation invitation to other donors who would then be included in the sample. A
total of 560 respondents answered the survey from the 1st to the 20th of December
2019. However, the responses of 37 questionnaires were excluded from the analysis
due to missing data and straight-lining. The demographic information of the respon-
dents is shown in Table 2.

4 Data analysis and findings

The data analysis was conducted in SPSS 23 and SmartPLS 3. A systematic evaluation
of partial least squares structural equation modeling (PLS-SEM) followed the assess-
ments of the measurement models and the structural model. PLS-SEM supports the
complex model and the use of variable scores in a subsequent analysis such as
importance-performance matrix analysis (Hair Jr et al. 2016, p. 23). This study extends
the research model analysis by performing IPMA in SmartPLS to prioritize the
antecedent factors for the intention to donate via m-payment apps drawn on the two
dimensions of importance and performance.

4.1 Common method Bias

Since the data was collected from single respondents in a one-time survey, three post-
hoc statistical techniques including Harman’s single-factor test, correlation marker
technique, and full collinearity variance inflation factors (VIF) were conducted to
check for CMB. Harman’s single-factor test indicates CMB as an issue if an explor-
atory factor analysis with all study variables produces eigenvalues suggesting the first
factor accounts for more than 50%. In this study, the total variance for a single factor is
30.00% and less than 50%; this suggests that CMB does not affect the data. Also, to
conduct the correlation marker technique (Lindell and Whitney 2001), eye color (light
or dark) as theoretically unrelated to the dependent variables was added as a marker
variable. The results show that the correlation between the marker variable and the
study’s principal variables in the proposed model was very low. Furthermore, the full
collinearity test was assessed to generate VIFs for all latent variables in the model.

4.2 Measurement model evaluation

The measurement model evaluation of PLS-SEM estimates the reliability and validity
of the construct measures. The evaluation of the reflective measurement models
includes internal consistency, convergent validity, and discriminant validity (Hair Jr
et al. 2016).

Cronbach’s α, composite reliability (CR), and Dijkstra–Henseler’s rho are reported
to evaluate the internal consistency of latent variables. According to the minimum
threshold of 0.7 for the three criteria (Hair Jr et al. 2016), the internal consistency
reliability of the constructs was confirmed (Table 3).

Furthermore, convergent validity is assessed according to the reliability of indicators
(outer loadings) and the average variance extracted (AVE) from the constructs. Taking
into account the minimum threshold of 0.7 for the factor loadings of the indicators, all
indicators are reliable. Also, due to the threshold of 0.50 for AVE, the model’s
convergent validity was approved (Table 3).

Finally, the discriminant validity of the constructs is assessed by cross loading tests,
Fornell–Larcker criterion, and Heterotrait-Monotrait Ratio (HTMT) approach. Accord-
ing to cross-loading tests, the own loadings of each construct should be higher than (at
least 0.2) the cross-loadings with other constructs (Table 4).

The Fornell–Larcker criterion requires that the constructs share more variance with
their indicators than with any other construct. So the squared AVE of the
constructsshould be higher the correlations between constructs (Table 5).

Also, due to the maximum threshold of 0.85 for HTMT (Henseler et al. 2015), the

results approved the discriminant validity of the constructs (Table 6).

4.3 Assessment of structural model

After confirming the measurement model validity and reliability, a systematic approach
to the assessment of the structural model includes the assessment of collinearity, the
significance and relevance of the structural model relationships (Hypothesis testing),
the level of R squared, the f2 effect size, and the predictive relevance Q2 (Hair Jr et al.
2016, p. 206). Moreover, the full VIF is measured to assess the collinearity. As the VIF
values were below 5, it was confirmed that collinearity is not a problem (Hair Jr et al.
2016).

The results of testing the hypotheses are shown in Table 7. Ten of the thirteen
hypotheses were supported. Results indicate that donor-brand engagement, affective
commitment, attitude toward helping others, helper’s high, and system trust are signif-
icant antecedents of intention to donate via m-payment apps lending support to H5, H6,
H7, H8, and H12. Also, donor involvement and charity reputation significantly affect
donor-brand engagement, which means that H1 and H3 were supported. Moreover, H2
and H4, which predicted that donor-brand engagement and donor involvement signif-
icantly influence affective commitment, were supported. But the effect of religiosity on
the intention to donate via m-payment apps, and attitude toward helping others are
insignificant, which is counter to H9 and H10. Furthermore, effort expectancy does not
influence the intention to donate via m-payment apps, which is counter to H11. Finally,
the intention to donate via m-payment apps significantly affects the intention to
recommend donating via m-payment apps, lending support to H13.

4.4 IPMA results

The IPMA focuses on identifying antecedents that have relatively high importance but
also a relatively low performance for the target construct. This analysis highlights the
area that needs great attention. The IPMA includes four quadrants: “concentrate here
represents factors of high importance but a low performance, where focus should be on
the improvement of those factors; “maintain represents factors of high importance and
high performance, which should remain as they are; “possible overkill
represents
factors of low importance but high performance; and “lower priority represents factors
of low importance and low performance (Streukens et al. 2017).

To conduct IPMA in SmartPLS, three requirements need to be checked. First, all the
indicators are cheeked to be metrics and have the same coding (minimum value
represents the worst outcome, and the maximum value represents the best outcome
of an indicator). Second, all the weights of the scales must be positive. Third, VIF
values need to be checked to be below 5 (Ringle and Sarstedt 2016). According to the
results in Table 3, all requirements are met.

consequences. The analysis of this model showed that donor-brand engagement exerted
a highly significant influence on the intention to donate via m-payment apps. The
drivers of engagement that have been suggested by the literature in the context of
charities and non-profit organizations (Bennett 2013, Algharabat et al. 2018) offered a
sound explanation of donor-brand engagement in the context of donating via m-

reputation plays a vital role in convincing donors to make contributions (Meijer, 2009).
Reputation influences donors’ experiences and makes it develop by deeply embedding
the charity values in its activities. So, charities with an excellent reputation are expected
to create high levels of engagement. Also, the involved donors have a remarkable
relevance to a branded charity. Thus donor involvement is an antecedent of donor
engagement, and a higher level of involvement leads to a higher level of engagement.
Likewise, a strong feeling of involvement boosts affective commitment. Involvement,
as the perceived relevance to the charity according to the donors’ inherent needs,
values, and interests, influences affective commitment.

Engaged donors are highly likely to donate via m-payment apps that are similar to
the results of previous researches (Bennett 2013, Algharabat et al. 2018) in the context
of donation. The donor-brand engagement, which involves dedication to the branded
charity, goes beyond involvement to affective commitment to donation. Engagement
engenders a close and strong bond between donors and the branded charity. Engaged
donors would be eager to maintain the valued relationship by developing affective
commitment, which is a psychological attachment to the branded charity who is their
partner in this relationship.

Moreover, affectively committed donors feel a more emotional bond with the
branded charity. These donors have a greater tendency to donate via m-payment apps.
Commitment and donation intention are related concepts. There is a significant rela-
tionship between the level of a donor’s commitment and her/his intention to donate
(Merchant, Ford and Rose 2011). Individuals who are engaged in charity are more
likely to perceive themselves as committed donors to the charity. The affectively
committed donors have a high tendency to remain adherent to their commitment to
the charity and make donations via m-payment apps.

In this study, attitude toward helping others, helper’s high, and religiosity were
investigated as donor-related factors. Results show that attitude toward helping others
and helper’s high affect the intention to donate via m-payment apps positively. These
results are supported by previous studies (Bennett 2006; Veludo-de-Oliveira et al.
2017; Bennett 2018; Kwak et al. 2018; Allred and Amos 2018). Attitude toward
helping others can be influenced by social-cultural phenomena and beliefs (Roberts
& David, 2019), but the helper’s high arises from the experience of positive inner

emotion and donors’ self-esteem. The effect of religiosity on attitude toward helping
others and intention to donate via m-payment apps were insignificant, which is in line
with the studies of Lwin et al. (2014) and Arli & Lasmono (2015), but are counter to
Roberts & David (2019). Iran is a Muslim country, but religion does not necessarily
affect donors’ intention to donate and attitude toward helping others. According to
Finney (1978), religious commitment has a different dimension, including ritual prac-
tice, knowledge, experience, belief, and devotional practice. In Iran, religious teachings
are often concentrated on ritual practices rather than the devotional practice, which can
be due to the dominance of jurisprudence. On the other hand, all religious commitment
dimensions have not necessarily developed evenly in a religious person; in other words,
all religious commitment may not be seen in religious individuals’ behaviors and
intentions.

On the one hand, donors remain anonymous when donating via m-payment apps;
hence primary donation motivations may be restricted to internalized ones. On the other
hand, in the theory of the leisure class, Veblen (1899) considers conspicuous consump-
tion as a way of showing wealth and social status. He refers to the concept of
conspicuous consumption as consumption, not merely to satisfy needs, but also to
acquire greater respect and dignity and resemblance to the upper classes in modern and
urban societies. Furthermore, conspicuous displays of different affiliations such as
religious affiliations are a way of signaling belonging to a special social group and
represent a person’s perceived or actual place in that society (Johnson and Tariq 2016).
According to the results of this study, it can be concluded that Iranian religious donors
are motivated by a desire to be seen as responsible members of the religious society,
and consequently be awarded social esteem. As the identity of donors remains un-
known when donating via m-payment apps, Iranian religious donors would not be
motivated only by altruistic reasons.

Effort expectancy and trust in the m-payment system were examined as m-payment
system-related factors affecting the intention to donate via m-payment apps. Effort
expectancy does not influence the intention to donate via m-payment apps. However,
effort expectancy has been acknowledged as an effective factor in the early stages of
the adoption of m-payment; since charity is one of the newly added services to m-
payment apps, it has become easier for users to use these apps. On the other hand, the
process of donating via m-payment apps is effortless and has no complexity. So, effort
expectancy does not significantly affect donations via m-payment apps.

M-payment technology is well-known and has passed its early stages and has now
been proliferated; therefore, it is trusted by consumers. In other words, the m-payment
transaction process satisfies consumers’ expectations. The reason is that if anything
goes wrong, or something contrary to consumer expectations occurs, consumers would
just stop using m-payment apps and move onto another transfer method. So, trust in the
m-payment system plays an important role in consumers’ intention to use it. Trust in an
m-payment system affects donors’ intention to donate via m-payment apps as a method
of monetary transfer.

As can be seen, helper’s high which is in the lowest right area of the importance-
performance map, has high importance for the intention to donate via m-payment apps,
but shows the lowest performance. Thus, there is a notably high potential for improving
the performance of the intention to donate via m-payment apps by concentrating on
helper’s high. In addition, helper’s high would possibly prohibit the donors from

F. Maleki, S. M. Hosseini

distinguishing between or involving in charity organizations, and experiencing the
warm glow of giving could be an incentive to donate via m-payment apps. So,
reputation and involvement are less important than the act of donation.

Moreover, m-payment system-related factors have lower importance relative to the
other constructs (donor-related factors and charity brand-related factors) in the
importance-performance map, and have a lower priority for performance improve-
ments. Hence investing in the performance improvement of m-payment system-related
factors which have very small importance for improving the intention to donate via m-
payment apps would not be logical. However, m-payment apps remove restrictions on
time and location and help users to transfer money anytime and anywhere. Passing the
early stages of proliferation facilitates the technology and makes it a simple operation
for users. But, regardless of effort expectancy, if the user sees the advantages of using
the m-payment system are incompatible with the intended purpose, he or she may not
be willing to use m-payment for transferring money (Oliveira et al. 2016). According to
the theory of social exchange, if donation via m-payment apps does not have any
special social benefits for the donor, the incompatibility with the donors’ need for social
exchange dissuades him or her from donating via m-payment.

Furthermore, religiosity has the lowest importance but relatively high performance.
So, focusing on religious donors to create a stronger intention to donate via m-payment
apps is a wasted effort.

6 Conclusion and implication

This study aims to examine the impact of donor-related factors (helper’s high, religi-
osity, and attitude toward helping others), m-payment system-related factors (system
trust, and effort expectancy) and charity brand-related factors (a donor-brand engage-
ment model) on the intention to donate via m-payment apps and intention to recom-
mend this technology to others. This research suggests a donor-brand engagement in
which charity reputation and donor involvement are the antecedents, and affective
commitment and intention to donate via m-payment apps are the consequences of
donor-brand engagement.

As the donation payment method influences tangible and intangible costs and
benefits, donation via m-payment would contribute to the donation intention. Donation
via m-payment apps would affect the market segmentation and target group selection
process of charities. Patently, wealthy donors who seek social status by donating in
public are not the target group anymore. Donating via m-payment apps, the donors
remain unknown, and the donor is not directly observable. Remaining unknown will
affect donors’ donation intention. So, charities need to revise the demands and needs of
their target group.

Self and social identities are important factors contributing to donation intention.
Since in the context of donation via m-payment apps social identity is not salient, the
donors’ intention to donate via m-payment apps should match his/her self-identity.
According to Terry et al. (1999) the influence of self-identity on behavioral intention is
not dependent on whether the behavior had been performed in the past or not. So self-
identity would motivate individual donation intention if the charity is one that the
person has already engaged with (an engaged donor), or one that motivates a donor to

Charity donation intention via m-payment apps: donor-related,...

support at that moment in time from the shortlist of charity brands (e.g., due to the
awareness of the issue).

On the other hand, as charity brands are a focal cue for donors engaging in pro-social
behavior, charities need strong brands more than ever. A strong brand is well-known to
the target groups of the charity (Wymer et al. 2016), and has a good chance of being
selected by donors in a shortlist of the m-payment app.

As the results show in this study, donor-brand engagement is the most crucial factor
in donation intention via m-payment apps in Iran. So, charities need to enhance donors’
engagement. On the other hand, donors’ involvement and the charity’s reputation are
important constructs in explaining donor-brand engagement. Thus charities can get
more engagement from donors by intensifying donors’ involvement and the charity’s
reputation.

Charities can hold meetings with donors and talk about their activities. By providing
opportunities for effective interaction, charities can raise donors’ perceived relevance and
feelings of identification, which leads to donors’ involvement and, in turn, engenders
engaged donors. Likewise, charities need to consider effective communication strategies
that speak to their donors. Also, they should connect with donors, whose donations they
rely on, and inform them about the positive differences their activities have made to
people’s lives. This can raise awareness about the charity’s reputation. Furthermore, as the
need for social representation and identification is a driver of commitment (Bloemer and
Odekerken-Schröder 2003), it is important that donors feel they are a part of the conver-
sations and communications. So, the meetings and effective interactions between charities
and donors can boost donors’ affective commitment and engagement, which, in turn,
causes greater future intention to donate via m-payment apps.

Since engagement is the donors’ pride and passion for the branded charity, it derives
from the psychological and interactive bond between the donors and branded charities,
the charity managers should focus on the application of relationship marketing as an
instrument for sustaining the donor-brand relationship and connection.

Moreover, sharing experiences with others is likely to make donors feel more
engaged in a brand (Bennett 2013), so a growth in brand engagement can lead to
stronger tendencies to recommend donating to the respective charity via m-payment
apps. Thus, as the most valuable asset of charity brands is their ability to achieve
positive differentiation in the minds of their donors, branding strategies should be
applied to attract more engaged donors.

The helper’s high is the second most important construct that affects the intention to
donate via m-payment apps in this study. Pro-social behavior including donating
money to charities creates a pleasurable “helper’s high” that benefits the giver. Rudd
et al. (2014) believe that “the hedonic benefit givers receive from their pro-social acts
depends, at least in part, on the degree to which givers perceive that their actions had
the desired pro-social effect . So, if the donors know that their donation made a positive
difference in other people’s lives, they feel more positive emotions and happiness.
Thus, charities can boost helper’s high, and consequently, the intention to donate via m-
payment apps by defining a tracking code. A tracking code can specify the final pro-
social effect of a donation, and donors would be influenced by the desired pro-social
effect which their donations have created.

Despite the fact that m-payment system-related factors may be important in the early
stages of development and adoption of these technologies, they are not sufficient to

F. Maleki, S. M. Hosseini

persuade donors to donate via m-payment apps. So, m-payment app providers should
design these apps considering donors’ involvement and engagement in a way that
donors are inspired to donate via m-payment apps. Involved consumers contemplate
the future benefit of the relationship (Carvalho and Fernandes 2018). Thus, as
expressed by the social exchange theory, cost-benefit analysis of involved consumers
causes dedication and commitment (Osei-Frimpong and McLean 2018). Conforming to
social exchange theory, donations are a result of cost-benefit analysis in that donors
compare the benefits of donating against the cost of such behavior. So, app providers
can suggest free services according to the donation sum or frequency of donation via
m-payment apps, which can be considered as benefits of donating via m-payment apps.
As a consequence, donors would receive the intangible benefit of donating via m-
payment for the tangible benefit of using free services.

Users’ tendency to recommend a technology can be pivotal post-adoption behavior,
the result of which is standing out in the respective market. In comparison to other recent
studies (Miltgen et al. 2013; Oliveira et al. 2016; Verkijika 2020) that have investigated
the impact of intention to use other information technology services on recommending
using the technologies to others, this study shows a lower value of the variance when it
comes to recommendation. Vargo (2016) stated that consumers would share information
with friends in order to give them a chance to receive tangible value, considerable gain,
and benefit or free things. Also, Saxton (1995), believed “The environmental motivation
is the simplest of forces: people do things because their peers are doing it, or their
immediate environment will change, or they may directly benefit . So, charity managers
should offer giveaways, grants, or other obvious beneficial suggestions as ways to
encourage donors to recommend donating via m-payment apps.

According to the demographic information of the sample, relatively young middle-
income donors can be a good target market for charities looking to achieve fundraising
goals via an online platform such as m-payment apps. They are more inclined to use
technology, and due to their middle-income, their donation sums are possibly small. So,
they would prefer anonymity to conspicuous giving.

7 Future directions

Like any empirical study, this study is subject to limitations that future research can
overcome. Since donation is a social-cultural behavior, more cross-cultural investigation
of intention to donate via m-payment apps should be done. Also, more research should be
done into the branding strategies in the context of donation via online platforms especially
m-payment apps. Furthermore, future research may study to what extent technology-
mediated or mobile-mediated civic engagement is welcomed in religious societies.
