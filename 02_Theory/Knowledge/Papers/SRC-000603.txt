Donation Intention in Nonprofit Giving
Donation intention in charitable giving is mainly explained by psychological drivers, organizational credibility
cues, and appeal design, while evidence on donor satisfaction and repeat giving is sparse in the supplied literature
(White et al., 2023; Chapman et al., 2021). The evidence clusters into three facets: core predictors of intention, trust
and reputation under nonprofit uncertainty, and differences across motives, donation types, and fundraising
appeals (Benli & Kocaman, 2025; Robson & Hart, 2020).

Core Predictors
Across studies, donation intention is most consistently predicted by TPB variables, moral norms, past behavior, and
identification, with some variation by context and donation channel (White et al., 2023; Mittelman & Rojas Méndez,
2018).

‐

• Perceived behavioral control is the strongest TPB predictor in meta-analysis, with r = 0.562 for intention (White

et al., 2023).
• TPB predictors explain 44% of intention variance, rising to 52% when moral norm is added (White et al., 2023).
• In nonprofit-specific samples, attitudes, subjective norms, perceived control, past behavior, and brand

identification all predict donation intention, although altruism and self-esteem are not always significant (Lee &
Kim, 2023; Millán et al., 2023; Kim & Han, 2020).

Trust and Reputation
Trust and reputation matter because charities are hard to evaluate directly, so donors rely on organizational signals
to reduce uncertainty (Chapman et al., 2021; Ghoorah et al., 2021). The strongest evidence shows a positive but
moderate trust–giving relationship, and reputational cues often work partly by building trust (Chapman et al., 2021;
Ghoorah et al., 2021).

• Trust is positively associated with giving overall, with meta-analytic r = .22; organizational trust is stronger than

generalized trust (Chapman et al., 2021).
• In charitable organization studies, perceived ability, integrity, and benevolence all positively influence donation

attitudes or intention (Lee & Kim, 2023; Li et al., 2022).
• Reputation shapes donation intention directly and indirectly: CEO reputation changes which TPB predictors

matter, and decision-useful financial disclosures improve perceived reputation, then trust, then intention (Kim &
Han, 2020; Ghoorah et al., 2021).

Motives and Appeals
Warm glow, giving form, and fundraising context all influence donor behavior, so money, time, and crowdfunding
donations do not respond to the same cues (Benli & Kocaman, 2025; Chen et al., 2019).
• Warm glow appears important but not sufficient: it was the strongest driver in one donor survey, yet field and

fundraising studies indicate it operates alongside altruism, trust, identity, and other motives (Amonhaemanon et
al., 2024; Carpenter, 2021; Chapman et al., 2021).
• Time and money giving differ: time donations are more emotional and social, while money donations are more

utility-oriented; in crowdfunding, time intention was predicted by social presence, trust, and control, whereas
subjective norm was insignificant for money intention (Chen et al., 2019).
• Fundraising appeals change giving: self-benefit or warm-glow appeals increased giving likelihood by 6.6% and

donation amount by 23%, while recipient-benefit appeals showed no significant average effect; persuasive
project text also predicts crowdfunding donation intention (List et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022).
Overall, donation intention in nonprofits is predictably shaped by TPB cognitions, trust, reputation,
identification, and appeal framing. The supplied papers give little direct evidence on donor satisfaction as a
predictor of loyalty or repeat giving, and they address credence-good dynamics mostly indirectly through trust,
disclosure, and reputation rather than with a standardized credence-goods model.
