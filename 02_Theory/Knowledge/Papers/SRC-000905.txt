ABSTRACT
The aim of this study is to expand the existing understanding of green consumers' behaviour
by proposing and testing an integrated conceptual model that explores the inﬂuence of consumers' personal concerns for the environment and general attitudes towards green products
on brand-related knowledge structures (image and associations) and relationship preferences
(trust and brand equity) for green brands. A questionnaire-based survey method was used to
collect data using convenience sampling. One hundred and ninety-nine usable responses
were obtained. A structural equation modelling procedure was used to test the proposed
hypotheses. The results conﬁrmed that a positive relationship exists between consumer concern for environmental values and general attitudes towards green products. Both these constructs inﬂuence consumers' knowledge structure of a green brand (image and associations).
Furthermore, a strong relationship exists between consumers' knowledge structure (image
and associations) and their relational preference (trust and brand equity) with green brands.
These ﬁndings are important for business strategy formulation by providing empirical support
for the idea that a ﬁrm should invest its resources not only to project its environmentally
friendly brands but also to build consumers' concern for environmental values and their attitude towards green products. Copyright © 2016 John Wiley & Sons, Ltd and ERP Environment

A recent review of the existing environmental marketing literature by Chamorro et al. (2009) indicates the
presence of six main research streams. Of the six categories of research topics, two streams, green consumers
and green communication, explain 50% of the publications in green marketing. The ﬁrst stream, which we categorized as the behavioural perspective (referred to by Chamorro et al., 2009, as green consumers), mainly focuses on
investigating the relationship between consumer concern for environmental and other consumption values, general
attitudes towards green products and eco-friendly consumption behaviour. This stream of research bases its conceptual models on social adoption theory, value–belief–norm theory, theory of reasoned action and other similar
hierarchal models of values, beliefs, attitudes and behaviour (Ajzen and Fishbein, 1980; Biswas and Roy, 2015;
Jansson, 2011; Lin and Huang, 2012; Maniatis, 2015; Papista and Krystallis, 2013; Testa et al., 2015; Zhao et al.,
2014). Developed in the area of environmental psychology and consumer behaviour, this stream of research only
provides a generic model of consumer green purchasing behaviour, generally providing empirical evidence in the
context of low involvement post purchase behaviour (Jansson, 2011).
The second stream of research, which we categorized as the branding perspective (referred to by Chamorro et al.,
2009, as green communication and its subcategory as anatomy of green ads) is relatively new in green marketing literature, but is gaining momentum as it tries to explain the impact of green branding initiatives on consumer attitudes
and behaviour towards these brands (Bekk et al., 2016; Chen, 2010; Chang and Chen, 2014; Kang and Hur, 2012;
Ng et al., 2014; Testa et al., 2015; Tucker et al., 2012). One can argue that this school of research is fundamentally
interested in establishing how green brand knowledge structure, which exists in consumers' minds in the form
of brand image, awareness, associations and attributes (Esch et al., 2006; Fournier, 1998), inﬂuences their
responses towards a green brand in shape of their brand trust, satisfaction and long term behavioural commitments
(Esch et al., 2006). Associative network memory models, brand signalling theory and social exchange theory form
the theoretical underpinnings of this stream of study (Collins and Loftus, 1975; Erdem et al., 2006; Fournier, 1998;
Morgan and Hunt, 1994).
The divergence of perspective in these streams of research is evident by the fact that they use completely different
theoretical models to investigate green consumption behaviour. The behaviouristic schools explore the issue from a
global view of consuming green products, while the branding perspective investigates how speciﬁc brands facilitate
consumer aspirations towards becoming a green consumer.
Furthermore, despite the signiﬁcant contribution of both streams of research to green marketing literature, they
have their own shortcomings. For example, the green branding stream of research completely ignores the role of
personal values and attitudes and other socio-demographic variables in shaping consumer perceptions towards
green brands. On the other hand, behaviouristic researchers ignore the role of branding in facilitating consumers'
ability to convert their intentions into actions.
It may be that this exclusion of a branding perspective is one of the core reasons that researchers have struggled
to establish a strong relationship between environmental values and attitudes and green consumption behaviour.
Some studies suggest a very strong relationship while others report weak or moderate relationship between values,
attitudes and green consumption (Akehurst et al., 2012; Mostafa, 2007; Tseng and Hung, 2013). This shortcoming
might be due to the fact that the ‘means to behave’ in an eco-friendly manner largely depends on availability of
brands that offer green attributes. In our view, the most appropriate approach to address the above shortcomings
is by integrating the two schools of thought and moving towards a more holistic model, linking consumer personal
values and attitudes with their brand-related cognitive and relationship-oriented constructs. Such a model will provide ﬁrms with the much needed empirical evidence that investing in consumers' green education will also pave the
way to green brand performance.
This research will strengthen our argument that adopting a holistic green business strategy can help ﬁrms to
integrate stakeholder's interest, create value on multiple fronts and elevate their role from a political/economic actor
to a social actor (Haddock-Fraser and Tourelle, 2010). One way a ﬁrm can achieve this objective is by engaging in
building environmental collaborations to reduce the cost of educating consumers. For example, recent research

by establishing a positive linkage of consumer general green attitude and environmental values with green perceptions towards a speciﬁc brand.
We use the theory of individual self-enhancement (Rogers, 1951), the image congruence hypothesis (Gutman,
1982; Hogg et al., 2000) and the means–end model (Dibley and Baker, 2001) to provide theoretical justiﬁcations
for the proposed link of consumer concern for environmental values and attitudes with their brand-related cognitive
and relationship-oriented constructs. This paper is organized as follows. First it starts with reviewing extant literature, followed by hypothesis development. Sampling, data collection techniques and measurement of variables
are discussed in the methodology and measurement section. Results of structural equation modelling (SEM) are
given, along with a brief commentary. Finally, conclusions, limitations and implications are discussed.

Theoretical Foundations and Hypotheses
Consumer Values and Attitude Towards Green Products
From a psychological perspective, a value is a belief about one's desired end state (Kim and Chung, 2011; Rokeach,
1973; Schwartz and Blisky, 1987). Values are the most fundamental elements of the human belief system (Kim and
Chung, 2011; Vaske and Donnelly, 1999) and play a signiﬁcant role in moulding individual behaviour and attitudes
(Poortinga et al., 2004; Rokeach, 1973). From a social adaptation theory perspective (Bhate 2002; Homer and Kahle,
1988; Kahle, 1983; Leonidou and Leonidou, 2011), values as the most basic abstraction of social cognition serve as a
prototype from which speciﬁc attitudes and behaviours emerge (Homer and Kahle, 1988; Kahle, 1980). Therefore,
in a given context such as green marketing, environmental values will inﬂuence attitudes, which will subsequently
determine consumer green consumption behaviour. Similarly, value–belief–norm theory also postulates that attitudes and personal values are the most important motivational factors predicting consumers' ecological behaviour
(Stern et al., 1999). Other researchers have used the theory of reasoned action, theory of planned behaviour and
other hierarchal models of values, beliefs, attitude and behaviour to explain the linkage between consumer values,
beliefs, attitudes and intentions towards purchasing green products (Ajzen and Fishbein, 1980; Papista and
Krystallis, 2013).
Several past studies reported a positive relationship between consumer values and their behaviour in multiple
contexts (Weng and De Run, 2013). In the context of green consumption behaviour, past studies have reported that
environmentally conscious consumers are more willing to alter their consumption and purchasing habits in order to
contribute towards building a safer environment and a better world (Peattie, 2001; Kim and Chung, 2011). In a recent study, Kim and Chung (2011) reported that personal values such as environment consciousness and appearance
consciousness play a more crucial role than other personal values (such as health consciousness) in inﬂuencing consumer attitudes towards environmentally friendly products. Therefore, we propose the following hypothesis.
H1: Consumer environmental values will signiﬁcantly inﬂuence their attitudes towards green products.
Consumers who are concerned about the environmental consequences of their consumption habits generally
tend to hold more positive attitudes towards environmentally friendly brands (Ottman et al., 2006). The

means–end theory postulates that brand attributes lead to beneﬁts, which serve as a means to satisfy personal values
(Dibley and Baker, 2001). In the context of the present study, the ‘means–end theory’ provides support for the argument that eco-friendly attributes of a green brand provide environmental beneﬁts to its consumers, which serve
as a means to satisfy their concern for environment values. Thus, in accordance with means–end theory, green
brands have an environmentally favourable image due to their ability to satisfy consumer concern for environmental
values (Gutman, 1982; Dibley and Baker, 2001). Therefore, this study proposes the following hypothesis.
H2: Consumer environmental values are positively related to green brand image.
Attitude is the psychological evaluation of an object, measured by its attributes (Petty et al., 1997). Attitudes are
the most consistent explanation of consumers' willingness to actively engage in eco-friendly consumption behaviour
(Kanchanapibul et al., 2014). According to value–belief–norm theory, attitudes and personal values are the most
important motivational factors predicting consumers' ecological behaviour (Stern et al., 1999). Furthermore, measuring consumer general attitudes towards a product category is fundamentally important because of its tendency
to correlate with consumer preferences for a speciﬁc brand within that product category (Shamim and Butt, 2013;
Kim and Pysarchik, 2000; Walgren et al., 1995). For example, results of recent experimental research suggest that
those consumers who scored high on green product attitude also scored high on attitude towards the brand when
exposed to an advertisement of green functional appeal (Matthes et al., 2014).
Cretu and Brodie (2007) deﬁne brand image as the consumer's visualization of a speciﬁc brand, often related to
the speciﬁc products produced by a company. Consumers are constantly engaged in enhancing their self-concept
through symbolic consumption, manifested in acquiring brands that match with a speciﬁc dimension of themselves
(Grubb and Grathwohl, 1967; Sirgy, 1982). According to the image congruence hypothesis, people's inferences
about themselves and others depends on their choice of objects (Belk et al., 1982; Hogg et al., 2000). Furthermore,
later expansion in the image congruence hypothesis model proposes that consumption not only serves social symbolism objectives, but also caters to one's private self, where self-worth is achieved by striving to accomplish internal
standards (Greenwald, 1989; Hogg et al., 2000).
Thus, in the light of the image congruence hypothesis, one can argue that a consumer who has positive attitudes
towards green products will also hold a positive image towards green brands in order to achieve consistency between
personal goals and behaviour, either to fulﬁl his/her own standards or in an effort to generate symbolic recognition.
Therefore, this research proposes the following hypothesis.
H3: Consumer attitude towards green products will positively relate to green brand image.
Based on the proposed relationships in H1, H2 and H3, it is postulated that attitude towards the green products
might mediate the relationship between consumer concern for environmental values and green brand image.
Therefore we propose the following hypothesis.
H3a: Attitudes towards green products will mediate the relationship between consumer concern for environmental values
and green brand image.

perceptions are primarily created, built and managed by marketers (marginalizing and somewhat ignoring the other
sources that might help consumers to build knowledge about a particular brand). They also believe that it is not the
green products that consumers purchase, but the brands that develop these products. For example, within a product
category such as hybrid cars, some companies were able to enjoy early acceptance of new green products, as in the

In the context of green marketing, a green brand image is deﬁned as ‘a whole range of impressions, conceptions
and apprehensions towards a brand in the customer's memory, which is correlated to the sustainability and ecofriendly concerns’ (Chen, 2010, p. 309; cf. Ng et al., 2014). A successful green brand image only stays in the

attitudes and behaviour (Chen, 2008).
When it comes to building positive attitudes and perceptions towards a green brand, ﬁrms face a daunting challenge to build consumer trust in their brands in order to foster long term relationships with their customers. In the
context of green marketing, managing and maintaining consumers' green trust is a signiﬁcant challenge, considering the fact that, in the recent past, claims made by several organizations about the green attributes of their products
or services brands have been found to be at best ambiguous, if not overwhelmingly deceptive (Chen and Chang,
2012; Kalafatis et al., 1999; Ng et al., 2014).
Rousseau et al. (1998) deﬁne trust as ‘a psychological state composing the intention to accept vulnerability based
on expectations about the intentions or behaviour of another’ (p. 395). Chen (2010) deﬁnes consumer green trust as
a ‘willingness to depend on a product, service, or brand based on the belief or expectation resulting from its credibility, benevolence, and ability with regard to its environmental performance’ (p. 310).
Nonetheless, those brands with a positive image do inﬂuence consumer trust, because a positive brand image is
able to reduce consumers' perceived risk of buying a brand (Chang and Chen, 2014). Therefore, the following
hypothesis is proposed.
H5: Green brand image relates positively with green brand trust.

The Effect of Green Image and Green Trust on Green Brand Equity
Creating an effective green brand image depends on an organization's ability to channel consumers' green or environmentally afﬁrmative emotions, perceptions, attitudes and behaviour towards its brand (Chen, 2008).
Brand image is considered to be one of the most important contributors towards building consumer base brand
equity. This has been widely discussed and reported in the past literature (Aaker, 1991; Keller, 1993; Martenson,
2007). Aaker (1991) deﬁned consumer-based brand equity as the ‘assets or liabilities which are associated to the
name, term, logo or emblem of a brand, whereby it may enhance or depreciate the value that are generated by a good
or/and service to the companies' brands or customers’ (p. 103). Based on Aaker's (1991) deﬁnition of brand equity,
Chen (2010) deﬁned green brand equity as ‘a set of brand assets and liabilities about green commitment and environmental concerns which are associated with the brand name, symbol and logos that can either elevate or decrease
the value given by the eco-friendly goods and services’ (p. 313). In this environmentally conscious era, cultivating
robust green brand equity can enable companies to achieve competitive advantage by positioning their products differently in the market (Ailawadi and Keller, 2004).
Recent research in the area of green brand equity also reported a positive relationship between green brand
image and green brand equity. For example, Chen (2010) ﬁnds that green brand image has a positive effect on green
brand equity. Similarly, Ng et al. (2014) also reported a positive relationship between green brand image and green
brand equity.
Therefore, this study also proposes that a green brand image will positively inﬂuence consumer-based green
brand equity.
H6: Green brand image relates positively with green brand equity.
Several past studies such as those by Delgado-Ballester and Munuera-Alemán (2005), Morgan and Hunt (1994)
and Jevons and Gabbott (2000) provide empirical evidence suggesting that trust in a brand can exert a positive and
direct inﬂuence in developing its brand equity. Hence, brand trust acts as one of the most important antecedents of

Research Methodology
Sample and Data Collection
To test the proposed hypotheses, a questionnaire-based survey was conducted among undergraduate and postgraduate students of an international university located in the capital city of Islamabad during the year 2012–13. The
potential respondents were required to answer a screening question intended to ensure that they had purchased
an environmentally friendly product within the last six months at the time of survey. Only those who answered
in the afﬁrmative that they had purchased a green product in last six months were invited to proceed further with
the survey questions. The sample comprises undergraduate and postgraduate students, and is based on not only
convenience but also the importance of investigating how these young adults perceive environmental issues and
its subsequent effect on their consumption patterns.
Young adults form a signiﬁcant proportion of developing economies, and thus it is not surprising to notice that
several researchers have raised the importance of having a sophisticated understanding of this segment of the population (Frank and Chong, 2002; Kanchanapibul et al., 2014). It is also an established fact that the future of environmental protection depends on young consumers taking responsibility for preserving nature by adopting responsible
consumption habits (Cini et al., 2012; Maggi et al., 2001). Young consumers are considered more innovative, are
socially, environmentally and culturally conscious and demand more rationality compared with other generations

Results
Results of reliability tests showed that the Cronbach alpha score of consumer concern was 0.748 for environmental
values (CECV), 0.808 for attitudes towards green products (ATE), 0.832 for green brand image (GBIM), 0.848 for
green brand trust (GBTRU) and 0.821 for green brand equity (GBEQIT), indicating acceptable internal consistency
for the scales (Nunnally, 1978). To examine the common method bias, Harman's one-factor test for common
method variance (CMV) was applied to the data (Podsakoff et al., 2003). Exploratory factor analysis (EFA) on one
ﬁxed factor revealed that this factor only explained approximately 38% of variance of the 17 variables. This implies
that no CMV exists (Hair et al., 2010). Furthermore, the proposed model is quite complex in its nature and thus
inherently reduces potential common method bias.
Conﬁrmatory factor analysis was conducted to establish the convergent and discriminant validity of measurement scales. Measurement items that failed to yield a standard loading of more than 0.50 were deleted from the
model (Hair et al., 2010). This resulted in removing two indicators, one item from the consumer green brand image
scale and one item from the consumer green brand equity scale. Table 1 reports the AVE (average variance extracted)
and CR (composite reliability) for each construct in this research model. All the scales yielded acceptable values for
AVE (>0.50) and construct reliability (>0.70), thus establishing convergent reliability (Chen and Chang, 2012; Yap
and Khong, 2006). The analysis of measurement model indices indicates that the data have a good ﬁt with the
model. Chi square was 127.371 (df =109, p = 0.110), CMIN/df was 1.169, RMSEA was 0.035, IFI was 0.983, TLI
was 0.978 and CFI was 0.982.

In accordance with the two-stage modelling, the next step after the conﬁrmation of measurement model is to

model. Chi square (χ 2) was 121.619, p = 0.273 with 113 degrees of freedom. The RMSEA was 0.025, while IFI, TLI
and CFI were 0.991, 0.989 and 0.990, respectively. These results implied that the model has absolute and incremental goodness of ﬁt. The χ 2/df (1.076) was between the thresholds of 1 and 2, further indicating model parsimony. A further test was conducted to validate the sample variance–covariance on the structural model. Since
there were 199 usable responses, a relatively small sample size, a test for multivariate non-normality was performed. The results show that Mardia'’s coefﬁcient implied multivariate non-normality on the dataset (Arbuckle,
2009). Subsequently a bootstrap test was performed on the model with 2000 bootstraps. The Bollen–Stine bootstrap p-value was 0.06, indicating that the model has a good ﬁt (Arbuckle, 2009). The overall bootstrapped model

Furthermore, the residuals of covariance were small, indicating that the model could predict the variance–
covariance matrix. All associations were signiﬁcant (p < 0.05) except for hypothesis H6. These results support our
initial assumption that there is a possibility of a mediating effect in our model (H3a and H7a). H6 posits a positive
relationship between green brand image (GBIM) and green brand equity (GBEQIT), which was not supported in the
present study, indicating the possible inﬂuence of a mediating variable (green brand trust, GBTRU). Additionally,
while consumer environment concern values (CECV) have a positive relationship with green brand equity (GBIM),
consumer attitudes towards green products (ATE) can mediate this relationship.
Based on these results, we further tested for the presence of the mediation effect of attitudes towards green
products and green brand trust (H3a and H7a) by applying a bootstrapping mediation test procedure in order to
establish the possible mediations amongst these associations. A bootstrapping procedure is considered suitable
due to its ability to analyse mediation of complex latent constructs (Kenny, 2012). The results of mediation analysis
are presented in Table 4, based on the extraction of 2000 bootstrap samples with 95% bias-corrected conﬁdence
intervals (Table 5).
Our test for mediation showed that attitudes towards green products (ATE) mediated the association between
concern for environmental values (CECV) and green brand image (GBIM). Similarly, green brand trust (GBTRU)
mediated the association between green brand image (GBIM) and green brand equity (GBEQIT). Furthermore,
mediations between (1) GBIM to GBEQIT (via GBTRU) and (2) CECV to GBIM (via ATE) were signiﬁcant at
α = 0.025%. The direct effect of GBIM and GBEQIT was not signiﬁcant, while the indirect effect was signiﬁcant,
implying that full mediations were present. Meanwhile, the direct and indirect effects of CECV to GBIM were signiﬁcant, implying that partial mediation was present. As a result, we concluded that GBTRU fully mediated the
relationship between GBIM and GBEQIT while ATE partially mediated the relationship and CECV and GBIM.

Discussion and Conclusion
The renewed interest in the area of green marketing has generated new perspectives in the literature from both
organizational and consumer perspectives (Chen and Chang, 2012; Lee et al., 2010; Park et al., 2012). In order to
expand the existing literature, this study proposed and tested an integrated model examining the effect of consumers' concern for environmental values and their general attitudes towards green products on green brand image
and its subsequent inﬂuence on green brand trust and green brand equity. In other words, consumer environmental
concern values are positively related to brand relationship constructs via a series of mediating variables. This is a
signiﬁcant step forward in the literature of green marketing, as it provides the ﬁrst empirical evidence that the
two schools of research (behaviouristic and branding oriented) are interlinked.
In line with social cognition theory, the results suggest a strong linkage between consumers' concern for environmental values and their general attitude towards green products. This validates several previous studies, which have
demonstrated a positive relationship between consumers' concern for environmental values and their buying attitude towards green products (Kim and Chung, 2011; McCarty and Shrum, 1994; Milfont, 2007).
Furthermore, it also provides empirical evidence that these values partially mediate through consumer general
attitudes towards green product to inﬂuence green brands' image in a positive way, thus providing meaningful support to our argument that there is a positive association between consumers' general attitudes and values and their
perception of a speciﬁc brand. The positive linkage between consumer general attitude towards green products and
brand image suggests that marketers not only need to invest in building positive perceptions of their own green

than a unilateral approach. This could be achieved through a two-tiered marketing communication strategy aimed at
creating general awareness and building environmental concern values among consumers as well as promoting the

In addition, strong and positive relationships were found among brand knowledge structure and consumer relational preferences, such as a positive relationship between green brand image and trust. These results are in support
of most of the previous ﬁndings that explored relationships between consumers' brand knowledge structure and
their relational preferences in a green marketing context (Chen, 2010; Kang and Hur, 2012).
Nonetheless, our results showed no direct relationship between green brand image and green brand equity. This
is due to the presence of green brand trust as a fully mediating variable for the relationship between the two constructs. The results of this mediation test showed that trust is an important dimension in building green brand
equity, since image alone is insufﬁcient to generate green brand equity unless it is supplemented by consumer trust
of that brand. Therefore, marketers interested in pursuing green marketing strategies ﬁrst need to build into their
products green attributes, which are then supported by communication messages, thereby building trust among
consumers.
We draw several implications from the results of this study. This study provides empirical evidence that consumer concern for environmental values and general attitudes towards green products are important antecedents
of green brand image. Thus, organizations that are involved in the development and promotion of green products
can pool their resources together through environmental collaborations to build a national or international promotional campaign that helps strengthen consumer concern for environmental values and general attitudes towards
green products. For example, in a recent study, researchers empirically validated the positive impact of a ﬁrm's
ability to develop and implement environmental collaborations on the ﬁrm's market and image performance
(Dangelico and Pontrandolfo, 2015). The advertising campaign ‘Got Milk’ is one example from the past that shows
how a particular industry can pool together to build positive public opinion towards a product category (Bower and
Mateer, 2008; Hsu and McDonald, 2002). Therefore, we also suggest that, apart from individual investment in
promoting their green brands, ﬁrms can pool resources to launch a systematic campaign to create awareness of
green products in general and to reinforce consumers' environmental values and attitudes towards green
products.
Furthermore, when a ﬁrm integrates green marketing approaches in its overall business strategy, it stimulates a
culture of innovation to incorporate new attributes into products, which when experienced by consumers will
encourage trust in them, which in turn can have a positive effect on brand equity. Thus only those organizations
that incorporated green marketing initiatives as an integral part of their overall business strategy were successful
in attracting environmentally concerned customers (Chamorro and Bañegil, 2006). This is further supported by
recent research that provides empirical evidence suggesting a positive relationship between consumer innovativeness and early adoption of products with green attributes (Jansson, 2011). A ﬁrm may by positioning itself as a
frontrunner for green innovation ﬁnd it easier to generate a feeling of self-congruence between its brand and early
adopters, which are the most important target segment for markets of new products.

the implicit motivations for preference for green products using qualitative approaches such as projective techniques or laddering. Based on these methods, new dimensions of why and how consumers favour green brands
