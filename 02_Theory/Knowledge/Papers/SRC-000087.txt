Abstract 

This systematic review examines how different components of brand equity models, particularly Consumer-Based 
Brand  Equity  (CBBE),  effectively  measure  and  explain  brand  value  across  industries.  The  initial  search  was   
conducted electronically in Scopus, Web of Science, ScienceDirect, JSTOR, EBSCOhost, ProQuest, and Google 
Scholar using keywords such as “brand equity” or “value” and “consumer” and “customer-based brand equity”. 
Analysis of 24 peer-reviewed articles confirms that four key dimensions, brand awareness, brand associations, 
brand loyalty, and perceived quality, serve as reliable measures of brand equity across different industry sectors. 
The study also revealed a structural relationship between the brand equity dimensions. This study identifies the 
core  components  of  brand  equity  that  organisations  can  leverage  to  strengthen  their  brand  positioning  and 
competitive advantage. It explained the relationship between the components of the key brand equity dimensions 
that would guide firms in developing their brand equity approach to advance their brand competitiveness in the 
market. 

Keywords:  brand  equity,  CBBE,  brand  identity,  brand  loyalty,  brand  awareness,  brand  associations,  perceived 
quality 

1. Introduction 

Contemporary marketing strategies focus on building strong consumer relationships and brand differentiation to 
enhance  market  competitiveness.  This  shift  has  led  companies  to  develop  distinctive  brand  identities  as  a  key 
differentiating factor in competitive markets. Marketing campaigns are currently aimed at establishing solid brands 
alongside powerful brand equity to enhance market competitiveness effectively. A strong brand shapes a business’s 
marketing strategies and guarantees high brand equity, enabling firms to relish higher margins, greater customer 
satisfaction and loyalty, and enhanced cooperation.    This, therefore, makes it essential for brands to conceptualise 
equity models and measures.   

Brand equity, a fundamental concept in this respect, has been defined by researchers in various ways. Aaker refers 
to it as “a set of brand assets and liabilities linked to a brand, its name and symbol that add to or subtract from the 
value provided by product or service to a firm and its customers” (Aaker, 1991). On the other hand, Keller (1993, 
p.1)  defines  brand  equity  as  “the  differential  effect  of  brand  knowledge  on  consumer  response  to  the  brand's 
marketing”. Yoo and Donthu similarly define it as “a consumer's different response between a focal brand and an 
unbranded product when both have the same level of the marketing stimuli and product attributes” (Yoo & Donthu, 
2001). Regardless, there seems to be a consensus on brand equity, simplifying it as the incremental utility or value 
added to a product by the brand's name (Farquhar, 1989).   

Previously, Keller and Lehmann stated that brand equity could be measured from corporate or financial-focused 
brand equity (FBBE) and perception or consumer-based brand equity (CBBE), tentatively the primary brand equity 
models  (Keller  &  Lehmann,  2006;  2003).  However,  the  FBBE  exhibited  limitations  in  terms  of  providing  an 
unbiased estimation of a brand’s intrinsic value merely on business finances or accounting value (Aaker, 1991; 
Keller, 2010). Hence, the CBBE approach has become much preferable in marketing research due to its capacity 
to accord businesses a strategic vision of consumer behaviors to develop brand strategies appropriately (Kim et al., 
2008; Prasad & Dev, 2000), particularly by positively influencing the brand’s equity on customers’ brand choice 
and commitment (Cobb-Walgren et al., 1995), firm’s financial performance (Tolba & Hassan, 2009), and brand 
extension (Pitta & Katsanis, 1995). Aaker (1991) identified the four major dimensions of CBBE: brand awareness, 
perceived quality, brand loyalty and brand association, which constitute brand equity measurements.   

Accordingly, these perceptual dimensions from Aaker (1996), Keller (2010) and Yoo and Donthu (2001) form the

framework of a scale to measure brand equity. However, reservations are made on the capacity of this model to 
fully and sufficiently explain brand equity since the CBBE model centres around Keller’s and Aaker’s concept, 
mainly because Keller, albeit giving a framework to measure the dimensions, fails to provide a nexus between 
brand awareness and brand equity as core measures of brand equity. Aaker, on similar accounts, only indicates the 
items  contributing  to  brand  equity  but  does  not  explain  which  items  are  combined  to  attribute  brand  equity. 
However, Nam et al.’s model does not recognise brand awareness, a crucial equity dimension, per Aaker’s (1991) 
and Keller’s (1993) models.   

The  current  study  concerns  whether  these  dimensions  are  exhaustive  or  whether  other  crucial  measurement 
elements would reliably and validly exhibit brand quality. In particular, the choice of items measuring brand equity 
is arbitrary due to the need for more literature on the extent to which the existing dimensions contribute to brand 
equity attainment. This review investigates the extent to which the various dimensions or elements identified by 
various articles exhibit a constructive and comprehensive assessment, explanation or meaning of brand equity as 
conceptualised by existing brand equity models, including CBBE.   

2. Method 

We  conducted  this  systematic  review  following  established  PRISMA  guidelines  to  ensure  comprehensive  and 
transparent reporting.   

2.1 Search Strategy 

To  search  published  research  in  English  without  regard  to  the  publishing  timeframe,  we  utilise  seven 
comprehensive separate databases. We also searched these databases to identify any existing systematic reviews 
on  brand  equity  measurement  to  avoid  duplication  and  identify  research  gaps.  The  systematic  search  was 
conducted between March and May 2024, covering publications from 1990 to December 2023. Using Endnote 
software, we merged findings from several databases and eliminated duplicate results using the following search 
terms: "brand equity" OR "consumer-based brand equity" OR "CBBE" AND "measurement" OR "scale" combined 
with “brand awareness”, “brand loyalty”, “perceived quality,” and “brand associations”. Final search validation 
was completed on May 10, 2024. Search strategies are listed in Supplementary file 1. 

2.2 Studies Selection 

Duplicates  were  removed,  and  every  article  was  extracted  and  saved  in  its  own  EndNote  library (ENDNOTE, 
2015). Two separate reviewers chose which studies to include. While reviewer 2 approved papers based on the 
data  and  resolved  any  arguments  on  any  included  research,  reviewer  1  reviewed  abstracts  and  titles  twice, 
independently. After being thoroughly reviewed by reviewers, the publications were selected for inclusion based 
on the inclusion and exclusion criteria listed in Table 1 to determine if they contained the pertinent data for the 
systematic review.

2.3 Risk Bias Assessment   

The Newcastle Ottawa Scale (NOS) was used to assess the risk bias of the included studies. Studies were given 
low, medium, and high scores according to the following criteria: bias in the selection process, bias in interventions, 
bias in departures from interventions, bias resulting from missing data, bias in outcomes, and bias in results. Using 
inclusion and exclusion criteria, preference for selection was rated. Performance bias was assessed by outlining a 
control  arm  and  taking  allocation  concealment  into  account.  Biased  reporting,  selective  reporting,  data 
management,  and  full  industry  sponsorship  were  ranked  differently.  At  many  meetings,  reviewers  looked  at 
eligibility  restrictions  and  reporting  uniformity.  Before  choosing  a  research  topic,  a  second  reviewer  took  into 
account any discrepancies in the reviewers' ratings. 

3. Results 

3.1 Search Results 

The search process results are summarised in the PRISMA flow chart below (Fig.1). The search strategy yielded 
a cumulative of 6749 publications. Four more sources were identified via reference or bibliography list tracking 
and  included  in  the  review. After  de-duplication,  3412  articles  were  left,  amongst  which  2197  records  were 
excluded after the title and abstract screening. Of the 103 articles subjected to full-text screening, 79 were further 
excluded.  Only  24  articles  that  report  brand  equity  dimensions  and  allied  measurements  were  considered  and 
included in this review. Twenty-four articles were included in the final analysis, as the study specifically focused 
on  empirical  studies  using  surveys  or  questionnaire-based  data  collection  methods  to  measure  brand  equity 
components.

3.4 Underlying Brand Equity Definitions and Models Conceptualisation   

Most researchers have adopted varied approaches to measure and quantify brand equity. Most researchers are, 
however, guided by the original conceptualisation of Aaker (Aaker, 1996; Aaker, 1991) and Keller (Keller, 1993; 
2010) on the brand equity concept. Keller (1993), for instance, referred to CBBE as “the differential effect of brand 
knowledge  on  consumer  response  to  the  marketing  of  the  brand”,  implying  concepts  of  brand  knowledge 
encompassing brand awareness and image. Per Aaker (1991), brand equity encompasses brand assets and liabilities, 
not limited to customer- or consumer-based. Aaker (1996) further introduced satisfaction and price premium as 
brand loyalty dimensions, leadership as a subset of perceived quality, organisational associations, brand personality 
and perceived value  as  subsets  for brand  differentiation or  associations measure  components,  especially  in  the 
hotel and tourism industry. Researchers such as Yoo and Donthu (2001) included brand equity similar to brand 
loyalty.  The  other  CBBE  components  that  have  since  been  introduced  to  conceptualise  brand  equity  include 
sustainability, brand trust, reliability, quality of experience, personal behaviour and attitude (Sürücü et al., 2019), 
brand engagement (Huerta-Álvarez et al., 2020), and brand uniqueness (Lin et al., 2015). Comprehensively,

3.5 Measuring Brand Equity   

3.5.1 Brand awareness   

Among  the  identified  measures,  most  studies  have  frequently  used  brand  awareness  as  a  measurement  or 
component  of  brand  equity  in  CBBE  measurement  development.  Initially  regarded  as  a  distinct  component  of 
CBBE,  and  particularly  a  launchpad  for  building  up  to  other  components, Aaker  (1991)  has  idealised  brand 
awareness as the ability of potential consumers or buyers to recognise or recall a brand into specific categories 
given various brand sets. On this same note, familiarity implies the customer’s knowledge and experience with a 
particular  brand  and  is  acknowledged  to  impact  customer  behavioural  variables  significantly.  Given  the 
conceptualisations, brand awareness is thus operationalised as an ability to distinguish and recall one brand among 
many, thus a measure of equity value. Twelve included studies discussed brand awareness, revealing its significant 
mediator role as a measure of brand equity (Chen & Tseng, 2010; Chen et al., 2019; Han et al., 2015; Hurriyati & 
Setiawan, 2017; Jeon & Yoo, 2021; Menon, 2019; Muhammad & Muath, 2018; Naggar & Bendary, 2017; Poonam 
& Dr. Sushil Sharma, 2023; Qiao et al., 2022; Shariq, 2019; Sürücü et al., 2019). For instance, Shariq (2019) found 
that brand awareness significantly influences brand loyalty, substantially impacting brand equity in the fast-moving 
consumer goods industry. Shabbir similarly implied that brand awareness fully mediates the influence of brand 
loyalty  and  brand  image  on overall  consumer  brand  equity  (Shabbir  et  al.,  2017). Accordingly,  Chen  & Tseng 
(2010),  in  the  context  of  the  airline  service  industry,  connote  that  service  quality  boosted  the  influence  of 
consumers’ repurchase intention by fostering a positive link between brand awareness and consumer perceived 
value on brand equity. In the identified measures, brand awareness has been widely used in most studies as either 
a measurement or as part of brand equity CBBE measurement development. Nonetheless, most of the studies have 
not provided psychometric validation or cited published scales to measure brand awareness, as evident in the study 
conducted  by Yoo  &  Donthu  (2001)  and  Netemeyer  et  al.  (2004),  demonstrating  a  deficiency  of  the  available 
literature concerning standard measure accreditation. 

3.5.2 Brand Association/Image   

Aaker (1996) defines brand identity as a “unique set of brand associations that brand strategists aspire to create 
and maintain. Thus, these associations represent what the brand stands for, including a promise to customers”. 
According to Aaker (1991), brand association refers to a customer’s memory of a brand. For Keller 1993), brand 
associations are “the other informational nodes linked to the brand node in memory and contain the meaning of 
the brand”, hence the “image”. This implies brand association or image as anything according to the meaning of a 
brand.  In  this  study,  eight  articles  conceptualised  brand  associations  or  image  as  CBBE's  distinct  measure, 
including other distinct brand personality constructs, especially in the hotel and tourism industry (Dada, 2021; Han 
et al., 2015; H. M. Jeon & Yoo, 2021; Menon, 2019; Muhammad & Muath, 2018; Naggar & Bendary, 2017; Shariq, 
2019; Sürücü et al., 2019). Mainly, Han et al. (2015), for example, attribute brand reputation as a crucial subset of 
brand association, critical in establishing brand trust for overall brand equity. Jeon & Yoo (2021) also attribute 
brand experience to perceived value as a crucial construct of brand association or image, perceived quality, and 
brand awareness, which constitute fundamental measures for brand loyalty and attainable overall brand equity in 
the  context  of  food  service  grocery  consumers. According  to  Naggar  &  Bendary  (2017),  brand  loyalty  was  a 
product of CBBE measures such as brand association, brand experience and perceived brand quality. In another 
study,  Qiao  et  al.  (2022)  acknowledged  that  even  though  product  perceived  value  had  no  formidable  direct 
association with consumer-based brand equity, it positively influences consumer affective commitment and brand 
resonance, which mediates brand-perceived value association and, ultimately, brand equity. There has been some 
benefit in that brand association is still one of the leading constructs, although few studies administered validated 
scales or cite existing frameworks to measure it, which limits the possibility of comparing findings. 

3.5.3 Perceived Quality   

Perceived quality generally relates to the opinions of consumers and customers on the extent to which a product 
or service meets their expectations. Parasuraman et al. (1988) attribute perceived quality as a five-dimensional 
concept of reliability, tangibles, responsiveness, assurance and empathy (SERVQUAL); unlike other scholars who 
operate the CBBE framework, it refers to operationalising it as a consumer value dimension. However, based on 
the CBBE framework, it refers to the customer’s judgement on a product's overall excellence or superiority. In this 
regard, it is arguable that perceived quality has no direct relationship with the actual brand or product performance. 
Among the studies included in this study, ten discussed perceived quality as a dimensional measure of brand equity 
or antecedent of other dimensional components based on the industry contexts (Chen & Tseng, 2010; Choi et al., 
2017; Han et al., 2015; Jeon & Yoo, 2021; Muhammad & Muath, 2018; Naggar & Bendary, 2017; Netemeyer et 
al.,  2004;  Poonam  &  Dr. Sushil  Sharma,  2023;  Shariq,  2019;  Sürücü  et  al., 2019).  Consumers'  high perceived

Trujillo  (2017),  self-identification  with  a  brand,  termed  self-congruence,  and  perceived  brand  quality  are  key 
variable measures for brand loyalty. In the context of the brewing industry, Juga et al. (2018) found that value-
adding services were crucial in establishing a company’s brand equity via perceived service quality, contributing 
to its significant dimensional measure. Again, Jeon & Yoo (2021) demonstrated the influence of brand experience 
on perceived value as an antecedent of the perceived quality measure of consumer brand equity, incorporating the 
affective,  sensory,  behavioural  and  intellectual  factors.  From  a  financial  perspective,  Netemeyer  et  al.  (2004) 
suggest  that  perceived  quality,  alongside  perceived  value  for  the  cost  and  uniqueness  of  a  brand,  are  typically 
essential direct measures of willingness to pay a price premium for a product, which consequently translates to 
customers’ brand purchase behaviour. Naggar and Bendary (2017) also argued that besides brand associations, the 
perceived brand quality potentially bolsters brand loyalty by establishing a trustful and reliable association among 
consumers of mobile services, hence, according to brand equity. Although being widespread, the operationalisation 
of  perceived  quality  was  varied  as  well,  and  most  of  the  research  did  not  refer  to  well-known  scales  such  as 
SERVQUAL or other scales used basis. 

3.5.4 Brand Loyalty 

Brand loyalty is defined as “an attained state of enduring preference to the point of determined defence”, where 
the endurance is attributed to consumers purchasing the brand repeatedly, and the point of defence implies the 
consumers  defending  themselves  from  competitor  brands.  However,  brand  loyalty  has  traversed  various 
operational  and  conceptual  crises  similar  to  the  above  dimensions. Definitions  from  past  scholars  reflect  overt 
behaviours in conceptualising brand loyalty, also termed as customer or consumer loyalty, and include intentions 
and  positive  word  of  mouth,  repeat  purchases,  and  covert  attitudinal  behaviours  (price  sensitivity,  liking  and 
preference). The initial operationalisation of the CBBE framework failed to capture the attitudinal and behavioural 
nature of loyalty, defining brand loyalty as “the attachment that a customer has to a brand” (Aaker, 1991). Recent 
studies have, however, included satisfaction and price premium as indicators or measures of brand loyalty. In this 
review, 17 studies operationalized and conceptualized brand loyalty and its antecedents as crucial measures of 
brand equity (Bose et al., 2020; Chen & Tseng, 2010; Choi et al., 2017; Dada, 2021; Ebrahim, 2020; Hurriyati & 
Setiawan, 2017; Jeon & Yoo, 2021; Juga et al., 2018; Kressmann et al., 2006; Mabkhot et al., 2017; Muhammad 
& Muath, 2018; Naggar & Bendary, 2017; Ong et al., 2015; Poonam & Dr. Sushil Sharma, 2023; Şahin et al., 2011; 
Shariq, 2019; Vera & Trujillo, 2017). Findings by Choi et al. 2017) extrapolate that brand personality and brand 
experience directly influence brand prestige, which consequently impacts the quality of brand relationships and 
behavioural  attitudinal  loyalty.  In  the  context  of  the hotel and  tourism  industry,  Sürücü  et  al.  2019)  claim  that 
enhanced customer satisfaction and brand trust are significant measures worth considering while building customer 
loyalty as an overall measure or dimension for CBBE. On the same course, Sahin et al. (2011) allude that brand 
trust, experience and satisfaction certainly positively influence brand loyalty as a measure of brand equity. In the 
assessment of factors that influenced decisions for visitation to a particular tourist site, Hurriyati and Setiawan 
(2017) established that destination personality, despite positively correlating with brand attractiveness, is both a 
critical measure of brand loyalty since it positively influences customer loyalty to the brand, and thus overall brand 
equity. Research by Ong et al. (2015) also ascertained that brand experience is probably a measure of brand equity 
through its critical mediating influence on behavioural and attitudinal brand loyalty. Even though brand loyalty 
was the most frequently addressed indicator, in less than half of the papers, there was the use of validated scales 
or declarations to original works like that of Aaker (1991) or Keller (1993) in operational definitions. 

3.5.5 Structural Associations among Brand Equity Components   

Although the CBBE conceptualisation by Aaker (1996) does not suggest any imminent relationship between the 
dimensional  constructs,  awareness  was  implied  as  the  primary  measure  for  the  other  components  since  it 
influenced attitudes and perceptions. Keller's (1993) model alternatively presented a pyramid with brand salience 
at  the  base,  followed  by  brand  performance  and  imagery,  brand  judgments  defined  by  brand  performance, 
consumer  effect  defined  by  imagery,  and  consumer  brand  resonance.  However,  there  needed  to  be  an 
operationalised clear relationship among the core equity components. In this regard, researchers have revealed 
various  structural  correlations  among  these  CBBE  model  dimensions.  For  instance,  loyalty  is  considered  an 
outcome or a component of CBBE. Concerning Shabbir et al. (2017), findings reported that brand awareness fully 
mediated the influence of brand loyalty on brand equity, implying that brand loyalty was an outcome of brand 
awareness. In the article by Bose et al. (2020), brand equity is perceived as a CBBE component mediated by place 
brand salience, engagement, and perceived quality to boost brand equity. In the investigation by Mabkhot et al. 
(2017), a quality relationship was found between brand loyalty and brand image. Brand trust was also noted as a 
measure of brand personality and brand loyalty, depicting the latter as distinct customer brand equity components.

Ong et al. (2015) also conceptualised brand loyalty as a CBBE component by reporting that brand experience 
mediated behavioural and attitudinal loyalty facets of brand equity. The same was the case in other studies, which 
acknowledged the structural relationship of CBBE measures by considering brand loyalty as an integral component, 
alluding to the mediating roles played by brand trust, experience, destination personality, brand attractiveness and 
satisfaction with brand loyalty (Hurriyati & Setiawan, 2017; Sahin et al., 2011). 

4. Discussion 

Our analysis reveals ongoing challenges in brand equity research, particularly regarding standardised definitions 
and measurement approaches across different contexts and industries (Buil et al., 2008; 2013; Veloutsou et al., 
2013; 2020). Despite brand equity being a key issue in marketing, its conceptualisation still needs to be revised 
due to the contradictory viewpoints on its theoretical framework and dimensional building blocks that act as its 
antecedents and measures as an intangible marketing asset (Ishaq & Maria, 2020). Whereas consumer-based brand 
equity (CBBE) is of most interest in terms of brand equity to most studies because of its implications on strategies 
of  company  behaviour,  financial-based  brand  equity  (FBBE)  is  of  significant  consideration  in  determining  the 
conversion of brand value into firm performance and shareholder value. A contrasting take on the integration of 
CBBE  and  FBBE  would  render  a  greater  picture  of  brand  equity,  particularly  when  it  comes  to  resonatingthe 
resonance  of  marketing  with  financial  results.  Current  research  predominantly  focuses  on  consumer-based 
measures,  with  limited  integration  of  financial  perspectives  in  brand  equity  assessment. As  an  example,  the 
Interbrand brand valuation method, or a brand contribution ranking view, is financial-based in nature and offers a 
quantitative, balance sheet-oriented analysis. CBBE, on the contrary, is more dependent on the perceptions of the 
consumer. A healthy debate on brand equity should focus on both sides to make it more theoretical and practical 

Brand equity presents both perceptual and financial dimensions. However, perceptual equity has stood as a proxy 
for financial equity since most studies have focused on establishing the influence of most perceptual indicators in 
different industrial brand contexts. This one-dimensional CBBE approach to dimensions - brand awareness, loyalty, 
perceived quality, brand associations - confines us to the understanding of the contribution of intangible perceptual 
factors to tangible financial performance. Also, it can be emphasised that the discussion of brand equity can be 
enhanced by incorporating more general theoretical developments that started to guide the resources-based view 
(RBV) and signalling theory. Within the RBV, brand equity can be viewed as a strategic intangible resource which 
is in the form of a sustainable competitive advantage. Through a signalling theory perspective, dimensions of brand 
equity are plausible indicators of product quality and reliability of the firm, which affects consumer choice in the 
presence of asymmetric information. Such theories fill the gap between the perceptual and the financial strategies 
towards brand equity.   

This  study,  however,  reveals  that  the  multifaceted  brand  equity  elements  and  their  interrelations  are  built  and 
measured in numerous diverse ways based on the context of the brand product or service industry. As such, there 
should  be  standardisation  to  develop  a  robust  measure  of  brand  equity  for  each  product  or  brand  context.  To 
overcome  these  shortcomings,  this  paper  postulates  a  consolidated  brand  equity  model  which  integrates  both 
CBBE and FBBE based on a dual lens; the consumer perception and the financial perspective lens, which is based 
on  RBV  and  signalling  theories.  Having  such  a  framework  can  help  coordinate  the  evaluations  of  brands  by 
marketing people and financial managers in an evidence-based approach. Towards this call, this suggests deploying 
uniform CBBE dimensions, measures and structural relationships. However, brand awareness is considered brand 
familiarity, brand association or brand image, brand loyalty, perceived quality or consumer value are considered 
the complete CBBE components, and their relations and antecedents can be standardised as a measure for brand 
equity.     

The first limitation of this study is that it was only possible to include some of the identified resources on this topic, 
limiting the robustness of this analysis. Again, despite following the PRISMA guidelines, the subjective bias during 
the process might have led to a lack of critical literature that could be insightful for this topic. There needed to be 
more evaluation of evidence quality, and this study only provided a descriptive summary of the measurements 
identified from the included articles, implying a lack of reference accreditation and validation standards for each 
identified measure or construct of brand equity 

5. Conclusions and Future Directions 

Brand equity is considered an essential asset for any industry; hence, this critical review aims to highlight this 
concept  and  mainly  review  variables  of  its  conceptualization,  dimensions  and  measurements  and,  finally,  the 
structural interrelationships presented by its components, the recent business competition, firms have recognized 
the importance of understanding or establishing brand equity to develop and enhance competitive advantage in the 
business  competitive  world.  This  review  summarises  empirical  studies  attributing  brand  equity  to  various

equity components and their interrelationships, offering practical insights for brand management strategies.   

This study advocates for future research that needs to distinguish the crucial measures of financial-based brand 
equity from consumer or customer-based brand equity. This is so because, despite FBBE and CBBE being related 
or consummate concepts, CBBE tends to focus on consumer or customer perspectives, measured by perceptual 
elements. At the same time, FBBE attributes to the financial basis that depicts a brand’s monetary value from the 
equity  components.  Moreover,  this  study  identifies  a  close  relationship  between  brand  equity,  its  components, 
antecedents,  and  consequences.  Strong  brands  typically  demonstrate  positive  reinforcement  cycles  where  high 
equity  drives  further brand  strength,  while weak  brands may  experience  declining  equity  over  time. The  latter 
presents a significant research gap that needs to be addressed by future studies evaluating such feedback's impact 
on product value or brand equity.
