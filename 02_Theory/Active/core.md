# BEBA Core Theory

## Working Definition

Brand-Enabled Behaviour Architecture (BEBA) conceptualises nonprofit brand equity as a multi-layer architecture linking institutional context, observable signals, donor perceptions, psychological belief formation, behavioural enactment and resource mobilisation.

## Core Claim

Nonprofit brands become behaviourally relevant because donors face epistemic uncertainty about organisational effectiveness, integrity and impact. Brand equity functions as a structured signal system that reduces or reorganises this uncertainty and thereby changes the psychological conditions under which philanthropic intentions and behaviours emerge.

## Why BEBA Is an Architecture

BEBA is not a single latent variable and not a simple causal chain. It is an architecture because it connects different functional layers:

1. Institutional Layer: welfare state, nonprofit field, legitimacy, accountability and organisational role.
2. Signal Layer: brands, reputation, certifications, communication and observable organisational cues.
3. Brand Equity Layer: awareness, image, personality, trust, commitment, authenticity and reputation.
4. Belief Formation Layer: attitude, norms, perceived behavioural control and related beliefs.
5. Enactment Layer: knowledge, salience, constraints, habit and opportunity.
6. Resource Layer: donations, volunteering, advocacy, in-kind support and legacy giving.

## Difference from Existing Models

Faircloth, Boenigk and Becker, and Rios Romero et al. are treated as existing measurement architectures of nonprofit brand equity. BEBA extends this logic from measurement to behaviour. It asks not only which construct structure fits, but how brand signals become behaviourally relevant under uncertainty.

## Preliminary Propositions

P1: The more difficult it is for donors to observe organisational effectiveness directly, the more relevant brand-based signals become.

P2: Awareness is a necessary access condition but not sufficient for resource mobilisation.

P3: Trust and commitment are likely to be closer to behavioural outcomes than general familiarity or image.

P4: Brand equity effects on donation behaviour are mediated by behavioural beliefs and intentions.

P5: The intention-behaviour relationship is moderated by enactment conditions such as knowledge, salience, habit and constraints.

P6: Brand-equity components may follow a maturity logic: awareness precedes image, trust, commitment and advocacy, but their marginal relevance changes across stages.

## Boundary Conditions

- Donations may be driven by moral duty or social norms independent of brand signals.
- Highly institutionalised organisations may benefit from sectoral trust even when brand equity is weak.
- Scandals can reverse the function of strong awareness by making negative information salient.
- Strong brand awareness without trust may not translate into giving.
- Different resource behaviours may depend on different layers of the architecture.

## Status

Active working theory. To be refined through literature reviews, empirical modelling and chapter drafting.
